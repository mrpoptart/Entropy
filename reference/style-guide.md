# Entropy — Style Guide

This document defines the prose style, conventions, and voice for the Entropy trilogy. It should be referenced during all drafting to maintain consistency.

---

## Point of View

**Third person limited, Ash only.**

The entire trilogy is told from Ash's perspective. The reader never sees a scene Ash isn't present for. We never enter another character's thoughts — we only know what other characters think through what Ash observes: their words, their expressions, their actions.

This means:
- Other characters can surprise both Ash and the reader.
- Ash's blind spots are the reader's blind spots. When he's in denial, the prose may reflect that denial. When he misreads someone, the reader misreads them too.
- Information Ash doesn't have access to must reach him (and the reader) through dialogue, observation, or discovery.

---

## Tense

**Past tense.**

This is a story being told about things that happened. The past tense creates the sense of a tale passed down — fitting for a generational story about the end of an era.

> He crossed the bridge and paused at the far side, one hand resting on the stone where the old channeling had started to crack.

Not:

> He crosses the bridge and pauses at the far side.

---

## Narrative Distance

**Variable — grounded baseline, deep close for emotional peaks.**

The default narrative distance is **medium third person**. The narrator is warm, close to Ash, but maintains its own voice. This gives room for clean worldbuilding, scene-setting, and the conversational tone that defines the series.

At emotional peaks — moments of fear, grief, wonder, self-deception, intimacy — the prose pulls into **deep close third**. The narrator disappears. The prose becomes Ash's mind: his rhythms, his fragments, his patterns of thought.

The shift in distance is itself a storytelling tool. When the prose changes, the reader feels the change before they understand it.

### Medium Distance (Default)

Used for: scene-setting, routine action, dialogue scenes, worldbuilding, transitions.

> The inspection had taken most of the morning. Ash worked his way along the Thornwall aqueduct, checking each joint where the old channeling had started to fracture, marking the worst spots in his notebook with Dorenne's shorthand. Routine work. The kind he was good at.

Characteristics:
- Clean, declarative sentences.
- The narrator observes Ash with proximity and warmth.
- Can describe things Ash might not articulate himself.
- Worldbuilding woven into action naturally.

### Deep Close (Emotional Peaks)

Used for: activation, the math scene, the earthquake, sibling conversations, moments of grief or wonder, any scene where the reader needs to feel what Ash feels.

> Heat. In his chest, in his arms, in the roots of his teeth. Not pain exactly but the memory of pain, the shape of it, every nerve in his body firing at once and he couldn't breathe, couldn't think, couldn't—
>
> The stone beneath his palm was glowing.

Characteristics:
- Sentence fragments. Repetition. Shorter paragraphs.
- Ash's thought patterns take over the prose.
- Physical sensation as the primary register of emotion.
- When Ash is lying to himself, the prose lies too.
- Interior monologue surfaces (italicized).

### Transition Between Distances

The shift should feel organic, not jarring. Often a single line bridges the gap — a physical detail, an interrupted thought, a sensory intrusion that pulls us from the narrative surface into Ash's interior.

> Routine work. The kind he was good at.
>
> Then his hand touched the crack at the base of the fourth pillar, and the world turned white.

---

## Prose Style

**Conversational and grounded, with controlled lyrical peaks.**

The baseline voice is clean, modern, and accessible. Short-to-medium sentences. Precise verbs. Minimal adjectives. The prose trusts the reader and doesn't overexplain. It feels like a person telling a story, not an author performing.

Think: Brandon Sanderson's clarity and pacing, Becky Chambers' warmth, Patrick Ness's restraint. Not Robert Jordan's density. Not purple prose.

### The Grounded Baseline

> Haran's workshop smelled like sawdust and oil and the sharp tang of cut metal. Ash had spent half his life in this room, and he still didn't know where half the tools went.

Characteristics:
- Concrete sensory detail (smell, texture, sound) over abstract description.
- Dry humor when appropriate — Ash notices absurdity.
- Sentences vary in length but trend short-to-medium.
- No thesaurus words. If "said" works, use "said." If "walked" works, use "walked."
- Let subtext do the work. Characters don't say everything they mean.

### Controlled Lyrical Peaks

At key moments — maybe once or twice per chapter — the prose is allowed to be beautiful. Not ornate. Not metaphor-heavy. Just a sentence or passage where the rhythm opens up and lands with weight.

> The last of the light left the stone like a breath going out. Ash stood in the dark with his hands open and nothing in them, and for the first time since the Wellspring had cracked open inside him, the silence didn't feel like something missing. It felt like something he'd chosen.

These moments are earned by pages of grounded writing. They mark turning points, realizations, endings. They should never feel like the author showing off — they should feel inevitable.

### What to Avoid

- **Over-description.** Trust the reader to fill in gaps. A room needs two or three details, not ten.
- **Explaining emotions.** Show physical response and behavior. Don't write "He felt sad." Write what sadness looks like in his body and actions.
- **Exposition dumps.** The magic system, the political landscape, the history — all of it enters through action, dialogue, and Ash's lived experience. Never pause the story to lecture.
- **Adverb-heavy dialogue tags.** "He said angrily" is almost always weaker than showing the anger in the dialogue and action.
- **Repetitive sentence structure.** Vary rhythm. A string of short sentences is punchy; a string of long ones is hypnotic. Use both deliberately.

## Show Don't Tell — Pattern Taxonomy

Telling intrudes in four recognizable patterns. All four share the same root impulse: the narrator stepping in front of the scene to interpret it rather than trusting the scene to speak.

### Pattern 1: Abstract Behavior Description

The prose describes the *nature* of an action instead of performing it. Steps back and editorializes on what a character is doing rather than showing what they are actually doing.

**Tell:**
> He'd been watching the way you watched when watching was your only tool.

**Show:**
> He'd noticed things, that second week. Which aides arrived early. Which ones checked the door. Where Dorenne's hands were when she listened — still when she agreed, moving when she didn't.

The fix: replace the abstract summary with the specific observations themselves.

---

### Pattern 2: State Declarations

Labeling an internal state — emotion, disposition, quality — instead of rendering it through behavior, sensation, or action.

**Tell:**
> He was nervous. She was angry. He felt out of place.

**Show:**
> He straightened the jar of pens on his desk. Straightened it again.

The fix: find the physical behavior the state would produce. Show that instead.

---

### Pattern 3: Appositive Editorializing

The narrator reaches into the scene to interpret something the scene could convey itself. Often introduced by *which*, *that*, *the kind of*, or *the sort of*.

**Tell:**
> Her voice did not carry, which was worse.
> The kind of volume that came from years of speaking in rooms where everyone else was paid to listen.

**Show:**
> Her voice did not carry. Aldren had to lean forward.

The fix: cut the interpretation. Let the scene action carry the meaning. If the meaning isn't legible without the editorial, the scene detail needs to be stronger — not the annotation.

---

### Pattern 4: Passive Voice as Distance

Passive constructions often signal the prose pulling away from a concrete agent doing a concrete thing — structurally the same impulse as telling. The passive voice removes the actor and makes things happen *to* the world rather than *in* it.

**Tell (passive):**
> The allocation had been approved three months ago.
> Channelers had been redirected to the emergency.
> He was not praised for it.

**Show (active):**
> The Council had approved it three months ago.
> Dorenne had redirected the channelers.
> Nobody praised him for it.

Active constructions force a subject — someone doing something — which naturally produces concrete, scene-grounded prose. When passive voice clusters, it's often a sign the scene needs to be dramatized rather than summarized.

---

### Quick Diagnostic

When revising a sentence or paragraph, ask:
- **Who is doing what?** If the answer is vague or absent, it's likely passive or abstract telling.
- **What would a camera see?** If the prose describes something a camera can't capture (an internal state, a meta-quality, a category of behavior), it's telling.
- **Is this the narrator's conclusion or the reader's?** If the prose is announcing a conclusion the scene should produce naturally, cut the announcement and strengthen the scene detail.

## Dialogue

### Quotation Style

**Double quotes.**

> "You're going to burn through it," Maren said. "All of it. And then what?"

### Dialogue Tags

**Varied but purposeful.** "Said" and "asked" remain the workhorses — invisible, functional. But varied tags are encouraged when they add genuine information the dialogue itself doesn't carry.

Good use of varied tags:
> "I'm fine," he muttered, not looking up.
>
> "That's not what I asked," Maren said.
>
> "It's what I answered." He dropped the wrench on the bench harder than he meant to. "Can we just—"
>
> "No." She stepped closer. "We can't just."

The tag should tell us *how* the line is delivered when it's not obvious from context. If the dialogue and action already make the delivery clear, use "said" or drop the tag entirely.

**Avoid:** tags that describe the content of the line rather than the delivery ("he explained," "she informed him," "he joked"). The reader can tell it's a joke.

### Dialogue Style

**Naturalistic.** Characters speak in fragments, interrupt each other, trail off, and leave things unsaid. People don't talk in complete paragraphs unless they're giving a speech.

- Em dashes for interruptions (including self-interruptions): "I just think that maybe—"
- Ellipses for trailing off: "I thought it would be different..."
- Characters talk past each other. Not every question gets answered. Not every point gets addressed.
- Each character should have a distinct speech pattern (developed further in character voice notes).

### Interior Monologue

**Italicized.** Used sparingly for direct, in-the-moment thought — the voice in Ash's head that's distinct from the narrator's rendering of his perspective.

> He looked at the number again. Checked it twice.
>
> *That can't be right.*
>
> But it was right. He'd done the math three times, and the number hadn't changed.

Use italicized thought for:
- Moments of sharp realization or denial.
- The gap between what Ash says and what he thinks.
- Fragments of self-talk during stress.

Don't overuse. Most of Ash's interiority should come through free indirect discourse (the narrator channeling his perspective in regular prose) rather than direct italicized thought. The italics are for when the thought is so immediate, so raw, that it can only be rendered in his own first-person voice.

## Showing the World

### Magic System: Show, Don't Tell

The magic system is detailed and quantified in the reference documents — but in the prose, it must be **shown through use, not explained through exposition.**

Characters don't think about how magic works any more than we think about how electricity works. They just use it. The reader learns the system by watching people channel, hearing them complain about their pool, noticing what they can and can't do.

**Do this:**
> Maren pressed her palm flat against the cracked stone and held it there, eyes closed, lips moving slightly. The crack sealed itself in a slow crawl from bottom to top. When she pulled her hand away, she flexed her fingers and winced — the kind of wince that meant she'd used most of what she had for the day.

**Not this:**
> Maren used her daily pool of magical energy to repair the crack. Each person in her generation received approximately 1,000 kilojoules per day, and structural repair was one of the more energy-intensive applications of channeling.

The quantified system (kJ costs, pool amounts, efficiency tiers) is an author tool for consistency. It stays behind the curtain. Characters use natural language: "I'm running low," "That'll cost me," "She's stronger than anyone I've seen."

The **early chapters** should take extra time demonstrating the world and its magic through Ash's daily life. Because he has no magic himself, he's a perfect lens — he watches others do what he can't, which lets the reader observe and understand the system through his outsider perspective.

### Setting Description

**Immersive but efficient.** A mix of physical description and functional detail, filtered through Ash's perspective. The world should feel real and lived-in without overwhelming the reader.

- Two or three strong sensory details per new location. Pick the ones that matter — the ones that tell you something about the place, the people, or Ash's state of mind.
- Prioritize details Ash would actually notice. He's practical, mechanical-minded — he sees how things are built, where they're breaking, what they're made of.
- Let the city reveal itself over time. Don't describe all of Solathis in chapter one. Each new district, each new location earns its details when Ash goes there.

> The Thornwall district clung to the eastern edge of the plateau like an afterthought. Everything here leaned — the buildings, the walkways, the tempers. Three generations ago, someone with real power had fused the foundation stone to the cliff face, and the channeling still held, but just barely. Ash could see where it was thinning at the joints. Another decade, maybe less, and the whole district would need reshoring.

## Handling Key Scenes

### Big Moments (Activation, Earthquake, Math Scene)

The prose rhythm shifts to signal scale and stakes.

Techniques:
- **Slow time down.** Break a ten-second event across a full page. Every sensation gets its moment.
- **Shorter paragraphs.** Single-sentence paragraphs for impact. White space on the page mirrors the pause in Ash's comprehension.
- **Sensory overload.** Layer sight, sound, physical sensation. Then cut one — sudden silence, or blindness, or numbness — to heighten the rest.
- **Pull narrative distance to deep close.** These are moments where the reader must be inside Ash's body.
- **Control the release.** After the peak, give the reader (and Ash) a beat to breathe. A mundane detail. A moment of quiet. The contrast does the emotional work.

### Emotional Beats (Grief, Anger, Fear, Joy)

**Physical sensation first.** Emotion lives in the body before it reaches the mind.

- Grief: heaviness, throat tightening, the inability to look at something directly.
- Anger: heat, jaw tension, the impulse to move or break something.
- Fear: cold, stillness, hyper-awareness of small sounds.
- Joy: lightness, expansion, the strange disorientation of not bracing for something.

Don't name the emotion if the physical description makes it clear. Trust the reader.

> His throat closed around something he couldn't swallow. He looked at the wall instead, at the crack running along the mortar line, and counted the bricks until the feeling passed. It didn't pass.

Understatement is almost always more powerful than overstatement for this story. Ash is someone who performs okayness — so the moments where the performance fails should be quiet, physical, and devastating.

## Chapter Structure

### Length

**Target: ~8,000 words per chapter.** With approximately 12 chapters, this puts the book at ~90,000 words. (Note: the current outline has 22 chapters — these will be consolidated during the transition to drafting.)

### Chapter Openings

Dive in. No epigraphs, no headers, no timestamps. The first line of each chapter should orient the reader in scene — where Ash is, what he's doing, what time of day it is — within the first paragraph or two.

### Scene Breaks

Within chapters, scene breaks (shifts in time or location) are marked with a centered `* * *` on its own line.

### Chapter Numbering

**Numbered only.** "Chapter 1," "Chapter 2," etc. No titles.

## Names and Formatting

### Character Names

- **Ash** is the default. Used in narration and by everyone who knows him.
- **Ashlyn** is his full name. Used only by his parents, and only in specific emotional registers — tenderness, worry, anger.
- Other characters are referred to by their most common name in narration (Maren, Haran, Ryn, Dorenne). Full names appear when formally introduced or in formal contexts.

### Numbers

**Chicago Manual of Style convention.** Spell out numbers ten and below. Use numerals for 11 and above.

> He checked the bridge three times.
>
> The district housed roughly 400 families.

### Punctuation and Formatting

- **Em dashes** for interruptions, asides, and abrupt shifts — no spaces around them.
- **Ellipses** for trailing off... three dots, no spaces before.
- **Italics** for interior monologue, emphasis (sparingly), and foreign/technical terms on first use.
- **No bold text** in prose, ever.
- **Scene breaks:** centered `* * *`

Working terms in use:
- **Channeling** — the act of using magic. Characters "channel" energy into effects.
- **Pool** — a person's daily renewable magical budget. Common, casual term.
- **Wellspring** — Ash's massive reservoir. Rare, possibly unique phenomenon.
- **Reservoir** — the total stored energy (Ash's specific pool). May also refer to the planetary source.

These terms should feel natural and lived-in, like how we say "battery" or "charge" without thinking about electrochemistry. Characters don't define them. They just use them.

## Voice Checklist (Quick Reference for Drafting)

Before writing or revising any scene, check:

- [ ] Is this in past tense, third person limited (Ash only)?
- [ ] Does the narrative distance match the emotional register of the scene?
- [ ] Am I showing the world rather than explaining it?
- [ ] Are dialogue tags varied but purposeful — not showy?
- [ ] Does every character sound like themselves, not like the narrator?
- [ ] Am I trusting the reader? (No over-explanation, no hand-holding.)
- [ ] Are emotions rendered physically, not labeled?
- [ ] Is this the simplest, clearest version of this sentence?
- [ ] Have I earned any lyrical moments with grounded pages around them?
- [ ] Does the chapter open in scene, not in summary?
