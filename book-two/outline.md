# Book Two Outline — "The Long Shadow"

**Question:** What is change worth if it can't outlast you?
**Reservoir:** ~50% → ~15-20%
**Time span:** Approximately one year
**Antagonistic force:** The Levelers (righteous force/anger)

---

## ACT ONE — The Builder's Hubris

### Chapter 1 — The Question

**POV:** Ash at the workshop. A new beginning built on ruins.

A few weeks after the break with Dorenne. The book opens in Haran's workshop — not in the family apartment, not in Dorenne's office. The contrast with Book One's opening is deliberate: there, Ash woke in a childhood bedroom he'd outgrown, performing cheerfulness in a world that had no place for him. Here, he wakes early and walks downhill to the one place that always had a place for him, carrying a question he doesn't know how to answer yet.

His workbench is exactly as Haran left it — tools arranged, surface cleared. He's been coming every day since the break. Not channeling. Working with his hands. The mechanical devices he abandoned during the ascent are still here: the spring-loaded pump design, the geared mechanism he never finished. He picks up where he left off, and the physical work engages his mind the way it always has — completely, honestly, without performance.

The reservoir sits inside him like a held breath. He can feel it now — the edges, the shape, the finite volume that used to feel oceanic. He doesn't channel. Not because he's afraid of spending, but because he's afraid of spending without purpose. Haran's question — *what do you want to build with it?* — has been turning in his head for weeks, and every answer he reaches for dissolves under scrutiny.

Haran arrives. They work in parallel for a while — the comfortable silence of shared craft. Haran doesn't ask how he's doing. He asks what Ash is building. The geared mechanism. Ash explains the design problem he's stuck on — a physical challenge, nothing magical about it. Haran listens, suggests a modification, hands him a different tool. They work.

Other tinkerers drift in. The workshop community has continued without Ash — projects advancing, new faces appearing, the pump system he abandoned now installed and running in twenty homes. Nobody treats his return as a dramatic event. He left. He came back. The bench was here. This is how Haran's world works: it doesn't orbit anyone.

But the city outside the workshop is different. The earthquake's aftermath has hardened into political fault lines. Reconstruction is underway — Dorenne is managing it with her usual brilliance — but the damage exposed fractures that can't be plastered over. Young channelers are organizing. Broadsheets that were footnotes in Book One are now posted on every wall in the lower districts. The word "redistribution" appears in Council debates for the first time. Ash reads a broadsheet on his walk to the workshop: *The earthquake didn't choose who it crushed. The decline did.*

He arrives at the workshop carrying the broadsheet. He puts it on Haran's desk. Haran reads it, sets it aside, and says: "They're not wrong about the problem. What's your answer?"

Ash doesn't have one. Not yet. But the question is sharper now — not just *what do you want to build?* but *what can you build that outlasts the building?*

**Establish:** The tonal shift from Book One — urgency replacing euphoria, purpose replacing intoxication. The workshop as Ash's true starting point. The post-earthquake political landscape heating up. Haran's philosophy unchanged and vindicated: systems that don't orbit one person. The reservoir as a felt presence — finite, precious, waiting to be spent wisely.

**Emotional beat:** Determined quiet. Not the brittle cheerfulness of Book One's opening and not the devastated awareness of Book One's close. Something harder and more adult: the readiness to work without knowing if the work will matter.

### Chapter 2 — The Failed District

**POV:** Ash and Haran survey the district that will become the proof-of-concept. The scope of what needs building.

Haran has been thinking about this longer than Ash has. Years, maybe decades. He pulls out sketches — not polished blueprints, but the accumulated notebooks of a man who has been designing post-magic infrastructure in the margins of a world that wasn't ready to hear about it. Mechanical water delivery. Gravity-fed heating systems. Cooperative maintenance structures that distribute the work across a community rather than depending on individual channelers. Solutions for a problem nobody wanted to admit existed.

They go together to look at the district. It's in the lower city — one of the young/poor neighborhoods that the earthquake devastated. Ash walked through rubble here during the response; he held up buildings here; the reservoir remembers. The reconstruction crews have cleared the worst of the damage, but what's left is almost worse: streets of buildings that survived but shouldn't have. Cracked foundations. Heating lattices that flicker and fail. Water that runs two hours a day because the channelers assigned to the aqueduct don't have the trickle strength to push it further.

The people here are mostly young-generation residents with weak trickles, doing manual work that used to be done by channeling. Children carry water in buckets. A woman re-lights her heating stone for the fourth time in an hour because it won't hold. An old man — elder generation, strong trickle — sits on his stoop channeling warmth into the wall of a building that's bleeding heat through cracks. He does this every day. He looks exhausted.

Ash sees the whole district as a system — the analytical mind engaging the way it did with Dorenne's charts, but differently now. Not *what can the Wellspring maintain?* but *what would this place need to work without magic entirely?* The water problem is mechanical: elevation, pressure, pipes. The heating problem is material: insulation, thermal mass, combustion alternatives. The structural problem is engineering: load distribution, reinforcement, design for the materials available rather than the magic assumed.

Haran watches him think. The old man's quiet pleasure — visible to the reader, invisible to Ash — at seeing his student finally ask the right questions.

They talk to residents. Not as officials or saviors — Haran is known here, liked here, trusted in the way that a person who has fixed things for free in your neighborhood for twenty years is trusted. Ash is recognized from the earthquake. The reactions are complicated: gratitude, wariness, a low-burning resentment from some. *Where were you before the ground shook?* One woman says it directly: "The Wellspring saved us from the earthquake. Who saves us from tomorrow?"

By evening they have a rough plan. Not a blueprint — a philosophy made geographic. One district. Proof that a neighborhood can function — imperfectly, grindingly, but sustainably — without depending on magic it won't have in twenty years. Mechanical water systems where the tinkerers have already proven the physics. Non-magical heating using insulation and combustion, designed by Haran, refined by Ash's engineering mind. Cooperative maintenance: train residents to fix the systems themselves, so the district doesn't need a channeler or a Wellspring to survive.

The key insight, articulated between them: use the Wellspring power to BUILD — to construct the infrastructure, to power the initial installation, to do the heavy lifting that physics alone would take years to accomplish. Catalytic expenditure. Spend the reservoir on creating something permanent, then walk away. The opposite of Dorenne's model, which used him to maintain systems that would fail the moment he stopped.

Ash feels something he hasn't felt since the first days of the Wellspring: excitement that isn't about him. The plan isn't about being extraordinary. It's about making the extraordinary unnecessary.

**Establish:** The district as microcosm of the world's decline — young, poor, underserved, surviving on effort rather than power. Haran's decades of preparation revealed as foresight, not eccentricity. The catalytic model: Wellspring as construction tool, not maintenance engine. The residents as real people with justified wariness, not grateful recipients.

**Emotional beat:** The thrill of right work. Not the intoxication of power but the quieter, deeper satisfaction of a good plan meeting a real need. And underneath, the ticking awareness: every piece of this will cost reservoir that doesn't come back.

### Chapter 3 — The Negotiation

**POV:** Ash goes back to Dorenne. He needs something from the person he walked away from.

He can't avoid it. The proof-of-concept needs things that live inside Dorenne's institutional framework: permits for construction in a city district. Access to materials — pipes, insulation compounds, mechanical components — that are managed through her office's supply chains. Workforce coordination with the existing reconstruction crews. The district is under her jurisdiction. He can't build there without her knowing, and he can't build it at the scale needed without her cooperation.

He walks into the Hall of Infrastructure for the first time since the break. The building hasn't changed. The charts are still on Dorenne's wall — deployment schedules, resource allocation, the carefully managed decline of a city whose infrastructure grows more fragile every quarter. But his name has been removed from the deployment schedule. Where it used to say WELLSPRING — KHARREN DISTRICT or WELLSPRING — AQUEDUCT REINFORCEMENT, there are gaps. Reassignments. Workarounds. She's managing without him. She always could. She just managed better with him.

Dorenne receives him. The conversation is layered and careful — two intelligent people who know each other well enough to read the subtext under every word. She's glad to see him. She's also furious with him, in the quiet, institutional way that registers as pointed courtesy rather than raised voices. He left her with a reconstruction crisis and a depleted political position. The Council questions whether the Wellspring is a reliable asset. Her opponents suggest the earthquake response was a fluke. She's spent weeks absorbing damage he caused by walking away.

She doesn't say any of this directly. She asks how he's been. She offers tea.

He explains the plan. The proof-of-concept district. Mechanical infrastructure. Catalytic Wellspring expenditure. A model for post-magic life. She listens with the attentiveness of someone evaluating a proposal, not a person hearing a friend's dream.

Her response is complex. She sees the value — if it works, it validates her leadership and provides a framework for managing the decline she's been fighting alone. She also sees the threat: this is Ash building *without her*, using her systems as scaffolding for a vision that doesn't need her once it's standing. The proof-of-concept, if it succeeds, proves that Dorenne's model — centralized maintenance powered by channelers and Wellspring force — was always the wrong answer. She's smart enough to see this immediately.

She gives him the district. Not generously — strategically. It's a neighborhood she's already written off: too damaged, too expensive to rebuild conventionally, too politically marginal to justify major resource allocation. If Ash succeeds, she claims credit for approving an innovative pilot program. If he fails, it cost her nothing. She provides permits, limited material access, and a workforce liaison. She does not provide her full institutional weight. She holds that in reserve, watching.

The conditions, spoken and unspoken: he doesn't use Wellspring power on other projects without consulting her. He doesn't undermine her reconstruction program publicly. He reports progress. He remains, at least nominally, within her institutional framework. He's free, but the freedom has walls she built.

He accepts. He has no leverage and he knows it. Walking away a second time would cost the project, not just the relationship.

On his way out, she says: "Ash. If this works — what you're building — it means everything I've been doing was wrong." A pause. "I'd like to see it work anyway." He can't tell if she means it. She might not know either.

**Establish:** Dorenne as diminished but not defeated — her institutional power is real and necessary. The transactional core of the relationship exposed but functional. The political landscape: Council dynamics, reconstruction politics, Dorenne's opponents. The proof-of-concept as a bet neither of them fully controls.

**Emotional beat:** The discomfort of needing someone you've broken with. Both people holding positions they believe in, neither able to fully trust the other, both proceeding anyway because the work matters more than the wound. Ash's quiet recognition that Dorenne's last words might be the most honest thing she's ever said to him.

### Chapter 4 — The Foundation

**POV:** First days in the district. Ash begins the work of building rather than saving.

The proof-of-concept district has a name — the residents call it something; Ash learns it on his first day and uses it from then on. Not "the project" or "the proof-of-concept." A place name, because it's a place where people live.

He starts with water. The district's channeled aqueduct feed hasn't reliably served the lower blocks since before the earthquake — the channelers assigned to the route are young, their trickles thin, and the elevation change from the main reservoir to the district's lowest streets defeats them by mid-afternoon. Haran's mechanical solution: a gravity-fed pipe system running from a rooftop cistern, filled by a hand-crank pump at a high-point tap. The physics are simple. The construction is not.

Ash channels. For the first time since the break with Dorenne, he spends reservoir — and the expenditure feels different than it ever has before. During the ascent, channeling was intoxication: raw force poured into problems, the thrilling excess of power without limit. During the earthquake, it was desperation: everything he had, thrown at catastrophe. Now it's precision. Not Haran's micro-pulse precision — he's still far from that — but deliberate, considered, purposeful. He channels reinforcing force into a foundation wall. He fuses pipe joints that would take a crew of workers days to seal by hand. He reshapes a stone cistern into a water-tight reservoir in an afternoon of focused work.

Each expenditure has a number now. He tracks it — roughly, mentally, the way Dorenne taught him to think about allocation. The difference is that Dorenne tracked expenditure against institutional need. Ash tracks expenditure against permanence. *Will this still be here when the reservoir is gone?* The pipe joint: yes. The cistern: yes. The wall reinforcement: yes. These are things that, once built, work on physics alone. Catalytic expenditure — Haran's phrase, now Ash's operating principle.

District workers arrive — people Dorenne's liaison assigned, mostly young residents from the neighborhood itself. They don't know mechanical systems. Most of them have spent their working lives channeling — pushing water through pipes with trickle force, heating walls with channeled warmth, maintaining structures through daily magical upkeep. The idea that a pipe could carry water by gravity alone, without anyone channeling it, is not intuitive. Some are skeptical. Some are insulted — is this what the Wellspring thinks of them? Relegated to hand tools and crank pumps while the elder districts hum with channeled comfort?

Ash recognizes the reaction because he lived it. He was the hand-tool person in a channeling world for twenty-three years. He knows how it feels to be offered the mechanical alternative — like being told you're not good enough for the real thing. He doesn't argue. He shows them the pump system Haran installed in the workshop district. He turns the crank. Water rises. No trickle required. "This works when nobody's channeling it. This works at three in the morning when everyone's asleep. This works for your children, whose trickles will be weaker than yours."

That last point lands. The decline isn't abstract to these people. They've watched their own trickles weaken year over year. Their children can barely warm a cup. The mechanical pump suddenly looks less like a downgrade and more like insurance.

**Establish:** The practical reality of building — not a montage, but the daily work of persuading people to trust something unfamiliar. Ash's channeling transformed: deliberate, tracked, purposeful. The emotional complexity of offering mechanical alternatives to people whose identity is tied to magic. The district as a living place, not a project site.

**Emotional beat:** The quiet satisfaction of useful work — earned, not performed. Ash recognizing his own old wound in the workers' resistance and knowing, for the first time, that his twenty-three years of powerlessness gave him something nobody else in this situation has: credibility. He knows what it's like to live without magic. He's the only powerful person who can say that honestly.

### Chapter 5 — The Blueprints

**POV:** The district takes shape. Haran's decades of preparation become real. The first successes — and the first costs.

Weeks pass. The proof-of-concept evolves from a plan into a place. Haran moves into the district — not dramatically, not as a statement. He brings tools, sets up a secondary workshop in a cleared ground-floor space, and starts teaching. He's been waiting decades for this: a place where his mechanical designs can be tested at community scale rather than in isolated workshops.

The water system works first. Three buildings connected to the gravity-fed cistern, hand-crank pump filling it daily. Residents are trained — reluctantly at first, then with the proprietary pride of people who've learned a new skill. A teenage girl becomes the fastest pump operator in the district and takes visible satisfaction in this. Water flows without channeling. It's slower, less convenient, less elegant than the channeled aqueduct. It doesn't stop at two in the afternoon.

Heating comes next, and it's harder. Haran's insulation approach — filling wall cavities with compressed fiber, sealing windows with mechanical frames instead of channeled barriers, installing small combustion stoves that burn processed fuel — works, but requires a level of craftsmanship that the district doesn't have yet. Ash channels the difficult parts: shaping insulation into walls, fusing window frames, reinforcing chimney flues. Each act of channeling is tracked. Each one is permanent. He walks through a building he's worked on and touches the walls — warm from insulation, not from magic. Something he built that doesn't need him.

The reservoir tracks. He's spending roughly 1-2% per week on construction — sustainable at this rate, but the district is one neighborhood and the city has dozens. The math of scaling is a shadow he sees but doesn't speak aloud yet.

Haran teaches. Not channeling — mechanical maintenance, troubleshooting, the intuition for how physical systems behave. He teaches the way he's always taught: by doing, by handing tools, by asking questions and letting people find the answers. A few workers become genuinely good at it. Most are adequate. Some never take to it. Haran doesn't force it. "You can't train everyone," he tells Ash. "You train enough, and the enough train more."

A scene: Ash watches Haran repair a broken pump valve. The old man's hands move with decades of practice — physical dexterity layered with the nearly invisible micro-channeling that Ash glimpsed in Book One. The repair takes minutes. A worker tries to replicate it. It takes an hour and the result is rougher. Haran approves it. "Works," he says. The worker is proud. Ash sees the gap between Haran's mastery and what can be taught in weeks, and files it away.

Dorenne's liaison visits. He's a mid-level administrator — competent, cautious, reporting back. He tours the water system, asks questions, takes notes. He's impressed despite himself. He also asks the question Dorenne would ask: "What happens when the Wellspring isn't here to install the next system?"

Ash says: "The people here install it."

The liaison's expression says what he doesn't: *They're not ready for that.*

**Establish:** The proof-of-concept as a real, functioning thing — imperfect, incomplete, working. Haran's vindication through quiet competence. The training challenge: building systems is one problem; building people who can maintain systems is a different, harder one. The reservoir math: sustainable for one district, impossible to scale at this rate.

**Emotional beat:** Hope — genuine, earned, grounded in work rather than spectacle. And the hairline fracture underneath: the liaison's unspoken question is the question Ash hasn't answered yet. *What happens when you're not here?* He thinks the answer is "the people take over." He hasn't tested that belief.

### Chapter 6 — Home

**POV:** Ash reconnects with his family. Maren visits the district. The ground is shifting under all of them.

Ash has been so absorbed in the district that he's fallen into a different version of the Book One drift — not toward power and spectacle, but toward work and purpose. He's been sleeping at the workshop, eating at the district, spending his days in construction and his evenings in planning. His mother has sent messages. He's answered them briefly. The guilt is different from Book One's guilt: he's doing the right thing this time, so why does distance still feel like abandonment?

He goes home for dinner. The apartment Dorenne arranged after the earthquake — still nicer than what they had, still slightly wrong. His mother has made the space her own: the cracked bowl from the old apartment sits on a shelf, the dented kettle is in the kitchen, the singed curtain hangs in the bedroom. She's carried the old home into the new one piece by piece. His father has settled more easily — he likes the light, the space, the working heating lattice. The neighborhood is quiet and polite and nobody knows them.

His mother asks about the district project. He explains — the water systems, the mechanical heating, the training program. She listens with fierce attention, the way she listened to everything about his life when he was magicless and she was the guardian between him and a world that had no place for him. She's trying to find her role in this new version of her son's life, and she hasn't found it yet. She offers to help. He says yes without thinking about what she'd actually do. She notices this. She doesn't say anything.

His father asks practical questions — the kind that reveal a lifetime of fixing things with his hands, the skill set Ash inherited before the Wellspring. How do the pipe joints hold under thermal stress? What's the pressure tolerance on the hand-crank mechanism? Ash answers with genuine pleasure. For a few minutes, they're two men talking about how things work, and the Wellspring doesn't exist.

Maren arrives late — she's been working, some professional obligation she doesn't elaborate on. She's thinner than she was a few months ago. Not dramatically — the kind of thinning you notice only if you're looking, the cost of effort that used to be effortless. She hugs Ash. She asks about the district. She's genuinely interested — she wants to see it.

The next day, she comes. She walks through the water system, examines the insulation work, watches a training session. Her questions are sharp and technical — she's a skilled channeler, and she sees immediately where Ash's catalytic construction has done things that would otherwise require a team of trained workers. She's impressed. She says so.

Then she tries the hand-crank pump. She's strong, coordinated — she should have no trouble. But she tires faster than the teenage girl who operates it daily. It's a small moment, easily explained: she's not used to the specific motion, she's been working long hours. But Ash catches something in her face — a flicker of frustration, quickly controlled, the discipline-mask she's been wearing since childhood. And later, when she channels warmth into her tea — a casual act she's done a thousand times — there's a faint hesitation. A micro-pause between intention and output. The trickle isn't flowing the way it used to.

She doesn't mention it. She talks about the project, about the potential, about scaling. She's excited for him. When she leaves, she hugs him again, and he feels how thin her shoulders have gotten. He files it away under *Maren's working too hard* and doesn't look at it more closely. He's not ready to see what's happening to his sister.

**Establish:** The family as living, complicated people adjusting to Ash's third reinvention (magicless → Wellspring → builder). The mother's displaced protectiveness. The father's quiet connection through craft. Maren's trickle weakening — planted here as texture, not crisis. The emotional pattern: Ash absorbed in good work, still drifting from the people who love him, the form different but the distance familiar.

**Emotional beat:** Warmth shot through with worry. The dinner scene should feel like a real family navigating real change — not dysfunction, just the ordinary difficulty of people who love each other trying to keep up with who each of them is becoming. And Maren's hesitation at the tea — the smallest possible signal of the crisis that will consume her arc.

### Chapter 7 — What's Growing

**POV:** The district's success attracts attention. Ash's two worlds begin to converge. The seeds of the Levelers take root.

The proof-of-concept is working. Not perfectly — the heating system in Building Four keeps failing, the pump in the eastern block needs a part nobody can fabricate locally, two workers have quit because they resented being trained "like children." But the water flows. The walls are warm. People who lived through the earthquake in this neighborhood — who watched their buildings crack while elder districts held — now have running water that doesn't depend on a channeler's morning shift. The district's residents have started calling the mechanical systems by nicknames, the way you name things you're beginning to trust.

Word spreads. A journalist from one of the city broadsheets visits and writes a piece — favorable, curious, slightly condescending. "THE WELLSPRING'S WORKSHOP: Can Hand Cranks Replace Channeling?" Ash reads it and recognizes Dorenne's influence in the framing: the proof-of-concept positioned as an experiment *within* her institutional authority, not a challenge to it. He doesn't fight the framing. The work matters more than the credit.

Other districts send inquiries. A maintenance crew chief from a middle-district neighborhood wants to see the water system — his channelers can barely keep pressure in the pipes, and the gravity-fed alternative might supplement what they have. A rural liaison — someone from a farming community three days' travel from the city — appears at the workshop asking about the mechanical heating designs. Haran talks to him for hours. The rural communities, it turns out, have been adapting longer than the city has. Their trickles failed first. They've already built crude mechanical alternatives out of necessity. What they lack is design refinement. What Haran has is forty years of design refinement.

But the attention brings friction. The broadsheet piece prompts responses. A letter from a young channelers' collective: *"The Wellspring builds toys for the poor while the powerful keep their channeled comfort. When does redistribution start?"* Another, from an elder-generation council member: *"Mechanical alternatives are a retreat from progress. We should be strengthening our channeling infrastructure, not abandoning it."*

Ash reads the young channelers' letter and recognizes the argument from the broadsheets he's been reading since Book One — the seeds of what will become the Levelers. The anger is sharper now, more organized. The earthquake crystallized something: the generation that watched their neighborhoods collapse while elder districts held has stopped asking nicely. They don't want mechanical pumps. They want the elder generation's trickles redirected to the people who need them. They want the Wellspring's reservoir put under collective authority. They want justice, and justice means sharing what remains, not building past it.

He understands the argument. He felt it for twenty-three years. The distinction between his position and theirs — that he's building for a future without magic rather than demanding a fairer share of the magic that's left — feels rational in his own mind and sounds like privilege from the outside. The most powerful person alive, telling the powerless to be patient while he tinkers.

An encounter. Walking back from the district, he passes a gathering — thirty or forty young channelers in a public square, listening to a speaker. The speaker is good: clear, precise, building an argument the way an engineer builds a structure — each point supporting the next. The crowd nods. Some call out agreement. Ash stops to listen. The speaker catches sight of him — recognizes the Wellspring — and pivots: *"And there he is. The man with an ocean, asking the thirsty to wait."* The crowd turns. Some faces show hostility. Others show something more complicated: hope, hunger, the desperate wish that the most powerful person alive would choose their side.

He walks away. He doesn't have an answer yet. He's building one.

Back at the district, the evening shift is changing. A worker — one of the early skeptics who came around — is teaching a new recruit how to operate the pump system. She's patient, competent, proud of what she knows. Ash watches from the doorway. This is what he's building. Not pipes and pumps. People who know things, teaching other people who don't know them yet.

But even as he watches, he sees what the scene doesn't show: the worker explaining the system the way he taught her to explain it, using his examples, his language, his framework. She's good. She's also his student, not her own teacher. The distinction matters, and he doesn't see it yet.

**Establish:** The proof-of-concept gaining traction — imperfect, real, noticed. The political response from both sides: young channelers demanding redistribution, elders defending the status quo. Ash caught between. The rural connection through Haran's networks — the city isn't the only story. The first Leveler-adjacent encounter: personal, uncomfortable, unanswered. The training success that conceals a deeper problem — the workers are competent but dependent on Ash's framework, not yet building their own.

**Emotional beat:** The collision between doing good work and being asked why the work isn't enough. Ash's recognition that his position — powerful person building alternatives rather than sharing power — is genuinely uncomfortable, not because the critics are wrong about everything, but because they're right about the optics and possibly right about the urgency. The worker teaching the recruit as both validation and warning: it's working, but is it working *independently*?

## Seeds Planted for Act Two

- **The dependency problem:** The proof-of-concept's workers are competent but Ash-dependent — trained in his framework, reliant on his construction, not yet self-sustaining. This is the core lesson of Act Two: he built systems, not people.
- **The Levelers:** Organized, articulate, gaining political power. Davan not yet introduced, but the movement's argument is fully formed and personally challenging to Ash.
- **Maren's trickle:** Declining faster than expected. She's compensating with effort and discipline. Nobody's named it yet.
- **Dorenne's strategic distance:** She gave Ash enough rope. If the proof-of-concept succeeds, she claims credit. If it fails, she's insulated. The institutional dance continues.
- **Rural connection:** Haran's networks extending beyond the city. Communities that adapted first. The answer might be outside Solathis.
- **Ryn's absence:** The reconnection hasn't happened. Ash is doing better work this time, but he's still absorbed, still drifting from the people who ground him. The pattern isn't broken — just redirected.
- **The scaling problem:** One district, 6% of reservoir spent. The city has dozens of districts. The math doesn't work at this rate. Ash sees this shadow but hasn't spoken it.
