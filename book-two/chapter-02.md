# Chapter 2

The notebooks filled the bench.

Haran set them down one at a time, pulling them from the deep drawer beneath his station where Ash had never looked—where he'd assumed there were tools or stock or the accumulated detritus of decades. Instead: notebooks. Dozens. Some leather-bound, some stitched from folded paper. The oldest were yellowed at the edges, the newest scarcely worn. Haran lined them up in no particular order, though Ash suspected there was an order he couldn't see.

"How long?" Ash asked.

"Depends what you mean." Haran opened the nearest one. The pages were dense with drawings—cross-sections, pipe layouts, annotations in a hand that had changed over the years from cramped and hurried to loose and confident. "This one's from before you were born."

Ash turned a page. A schematic for a gravity-fed water system. The drawings were rough, unpolished, the work of a mind sketching faster than the hand could follow. Notes in the margins: *elevation differential minimum 4m for adequate pressure. Pipe diameter determines flow rate—calculate for household of 5.* Below that, a separate sketch—an insulated wall section, layered: stone, air gap, packed fiber, stone again. The annotation read *thermal mass retains heat 6-8 hrs without channeling. Test with clay brick variant.*

He turned another page. Another. The designs shifted and evolved—water systems, heating systems, structural reinforcements. Some were crossed out. Some had been redrawn three or four times, each iteration cleaner. Some had notes in the margin that said things like *won't work—seal material doesn't exist* or *needs a flexible gasket—revisit* or simply *not yet.*

"You've been designing for a world without magic," Ash said.

Haran didn't answer. He opened another notebook—a newer one—and set it beside the first. This one held cooperative maintenance schedules. Diagrams showing which residents would inspect which systems and when. Training sequences: how to clear a pipe blockage, how to repack insulation, how to adjust a pump valve. Written for people who had never held a wrench.

Ash stared at the bench. The notebooks covered its entire surface, and each one held years of thinking about a problem that most of Solathis still refused to name.

"Haran."

"Hm."

"Why didn't you show me these before?"

Haran picked up the oldest notebook and turned it in his hands. The leather was soft with age. "You weren't asking the right question."

* * *

They left the workshop at mid-morning. The route down from The Splits toward the lower terraces was one Ash knew—he'd walked it during the earthquake response, running toward the worst of the damage while the dust was still settling. The streets narrowed as they descended. The stone underfoot shifted from dressed blocks to rough-cut slabs, some cracked, some replaced with timber planking where the originals had shattered.

The district announced itself before they reached it.

A water line ran along the left side of the street, a temporary pipe lashed to the building faces with iron brackets. It was dry. A girl, maybe ten, sat on the stoop of a ground-floor apartment with two buckets between her knees. She looked up when they passed. Looked away.

"The channelers assigned to the aqueduct can push water through for two hours in the morning," Haran said. "Their pools can't sustain more."

Two hours. For a district of—Ash counted doorways, estimated floors, multiplied. Three hundred people. Maybe more.

The reconstruction crews had done their work. The rubble was gone, the collapsed buildings reduced to cleared lots or rebuilt shells. But the buildings that remained were the ones that had survived by luck rather than design. A crack ran the full height of a residential block, wide enough at the base to fit a hand. Timber shoring held the wall in place, bolted at angles that suggested urgency rather than engineering. The heating lattice in the nearest window flickered—a dim, unsteady glow that meant someone inside was channeling warmth into the wall, feeding it from a pool that wasn't deep enough to hold.

Ash felt the reservoir shift in his chest. A reflexive tightening, like a muscle tensing before a lift. The warmth behind his ribs responding to proximity—to the visible evidence of what magic couldn't sustain.

He didn't channel. He looked.

A woman stood in a doorway across the street, pressing her palm flat against a heating stone set into the wall beside the frame. The stone glowed faintly, dimmed, glowed again. She pressed harder, leaning her weight into it, the tendons in her wrist standing out. The stone held its warmth for perhaps ten seconds after she pulled her hand away. Then it faded.

She pressed her palm to it again.

Haran watched Ash watch the woman.

They walked further. The street opened into a small square where three lanes met around a dry fountain basin. A boy crossed the space carrying a bucket in each hand, arms locked straight, water sloshing at the rims. Two girls hauled a wooden trough between them. None of them were older than twelve.

On the far side of the square, an old man sat in a chair against the south-facing wall of a building, both hands pressed flat to the stone. Channeling. The wall around his palms was warm—Ash could feel it from across the square. Elder generation, strong pool. One of the few down here who had any depth at all, and he was spending it into a wall.

The chair sat in worn grooves in the flagstone. He did this every day.

He opened his eyes. Nodded at Haran. Looked at Ash—held the look for a beat, assessing—and went back to the wall.

Ash stood in the square and the district settled over him—not as a list of problems, but as a weight. The old man's hands on the wall. The girl's arms straight under the buckets. The woman pressing her palm to a stone that wouldn't hold.

His mind did what it always did: reached for the structure underneath the feeling. Water first—the temporary pipe was undersized, the channelers overtaxed, and somewhere in the back of his thoughts the pump mechanism on his workbench clicked through its cycle. Heating—the lattices failed because they needed constant feeding, and Haran's oldest notebook held a wall section that didn't. Structure—the cracks needed reinforcement that would hold on its own.

He couldn't see all of it yet. But the shape was there, the way a mechanism's shape was there before you machined the parts.

"You're making a face," Haran said.

Ash turned. Haran stood a few paces behind him, hands in his coat pockets.

"What face?"

"The one where you're trying to solve a neighborhood like it's a gear ratio." The corner of Haran's mouth moved. "It's a start."

* * *

They walked the district for two hours. Haran knew the streets the way he knew his workshop—by long familiarity, by the accumulated evidence of years of presence. He nodded to residents. Stopped to examine a cracked lintel. Pointed out where the temporary water line leaked at a joint, the slow drip pooling on the flagstones and freezing in the cold.

People knew him. An older woman leaning from a second-floor window called down something Ash didn't catch, and Haran raised a hand in acknowledgment. A man repairing a shutter hinge stopped and exchanged a few words about the weather and the hinge and a door that was sticking. A child—the girl with the buckets from earlier—followed them for half a block before disappearing into an alley.

The interactions had the quality of routine. Haran had been coming here for years. Fixing things. Asking nothing. Building the kind of trust that couldn't be constructed from a single act of salvation.

Ash's reception was different.

People recognized him. He saw it in their faces—the slight widening, the reassessment, the shift from stranger to known quantity. He had been here during the earthquake. He had held buildings up with the reservoir's strength while people ran. He had pulled rubble with his hands and his power while the ground still shook.

A man standing in a doorway watched him pass. No gratitude. No hostility. The look of a person calculating what another person's presence meant and whether it would cost them.

A younger man, further down, was less guarded. He stepped into their path with his arms folded. "Here to fix us?" The question was directed at Ash. The tone made it clear he didn't want fixing.

Haran answered before Ash could. "Here to look."

"Looking's free," the man said, and stepped aside. But his eyes stayed on Ash, and what they held was a challenge: *prove you're different from the last person who came down here with plans.*

A woman stopped them on the next street. She was perhaps forty, thin-faced, her coat patched at the elbows. She looked at Ash with directness that had nothing tentative in it.

"You're the Wellspring."

"Yes."

"You were here. During the quake."

"Yes."

She studied him. Her arms were crossed, hands gripping her own sleeves. "The Wellspring saved us from the earthquake," she said. "Who saves us from tomorrow?"

The question sat in the cold air between them.

"We're working on that," he said.

It wasn't enough. The woman's expression didn't change. She nodded once and went back inside.

Haran said nothing. They walked on.

* * *

By late afternoon they sat on a low wall at the district's upper edge, where the street climbed toward the next terrace and the buildings thinned. The city spread below them in its terraced layers, the upper districts catching the last of the light, the lower ones already in shadow. The proof-of-concept district—Ash was already thinking of it that way, though it had no name yet—occupied the middle distance: a grid of streets and rooftops and cracked walls, small enough to see whole, large enough to matter.

Haran had a notebook open on his knee. Not one of the old ones. A new one, its pages mostly blank.

"One district," Ash said.

"One district."

"Mechanical water. We put a header tank at the high point—there." He pointed to a flat-roofed building at the district's upper corner, three stories, the highest point in the immediate area. "Gravity feeds down through the streets. Pumps to fill the tank. Hand-cranked, like the Velden system, but larger. Multiple pumps connected to a shared main."

"Pipe routing?"

"Follow the existing water line paths. The temporary pipes already show the distribution logic—they just need permanent material and the right diameters."

Haran made a note. His pencil moved in short, precise strokes—dimensions, angles, the shorthand of decades.

"Heating," Ash said. "Insulation first. Your layered wall design from the notebooks—stone, air gap, fiber, stone. Retrofit the existing buildings. Then combustion heating. Chimney stoves in central locations, thermal mass walls to distribute and hold."

"Fuel?"

"That's the bottleneck." Ash paused. "Petra's vulcanization process. You said it throws off heat."

"Significant heat."

"If we can capture that as a byproduct of sap processing—material production and heating from the same process—"

"You'd need a thermal transfer system. Water, probably. Heated in a jacket around the vulcanization chamber, piped to a distribution loop."

Ash turned the thought over. Something was connecting—Petra's process producing heat as waste, the district needing heat as input—but the shape of it was still vague. A possibility, not a plan.

"And if we install any of this," he said, "the residents need to be able to fix it. Otherwise we've just built a different kind of dependency."

Haran wrote something in the notebook. Didn't look up. But his hand was steady and his pencil moved with the quickness of a man writing down something he'd been waiting to hear.

"And the construction," Ash said. He stopped. The thing that had been forming since the square—the old man's hands on the wall, the woman's question—it was close, but he couldn't see all of it yet. "I could channel the heavy work. Shape pipe. Set foundations. The things that would take months by hand."

Haran didn't look up from his notebook. "Spend reservoir on building."

"On building things that don't need me afterward." Ash heard it as he said it—heard what it meant. "Dorenne used me to maintain. Every day, the same expenditure, the same systems failing the moment I stopped. This would be—"

"Once," Haran said.

"Once. Build it. Walk away."

Haran set his pencil down. He looked at Ash, then at the district, then back. He didn't say anything for a long moment.

Haran picked up his pencil again. Wrote something Ash couldn't see. Closed the notebook.

"You'll need Dorenne's approval," he said. "The district falls under Infrastructure jurisdiction."

"I know."

"And you'll need the residents' trust. Not their gratitude. Not their permission. Their trust."

"I know."

"And every piece of this—" Haran tapped the closed notebook against his knee. "Every pipe you shape, every wall you reinforce, every foundation you set. That's reservoir. Gone."

"I know."

The warmth in his chest pulsed. Not a response to threat or need—a readiness. The finite store of everything he carried, waiting to become something that would outlast the carrying.

"How much?" Haran asked.

"I don't know yet. I'll need to calculate it once we have real plans. Pipe lengths, wall sections, structural loads."

"Rough estimate."

Ash closed his eyes. The reservoir was there—present, edged, the boundaries closer than they'd been three months ago. He tried to measure it the way he'd measure water in a vessel: by feel, by weight, by the sense of how much space remained.

"Significant," he said. "Not all of it. But significant."

Haran nodded. He didn't tell Ash to be careful. He didn't tell him the cost was too high. He stood, pocketed the notebook, and looked at the district the way he looked at a mechanism on his bench—assessing the components, the tolerances, the places where failure would come first.

"Tomorrow," he said. "We measure."

* * *

They walked back to The Splits as the light failed. The streets emptied around them—people moving inside, doors closing, the district settling into its evening rhythm of conservation and cold. A woman lit a candle in a window. A man carried firewood up a narrow stairway, the logs cradled against his chest.

Ash's legs ached from the walking. His hands were cold. The reservoir sat in his chest, untouched, full of a potential that for the first time in weeks felt like it pointed toward something specific.

Not toward spectacle. Not toward holding things up. Toward building things that held themselves.

The plan wasn't about him. That was the part that sat differently, that occupied a space in his thinking he hadn't known was empty. The plan was about a district of three hundred people waking up to water that ran without a channeler's pool, warmth that held without a channeler's hands, walls that stood without a channeler's will. About making the extraordinary unnecessary.

Underneath: the mathematics. Every meter of pipe shaped by channeling, every foundation reinforced, every wall section built—that was reservoir that didn't come back. The warmth behind his ribs, finite and edged, spending itself into permanence.

He didn't know if there would be enough.

He didn't say that.

They reached the workshop. Haran unlocked the door. The smell of oil and sawdust and the day's cold met them. Ash crossed to the stove and knelt to build the fire—kindling, shavings, the flint and its three-strike rhythm. The spark caught. He fed it. The heat began its slow, honest work of filling the room from the near wall to the far corners.

Haran set his new notebook on the bench beside the old ones. One notebook among dozens. One plan in a lifetime of plans. The difference was that this one might happen.

Ash sat at his bench. The gate valve prototype was where he'd left it, half-finished, the steel scribed with lines he'd cut the day before. He picked up the file.

Tomorrow they would measure. Tonight, the work was in front of him—small, precise, the kind that occupied the hands and let the mind hold what it had gathered.

The district. The woman's question. The old man at the wall.

*Who saves us from tomorrow?*

He set the file against the steel. The first stroke bit clean. The stove ticked. The notebooks lay open on Haran's bench, their decades of waiting finally met.

His hands moved. The fire built. Outside, the city settled into its long cold, and somewhere below, in a district that had no name yet, a girl carried water in buckets because there was no other way.

Not yet.
