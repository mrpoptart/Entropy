# Chapter 4

The first pipe section was already wrong.

Ash stood on the flat roof of the three-story building at the district's upper corner, holding a length of copper tubing against the morning light. The diameter was correct, he'd measured twice, but the fitting where it met the cistern base was a quarter-turn off true, and the sealant Petra had sent wouldn't grip an angled joint. He set the pipe down and looked at Haran, who was crouched at the cistern's edge with a plumb line and a face that said he'd already noticed.

"Off," Haran said.

"I see it."

"Good." Haran wound the plumb line around his fingers. "Fix it before you seal it or you'll be fixing it again tomorrow."

The rooftop overlooked the district in a way that made the work feel possible and enormous at once. Streets below, narrow, branching from the small square where the dry fountain sat. Buildings pressed close, their walls cracked and shored and patched with timber where the earthquake had tested them. Smoke from a cookfire. A woman hanging laundry on a line strung between window frames.

A boy crossed the square carrying two buckets. Arms straight, water sloshing.

Still carrying water. Five days since Ash had gotten the permits, and the buckets hadn't stopped.

"Ash." A voice from the stairwell. He turned. Corren, broad-shouldered, perhaps thirty, stood at the roof's edge with a coil of pipe over one shoulder. "Where does this run?"

"South face. Down to the first junction." Ash pointed along the roofline to where the pipe would descend. "Follow the chalk marks."

Corren looked at the chalk marks. Looked at the pipe. He set the coil down and called to someone in the stairwell to bring the next load, then went to work.

Haran had been here before dawn. He'd arrived with his new notebook and an older man from the district who'd helped him carry the pump casing up the stairs—a cast-iron assembly the size of a barrel, machined in The Splits, hauled down by handcart in three trips. The pump sat at the rooftop's edge now, bolted to a timber platform, its crank handle bright against the dull stone.

The cistern was the problem. The existing stone basin, originally a rainwater collector cracked in the earthquake and never repaired, needed to hold a volume it wasn't built for. The cracks needed sealing. The walls needed reinforcing. The base needed to be watertight.

This was where the reservoir came in.

Ash placed his palms flat on the cistern wall. The stone was cold. Gritty under his fingers, old limestone, the same pale yellow that built half the lower city. He could feel the fractures through his skin—hairline splits running through the grain, places where the earthquake's force had found the weak points and pried.

He closed his eyes. The reservoir sat behind his ribs, warm, present, edged. Not the surging flood of the earthquake months. Not the reckless heat of the early days, when channeling had felt like the first drink after a lifetime of thirst. Quieter. Contained. Like holding a full cup and choosing how much to pour.

He channeled.

The warmth moved through his palms into the stone. He could feel the limestone's structure: the compressed layers, the mineral grain, the places where age and trauma had broken continuity. He pushed energy into the fractures, not flooding them but threading the force along each crack, fusing the stone back to itself at the molecular level. Haran had shown him the principle in a notebook sketch: heat and pressure applied precisely along a fault line would re-bond stone the way a smith re-bonds steel. The technique required control. Not the brute force that came naturally, but a deliberate narrowing. Less water through the channel, more pressure at the point.

His hands trembled. The tremor wasn't exhaustion. It was the effort of restraint, of holding the reservoir's warmth to a thin stream when it wanted to pour.

The first crack sealed. He felt it close under his palms, a faint click, more sensation than sound, as the limestone's grain reconnected. He moved to the next. And the next. Working around the cistern's interior wall, palm over palm, spending reservoir into stone that would hold when he was gone.

*Will this still be here when the reservoir is gone?*

The thought arrived in Dorenne's voice, though she'd never said those words. She'd taught him to think about allocation. What goes where. What it costs. What it returns. Her framework had been institutional. His was different now. Every expenditure measured against permanence. Against what stayed.

The cistern wall warmed under his hands. He sealed the last crack and pulled back. His palms were flushed, the skin reddened from the energy transfer. The reservoir shifted in his chest. Lighter. A small draw, maybe two percent. But two percent that wouldn't come back.

"Done?" Haran asked.

"Sealed. Needs to cure overnight before we test the fill."

Haran pressed his own palm to the wall. Held it there. His eyes moved in the way Ash had learned to read, the slight unfocus of a channeler sensing structure.

"Clean work," Haran said. He pulled his hand away. "Better than the Velden job."

From Haran, that was a speech.

* * *

The pipe system took two days to lay.

Ash fused the joints himself, copper to copper, the reservoir's heat welding each connection into a seamless seal. The work had a rhythm. Measure, cut, fit, channel. Each joint cost something small and would hold for decades.

Haran designed the routing. The gravity-fed system ran from the rooftop cistern down through three main lines, branching at each street level into smaller feeds that would serve clusters of buildings. The elevation drop from the cistern to the district's lowest point was eleven meters, more than enough head pressure for reliable flow, according to Haran's calculations.

According to Haran's calculations.

On the morning of the third day, they opened the system. Ash stood at the lowest junction point, a tap installed at street level near the dry fountain, while Haran operated the pump on the roof three stories above. The crank's rhythm carried down through the pipe: a low, regular pulse as water climbed from the ground-level well tap into the cistern.

They waited. Haran had calculated fill time at forty minutes. At forty-two minutes, he sent Corren down with a nod.

Ash opened the tap.

Water came. A thin stream, unsteady, pushing air from the pipe in short bursts. Then it steadied. A trickle. Consistent, clear, running from the tap into the stone basin below.

A trickle.

Ash watched it. The woman from the first visit—the one who'd asked *who saves us from tomorrow*—stood in her doorway across the square. She watched too. Her arms were crossed.

The trickle didn't change. Didn't build to a flow. Just a thin, steady thread of water, barely enough to fill a cup in a minute.

Haran came down the stairs. He crossed the square. Looked at the tap. Looked at Ash.

"Beautiful joints," he said. "Wrong diameter."

The words sat between them. Ash stared at the trickle. He'd fused every connection in the lower sections with the precision of someone trying to prove he could be precise—seamless, elegant, perfectly sealed. And the pipe diameter in those lower sections was too narrow. He'd matched the copper stock Petra had sent without recalculating for the branching junctions, where the flow divided and the friction loss multiplied. Eleven meters of head pressure, split across three main lines and twelve secondary feeds, pushing through pipe that was a full centimeter too narrow in the final runs.

The physics were simple. He'd gotten them wrong.

"The lower feeds," he said.

"The lower feeds." Haran turned the tap off. The trickle stopped. "You calculated for a single run. The branches—"

"I know." His jaw tightened. "I know what I did."

Haran said nothing else. He didn't need to. The wasted reservoir was already doing the talking. The joints Ash had fused, perfect and useless, welded into pipe that was the wrong size. He'd have to break those seals, pull the sections, replace the narrow pipe with wider stock. Spend reservoir again on work he'd already spent reservoir on.

The woman in the doorway went back inside.

They tore out the lower sections that afternoon. Ash broke his own seals with channeling that felt like undoing, the reservoir's warmth reversed, dissolving bonds he'd built hours earlier. Each broken joint was a small, specific loss. Not dramatic. Not catastrophic. Just the quiet arithmetic of waste.

*That reservoir is gone twice.*

The thought was sharp enough to taste.

Haran worked beside him. Didn't hurry. Didn't comment on the cost. He measured the replacement pipe—wider stock, the diameter recalculated for branching friction loss, and marked the cut lines with the same short, precise pencil strokes he used for everything.

"Test before you seal," Haran said. "Every time."

"I know."

"You know now."

* * *

They rebuilt the lower feeds by evening. Wider pipe, recalculated junctions, every section tested with a bucket of water poured through before Ash channeled the seal. The testing was slow. Tedious. Each connection checked, each flow rate measured against Haran's numbers. Ash wanted to channel them all at once, flood the reservoir's warmth through the entire system in a single sustained push, the way he'd reinforced buildings during the earthquake. Fast. Powerful. Done.

He didn't. He sealed one joint. Tested the next section. Sealed it. Tested.

The reservoir drew down. He tracked it the way Dorenne had taught him to track institutional budgets. Not precisely, not with numbers he could write down, but with the bodily sense of *less*. The warmth behind his ribs had an edge to it now, a boundary he could feel. Forty-four percent when he'd started the week. Lower now. Maybe forty-two. Maybe forty-one.

Every joint permanent. Every joint a cost.

In the last light, they opened the system again. Ash stood at the same tap. Haran on the roof. The crank's pulse through the pipe. The pause.

He turned the handle.

Water came. Not a trickle. A flow. Steady, clear, filling the basin in the time it took to breathe twice. He held his hand under it. Cold. Clean. Running by gravity alone, with nobody channeling, nobody spending pool, nobody's daily energy draining into the pipe.

The old man who channeled warmth into the wall every day had come out to watch. He stood in the square with his hands at his sides—the same hands that pressed flat to stone each morning. He watched the water fill the basin. His expression didn't change.

But he stayed.

A girl appeared at the edge of the square. The same girl from the first visit, the one with the buckets. She was carrying them again, one in each hand, her arms locked straight. She stopped. Looked at the tap. Looked at the water running into the basin, free and steady, nobody touching it.

She set the buckets down.

* * *

The district was called Stonecurve.

Ash learned the name the next morning, standing in the square while Corren's crew assembled the secondary pump housing. An older woman, heavy-set, her coat the faded green of someone who'd worn it for a decade, stopped beside him and said, "You're putting in another pump? Good. The east end of Stonecurve hasn't had reliable water since before the quake."

Stonecurve. Already old in her mouth, already the thing everyone called it. The main street curved along a limestone shelf that formed the terrace edge, the buildings following the stone's natural arc.

He used it from then on.

Felden arrived that afternoon with six workers. Dorenne's liaison was a compact man with a clerk's posture and a habit of writing things down before anyone finished saying them. He introduced the workers without ceremony: four young men and two young women, all district residents, all under thirty.

"They're yours until the project completes or the Director reassigns them," Felden said. He made a note on his pad. "Weekly reports to my office. Material requisitions through me. Questions?"

"None."

Felden nodded once and left. The workers stood in the square and looked at the pump on the roof and the pipe running down the building face and the tap that was, even now, running water into the basin without anyone touching it.

"So," said a young woman at the front of the group. Tesha—she'd given her name when Felden introduced her, the only one who had. "What are we doing here?"

"Building a water system," Ash said. "And a heating system after that."

"With machines." She said it flat. Not a question.

"With machines."

A young man behind her, Dael, shifted his weight. "We can channel water through pipes. We've been doing it since the quake. What do we need cranks for?"

Ash looked at him. Recognized the set of the jaw, the folded arms, the specific defiance of a person being offered something that felt like less.

"How long does your channeling last?" Ash asked.

"Long enough."

"How long?"

Dael's jaw worked. "Two hours. Maybe three on a good day."

"And after that?"

Silence. Two hours. The same two hours the aqueduct channelers could sustain. The same two hours that left the district dry for the other twenty-two.

"The pump runs all day," Ash said. "All night. It runs when you're sleeping. It runs when your pool is empty. It runs when you're old and your children's pools are half what yours are now." He paused. "It just runs."

"It's a crank," Dael said. "Someone still has to turn it."

"Someone has to turn it. Nobody has to channel it." Ash crossed to the pump platform at the base of the cistern building, where Haran had installed a demonstration unit, a smaller version of the rooftop mechanism. He gripped the handle and turned it. Once. Twice. The cylinder engaged, the valve opened, and water rose through the feed pipe with a sound like someone clearing their throat. It spilled from the outlet into a catch basin. Clear. Steady. Mechanical.

"This works for your children," Ash said. "Whose pools will be weaker than yours."

The words hung in the square. Dael's arms stayed folded. But something shifted in his face. Ash had seen that look before, on the faces of channelers who'd watched their own pools shrink year after year. The look of someone who already knew the thing you were saying and hated you for saying it out loud.

Tesha stepped forward. Looked at the pump. Put her hand on the crank.

"Show me how it works," she said.

* * *

The next three days were a grind.

Ash had imagined, not in words he'd have admitted to, that the workers would see the water system and understand. That the tap running by gravity would be its own argument. That the physical evidence of mechanical function would convert skepticism the way it had converted him in Haran's workshop.

It didn't work that way.

Tesha learned fast. She had the hands for it—steady, patient, willing to repeat a task until the task taught her something. By the second day she could assemble a pipe junction without help, cutting the copper with a hand saw and fitting the sections with Petra's rubber seals before Ash channeled the permanent bond. She asked questions that showed she was thinking structurally: why this diameter, why this angle, what happens at the junction if pressure builds.

The others were slower. Not from stupidity. From strangeness. They'd spent their lives pushing energy through their palms. The idea that a pipe could do work, that water would flow downhill through copper without anyone willing it to, was counterintuitive at a level deeper than understanding. They understood the principle. They didn't trust it.

Dael was the hardest. He showed up, carried pipe, turned wrenches. But his body held a resistance Ash could read in every motion. The way he set down tools with more force than necessary. The way he looked at the pump casing with an expression that said *this is beneath me.*

Ash knew that expression. He'd worn it himself for twenty-three years, reversed.

On the fourth day, Dael dropped a pipe section. It rang against the flagstones and rolled. He stood over it with his hands at his sides.

"I can push water through that pipe right now," he said. Not to Ash. To the air, to the square, to whoever was listening. "I can channel it from the well to the roof without your pump."

"For how long?" Ash asked. He didn't look up from the junction he was fitting.

"Long enough."

"And tomorrow?"

Dael picked up the pipe. He didn't answer. He didn't need to. Tomorrow his pool would be full again and he'd push water for two hours and then the pipe would sit dry for twenty-two. Ash could see him working through it. The math that didn't need saying.

Ash stood. Crossed to where Dael was standing. Kept his voice low.

"I know what it—" He stopped. Started again. "Twenty-three years. No pool. I carried water in buckets while my sister channeled hers clean." The words came out wrong, too tight, not the thing he meant to say. "I know what the wrench feels like."

Dael looked at him. The hardness didn't leave his face. "So you had nothing. And now you have everything. And you're telling me to use a wrench."

Ash opened his mouth. Closed it. He didn't have an answer that wasn't defensive.

"I'm telling you it works tomorrow," he said. "That's all I've got."

He went back to the junction. Dael stood in the square for a long moment. Then he picked up the wrench and went to the secondary feed line. His hands were rough with the tool. Unpracticed. But he was there.

* * *

By the end of the first week, the primary water system was operational. The rooftop cistern filled by pump, water running by gravity through three main feeds and eleven of the planned twelve secondary branches. The twelfth, the eastern run serving the block farthest from the cistern, needed wider pipe stock that Felden hadn't sourced yet. A gap. But eleven branches flowing.

Ash sat on the low wall at the district's upper edge, where he and Haran had sat a week ago and planned this. The city below him. Stonecurve's rooftops close, the upper terraces distant, the Hall of Infrastructure visible as a pale shape on the administrative ridge.

His hands ached. Not from channeling. From work. Wrenches and pipe cutters and the physical labor of carrying stock up stairs and fitting joints in tight spaces. The body's honest fatigue, different from the reservoir's draw. He held both now: the ache in his hands and the lightness in his chest where two percent of himself used to be.

Haran sat beside him. Notebook open. Pencil working.

"The eastern branch," Ash said.

"Felden will have the pipe by midweek."

"And the heating system."

"Not yet." Haran closed the notebook. "Water first. Get it right. Then heat."

Ash watched the square below. The tap was running. A woman filled a jug. The girl who'd carried buckets stood nearby, watching the water the way Ash had once watched channelers—with the particular attention of someone seeing a thing work that shouldn't.

The old man was at his wall. Hands flat. Channeling warmth into stone that would cool the moment he stopped. Tomorrow he'd be back. And the next day. Spending himself, a little more each time, into a task that would never be finished because the solution required someone to keep giving.

The water system didn't require that. It just ran.

*Will this still be here when the reservoir is gone?*

Yes. The pipe would hold. The cistern would hold. The pump would turn. Mechanical. Permanent. Built once, by a man who wouldn't always be here, for people who would.

The arithmetic of it settled in him. Not comfort. Fact. He'd spent reservoir he couldn't recover. He'd spent it wrong once, and then right. The waste stung. The success didn't erase the waste. Both were true. Both stayed.

Haran pocketed his notebook and stood. The evening light caught the buildings of Stonecurve at the angle that turned limestone gold, and for a moment the cracked walls and timber shoring and patched rooftops looked like something other than damage. They looked like a place where people lived. Which is what they'd always been.

"Same time tomorrow?" Ash asked.

"Earlier," Haran said. "I want to survey the heating runs before the crews arrive."

He left. Ash sat on the wall a while longer. The tap ran. The square emptied as people went inside. Below, Dael was tightening bolts on the secondary feed line. He hadn't left early. He hadn't volunteered for extra work. But he was still here, and the wrench was in his hand, and he hadn't put it down.

Ash watched the water run. Counted the cost. Let the ache in his hands be what it was.

He climbed down and walked through Stonecurve toward the road that led back to The Splits. The street followed the limestone shelf's long curve, the buildings leaning close, the name making sense the way place names always did once you'd walked the ground. Lamplit windows. A woman's voice calling a child inside. The ordinary sounds of a district that was, for the first time in months, not carrying water in buckets.

Not all of it. Not yet. Eleven branches of twelve. The eastern block still dry. The heating system unbuilt. The workers still learning tools their hands didn't know.

But water ran where it hadn't. And it would run tomorrow without him.

He walked home in the dark. The reservoir sat behind his ribs, lighter than it had been, edged with a new awareness of its limits. Forty-two percent, maybe. Maybe less. He couldn't be sure. The warmth was still there—still vast, still more than almost anyone alive could claim. But the boundary was closer now. A wall he could feel when he reached.

He didn't reach.

He walked, and the city settled around him, and somewhere behind him in Stonecurve a tap ran water into a stone basin by gravity alone, and nobody was channeling, and nobody needed to.

Eleven branches of twelve. A start.
