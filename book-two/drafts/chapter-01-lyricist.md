# Chapter 1

The hill was steeper than he remembered.

Or he was slower. Three weeks of not channeling had done something to his sense of distance, some recalibration his body was still adjusting to, and the switchback path from the middle district to the Splits felt longer in the gray before dawn than it had in the months when he could have floated down it on a column of force and barely noticed the cost. He didn't float. He walked. Boots on cold stone, hands in his pockets, breath making small ghosts in the air ahead of him that dissolved before he reached them.

Solathis in early autumn. The city stacked above and below him on its great plateau, terraces carved into the rock like shelves in a cabinet, each one holding its portion of the population and the infrastructure that served them. From here, midway down, he could see both directions. Above: the elder districts catching the first gray light on their polished facades, the stone still humming with maintenance channeling so constant it was atmospheric, the buildings gleaming because someone up there could afford to make them gleam. Below: the Splits, wedged between the plateau's lower shelf and the gorge wall, where the streets were too steep for carts and the buildings leaned on each other and the heating lattices had gone intermittent years ago.

He was walking downhill. Toward the bottom.

The contrast landed differently now. Three months ago he'd have climbed this path in the other direction—forty flights up to the administrative level, to Dorenne's office, to the desk where he'd sat and the charts he'd read and the work that had felt, for a time, like the answer to the question of what his life was for. He'd climbed those stairs every morning for a year. He'd arrived early and stayed late and poured himself into the machinery of a system that had used him precisely and cared for him conditionally, and the pouring had felt like purpose because purpose was the thing he'd wanted more than anything, more than comfort, more than safety, more than the acknowledgment that he was a person and not a resource.

He wasn't climbing anymore. He was walking downhill, to the one place that had always had a place for him, carrying a question he couldn't answer and a reservoir he wouldn't spend and the memory of a lit window on the Second Terrace that he hadn't looked at in nineteen days.

The broadsheets started at the third switchback.

He'd been seeing them for weeks, but they were thickening. Someone had been busy in the night. Fresh paste, fresh ink, the paper rough and hand-pressed, the type set with the emphatic irregularity of a manual press operated by someone who cared more about the words than the margins. They covered the retaining walls like a second skin: plastered over old notices, pasted to the boards of shuttered shops, even stuck to the base of a dead transit lift that hadn't run since the earthquake cracked its shaft housing.

He slowed at one. Read it in the gray light.

*THE EARTHQUAKE DIDN'T CHOOSE WHO IT CRUSHED. THE DECLINE DID.*

*For every building shored up in the elder districts within a week, a building in the lower terraces still waits. For every heating lattice refreshed above the Second Terrace, a family below it wakes to cold stone.*

*Your children inherit what you permit.*

At the bottom: meeting times, locations, and the symbol he'd been seeing everywhere—a circle bisected by a single line, rough as a thumbprint, stamped or hand-drawn. He didn't know what it meant. He didn't know who was printing these. He knew the anger was real, and the numbers behind the anger were real, and the numbers were the same numbers he'd seen in Dorenne's charts, the same declining curve, the same inexorable arithmetic—just rendered in a different voice. Dorenne's voice said *allocation* and *optimization* and *managed decline.* This voice said *theft.*

He pulled the broadsheet from the wall. The paste was still tacky. He folded it and put it in his coat pocket.

Lower. The streets narrowing, the buildings older, the earthquake's damage visible in ways the upper terraces had already erased. Scaffolding on a residential block where the stone had sheared along an old channeling fault. A temporary water point at a junction, brass fittings already oxidized green, a row of buckets arranged in the careful order of a community that had learned to share by taking turns. Two condemned buildings on the south side, their windows dark, their doors stamped with the Infrastructure Directorate's red seal.

Dorenne's seal. Her mark on the city. The mark of a woman who managed decline the way she managed everything—brilliantly, precisely, with the optimized allocation of formidable competence—and who had reached for a pencil before she reached for him.

His jaw tightened. He walked past.

The flagstone three steps from the workshop door tipped under any weight. He stepped over it. The way he'd stepped over it since he was fourteen. The way his feet had learned this particular stone on a particular autumn afternoon when Haran had given him a key and said, *Come whenever you want. The work will be here.*

The key turned. The lock clicked.

He pushed open the door and the smell hit him in the chest.

* * *

Oil. Sawdust. The mineral bite of cut metal. Cold stone and old wood and the ghost of yesterday's heat clinging to the grain of the benches. A workshop that had been accumulating its own atmosphere for decades, every day adding another layer the way every year added another ring to a tree, and the smell was the sum of all of it—the work, the years, the patient accumulation of effort that didn't require anyone to be extraordinary.

He crossed to the stove in the dark, four steps from the door, left around the first bench, the iron box with its fire grate cleaned and its kindling laid. Haran's work, from the night before. The old man always left kindling for whoever arrived first.

Three strikes of the flint. The spark caught. Shavings, sticks, a split log. The fire grew with the small urgency of something that had been waiting to be useful. He adjusted the damper and the heat began to push outward through the iron—not the instant, even saturation of channeling, but the slow, honest progress of combustion through metal into air, reaching the near wall first and the far corners last.

He filled the kettle from the hand pump by the back wall—three strokes of the handle, the water rising through the pipe with a sound like a long inhalation—and set it on the stove. Lit the oil lamp on the center bench. Yellow light swelled into the room and the workshop assembled itself around him: the three workbenches in their row, the storage rack against the south wall, the big lathe in the back corner, the project boards pinned with diagrams and calculations in half a dozen different hands.

His bench. Third station from the door. The surface cleared, the tools arranged along the back edge in Haran's order: calipers, files, the small hammer with the chipped handle, the precision screwdrivers in their leather roll. Above the bench, pinned to the wall, the project notes he'd written before the Wellspring—before the activation, before the earthquake, before the months of burning through a reservoir that had seemed oceanic and turned out to have edges. His handwriting looked different to him now. Younger. The handwriting of someone who didn't yet know what he was carrying.

He sat on the stool. Opened the drawer. His fingers found the spring-loaded pump mechanism: the brass housing cold under his thumb, the nested gears inside, the mainspring that clicked when he pressed it. Unfinished. He'd left it here the day his world cracked open and power had poured into him like water into a dry channel, and he'd walked away from the bench and the file and the problem of a bore that was two-tenths of a millimeter too wide, and he'd walked toward Dorenne and the deployments and the earthquake and the math, and the model had waited in the drawer the way inanimate things waited—without judgment, without expectation, without the need for anything to be different.

He set it on the bench. Found the calipers. Measured.

Two-tenths. Still there.

He picked up the fine file, set it against the bore's inner edge, and began.

The rasp of steel on brass. Small and rhythmic. Each stroke taking material away in quantities too small to see and too real to doubt, the gap narrowing toward the dimension his fingers told him was right. The file moved and his breathing settled into the rhythm and the reservoir—

The reservoir sat in his chest like a held breath.

Warm. Dense. Present the way a heartbeat was present, felt only when the room was quiet enough and the body was still enough and the mind stopped reaching for the next thing. He'd learned its edges over the past three weeks, not by measurement but by sensation, the way you learned the walls of a room you lived in by reaching out in the dark and finding them closer than you expected. The oceanic fullness of the first weeks after activation was gone. In its place: something finite and shaped, a volume he could almost cup in his hands, warm as a stone that had been sitting in sunlight and was now giving it back.

He didn't channel. Not because he was afraid of spending—though the fear was there, banked like coals, ready to flare if he let it. He didn't channel because the file was the right tool and the bore was the right problem and the work in front of him had nothing to do with magic. A pump mechanism with a tolerance issue. Steel on brass. Patience.

*What do you want to build with what's left?*

Haran's question. Three weeks old. Still turning. Still unanswered.

The file moved. The bore narrowed. The kettle began to whisper on the stove.

* * *

Haran arrived an hour later.

Ash heard him before he saw him: the particular cadence of the old man's walk, unhurried, slightly favoring the left side where a knee had been stiffening for years. The door opened. Cold air. The door closed. Coat on the hook. Hands held over the stove, the brief wordless communion of a body greeting warmth.

Haran appeared at the end of the bench with two cups and the tin of tea. He looked at the mechanism on the bench. At the file in Ash's hand. At the brass shavings curling on the bench surface like tiny question marks.

"The bore," he said.

"Almost there. Another tenth."

Haran set the cups down. Picked up the mechanism. Turned the output shaft with one finger, slowly, his head tilted, listening. The gears meshed with a faint clicking—not quite regular. He rotated the shaft the other way. Listened again.

"The bore's not your problem."

Ash's hand stilled on the file. "It wobbles under load."

"It wobbles. But not from the bore." Haran held the model up to the lamp. Rotated the shaft. "Your drive gear. Count the teeth."

Ash took the model back. Counted. Twenty-four.

"For a three-to-one reduction on this throw, you want twenty-two. You've got two extra teeth. They're causing interference every third rotation—the pitch circle's wrong. That's your wobble."

Ash counted again. Haran was right.

He'd been filing the bore for an hour, correcting a symptom while the cause sat one component upstream, and the realization settled into him with the specific weight of mistakes that were obvious only after someone pointed at the right part. The kind of mistake you made when your hands were engaged but your mind was circling something else.

"I should have caught that."

"You would have. Today, tomorrow. You were looking at the wrong part." Haran poured tea. He handed a cup to Ash and took his own to the central bench, not to his own station—to the end of Ash's bench, close enough to work, far enough to not crowd. The distance of a man who understood proximity as grammar and spoke it fluently. "You'll need to cut a new gear. The dividing plate on the shelf has a twenty-two hole ring."

"You made a plate for this ratio?"

"I made plates for a lot of ratios." Haran sipped his tea. "What else are you stuck on?"

Not *how are you doing.* Not *how are you feeling.* What are you stuck on. The question of a man who treated another man as a colleague, who had always treated him as a colleague, who had given a fourteen-year-old boy a key to a workshop and harder problems and better tools because that was how Haran expressed care—through attention and trust and the assumption that you were capable of the work in front of you.

"The valve assembly," Ash said. "I redesigned it as a gate valve, but the seal material is the problem. I need something flexible and watertight and durable under compression. Leather leaks."

Haran reached into his vest pocket and produced a small dark disc, coin-sized, slightly flexible. He set it on the bench.

Ash picked it up. Dense. Slightly tacky. It gave under thumb pressure and returned to shape. He pressed it against the bench surface. The seal was immediate—airtight, complete. No gap.

"Vulcanized tree sap," Haran said. "Petra's been working on it. She heats the raw sap with sulfur and it cross-links. Hardens without losing flex. She made a batch last week."

"How long does it hold?"

"Oldest sample is six weeks. Still intact. She's testing water degradation."

Ash turned the disc in his fingers. A seal material. Produced from tree sap and sulfur—abundant, non-magical, accessible to anyone with a heat source and the process. The kind of material that changed not one design but the assumptions underneath a dozen designs.

"This changes the valve," he said.

"It changes a lot of things."

Ash set the disc beside the pump housing. Two objects on a bench. A mechanism and a material. Neither of them magical. Both of them answers to problems the city was pretending could still be solved the old way.

He pulled down his project board and pinned it flat. The butterfly valve drawings were in the center, designed around the limitation of available seals. He unpinned them. Picked up a pencil. Began to sketch.

The gate valve emerged in clean lines. Simpler housing—straight-through bore, sliding gate. The seal interface flattened. The part count dropped from seven to three. He worked fast, the pencil moving with the confidence of a mind that had been carrying this design in the background and needed only a catalyst to release it.

Haran watched. He didn't comment until Ash set the pencil down.

"That's better," he said. He reached over and made a single mark on the drawing—a small X where the gate edge met the seal surface. "This is where it'll fail first. The steel will score the vulcanized material over time. You need a radius on the leading edge."

Ash saw it. Sharp edge dragging across softer material. A groove deepening with each cycle. "Two millimeters?"

"That should do it."

Ash modified the drawing. The radius was a tiny change—barely visible—but it would double the seal life. Maybe more. The kind of detail that separated a mechanism that worked from a mechanism that lasted, and lasting was the thing that mattered now, the thing that had to matter, because anything he built that depended on him to maintain it was just another version of the problem.

They sat with the finished drawing between them. The tea cooled. The stove settled. The workshop was warm.

* * *

The gear took the rest of the morning.

Ash worked at the lathe—treadle-driven, foot-powered, the flywheel heavy enough to hold speed through the cutting stroke. Brass stock clamped in the chuck. The dividing plate clicked into each of its twenty-two positions with mechanical certainty, and the cutting tool advanced in precise increments, and the gear took shape tooth by tooth: each one a small act of accuracy that added up to a function. The brass curled away in thin ribbons. The sound was clean and high and specific—the sound of material being shaped by material, no channeling involved, no pool expended, just the physics of a hard edge meeting a softer one at the right angle.

One tooth. Index. Cut. Two. Index. Cut.

The rhythm occupied him completely. His hands on the stock, his foot on the treadle, his eyes on the cutting edge. No room for the thoughts that pressed in during simpler work—the lit window, the broadsheet, the reservoir warm and waiting. The gear didn't care about any of it. The gear was becoming itself, tooth by tooth, and the process demanded everything he had.

Twenty-two teeth. He unclamped the blank, cleaned the edges with a fine file, and tested it against the mating gear by hand. The mesh was smooth. No click. No interference. The pitch circles matched and the teeth engaged and disengaged with the easy regularity of components that belonged together.

He assembled the mechanism. New drive gear seated. Output shaft inserted. Mainspring attached. He wound the spring to half tension and released it, and the shaft oscillated—forty millimeters, back and forth—smooth and clean and silent except for the soft breathing of metal parts in motion.

Twelve seconds before the spring unwound. No wobble. No shudder.

He wound it again. Full tension this time. The mechanism ran faster, the stroke quickening, and still smooth, the gears meshing at speed with the confidence of surfaces that fit.

"Better," Haran said from across the room.

Ash set the model down. His hands were warm from the work and slightly trembling from the fine motor control the lathe demanded, and he flexed them and felt the ache in his fingers and the satisfaction that lived in the same place, the body's record of effort that had produced something real.

* * *

The workshop filled by mid-morning.

Petra arrived first—slight, sharp-featured, carrying a canvas bag that clinked with sample jars. She nodded at Ash and went to her bench and began arranging her vulcanization test pieces in a row, each one labeled in her cramped handwriting with dates and sulfur ratios and water-exposure times. She worked with the compressed energy of a mind that moved faster than the physical world allowed and resented the lag.

Davel came next. Broad-shouldered, quiet, with the careful movements of a man who placed each step with intention. He was working on thermal insulation—non-magical barriers that could keep a building warm without channeling. His bench was covered in test panels: compressed fiber, clay-and-straw composite, treated wool packed between thin boards. Some had been tested. Some had failed. The failures were still there, labeled and annotated, because in Haran's workshop you didn't discard a failure until you'd extracted everything it could teach you.

"Morning," Davel said, settling onto his stool. "Haran said the Velden pump is holding."

"Twenty families now," Ash said. He'd heard it from Haran earlier. Twenty homes with water that didn't depend on anyone's pool. The pump he'd designed before the activation—the crank-and-spring mechanism he'd built at this bench with nothing in his chest but the absence of magic—was moving water in a building where the channeled plumbing had failed and no one had the pool to restore it.

"Petra and I scaled the design while you were away," Davel said. Not accusation. Not resentment. Simple fact. "Different buildings. Different pipe configurations. Same core mechanism."

While you were away. The words carried no weight he didn't put there himself. Davel wasn't making a point. He was describing a timeline. Ash had been away—in Dorenne's office, in the earthquake, in the deployments, burning through the reservoir—and the workshop had continued. The pump had been built. The design had been scaled. The work had gone on because the work didn't orbit him.

More tinkerers arrived through the morning. Some Ash knew from before—the regulars, the small community of people who'd been building non-magical alternatives when doing so was an eccentricity, a hobby, the pastime of people too stubborn or too practical or too foresighted to accept that the old way would last forever. Some he didn't know. New faces. A young woman with graphite-stained fingers and a cautious manner, working on a hand-cranked ventilation fan. A man Ash's age in a heavy leather apron, grinding a part on the whetstone with the focused intensity of the recently converted. An older woman he'd never seen, sitting at the bench beside Haran's, threading wire through a frame she was building for something Ash couldn't identify.

The project boards had new diagrams. The tool racks had been reorganized. Someone had built a second storage shelf along the back wall, rough pine on iron brackets, already half-filled with supplies. The workshop was growing. Not because of him. Not because of the Wellspring or the earthquake or the political fractures that were cracking the city along lines the quake had only revealed. Because Haran had been doing this work for thirty years, and the work had its own momentum, and the people who came to it stayed because the bench was real and the problems were real and the solutions, when they came, worked without anyone being special.

Nobody treated Ash's return as dramatic. He'd left. He'd come back. The bench was here.

Davel asked him to hold a clamp. Petra asked his opinion on a threading technique. The young woman with the ventilation fan asked if the bench beside his was taken.

"It's open," Ash said.

She sat down. Unpacked her tools. Began working. The workshop noise rose to its midday level—files rasping, hammers tapping, the murmur of people solving problems in parallel—and Ash worked inside it the way a gear worked inside a mechanism, engaged, turning, part of something that moved.

* * *

He pulled the broadsheet from his pocket after the midday break.

The workshop had thinned. Davel gone to a repair job. Petra out collecting sap samples from the gorge forest. The newer tinkerers dispersed. Just Ash and Haran, the stove settling into its afternoon burn, the rain that had started at noon drumming on the workshop's slate roof in a rhythm that sounded almost deliberate.

He smoothed the broadsheet on Haran's bench, beside the fuel valve the old man had been assembling all morning.

Haran set down the valve. Picked up the broadsheet. Read.

His face didn't change while he read. It never did. Haran processed information the way he processed material: completely, without visible reaction until the assessment was finished. He read the broadsheet the way he'd read a technical drawing—not for the individual lines but for the whole shape, the system the lines described, the failure modes implicit in the design.

He set it down.

"Where'd you find it?"

"Posted on the condemned building near the bottom of the hill. There are more. Every wall between here and the middle district."

"The language is getting sharper," Haran said.

"They're right about the problem."

"They are." Haran picked up a brass fitting from his bench and turned it in his fingers—an idle motion, the thing his hands did when his mind was working on something larger. "Being right about the problem is the easy part."

"Their answer is redistribution. Compel the elders to spend."

"I read it."

"And?"

Haran set the fitting down. Looked at him. The look was the one Ash had learned over fifteen years—steady, unhurried, the gaze of a man who wouldn't give you the conclusion when you could reach it yourself.

"What do you think happens if they get it?"

Ash sat with the question. The broadsheets demanded accounting, transparency, redistribution. Take the surplus from the elders who heated empty rooms and channeled light for decoration. Give it to the young who woke to cold stone and ran dry by afternoon. Level the disparity. Share the resource.

The math was there. The same math. The same declining curve. If you redistributed the elders' surplus, you slowed the decline for a generation. Maybe two. Then the total was gone and you'd built nothing that worked without it.

"It buys time," Ash said. "Not enough."

"No." Haran picked up the fitting again. Turned it. Set it down. "They're not wrong about the problem. What's your answer?"

The question landed where Haran's questions always landed—in the body, in the hands, in the space between understanding and action. Not *what do you think.* Not *what do you believe.* What's your answer. What are you going to build.

The reservoir pulsed once, quiet and warm, the held breath behind his ribs. A season. Maybe two. Not enough to fix a city. Not enough to reverse a decline that was planetary and irreversible and had nothing to do with villains or curses, just the slow exhaustion of a resource the world had been drawing down for centuries. Not enough for any of the obvious things.

Maybe enough to build something. If he knew what.

"I don't have one," Ash said.

Haran nodded. He didn't look disappointed. He didn't look patient. He looked like a man who had been asking himself the same question for thirty years and considered honest uncertainty a better starting point than premature conviction.

"The pump," Haran said, after a moment. "The valve. Petra's seal material. Davel's insulation. The ventilation designs. The combustion stoves." He gestured at the project boards along the far wall—the web of diagrams and notes and connecting strings that mapped the workshop's accumulated work. "These are pieces of something."

"Pieces of what?"

"I don't know yet." Haran looked at the broadsheet on his bench. At the circle-and-line symbol at the bottom. "Something that still works when the last pool in this city goes dry." He stood. Picked up his cup. Found it empty. Set it down. "Something that doesn't need you. Or me. Or anyone in particular."

He went to refill the kettle. The conversation was over—not because it was finished but because it had reached the edge of what words could carry, and the next useful thing was doing.

Ash sat at his bench.

The broadsheet lay on Haran's desk, its symbol facing the ceiling. The pump mechanism sat in front of him, its new gear meshing clean. The rubber disc sat beside it, dense and flexible, a seal material that changed the assumptions underneath a dozen designs. And the reservoir sat in his chest, warm and finite and waiting—not demanding, not diminishing, just present, the way a tool was present, available and shaped for use.

*What can you build that outlasts the building?*

The question had edges now. Three weeks of filing and measuring and cutting gears had given it edges the way work gave edges to everything—precise, testable, hard enough to cut with. Not an answer. Not yet. But a question sharp enough to be useful, specific enough to build toward, and that was something. That was the beginning of something.

He picked up the pencil. The gate valve prototype needed dimensions transferred to steel. He began.

* * *

The afternoon turned cold.

The light in the high windows went from gray to darker gray, and the rain thickened, and the stove burned through its fuel faster than the morning and needed feeding twice before three o'clock. Ash cut steel for the valve housing. Measured, scribed, hacksawed. The work was slow and physical—no channeling, no shortcuts, just the saw blade eating through the metal at the pace the metal allowed, his arm aching by the end of each cut, the sweat cooling on his back whenever he paused.

He could have heated the steel to working temperature with a thought. Could have shaped the housing with his hands and his will, the crystalline structure rearranging at his direction, the metal flowing like clay under a power that had once felt bottomless. The impulse was there, faint, the reflex of a man who'd spent months solving problems with the reservoir. He let it pass the way you let a thought pass during careful work—acknowledged, released, replaced by the next stroke of the saw.

Haran left at four. Coat buttoned, tools put away, the stove banked. He paused at the door.

"Tomorrow?"

"Tomorrow."

The nod. The door. The quiet.

Ash cleaned his station. Swept the metal shavings. Put the tools in their places. Stood at the bench with his hands flat on the wood—warm from the day's work, smooth from years of use—and the workshop settled around him in the early dark, and the reservoir sat in his chest, and the broadsheet sat on Haran's desk, and the question sat in the space between them.

Not the old question. Not *what do you want to build with what's left*—that was Haran's question, and it was the right question, but it had been turning for three weeks and it wasn't enough anymore. It had narrowed. Sharpened. Become something harder and more specific, the way a tolerance narrowed when you filed it, the way a gear became itself when the teeth were cut.

*What can you build that outlasts the building?*

Not a pump. A pump was a thing. Not a valve. Not a seal. Not a stove or a lamp or a ventilation fan. Those were pieces. They worked. They mattered. Twenty families had water because of a crank and a spring and a mechanism he'd designed in this room before his life changed. But twenty families was not a city, and a pump was not a system, and a system that depended on him to design and install and maintain every piece of it was just another version of the dependency the whole world was drowning in.

He needed something else. Something that could grow without him. Something that could be taught, replicated, sustained by people who had never set foot in this workshop and never would. Something that didn't orbit anyone.

He didn't know what it was. The not-knowing pressed against his chest alongside the reservoir—two kinds of weight, two kinds of warmth, one diminishing and one growing, and the growing one was the question, and the question was becoming the most important thing he carried.

He locked the door. Stepped over the flagstone. The rain had stopped and the evening air was sharp and the broadsheets on the walls caught the last gray light, their circle-and-line symbols watching him like eyes.

He walked uphill. The stairs were long. He took them at the pace his legs knew, the pace he'd learned when forty flights was the only way up and dignity meant arriving without visible effort. His body still knew that pace. His body remembered everything—the stairs, the cold, the years of doing things the hard way in a world built for people who could do them easily. Those years were still in him. Under the reservoir, under the power, under everything that had changed, the years were still there, and they were not nothing.

The question climbed with him. Sharpening with every step. A fraction at a time, the way a file shaped metal—patient, precise, one stroke closer to the tolerance that mattered.

His hands remembered.

They always had.
