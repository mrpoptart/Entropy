# Worldbuilding

## Setting Overview
A world at a modern-equivalent level of development, built on magic rather than technology. Magic is personal and bodily — channeled through hands, voice, and intent. It is intimate, physical, and woven into every aspect of daily life. There is no magical "grid" or infrastructure that operates independently; everything depends on people channeling their personal power.

The magic is dying. It was never meant to last — not broken, not cursed, simply finite, like a geological age ending. The decline is generational, uneven, and politically contentious. The world is transitioning out of a magical era, and most of it isn't ready.

## The Nature of Magic

### The Planetary Resource
Magic is a finite planetary resource. Every person is born connected to it and draws a small personal reservoir that replenishes daily (the "trickle"). This trickle powers daily life — heating, healing, building, communicating, everything.

But the total planetary reservoir has been declining for generations. Each generation is born with a slightly weaker trickle than the last. The decline is slow enough to debate, fast enough to measure if you're paying attention.

**Magic was always temporary.** The decline is not caused by a villain, a curse, or overuse. Magic was a finite phase of the world's existence, like a resource laid down over eons and drawn down over centuries. There is nothing to reverse and no one to blame. The only question is what comes next.

### How Magic Works (The Personal Channel)
Magic is channeled through the body — through hands, voice, breath, and focused intent. It is a physical act, more like exertion than like pressing a button. Using magic feels like something. It's effort, it's craft, it's skill.

Key properties:
- **Personal and bodily.** There are no magical devices or automated systems. Everything magical requires a person actively channeling.
- **Skill-based.** Raw power matters, but technique and practice determine how efficiently and precisely you can channel. A skilled person with a weak trickle can outperform a sloppy person with a stronger one — within limits.
- **Exhaustible daily.** Each person's trickle replenishes overnight, but if you spend it all by noon, you're done until tomorrow. Managing your daily reserve is a basic life skill.
- **Intimate.** Healing someone means placing your hands on them and pouring your energy into their body. Warming a room means standing in it and channeling heat. Magic is not abstract — it's relational and present.

### What Daily Magic Looks Like
*(Working sketches — to be refined)*

**Heating & Shelter:** People warm their homes by channeling — standing in a room and radiating heat, or warming stones/walls that hold the energy for a while. Architecture is designed to retain channeled warmth. In colder seasons, heating is a daily chore, like stoking a fire but with your body.

**Healing:** Medical care is hands-on channeling. A healer places their hands on a patient and directs their personal magic into the injury or illness. It's effective but tiring — a healer has a limited number of patients per day based on their trickle. As trickles decline, wait times grow and serious conditions go undertreated.

**Communication:** Paired resonance — objects created together that transmit intent between them, but they require the user to channel into them to send a message. Like a phone you power with your own energy each time you use it. Range and clarity depend on how much you can channel.

**Transportation:** People channel to move vehicles — carriages or trams powered by teams of channelers working in shifts. Large-scale transit requires cooperative channeling — groups pouring their trickle into a shared system.

**Construction & Maintenance:** Buildings are raised and maintained through channeling. Grand structures from the height of the magical age require significant ongoing channeling to remain stable — built assuming a level of capacity that is no longer guaranteed.

**Agriculture:** Crops are grown with channeled encouragement — accelerated growth, pest resistance, soil enrichment. Labor-intensive and tied to the farmer's personal trickle. As trickles weaken, yields drop.

**The key insight for the reader:** Everything in this world depends on individual people choosing to spend their personal energy. There is no passive infrastructure. When people's trickles weaken, everything weakens.

### The Generational Power Gap
Older people were born when the planetary reservoir was fuller. Their trickles are measurably stronger than their children's, and dramatically stronger than their grandchildren's. This isn't subtle — it's quantifiable and visible.

**This creates a structural power imbalance:**
- Older generations hold disproportionate economic, political, and social power because they are literally more magically capable
- Institutions, infrastructure, and social expectations were built by and for people with stronger trickles
- Younger people inherit systems that demand more magical capacity than they have, and are told their struggles are personal failures rather than systemic decline
- The elderly establishment has deep incentives to deny or minimize the decline, because acknowledging it threatens the systems that keep them in power

**This IS generational wealth, made visible and physical.** An elder's stronger trickle isn't earned — it's an accident of birth timing. But the entire social order is built on the assumption that it's natural and right.

### The Decline as Climate Change
The magical decline maps to climate change in its social dynamics:

**Uneven impact.** Wealthy urban centers and powerful elders still have enough capacity to maintain comfortable lives. They can afford denial. Poor, rural, and young communities are already in crisis — daily magic isn't enough to maintain the systems they depend on.

**Generational divide.** Older people remember when magic was stronger and resent the decline. Younger people have never known anything else — some are angry, some are adapting, some are nihilistic.

**Political factions:**
- *Denialists:* The decline is exaggerated or cyclical. No action needed.
- *Preservationists:* Ration and regulate magic use to slow the decline. Often authoritarian in practice.
- *Accelerationists:* If magic is ending, spend it all now — one last golden age.
- *Technologists:* Build non-magical alternatives. Seen as either visionaries or traitors.
- *Authoritarians:* Centralize remaining magic under state control for "optimal allocation."

**Institutional failure.** Systems built for a high-magic world are slowly degrading. The cracks appear first in places with the fewest resources.

### Wellsprings (The Massive Finite Reservoir)
Rarely, the remaining planetary magic concentrates in a single person, creating a Wellspring — an individual with an enormous, finite, non-renewable reservoir of power. This may be a symptom of the decline itself.

Key properties:
- **Finite and non-renewable.** A Wellspring's reservoir was full at activation and only decreases. Roughly "a million points" — seemingly infinite at first, but absolutely not.
- **Scalable.** Can do tiny things or enormous things. Cost scales with effect.
- **Efficiency is learnable.** Can't create more power, but can learn to accomplish more with less.
- **No additional cost.** Using Wellspring power doesn't damage body, mind, or soul. The only loss is the power itself.
- **When empty, empty.** A depleted Wellspring has nothing — not even the ordinary trickle (if they never had one to begin with).

### The Protagonist's Unique Situation
The protagonist was born without any magical ability — not even the ordinary trickle. In a world where basic magic is as normal as breathing, they were effectively disabled. Then they became a Wellspring, going from nothing to the most powerful person alive.

This means:
- No prior magical experience — steep learning curve, wild early expenditures
- Never had the "normal" relationship with magic everyone else takes for granted
- When the reservoir empties, they return to zero — not to the baseline trickle, but to nothing
- They are the only person alive who already knows what life without magic feels like — uniquely and painfully qualified to guide others through the transition the whole world is heading toward

## The Primary Setting: The City

A grand metropolis built across generations of abundant magic. The architecture is breathtaking and impossible — structures too large, too delicate, or too complex to stand without constant magical maintenance. A monument to what magic could build at its peak.

**Stratification by generational power:**
- *Elder districts:* Maintained by residents with strong trickles. Buildings gleam. Infrastructure works. Life feels normal. The powerful live here and see no crisis.
- *Middle districts:* Things work, but with effort. Daily channeling leaves residents more tired than it used to. Maintenance is falling behind.
- *Young/poor districts:* Fraying edges. Infrastructure failing. Buildings cracking. Quiet experimentation with mechanical alternatives. Resentment toward the elder districts.

**The protagonist's experience of the city (before the Wellspring):** A person without magic in a city that runs on it. Can't use channeler-powered transit. Can't contribute to maintenance shifts. Can't do most jobs. Can't participate in basic social rituals that involve sharing magic. A person without electricity in a city built on the grid.

## The Rural World
*(To be developed in detail)*

Rural and frontier communities are where the decline hits hardest and earliest. They are also where non-magical alternatives are being invented out of necessity — crude mechanical tools, manual agricultural techniques, cooperative practices that don't depend on individual magical strength.

**The irony:** The rural world is already living in the future that the city refuses to imagine. By book three, the protagonist may recognize that these "backwards" communities figured out the answer before the sophisticated capital did.

## Emerging Technology
Non-magical technology is being developed as a direct response to the decline. Mechanical engineering, chemistry, combustion, basic medicine — invented or rediscovered by people who can no longer rely on channeling.

Technology carries social stigma in many circles. Using non-magical tools can be seen as an admission of weakness, a betrayal of tradition, or a sign of poverty. In other circles — particularly among the young, the poor, and the pragmatic — it's the future.
