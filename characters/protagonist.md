## Basics
- **Age:** Early-to-mid 20s at the start of Book One
- **Gender:** Male
- **Location:** The city (name TBD) — the world's political and cultural center
- **Magical Status (pre-Wellspring):** None. Born without any magical ability — not even the ordinary renewable trickle. Effectively disabled in a world that runs on magic.
- **Magical Status (post-Wellspring):** The most powerful channeler alive. Massive finite reservoir, non-renewable.

## Who He Is Before the Power

### Emotional Baseline
Outwardly cheerful, privately grieving. He has learned that bitterness is a luxury he can't afford — if he's angry, people stop feeling sorry for him and start avoiding him. So he smiles, he adapts, he finds workarounds. His cheerfulness isn't fake, exactly — he's genuinely warm and likable — but it's a performance built on survival. At night, alone, he sits with the weight of everything he can't do and everything he'll never be.

His cheerfulness is also an apology. Every smile is him trying to be worth the trouble. Every joke, every moment of warmth — that's him paying rent with the only currency he has: being pleasant to be around.

### Core Strengths (Independent of Magic)
**Physical capability.** In a world where everyone uses magic for physical tasks, most people are unremarkable in body. The protagonist has had to do everything by hand his entire life. He's one of the more physically fit people in the city — strong, enduring, practical with his body. He doesn't think of this as a strength; it's just the exhausting reality of his existence.

**Intellect.** This is his real weapon. He's brilliant — sharp, analytical, able to see systems and solutions that others miss. But intelligence without magic in this world is like being a brilliant engineer with no tools. He can diagnose problems he can't fix. He's the person in the tinkerer workshop who figures out why the mechanism isn't working, then has to watch someone with a trickle do the final step.

When the Wellspring activates, the intellect becomes critical. He doesn't just have power — he has power plus a mind that's spent a lifetime optimizing around constraints. This is why his reservoir lasts longer than a less thoughtful person's would. He's been doing more with less his entire life.

The intellect also makes the grief sharper. He's smart enough to do the math early. He can see the end coming long before he has to face it.

**Social intelligence and relationship-building.** Relationships are his only currency. He has spent his life refining his ability to connect with people, to be useful, to be liked, to hold onto anyone who treats him as a full person rather than a charity case. He has deep connections not because he's naturally charismatic, but because he's *worked at it* — being someone people want around despite his condition. This is a genuine skill that will matter throughout the trilogy.

### Economic Situation
He has recently been taken on by a powerful patron (character TBD) as an aide or assistant — a charity case dressed up as opportunity. This feels like the first real break in his life: a salary, dignity, relief from draining his family. He's deeply grateful and refuses to see the red flags.

Before the patron, he survived through a combination of family support and whatever physical labor he could find — the unglamorous work that nobody with magic would do.

In his spare time, he's part of the city's small tinkerer community — people experimenting with non-magical mechanical alternatives. It's not profitable. It's a hobby. But it's the one place where not having magic isn't a disability, and he's welcome there because he understands physical materials and mechanics intuitively after a lifetime of working with his hands.

### What He Wants (Start of Book One)
He wants to stop being a drain on the people he loves and start contributing. He wants to prove — to himself, to his family, to the world — that he belongs and is not a charity case. The patron position feels like the answer to this. He is hoping so hard that it's real that he can't see what it actually is.

### What He Needs (The Arc)
He needs to learn that his worth was never about capability — magical or otherwise. He needs to separate his identity from what he can or can't do. He needs to discover that the things he learned in his years of powerlessness — resilience, empathy, physical competence, the ability to connect, the knowledge of what it's like to live without magic — are more valuable than the Wellspring ever was.

## The Wellspring Arc

### Book One: Becoming
The Wellspring activates. He goes from nothing to the most powerful person alive. The euphoria is overwhelming — not just power, but *relief*. For the first time, he's not broken. He's not a burden. He's extraordinary.

He spends freely because the reservoir feels infinite and because every expenditure is a celebration of no longer being the person he was. He defines himself by the power. He starts to drift from the tinkerer community (why tinker with machines when you can reshape reality?), and his family relationships shift as he no longer needs their support.

Late in Book One, through his intellect, he realizes the reservoir is finite. The math doesn't lie. This isn't forever. The question shifts from "what can I become?" to "what should I do with what's left?"

### Book Two: Reckoning
He tries to use his remaining power to change the world — to fix the big problems directly. He learns that force-applied change doesn't stick, that the world's systems are more complex than any individual can override. Meanwhile the power keeps draining and the world's broader magical decline is becoming undeniable.

He confronts the temptation to hoard what's left for self-preservation — to stay powerful, to never go back to being *that person*. This is the deepest fear: not losing the power itself, but returning to the helpless, burdensome, excluded person he was before.

### Book Three: Letting Go
He knows what needs to be built and approximately how to build it. He spends his remaining reservoir deliberately — not on spectacle, but on preparing the world for the transition out of magic. He empowers others, teaches, builds systems.

The final expenditure drains him completely. He returns to zero — not to a normal trickle, but to the same nothing he started with. And he has to live with that. The epilogue shows him at peace — ordinary, contributing in non-magical ways, drawing on the skills and relationships he built across a lifetime. He is proof that the other side is livable.

## Contradictions to Preserve
- He's cheerful and warm, AND he's carrying deep private grief
- He's grateful to the people who help him, AND he resents needing help
- He's smart enough to see his situation clearly, AND he's capable of willful blindness when hope is on the line (the patron)
- He's physically capable, AND the world treats him as disabled
- After the Wellspring: he's the most powerful person alive, AND he's the least experienced magic user in the world
