# Chapter 21

The walk to Dorenne's office took forty minutes.

Ash climbed the terraced streets in the early light, hands in his pockets, the cold pressing through his coat at the seams. The heating lattices in the paving stones were dim this high up, not dead, but fading, the amber glow of channeling that hadn't been refreshed in days. His breath came in thin clouds. The reservoir hummed in his chest, steady and finite, and he carried the numbers the way you carried a stone in your pocket: aware of its weight with every step.

He'd left the workshop at dawn. Slept on the floor beside his workbench for a few hours, real sleep, deeper than he'd managed in the command post, the kind of sleep that came after the worst was known and the body simply gave out. He'd woken with the numbers still there, unchanged, and Haran's question still there too, and between them a quiet space that felt like the gap between exhaling and drawing the next breath.

He hadn't planned what to say. The numbers were plain enough. Dorenne dealt in numbers.

The Infrastructure Directorate sat at the Second Terrace's eastern edge, its facade reinforced so many times that the stone had a layered quality, like rings in a tree. The guards nodded him through. He climbed the interior stairs to the third floor, his boots on stone, the faint mineral smell of old channeling in the walls, and found Dorenne's office door open, as it always was before the building filled.

He stopped in the doorway.

She was at her desk. The desk was broad, dark wood, covered in the organized disorder of a person who knew where everything was: deployment schedules in stacked sheaves, district maps with colored pins, the thick ledger where she tracked cumulative expenditures in her precise, angular hand. A cup of tea sat near her right elbow, steam rising in a thin line. She'd warmed it herself; he could tell from the faint residual heat in the air, the efficiency of someone who had spent forty years not wasting anything.

She looked up.

"Ash." Her expression shifted, not dramatically, not the practiced welcome she gave petitioners and council colleagues. Something smaller. The slight easing around her eyes that meant she was glad to see him, or at least the version of glad that Dorenne permitted herself. "You're early."

"I need to talk to you."

She studied him. He watched her read his face the way she read her charts, systematically, pulling data from the set of his jaw, the stillness of his hands, the fact that he stood in the doorway instead of walking in and dropping into the chair the way he usually did.

"Sit down," she said.

He sat.

* * *

He told her the numbers.

Not the full accounting, not the reconstruction, the hours at the desk with the candle burning down, the scratch of pencil on paper as the columns resolved into a shape he couldn't unsee. He gave her what mattered. The way he'd given Haran. But differently. With Haran, he'd been telling a man he trusted about a wound. With Dorenne, he was delivering a report.

He didn't think about it that way. Not yet.

"The Wellspring started at roughly five thousand elder-days," he said. "I've used approximately twenty-eight hundred. Maybe three thousand." He paused. "The earthquake alone was fifteen hundred."

Dorenne's hands were flat on the desk. She didn't move.

"At current deployment rates, your schedule, three to six sites a day, I have approximately eighteen weeks. At expanded rates, eleven. At theoretical master efficiency, which I'm years from reaching, twenty-one." He looked at the desk between them, at the edge of a deployment chart visible under her left hand. "Call it a season. Maybe two."

He stopped.

The office held its silence. The tea steamed. Somewhere in the building, a door opened and closed, the first of the morning staff arriving, footsteps in the corridor below, the building beginning its slow daily animation.

Dorenne's face was still.

He waited for it. He didn't know what he was waiting for: shock, grief, the human response to learning that a person you'd worked beside for months was running down like a lamp burning the last of its oil. Something in her eyes, maybe. The flicker he'd seen before, in the moments between the meetings and the charts, when she looked at him and saw not the Wellspring but the young man she'd hired to carry files and organize her schedule and sit in the transit hub when the world was falling apart.

Her hands moved.

She pulled the deployment chart from under the stack at her left elbow. Unfolded it. The chart was covered in hash marks and annotations: sites completed, sites pending, resource allocations color-coded by district and priority level. She smoothed it flat with both palms.

"Eighteen weeks at current rates," she said. Her voice was level. Precise. The voice she used in council sessions, in briefings, in the meetings where she parsed budgets and allocated crews and made the decisions that kept water flowing and buildings standing. "What's the per-site average expenditure?"

Ash looked at her.

"Roughly," she said. "Ballpark."

"Depends on the site. Structural reinforcement runs eighty to two hundred thousand. Water system repairs, forty to a hundred. Heating grid work is cheaper, twenty to sixty."

She was writing. A pencil had appeared in her hand (he hadn't seen her pick it up) and she was making notes in the margin of the deployment chart. Small, precise figures. Calculations.

"If we reduce the western district work," she said, the pencil moving, "and focus on the three critical aqueducts, the Thornwall main, the Ridgeline feed, and the southern collector, we can extend the useful output by—" She paused. Calculated. "Six to eight weeks. Possibly more, if we shift the heating grid maintenance to conventional crews and reserve your capacity for structural and water."

She looked up. Her eyes were clear. Focused. The eyes of a woman who had received information and was processing it, not as news about a person, but as a constraint on a system she managed.

"The secondary bridge reinforcements can wait," she continued. "They're rated for another two years minimum without channeling. The eastern residential blocks, those are cosmetic, frankly. The cracks are unsightly but not structural. If we cut those entirely and redirect—"

"Dorenne."

She stopped. The pencil hovered over the chart.

He looked at her. She looked at him. The office was very quiet.

"I just told you I'm dying," he said.

The word surprised him. He hadn't planned it. It wasn't accurate; the Wellspring's depletion wouldn't kill him. He'd simply become what he'd been before: empty. A man with no pool, no reservoir, no magic at all. The dead weight. The charity case. The person Dorenne had hired out of a compassion so intertwined with strategy that neither of them could find the seam.

But the word had come out, and it sat between them on the desk like a stone dropped into still water.

Dorenne set the pencil down.

Something moved across her face. Not the institutional mask, something underneath it. A ripple in water that was usually so controlled its surface appeared solid. Her lips parted. Her hand, the one that had been holding the pencil, curled slightly on the desk, the fingers drawing inward toward the palm.

"I know," she said. Quietly. "I know what you told me."

"You started planning."

"Yes."

"Before you—" He stopped. He didn't know how to say it. Before you reacted. Before you felt anything. Before you looked at me and saw a person instead of a resource.

But he couldn't say that, because he wasn't sure it was fair. And he couldn't say it because some part of him, the part that had spent a lifetime reading rooms and managing other people's comfort, understood that fairness was beside the point. What mattered was what he'd watched. What he'd seen her hands do.

Dorenne pressed her palms flat on the desk again. The deployment chart crinkled under them.

"What would you have me do?" she asked.

The question was genuine. He heard it in her voice, not defensive, not rhetorical. She was asking. The woman who always knew the next move was asking what the move should be.

"I don't know," he said.

"I have a responsibility," she said. "To the districts that depend on this work. To the crews. To the forty thousand people who drink from the Thornwall aqueduct every day." She wasn't raising her voice. She never raised her voice. "You've told me that a critical resource is finite and declining. My job—my actual, specific job—is to manage finite resources for the greatest public benefit. That's what I was doing."

"I know."

"If a water main is failing and I have one crew, I send them to the main that serves the most people. That's not callous. That's responsible."

"I know." And he almost stopped there. He could feel the old machinery engaging: the accommodation, the smoothing-over, the part of him that had spent a lifetime making other people's discomfort go away. *She's right. She's doing her best. Let it go.* His mouth opened to say something conciliatory.

What came out was: "I'm not a water main."

Dorenne's jaw tightened. A small motion, the kind of motion you'd miss if you weren't watching for it, if you hadn't spent years in this office learning the topography of this particular face.

"No," she said. "You're not."

Silence.

"But the power is a resource," she said. "It has always been a resource. That's not—I didn't make that true, Ash. The Wellspring has always been finite and deployable and subject to allocation decisions. The only thing that's changed is that now you know the timeline."

She was right.

That was the thing he couldn't get past. She was right. The Wellspring was a resource. It had always been a resource. Every site he'd reinforced, every aqueduct he'd repaired, every building he'd held together during the earthquake, those were allocation decisions. Dorenne's decisions, or decisions she'd shaped, and they'd been good decisions. People had water because of those decisions. People were alive.

She was right, and the rightness of it sat in his chest like a fist.

* * *

"The charts," he said. "The deployment schedule. The hash marks. The projections running forward across months." He looked at the desk between them. "When did you start counting?"

Dorenne was quiet for a moment. "The second week of reconstruction."

"Before I knew."

"Yes."

"You watched the number climb. Week after week. You tracked it. You calculated what I was spending and you saw the trajectory, and you didn't tell me." His voice was level. Not accusing. Factual, the way she was factual. "You let me keep going."

"The work was necessary. Every site—"

"I know. Every site was necessary. Every deployment was justified. Every allocation was correct." He pressed his palms flat on his thighs. "That's not what I'm asking. I'm asking why you didn't tell me I was running down."

Her jaw tightened. The small motion, the one you'd miss if you weren't watching.

"Because you would have stopped," she said. Quietly. "Or slowed. Or started making your own calculations about which sites mattered and which didn't, and the work would have suffered, and people would have—"

"So you managed it."

"I managed the information. The way I manage—"

"The way you manage a resource." The word sat between them. He heard himself say it and felt the shape of the thing that had been building in his chest since the pencil. "That's what I am in the framework. In your framework. A resource with a depletion curve. And the optimal strategy for a depleting resource is to extract maximum value before it's gone."

"That is not—" Her voice cracked. Not much. A hairline fracture. "That is not what I—"

"I know." And he did. He could see it in her face: the genuine anguish, the woman behind the institution fighting to get past the walls she'd built around herself. She hadn't meant to. The framework had done it for her, the same framework that made her brilliant, that made her effective, that made her the best Director of Infrastructure in a generation. The framework that turned everything it touched into a system to be optimized. Including him.

"You care about me," he said. "I believe that. And you built a deployment schedule around my output and tracked my expenditure in columns and planned a six-month allocation strategy and never once asked me what I wanted to do with what's left. Both of those things are true. Both of them. At the same time."

Her hand moved to the tea cup. Lifted it. Set it down without drinking. The motion was small, controlled, the kind of displacement activity that meant the body needed to do something and the mind wouldn't let it do what it wanted.

"So what are you saying?" she asked.

"I'm saying I need time."

"Time."

"To think. About the deployment schedule. About the allocation. About—" He stopped. About whether I'm a person to you, or a line item that learned to talk. But he didn't say that. It would have been cruel, and it would have been only partly true, and Dorenne didn't deserve cruelty, even now. Especially now.

"About what I want to build with what's left," he said.

Something crossed her face at the word *build*. He couldn't read it. Surprise, maybe, or the faint recognition of a concept that didn't fit inside her framework.

"How much time?" she asked.

He almost laughed. The question was so perfectly Dorenne—so precisely, beautifully, devastatingly institutional, that it nearly broke the flat calm he'd maintained since sitting down. How much time. As though grief operated on a schedule. As though the process of deciding whether you were a person or a resource could be mapped onto a Gantt chart and assigned a completion date.

"I don't know," he said.

She nodded. The nod was careful. Measured.

"The Thornwall aqueduct—" she began.

"Not today."

She closed her mouth. Opened it. Closed it again.

"All right," she said.

He stood. The chair scraped against the stone floor, a small sound, loud in the quiet office. He looked at her across the desk, across the deployment charts and the district maps and the ledger of cumulative expenditures. She looked back.

Her eyes were wet.

Not crying. Dorenne didn't cry, or if she did, she did it in private, in the hours before the building filled, in the space between the role and the person. But her eyes were bright in a way that had nothing to do with the morning light coming through the window. Bright and still and fixed on him with an expression he'd never seen on her face before.

He didn't name it. He couldn't. It was there and then the institutional mind closed over it like water smoothing over a dropped stone, and her face was her face again, controlled, composed, the Director of Infrastructure receiving a status update and adjusting her plans accordingly.

"I'll hold the western district sites," she said. "Until you've decided."

"Thank you."

"The aqueducts can't wait more than two weeks. The Ridgeline feed is—"

"I know."

He turned toward the door.

"Ash."

He stopped. Didn't turn.

"The transit hub," she said. Behind him, her voice was quiet. Stripped of the institutional register, or almost. "After the earthquake. When you came in covered in dust and you couldn't stop shaking and I sat with you for an hour because you needed someone to sit with you."

He stood in the doorway and listened.

"That wasn't allocation," she said. "That was—I was there because I wanted to be there."

He believed her. That was the worst of it. He believed her completely, and it didn't change anything, because the woman who sat with him in the transit hub and the woman who pulled out the deployment chart in the same breath as learning he was running down, they were the same woman. They had always been the same woman. The care and the calculation lived in the same hands, drew from the same well, operated on the same face. He couldn't take one without the other, and he couldn't live with both.

"I know," he said.

He walked out.

His jaw ached. He realized he'd been clenching it since he sat down in her office. His chest was tight, not the dropping sensation of the math, but something that sat behind his ribs like a closed fist and wouldn't open.

He descended the stairs. The entrance hall was bright with morning light. He was three steps from the doors when he saw her.

Ryn was crossing the entrance hall.

She was coming from the east corridor, the one that led to the public records office, where maintenance crews filed completion reports and picked up work orders. She had a folded sheaf of papers under one arm and her work jacket on, the heavy canvas one with the singed patch on the left sleeve that she'd never bothered to repair. He'd made that patch, a channeling exercise gone wide, months ago, before he'd learned control. She still wore it. Her hair was pulled back. Her boots were dusty.

She saw him at the same moment he saw her.

She stopped.

He stopped.

The entrance hall moved around them, staff passing, doors opening, the building's morning rhythm asserting itself. They stood ten paces apart, and Ryn looked at his face.

She didn't ask.

He watched her read him the way she'd always read him, not the way Dorenne read him, pulling data points and processing patterns, but the way a person reads a face they've known since childhood. The way you read weather. Not analyzing it. Just seeing it.

Her expression changed. Not dramatically. The set of her mouth shifted. Something in her eyes; he couldn't name it, didn't try. She tucked the papers tighter under her arm.

She walked toward him.

Not fast. Not with urgency. She crossed the ten paces between them at the pace of a person going where they were going, and when she reached him she didn't stop. She fell into step beside him, matching his stride, and together they walked through the doors and out into the morning.

The Second Terrace was bright. The sun had cleared the eastern ridge and the light lay across the stone in long, clean lines, sharp-edged where the buildings threw shadows. The air was cold and thin. Their breath came in clouds that the wind pulled apart.

They walked.

Down the broad steps from the Directorate entrance. Along the terrace road, past the Council Hall and the records archive and the small plaza where a fountain ran on channeled pressure that someone (a team of middle-generation workers, probably) replenished each dawn. Past the tea vendor who was setting up his cart, the smell of dried herbs and hot water drifting across the walkway. Past a pair of maintenance workers heading the other direction, tool bags over their shoulders, faces set with the patient exhaustion of people who'd been up since before light.

They walked a block. Two blocks. Ryn said nothing. Ash said nothing. Their footsteps fell in an uneven rhythm on the stone, his boots heavier, hers quicker, the sound of two people who'd walked together enough times that their bodies remembered the choreography even when everything else had changed.

The terrace road curved south. The buildings thinned. Ahead, a junction where the road split: one branch descending toward the middle districts, the other continuing along the terrace toward the trade quarter where Ryn's crew was based. Her route. Not his.

She slowed.

He slowed.

They stopped at the junction. The city spread below them: terraces descending in broad steps, rooftops catching the morning light, the distant shimmer of the gorge where the river ran deep and cold. Somewhere in the lower districts, a pump ran on a hand crank and a spring, and three families turned it each morning, and water flowed.

Ryn turned to him.

She didn't say anything. Her face held the thing he'd seen in the entrance hall, the shifted expression, the something in her eyes that wasn't pity and wasn't worry and wasn't the careful, calibrated sympathy of a person managing someone's distress. It was simpler than any of those. It was a person looking at another person and seeing them.

She reached out and squeezed his arm.

Her hand was warm through his coat sleeve. Strong. The hand of someone who worked with tools all day, who channeled with the practical, unshowy competence of a person for whom magic was a means, not an identity. She held his arm for a moment, two seconds, three, and then she let go.

She turned and took the branch toward the trade quarter. Her boots on the stone. The work jacket, the singed patch, the papers under her arm. Walking away at the pace of a person with a shift to get to and a life that didn't stop because someone else's was falling apart.

His mouth opened.

*Ryn.*

The word was right there. Lodged in his throat, ready. One word. Her name. And after it, what? He didn't know. Come back. Wait. Stay. Something. Anything that would fill the space between them with more than silence, that would turn her around and bring her back to the junction where he stood with his fist still clenched and his chest still tight and the numbers still in his head and Dorenne's pencil still scratching on the deployment chart in the part of his mind where the last hour lived.

He didn't call.

She walked. The distance grew. Her figure diminished, the jacket, the boots, the papers, the straight line of her back, and then she turned a corner and was gone.

He stood at the junction.

The city hummed below him. The wind pressed against his coat. The reservoir sat in his chest, warm and finite, its edges as clear as the walls of a room he'd finally measured. Twenty-two hundred elder-days, give or take. A number. A budget. A countdown dressed up as a resource, or a resource dressed up as a countdown, and the difference between the two was supposed to matter but right now, standing alone on a terrace road in the morning cold, it didn't.

He thought about Dorenne's pencil. The way it had appeared in her hand (he hadn't seen her pick it up) and started moving before the silence between them had finished settling. Before the word *dying* had cooled. The pencil moving, the figures forming, the institutional mind engaging with the data the way it engaged with every piece of data that entered that office: as a problem to be solved. A resource to be allocated. A constraint to be optimized around.

She wasn't wrong. That was the thing that sat in his chest and wouldn't dissolve, the fist that wouldn't open. She wasn't wrong about the aqueducts or the western district or the calculation that said: if you have a diminishing resource, you allocate it where it does the most good. She wasn't wrong about any of it. She was a competent public servant performing her function with the precision and care that had made her the best Director of Infrastructure in a generation, and the forty thousand people who drank from the Thornwall aqueduct every morning would agree that her response was exactly right.

And Ryn had squeezed his arm and said nothing and walked away, and that had been exactly right too, and the two kinds of rightness had nothing to do with each other.

He turned downhill.

The walk was long. He didn't hurry. The terraces descended and the buildings changed, narrower, more worn, the stone showing cracks that had been patched and re-patched. The heating lattices dimmed as he went: amber on the upper terraces, then faint, then dark, the stones cold under his boots. He passed a water tap that dripped steadily into a basin no one had emptied. He passed a wall where someone had chalked a message he didn't stop to read.

He walked, and the tight thing in his chest didn't loosen, and he didn't try to make it. Some things didn't loosen. Some things sat where they were and you carried them, the way you carried a stone in your pocket, the way you carried a number in your head, the way you carried the knowledge that the first person outside your family who'd ever given you a chance had heard you say *I'm dying* and reached for a pencil.

The wind was cold on his face. He walked.

* * *

He didn't go to the workshop.

He didn't go to his parents' house. He didn't go to the command post, where his cot sat in the dark room and the papers were still spread on the desk and the candle had burned down to a stub of wax in a tin holder. He walked through the middle districts and down into the lower terraces and found a bench on a public overlook where the city fell away below in a cascade of rooftops and narrow streets and the distant sound of water in the gorge.

He sat.

The sun climbed. The city moved around him, people on their way to work, to market, to the hundred small tasks that made up a day in Solathis. A woman passed with a child on her hip, the child's hand trailing light from a tiny channeling exercise, the flickering glow of a pool so small it could barely warm a cup of tea. Two old men sat on a bench across the overlook, talking in low voices, one of them absently maintaining a warming field around both of them, the faint shimmer of channeled heat visible if you knew where to look. A maintenance crew worked on a cracked retaining wall three terraces down, their channeling visible as brief, controlled flares against the stone.

Ash watched them. The crew. Four people, three channeling in careful rotation while the fourth mixed mortar by hand. They worked with the practiced coordination of a team that had done this before: the lead channeler directing energy into the crack while the other two braced the surrounding stone, the hand-worker filling the gap behind them. Efficient. Methodical. The kind of work that used to be done by a single elder in an afternoon and now required four people and most of a morning.

The kind of work he'd been doing alone. Faster. Cheaper. One Wellspring, deployed to maximum effect, accomplishing in minutes what a crew of four needed hours to finish. Dorenne's math. The math that said: why send four when you can send one? Why spend four pools when you can spend a fraction of the Wellspring? Why maintain crews at all when the most efficient allocation is a single extraordinary resource applied at scale?

The math that said: he was a budget line.

He sat on the bench and watched the crew work and thought about the pump in the Velden building. The pump that ran on a crank and a spring. The pump that twelve households had paid for with a hundred and forty marks and six weeks of labor. The pump that Petra had calibrated and Davel had improved and that one kid from the building next door had watched being built every day until she asked to apprentice.

The pump that didn't need him.

He thought about Dorenne's chart. The hash marks. The sites completed, the sites pending, the color-coded priorities. His name, not written anywhere, because he wasn't a name on the chart. He was the resource the chart was built to optimize. The variable that every calculation assumed. The Wellspring. The unprecedented, irreplaceable, rapidly depleting asset around which an entire deployment strategy had been organized by a woman who was smarter than almost anyone alive and who had, in the moment she learned he was running down, reached for the chart instead of reaching for him.

His hands were on his knees. His knuckles were white.

He loosened them. Deliberately. Finger by finger, the way you released a grip on a tool you'd been holding too long. The ache in his hands was real, the ache of a clench he hadn't noticed holding.

He breathed. The air was cold and tasted of stone and distance and the faint mineral trace of old channeling in the retaining wall below. The city spread out beneath him: the terraces, the rooftops, the districts he'd reinforced and the districts he hadn't, the infrastructure that ran on magic that was running out and on people who were running down and on systems that had been built for a world that no longer existed.

He sat with it. Not with anger. Not with grief, exactly, though grief was there, folded into the tight space behind his ribs. With something flatter: the feeling of a door closing. Not slamming. The click of a latch. The silence after.

Both things. Always both things. The woman who sat with him in the transit hub and the woman who picked up the pencil. He couldn't separate them. He wasn't sure she could either.

The maintenance crew finished their work on the retaining wall. They packed their tools. One of them, the youngest, a woman about his age, wiped her face with the back of her hand. She looked tired. Spent. The kind of spent that meant she'd used most of her pool and still had the rest of a shift ahead. She worked the way Maren worked, precise, careful, stretching what she had because what she had was all there was. She shouldered her tool bag and followed her crew down the terrace steps.

He thought of Haran's question.

*What do you want to build?*

He didn't have an answer. Not yet. But the question was there, solid as a workbench, and the deployment chart was there too, and the pencil was there, and the pump was there, and Ryn's hand on his arm was there—warm, brief, gone—and all of it sat in the same space inside him, crowded and irreconcilable and true.

He stood.

The bench was cold where he'd been sitting. The sun was higher now, mid-morning, the city in full motion, the terraces busy with the traffic of people living their lives. He put his hands in his pockets and turned downhill.

He didn't know where he was going.
