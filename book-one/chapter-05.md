# Chapter 5

The basement of the Hall of Infrastructure smelled like lamp oil and old stone and something sharper underneath—the metallic residue of channeling done in a closed space, years of it soaked into the walls.

Ash followed the examiner down a corridor he'd walked a dozen times in three weeks of working for Dorenne. The supply rooms were down here, the archive, the maintenance closets where the building's channeling interfaces were serviced. He'd never noticed the door at the far end. It was unmarked, set flush with the wall, the kind of door that existed only for the people who already knew it was there.

The examiner—a man in his forties, thin-faced, carrying a clipboard—held it open without looking back. "Through here."

Three days since the apartment. Three days since his mother's blood on the rubble and the power pouring through his hands like something breaking open. His mother was healing—the bone was set, the healer came twice a day, the sound of channeled warmth moving through her body audible as a low hum through the wall of the temporary housing Dorenne had arranged. His father carried meals to her bedside. Maren came every morning and every evening, her schedule reorganized around the family's new geography with the silent efficiency she brought to everything.

This morning, his father had caught his arm at the door. "Be careful," he'd said—and then stood there, hand on Ash's sleeve, as if he wanted to say something else but couldn't find the shape of it. Ash had nodded. His father had let go.

He'd kept his hands in his pockets for three days.

The warmth hadn't gone away. It had settled—less violent than the first eruption, but present, a pressure behind his palms that responded to proximity the way a compass responded to north. He'd touched the wall once, reaching for the light switch, and the channeling interface had flared so bright it burned an afterimage into his vision and fused the switch into the stone. After that, he'd been careful. He'd been careful about everything, moving through the unfamiliar apartment with the deliberateness of a man carrying something explosive, which was—he was starting to understand—exactly what he was.

The room beyond the unmarked door was larger than the corridor suggested. Low ceiling, no windows, lit by channeling strips set into the walls at intervals that threw flat, even light across everything. Three long tables arranged in a U. Instruments on every surface—brass housings, glass dials, stone blocks of varying sizes mounted on wooden bases. A graduated scale painted on the far wall, the numbers running from one to ten, each number a different color that started at pale yellow and ended at deep violet.

Two other examiners sat at the tables. A woman with grey-streaked hair pulled back tight, writing something in a ledger without looking up. A younger man—late twenties, barely older than Ash—sorting through a case of what looked like polished stones, each one set in a wire cradle. Neither acknowledged his arrival.

The thin-faced examiner gestured to a chair in the center of the U. "Sit."

Ash sat. The chair was too small. Not dramatically—a few inches in the seat, a few in the legs—but enough that his knees came up higher than they should and his hands rested awkwardly on his thighs. A child's chair. Or close to it. Designed for someone younger, smaller, coming into their first awareness of channeling the way every child in Solathis did by age five or six.

He was twenty-three.

The thin-faced examiner consulted his clipboard. "Full name?"

"Ashlyn Torren."

"Date of birth?"

He gave it. The examiner noted it without reaction.

"Prior channeling history?"

"None."

A pause. The examiner's pen hovered. The woman at the far table glanced up for the first time—a quick, assessing look that took in Ash's frame, his age, his hands resting on his knees in the too-small chair. She went back to her ledger.

"None," the examiner repeated. "No childhood emergence, no adolescent development, no—"

"Nothing. I was—" Ash searched for the word they'd use, the clinical term. "Dead weight. Until three days ago."

The examiner wrote something. His expression didn't change. He might have processed hundreds of these assessments—maybe thousands—and the bureaucratic machinery of his day might have already absorbed this anomaly and filed it alongside every other form and measurement and graduated stone in this basement room. Or he was simply good at his job.

"We'll start with calibration," he said.

* * *

The calibration stone was the size of a fist, pale grey, mounted on a brass stand at the center of the nearest table. The younger examiner positioned it in front of Ash and stepped back.

"Place your palm flat on the surface," he said. "Channel into it. Gently."

Ash looked at the stone. It was smooth, lightly veined, the kind of mineral he'd seen in the walls of the upper districts—channeling-reactive, designed to absorb and express energy as visible change. The graduated scale on the wall behind it made the purpose clear: push energy in, watch the color shift, read the number. One through ten. Yellow to violet.

He placed his palm on the stone.

*Gently.*

He didn't know what gently meant. He had no frame of reference for small. The reservoir inside him was a weight, a presence, an ocean pressing against the inside of his skin, and he didn't know where the valve was or how to crack it open just a fraction. He couldn't find the edges. Couldn't feel the difference between a pool and a flood.

He pushed.

The stone turned yellow. Then orange. Then red. The colors moved through the lattice like dye spreading in water—fast, too fast, the progression that should have taken seconds compressing into a single heartbeat. The mineral structure sang with a high, thin sound that Ash felt in his teeth.

The stone turned violet.

Then white.

Then it cracked.

The fracture ran from the point of contact—his palm—outward in three clean lines that reached the base of the stone and split it into pieces. The pieces fell away from each other on the brass stand with a sound like breaking crockery. The last one toppled off the table and hit the floor.

The room was very quiet.

Ash pulled his hand back. His palm was warm. Not burned—warm, the way skin felt after holding a cup of tea. The air around him had thickened, heated by the energy that hadn't made it into the stone—the waste, the overflow, the vast majority of what he'd pushed that had bled off as ambient heat instead of reaching its target.

The thin-faced examiner looked at the broken stone. He looked at the younger examiner. He looked at his clipboard.

"Right," he said.

The woman at the far table had stopped writing.

* * *

The second test involved a metal ring the size of a dinner plate, suspended from a wire frame by four thin chains. The younger examiner positioned it at arm's length from Ash and explained: channel a field into the ring. Hold it stable. The instruments on the table—a set of glass dials connected to the ring's base by copper wire—would measure the field's intensity and consistency.

"Thirty seconds," the younger examiner said. "Just hold it steady."

Ash extended his hand toward the ring. He tried again to find a small amount—to crack the valve, to let out a thread instead of a river. The pressure behind his ribs shifted. He pushed.

The ring lit up.

Not gradually. The metal went from dull iron to incandescent white in less than a second, the channeled field wrapping it in a visible aura that made the examiner step back. The glass dials on the table swung to maximum—all of them, simultaneously, the needles slamming against the right side of their housings with a series of sharp clicks.

The needles stayed there.

Ash held the field. He could feel it—a connection between his hand and the metal, a circuit of energy flowing outward from the reservoir and into the ring and bleeding away at the edges as heat, tremendous heat. The air between his palm and the ring shimmered. The chains holding the ring glowed dull red where the field touched them.

And underneath the strain—underneath the effort of trying to hold something he couldn't shape—there was something else. A sensation he had no name for. The power moving through him, out of him, into the world. A circuit completed. A current that had been dammed for twenty-three years finally running, and the running itself—the sheer physical fact of energy leaving his body and doing something, anything, even badly. A door thrown open in a room where all the windows had been painted shut.

"You can stop," the thin-faced examiner said.

Ash pulled back. The ring dimmed. The dials dropped—not to zero, but to a faint reading at the bottom of their scales, as if the ring had absorbed some of the energy and was releasing it slowly. The chains cooled. The air cleared.

The room was noticeably warmer than it had been a minute ago.

The woman at the far table closed her ledger. She stood, crossed to the thin-faced examiner, and spoke to him in a voice too low for Ash to hear. He nodded. She left the room.

The thin-faced examiner made a note on his clipboard. The younger examiner was staring at the glass dials with an expression Ash couldn't read—not fear, exactly, but the blankness of someone whose assumptions about the day had been quietly demolished.

"We'll continue," the thin-faced examiner said. He said it the way he might have said *we'll take a break* or *we'll move to the next form*—with the practiced neutrality of a bureaucrat whose voice had been trained out of surprise.

But his pen had stopped moving.

* * *

The next test was a targeting exercise. A stone disc mounted on the wall, divided into concentric rings—channel energy into the center ring only, without spilling into the outer rings. Precision, not power.

Ash hit the center ring. He also hit every other ring. And the wall around the disc. And the floor beneath it. The stone disc cracked along its mounting bolts and fell in two pieces, trailing dust.

"Sorry," Ash said.

The thin-faced examiner made a note.

The next test was thermal regulation. A basin of water. Raise the temperature by exactly five degrees. Ash touched the basin and the water went from cold to boiling in two seconds. Steam erupted toward the ceiling. The younger examiner covered the basin with a metal lid and held it there while the water roiled underneath.

"Sorry," Ash said again. His face was hot. Not from the steam.

The next test involved a set of weights on a balanced beam—channel force into one side to tip the beam precisely to a marked angle. Ash tipped the beam so hard the weights on the opposite side launched off the surface and embedded in the ceiling.

He stared at the ceiling. One of the weights was still vibrating in the stone, half-buried, like a nail driven by a hammer.

"I can pay for that," he said.

The thin-faced examiner looked at his clipboard. He'd filled two pages. His pen rested on a third, and Ash could see the numbers from where he sat—columns of readings, each one followed by a notation he couldn't decipher, each one larger than the last.

"The calibration stone," the examiner said, speaking to the younger man. "Do we have another?"

"Not that size. We have the teaching set."

"Bring it."

The younger examiner opened a cabinet and produced a wooden tray holding six small stones, each the size of a walnut, arranged in order of sensitivity. Practice stones. The ones they gave to children who were just learning to channel—soft, forgiving, designed to respond to the faintest touch.

He set the first one in front of Ash.

"As gently as you can," the younger examiner said. "You're flooding everything. Try to find a thread."

Ash placed one finger on the stone. He tried to imagine the smallest possible push—not a stream, not a pool, just a whisper. The barest breath of energy, the minimum his body could release.

The stone turned violet and shattered.

He worked through all six. Each one broke. The last one—the most sensitive in the set, designed to register the channeling of a four-year-old—held for almost a full second before a hairline crack appeared at its center and it split cleanly in two.

The room was warm enough now that the thin-faced examiner had removed his jacket. The younger examiner's forehead was sheened with sweat. The waste heat from Ash's channeling—the enormous inefficiency of a man who had no training, no control, no framework for modulating the force that moved through him—had raised the temperature of the sealed basement room by several degrees. The stone walls themselves held the heat, absorbing what his body radiated with each failed attempt at gentleness.

"I'm sorry about the—" Ash gestured at the debris. Broken stones, a cracked disc, a dented basin, a weight embedded in the ceiling. The room looked like something had happened in it, which was accurate but insufficient.

"It's fine," the thin-faced examiner said. He'd given up writing. His pen rested on the clipboard and his hands rested on the pen and his expression had settled into something that wasn't boredom anymore but wasn't panic either. The institutional middle ground of a man encountering something his training had not prepared him for and deciding, professionally, to wait for someone whose training had.

The door opened.

The grey-haired woman came back. Behind her, moving with the deliberate pace of someone who had been interrupted from something she considered more important, was an older woman—late sixties, perhaps seventy. She was tall and slightly stooped, wearing a long coat with deep pockets. Her eyes were dark and sharp.

She stopped inside the doorway. Her gaze moved across the room—the broken stones, the cracked disc, the basin lid still in place, the weight in the ceiling—and something shifted behind her expression. Not surprise. Recalculation.

"This is the—" the grey-haired examiner began.

"I can see." The older woman crossed to Ash's chair and looked down at him. "Stand up, please."

He stood. He was taller than her by half a head.

"Your name."

"Ash."

"How old are you, Ash?"

"Twenty-three."

"And three days ago, you had no channeling capacity at all."

"None."

She studied him the way Haran studied a mechanism—not the surface, but the structure underneath. Then she reached into her coat pocket and produced a stone. Not like the calibration stones on the table. This one was dark, almost black, the size of an egg, polished to a sheen that caught the channeling light and held it. She set it on the table.

"Touch this," she said. "Don't push. Don't try to channel. Just touch it."

He touched it.

The stone pulsed. Once. A deep, slow wave of light that started at the point of contact and moved through the mineral in a circle, reaching the far side and returning. The color was not on the graduated scale—not yellow, not orange, not violet. It was something else. A deep, luminous blue that seemed to come from inside the stone rather than its surface, as if the light were being drawn up from a depth the stone shouldn't have possessed.

The older woman watched the pulse. She counted something under her breath. Then she placed her own hand on the stone, beside his—not touching his skin, but close enough that he could feel the faint warmth of her channeling, controlled and precise, a thread of energy so fine it made his own efforts seem like dumping a bucket.

She held her hand there for a long time. The stone pulsed again. And again. Each pulse the same deep blue, each one rising from the same impossible depth.

She removed her hand. She removed his, gently, lifting his fingers from the stone's surface with a touch that was clinical but not unkind.

"How do you feel?" she asked.

"Fine." He was fine. The pulsing, the channeling—he'd barely felt it leave him. The reservoir behind his ribs was unchanged, the pressure no different from when he'd sat down. As if everything he'd done in this room—the broken stones, the overloaded instruments, the weight in the ceiling—had cost him nothing.

Nothing at all.

The older woman's eyes moved from the stone in her hand to the thin-faced examiner's clipboard, then across the debris.

"I'll need the room," she said.

The three examiners left. The thin-faced man collected his clipboard. The younger one glanced back from the doorway, once, with an expression that Ash would later recognize as the face of a person who had just realized they had a story they'd be telling for the rest of their career.

The door closed.

The older woman sat on the edge of the table, one hand still resting on the dark stone. She was quiet for what felt like a long time. The room's heat was fading—slowly, the stone walls releasing the waste energy of Ash's channeling in a gradual exhale that brought the temperature back toward normal.

"My name is Vasra," she said. "I study channeling theory." She turned the dark stone in her fingers. "I've spent forty years measuring what people carry. The instruments in this room are calibrated for the full known range—from the weakest adolescent pool to the strongest elder reservoir on record."

"I broke the instruments."

"You exceeded them. There's a difference, though the result looks the same." She set the stone down. "The stone I used—the dark one—is not a calibration tool. It's a resonance reader. It measures depth, not intensity. How far down the energy goes."

He waited.

"Every pool I've ever measured has a floor. A bottom. You push into the stone and the light moves outward—a circle, like a ripple—and then it stops. The distance it travels before it stops tells me the depth of the reservoir. Elders, the strongest I've measured: the light goes about this far." She held her hands apart. The distance between them was perhaps the width of a dinner plate. "You. The light didn't stop."

The room was quiet.

"It pulsed," she said. "It reached the edge of the stone and came back and pulsed again. Over and over. Because the stone isn't big enough." She paused. "I've never seen that before. The stone has always been big enough."

Ash looked at the dark stone on the table. Small. Polished. An egg-sized piece of mineral that had measured every channeler Vasra had encountered in forty years of study and had run out of room for him.

"What does that mean?" he asked.

"It means your reservoir is not a pool." She chose the word carefully, turning it in her mouth the way she'd turned the stone in her fingers. "It's something else. A deep source. A wellspring—something that feeds from below, from a depth I don't have instruments to measure."

*Wellspring.*

The word landed in the room and stayed there. It had weight. It had shape. It felt, in some way he couldn't articulate, like a name—not for the thing itself but for the change it had made, the way a word like *flood* was a name not for water but for what water did when there was too much of it.

Vasra watched him. Her eyes were careful, attentive, recording.

"Can you tell me how much?" he asked. "How much I—how much there is?"

"Not precisely. The calibration instruments were designed for a range that ends well below what you demonstrated today. The resonance stone suggests a reservoir that is... orders of magnitude larger than any pool on record. By a significant factor."

"How significant?"

"I could give you an estimate that would be wrong in informative ways, or I could tell you the truth, which is that I don't have a number."

He sat with that. The chair was still too small. He shifted his weight and the wood creaked under him—the same creak as his stool in Haran's workshop, his body in a seat designed for someone else.

"Is it..." He stopped. The question formed and reformed in his mouth, each version inadequate. "Does it refill? Like a pool? Will it—is there more, in the morning?"

Vasra's expression didn't change. But something behind it did—a stillness, a careful arrangement of features that he recognized from Dorenne's office. The face of a person deciding how much truth was appropriate.

"The honest answer," she said, "is that we don't know."

He waited.

"Every pool replenishes during sleep. The connection establishes at birth, determines your daily capacity. That's how it works for everyone."

"But?"

"But yours didn't establish at birth. It was dormant for twenty-three years and activated under extreme stress. The mechanism that created it—or unsealed it—is not the same mechanism that governs a normal pool. Whether it follows the same rules..." She spread her hands. The gesture was small and precise and contained the entire scope of her uncertainty. "We don't know."

"What do you think?"

"I think the working assumption should be that it's a fixed reservoir. Like everyone else's, but vastly larger. The energy is there. Whether it replenishes, and at what rate, and from what source—those are questions I'll need time to answer. If I can answer them at all."

*Vastly larger.*

The words settled into him. They joined the pressure behind his ribs, the weight of the thing he carried, the ocean that pressed against the inside of his skin. Vastly larger. Orders of magnitude. A reservoir so deep that the instruments couldn't find the bottom and the stones couldn't hold the light.

The other words—*fixed*, *working assumption*, *if I can answer them*—were there too. He heard them. They registered. But they registered the way a buoy registers in an ocean: present, visible, too small to change the shape of the water.

The ocean didn't need a bottom.

Vasra was watching him. He wondered what she saw—what her forty years of measuring people told her about the expression on his face, the set of his shoulders, the way his hands rested on his knees with a stillness that felt, for the first time in three days, like something other than restraint.

"I have more questions," she said. "Tests I'd like to run, over the coming days. Different instruments, better controlled conditions. With your permission."

"Of course."

"And I'd like to understand the activation event itself. What happened, what you felt, the sequence. In your own words, when you're ready."

"I can tell you now."

"Not today." She stood from the table. Her knees made a sound that she ignored with the practiced disregard of a woman who had been ignoring her knees for a decade. "You've been channeling for three days. You've been awake since—"

"Early."

"You've been awake since early. Go home. Rest. Come back tomorrow and we'll begin properly."

She crossed to the door, then stopped. She looked back at him—still in the too-small chair, his hands on his knees, the broken stones and cracked instruments scattered around him like evidence.

"Ash."

"Yes?"

"The word I used—*wellspring*. I chose it because it's accurate. A deep source that feeds from below." She paused. The pause held something careful—not hesitation, but precision. "But springs, in nature, are not infinite. They draw from aquifers. Aquifers draw from rainfall. There is always a source, and the source is always finite." Her eyes held his.

She left. The door closed. The room was quiet—the flat, dense quiet of a basement sealed in stone, the kind of silence that had mass.

Ash sat in the too-small chair. The debris of the morning's tests surrounded him—broken stones, a cracked targeting disc, a basin of water that had gone from cold to boiling to lukewarm, a weight still lodged in the ceiling. Evidence of power applied without control, force without direction, an ocean pouring through a channel the width of a straw.

He looked at his hands. The same hands. Calloused, scarred, the left thumb bearing the white line from a chisel that had slipped in Haran's workshop six years ago. Three days ago these hands couldn't activate a heating lattice. Couldn't warm a basin of water. Couldn't do the thing that every four-year-old in Solathis learned as their first act of participation in the world.

Now they broke calibration stones by touching them.

He turned his palms up. The skin was warm—faintly, evenly, as if the reservoir's heat bled through him at rest, a background radiation that he was only now learning to notice. The pressure behind his ribs pulsed with his heartbeat. Steady. Patient. Vast.

*Wellspring.*

He closed his hands. Opened them. Closed them again. Testing the grip, the way he'd tested a new tool in Haran's workshop—the weight of it, the balance, the feel of something unfamiliar becoming familiar through repetition. His hands. His power. His.

The thin-faced examiner knocked and entered. He carried a folded document—the summary, sealed with the Hall's stamp.

"For Director Kharren," he said.

"Thank you."

The examiner hesitated. He was looking at Ash with an expression that had migrated from bureaucratic neutrality to something less professional and more human.

"You can exit through the corridor," he said. "The ramp is to your left."

He left. Ash stood, the folded document in his hand, and followed.

* * *

The corridor was cooler than the testing room. He walked it slowly, letting the stone walls draw the residual heat from his skin. The sealed document sat in his hand—light, a few pages, the weight of ink and paper and institutional assessment. Inside it, numbers he didn't understand described something nobody had seen before. A Wellspring. His Wellspring.

He passed the archive room where he'd pulled his building's maintenance records three weeks ago. The supply closet where he'd found cleaning rags on his second day. The junction where the corridor branched—left to the ramp, right to the lower storage levels he'd never had reason to visit.

He knew this building. He'd worked here. He'd walked these corridors as Dorenne's aide, carrying briefs and deployment schedules, learning the language of systems and allocation and institutional power. He'd been good at it—good enough that Dorenne had put him at the table, good enough that senior staff had changed plans based on his analysis.

That had been enough. That had been more than enough. A desk, a role, a place in the machine. Proof that he belonged.

He took the ramp up. Forty flights, the same ramp he'd climbed every morning for three weeks—the maintenance corridor along the outer wall, wide enough for equipment carts, the lattice strips glowing in their recesses. His legs drove him upward and his breathing was easy and the document in his hand was proof that he was more than a desk. More than a role. More than a man who climbed forty flights because he couldn't use the lift.

He could use the lift now. The thought arrived simply, without ceremony. He could channel. The transit lift that he'd walked past every morning—the smooth disc in the wall, the interface that every person in the building touched casually, the thing so basic it wasn't even called channeling—was available to him now. He could touch it and it would work.

He kept climbing. The ramp was familiar. His legs knew the rhythm and his body knew the effort and the effort felt like something he wanted to keep—the physical reminder of the person he'd been, the twenty-three years of stairs and cold water and hands that solved problems by touching them. His palms smelled like stone dust and ozone now, not the oil and sawdust of Haran's workshop. He hadn't been back to the workshop since the activation. Hadn't thought about it until just now, and the not-thinking felt like something he should pay attention to but didn't.

He kept climbing. The pressure behind his ribs pulsed in time with his steps. Steady. Patient.

Vast.

The ramp opened onto the fifth floor. Dorenne's floor. The light through the tall windows was afternoon light now—the testing had taken most of the morning, though it hadn't felt like it. The corridor was quiet. Most of the staff were at site visits or buried in their afternoon work.

Dorenne was in her office. She was always in her office. He knocked.

"Come."

He entered and placed the sealed document on her desk.

Her eyes went to the seal, then to him. Her hands rested on the desk the way they always rested—precise, composed, the controlled stillness of a woman who measured everything and showed very little.

"How was it?" she asked.

He thought about the broken stones. The cracked disc. The weight in the ceiling. The six practice stones meant for children, shattered one by one under the lightest touch he could manage. The room growing warmer with each failure. The older woman with the dark stone and the careful words and the forty years of measuring things that fit inside her instruments.

"I broke everything," he said.

Something moved in Dorenne's face. Not a smile—something preceding a smile, the groundwork for one, the way a foundation precedes a building.

"Tell me," she said.

So he told her. The calibration stone cracking. The field test pegging at maximum. The targeting disc, the basin, the weight in the ceiling. He told her about Vasra and the dark stone and the light that didn't stop and the word—*Wellspring*—that the scholar had pulled from somewhere and placed on him like a label on a file.

He told her about the efficiency. The waste heat. Almost all of the energy bleeding uselessly into the air. He told her this because it felt like a flaw, a thing to fix, and Dorenne understood fixing things.

He did not mention the question. *Does it refill?* He did not mention the careful non-answer, the honest uncertainty.

Dorenne listened. She listened the way she listened to briefings—with the focused, organized attention of a mind that was sorting information into categories as it arrived. Resources. Constraints. Opportunities. Risk.

"Vasra wants to run more tests," Ash said. "Starting tomorrow."

"I know. She sent word ahead." Dorenne picked up the sealed document but didn't open it. She held it the way she held everything—with the weight of assessment, the invisible ledger of usefulness. "The examiners will file their report with the Council records office by end of day. Vasra's report will come separately, through my office."

"Through your office?"

"I asked her to route it that way. The Council records office is..." She chose her word. "Permeable. I'd prefer the details of your assessment to reach the Council through proper channels, at the appropriate time, with the appropriate context."

Ash nodded. He understood. Or he thought he understood—the language of management, the grammar of institutional control. Dorenne was protecting him. Protecting the information. Making sure the story was told correctly before it could be told any other way.

"The temporary housing," he said. "My parents—"

"Settled. Your mother's healing is progressing well. The bone is set. The healer I sent is elder-generation, experienced. She'll walk again within the week." Dorenne paused. "Your father has been asking about the building assessment."

"The old building?"

"He wants to know if it can be saved."

Ash thought about the fifth floor. The blown-out wall. The ceiling open to the sky. The stove buried under stone.

"Can it?"

"Structurally, the damage is limited to your apartment and the adjacent units. The foundation reinforcement the team completed is holding. The building itself is sound." She set the document down. "But the political calculation has changed. A building that was a routine maintenance case is now the site where a Wellspring activated. The Council will want—" She stopped herself. Recalculated. "We'll discuss it later. You should rest."

"Vasra said the same thing."

"Vasra is right." She stood and walked him toward the door, which was unusual—Dorenne dismissed people from behind her desk, a habit of authority so ingrained it was practically architectural. In the narrow corridor outside her office, the light from the tall windows fell across them both, and she stopped.

Something moved behind her eyes. Not the calculation—something older, something that predated the strategy and the management and the institutional reflexes. Something that looked, for a moment, like a woman standing in front of a problem she genuinely didn't know how to solve.

"You did well today, Ash," she said.

He didn't know what *well* meant, in this context. He'd broken everything they put in front of him. He'd exceeded every measurement and controlled nothing.

But Dorenne said *well*, and he suspected the meaning she carried was not the same as the one he heard. What she probably meant: the Wellspring is confirmed. The power is real. The numbers are beyond anything on record. The situation is manageable.

What he heard: you did well.

"Thank you," he said. "For arranging this. For—all of it."

"Go home, Ash."

He went. Down the corridor, past the offices where the staff he'd worked beside for three weeks were filing their afternoon reports. Past Tessaly's desk—she looked up as he passed, her expression unreadable, the same sharp assessment she'd given him on his first morning. Past the ramp entrance, where the smooth disc of the transit lift waited in the wall.

He took the ramp down. Forty flights. His legs carried him and the pressure behind his ribs carried something else—a new weight, a new name, a word that had been coined in a basement room by a woman with forty years of measurements and a stone that ran out of room.

*Wellspring.*

The late afternoon light met him at the building's entrance. Solathis spread before him—the terraces descending toward the gorge, the cracked paving of the lower districts catching the low sun. Somewhere below, in the temporary housing Dorenne had arranged, his mother was healing and his father was asking about a building and his sister was doing whatever Maren did with the things she couldn't say.

And somewhere inside him—behind his ribs, beneath his skin, deeper than any stone could measure—the ocean sat. Patient. Full. Unmoved by a morning of broken instruments and careful questions and the honest uncertainty of a woman who didn't know if it would last.

He held it the way his mother held the old kettle she'd packed from the apartment—without looking at it, without explaining, without admitting that the holding was a kind of prayer.

He walked into the city. His hands at his sides. His stride longer than it had been three days ago, though he didn't notice. The pressure behind his ribs beating like a second heart—steady, vast, and his.
