# Chapter 8

The workshop smelled the same. Oil and sawdust and something sharper underneath, the mineral bite of cut metal, the warmth that accumulated in a low-ceilinged space full of people who worked with their hands. Three weeks since Ash had last been here and the smell hit him at the threshold like something returning.

His blood was still running fast. Three hours at the press event, a hall full of officials and careful questions and people looking at him with that particular attention, and the walk through the crowd afterward, and the reservoir pressing against the inside of his hands the whole way here. He'd wanted to use it. Not for anything important. Just to feel the edges of the ocean.

He stood in the doorway a moment. Inside, the workshop spread its usual productive chaos: benches covered in components, racks of tools along the south wall, the big lathe in the back corner that Haran kept threatening to replace and never did. Two of the tinkerers were at their stations: Petra bent over something at the near bench, her hair tucked back, a loupe against one eye; Davel at the window, running a file along the edge of a gear blank with the patient, rhythmic attention of a man who understood that hurrying this particular motion accomplished nothing. The low windows let in the afternoon light, which lay across the benches in warm yellow bars and illuminated a slow drift of sawdust that spun in the current from the door Ash had opened.

Haran was at the central bench.

He had his back half-turned, sleeves rolled above the elbow, both hands on a section of copper pipe and a half-assembled pump laid out across the work surface in careful disassembly. Not chaos. Arrangement. Each component laid where it would next be needed, an order that only looked random because Ash wasn't the one working.

"Close the door," Haran said. He hadn't turned around.

Ash closed the door.

"Press event run long?" Haran said.

"Three hours."

"Hm."

Ash crossed to the central bench. Haran glanced up at him with the brief, assessing look he gave everything that arrived in his workspace: measuring, sorting, noting.

The warmth of the workshop settled around him, and the buzzing behind his ribs, the press event, the walk through the crowd, the hour of people looking at him, began, almost reluctantly, to settle.

He looked at the pump. It was midway through being built: the housing was there, the basic cylinder and chamber, the inlet and outlet flanges waiting for the fittings that Haran had laid out in sequence. Mechanical, through and through. Springs and seals and precisely machined tolerances that would move water by pressure differential and the simple physics of a well-fitted piston. No channeling interface. No stone discs set flush into the surface. Just the mechanism.

"The pump in the Velden building failed," Haran said. "Third time in a year. The district system's been throttled since winter, and the residents have been hauling water from the terrace taps." He set a fitting down with the care of a man placing a piece in a puzzle he intended to solve. "Thought I'd make them something that works without needing anyone to top up the channeling every six weeks."

*He'll finish it in three weeks. Maybe four.*

The reservoir hummed. Full as a tide, patient and enormous, and the light in the workshop was good, and his hands were right here.

He looked at the outlet flange, a brass sleeve, threaded to receive the elbow joint, waiting to be seated against the pump housing. The gap between the sleeve and the housing was visible: a fraction too wide, the threading not quite engaged, the joint stuck at the top of its travel with just enough resistance to prevent it seating cleanly.

He wanted to help. Wanted his hands to be useful in this space where they'd always been useful, to prove the power could do what his hands had always done, only faster.

He reached for it. A thread of the Wellspring moved through his fingertip, finding the stuck fitting the way a finger finds a tight jar lid. He pushed. The fitting gave. And gave. And fused; the brass deformed slightly under the channeled pressure, the threads pressing together hard enough that the friction between the metal faces exceeded what the original design had required by a considerable margin.

Done in a second. Faster than his hands could have managed.

He took his hand back. The fitting sat perfectly flush against the housing. Solid. Clean.

Haran looked at it.

Ash looked at it.

Neither of them said anything.

* * *

The next task that presented itself was the outlet pipe, a copper section, about an arm's length, that needed to come off a straight run and angle downward at roughly thirty degrees toward the floor fitting. Haran had a bending jig for this, a simple form clamped to the edge of the bench, and copper bent with a small amount of heat and patient pressure.

Ash didn't reach for the jig.

He held the pipe in both hands and ran a current of force through it, not heat, just pressure, pushing from outside, shaping. The copper moved. It bent cleanly. Thirty degrees, maybe a little more. He lifted his hands and examined the result: the angle was right, the radius was uniform.

He was already setting it down when he noticed that the copper had stiffened, not much, but measurably. The channeled force had done something to the grain structure of the metal, and the pipe was now marginally more rigid than it had been. It wouldn't flex if someone needed to work around it later. It wouldn't ease if the fitting on one end turned out to need adjustment. The copper that had been soft enough to reshape was now copper that was done.

He set it on the bench beside the pump housing.

Davel glanced over from the window. Not a long look. Not unfriendly. The glance someone casts when they're noting a fact for later.

Haran had moved to the far end of the pump, where the inlet fitting was, a different style of joint, designed to be removable, a quarter-turn bayonet mount that allowed the inlet side to be detached for cleaning. Standard in this type of pump. You built them for maintenance. You built them for the person who would service the pump in three years' time, when Haran was somewhere else and the Velden building's residents needed someone to clear a blockage.

There was a seal at the base of the bayonet mount. It sat slightly proud of its channel; Ash could see it from where he stood, the rubber edge not quite settled, the gap where the seal should have been pressing even against both faces. He reached out. Ran a thread of channeling into the joint. Fused the gap. Filled it. The seal compressed into its channel and the gap disappeared.

He stepped back.

Haran, from the far end of the bench, looked at the joint. Then at Ash. Then back at the joint. His jaw shifted, something passing across his face and going.

He didn't say anything. He turned back to the inlet fitting he was working on.

Petra had straightened from her loupe. She looked at the bench, at the fused fitting, the rigid pipe, the outlet flange Ash had channeled into place, with the careful assessment of a woman who understood mechanism and had just watched someone alter several of them. Then she picked up her own work again and went back to it.

The workshop held its sounds: the file, the lathe in its idle clatter, the small creak of the building. The warmth hadn't changed. The afternoon light still lay across the benches in its yellow bars.

Ash looked at the pump. He had helped, genuinely, with three parts of it in the last five minutes. The outlet flange was seated. The pipe was bent. The seal was compressed.

His hands were still. The workshop was quiet around them.

* * *

The valve seat was the next piece that needed attention.

Haran brought it to the central bench himself: a small brass disc, precisely machined, with a tapered face that would mate against the interior of the pump body to regulate flow. It was a calibration task: the valve seat had to be worked gradually into its socket, the taper on the face mated to the taper in the housing until the surfaces matched closely enough to seat cleanly. You used lapping compound. You rotated the valve in the housing, pressed lightly, lifted it and examined the contact pattern on the brass face, and repeated, adding compound, adjusting the angle, working until the contact was even and the seal was right.

Ash had done this before. His hands knew the motion, the pressure, the judgment of how much was enough and how much was too much. In his life before the activation, he had lapped valve seats in this workshop with Haran watching and occasionally adjusting his grip. He was good at it. Better than most of the younger tinkerers, because he had learned early that hurrying this particular operation was worse than not doing it. A bad valve seat was a pump that leaked, and a pump that leaked was worse than no pump at all.

He picked up the valve. He spread a thin film of compound on the face. He placed the valve in the housing.

And then he pushed.

Not much. He hadn't intended to push at all—he'd intended to rotate the valve the way he'd always rotated it, with the even, gentle, exploratory pressure of his fingertips. But the reservoir was there, behind everything, pressing gently against the inside of his hands the way water pressed against a dike, and when his fingers touched the valve and the surface resistance of the housing came back through his grip, the power leaned into it.

He felt the valve shift under more force than he'd meant to apply. He pulled back. Tried again. Lighter this time, a conscious, deliberate effort to keep his hands ahead of the reservoir, to work purely in the mechanical register, fingers only, no channeling. He rotated the valve one careful quarter-turn.

The resistance changed. Not wrong, just different; he'd used a fraction more force than the compound could compensate for, and the contact pattern on the brass face was going to show it. He lifted the valve and looked.

Uneven. The contact ran heavy on one side and light on the other, the compound's grey residue telling the story clearly: he'd pushed harder on the near edge without meaning to, and the taper had mated incorrectly, and now the valve needed to be worked back.

He applied more compound. Tried again. His hands were trying to be precise and the reservoir was trying to be helpful and they were fighting each other, and what reached the valve seat was something between the two: not his hands' precision, not the Wellspring's force, but a confused negotiation that changed direction three times in a single rotation. He heard the faint sound of brass against brass under more pressure than he'd intended.

He stopped. Lifted the valve. Looked.

The taper on one edge of the valve face was different from the taper on the other. Not dramatically, a matter of fractions, but measurably, the brass deformed in a single spot where the channeling had gone where his hands hadn't meant it to go.

*I nearly ruined it.*

He set the valve on the bench. Set down the compound. Stepped back from the housing.

The workshop had gone quiet. Not completely (Davel still moved the file, Petra still worked) but the quality of the silence had changed, the way silence changed in a room where people had noticed something and were choosing not to comment on it. Ash stood with his hands at his sides and looked at the valve with the flat assessment of someone taking an honest inventory.

He had done it faster. He had done most of it worse.

* * *

Haran came and stood beside him.

He looked at the valve, picked it up, examined the face, turned it in the light to read the contact pattern and the edge where the taper had been altered. He said nothing for a moment. Just looked at it the way he looked at everything: with attention, with patience, without hurry.

"Your hands still work," Haran said.

He set the valve down. He looked at Ash, not with the expression of a man about to deliver a lesson, but with the level, interested expression of a man observing something that confirmed what he already suspected.

"The power doesn't replace them. It's another tool." He picked up the valve again and the tin of lapping compound from the shelf. "You don't use a hammer on glass."

He sat on the stool at the central bench and took the valve in his left hand. He spread a fresh thin film of compound on the face, just enough, the movement practiced, no hesitation in the amount. He seated the valve in the housing.

Ash watched his hands.

They moved the way hands moved after decades of the same motion. Not mechanical. Deliberate. Haran's right hand applied a light, circular rotation, and Ash could see from where he stood that the pressure was even across the face, not from any special effort to make it even, but because Haran had done this long enough that evenness was the default. His hands knew how much was enough. They'd learned it across ten thousand valve seats and remembered it without being told.

Three rotations. Four. He lifted the valve and looked at the contact pattern. Nodded once. Applied more compound. Seated it again.

The workshop's sounds continued. Davel's file. The lathe's idle clatter. Haran's hands, moving in the same patient circle. Ash stood beside the bench and watched and felt the afternoon light across his back and the warmth of the workshop against his arms and the reservoir, humming—patient, enormous, and for the moment, completely useless.

Haran completed the calibration in about eight minutes. The valve seat, when he lifted it for the last time, showed a contact pattern that was even across the full face of the taper: grey compound spread uniformly, no heavy spots, no gaps, the brass telling the story of a surface that had met its mate correctly. He set the valve on the bench with a small, conclusive sound.

"How did you do it?" Ash said. "The pressure. I couldn't keep the channeling from—"

"I wasn't channeling."

Ash looked at him.

"For this?" Haran said. "Not useful." He turned the valve in his hands. "I know how to do this with my hands. So I do it with my hands. The pool stays where it is."

Ash was quiet for a moment. Then: "I thought—" He stopped. Started again. "I assumed you were using it. For the detail work. For the feel."

"The feel is in my hands." Haran set the valve down. "Has been since before you were born."

* * *

They worked for a while after that.

Haran at the pump, which was still less than half-assembled, which would take weeks in the ordinary course, nights and weekends and early mornings when the workshop was quiet and the light was good. Ash beside him, doing with his hands what his hands still remembered how to do: fetching components from the rack, holding a fitting steady while Haran torqued the threading, passing tools without being asked because he'd spent enough time at this bench to know which tool came next.

He did not channel. He kept the reservoir where it was and worked with his hands, and his hands were good, they'd always been good, practically useful in the way that years of working this way had made him. He could feel the difference now between his hands' knowledge and the reservoir's reach. They were not the same thing.

After a while, Haran said: "Try it again. The small stuff. But smaller."

Ash looked at him.

"The force applications. There's a technique: micro-pulses. Tiny inputs. Surgical." He picked up a small brass test piece, set it on the bench between them. "Heat the center. Not the whole thing. Just—" he tapped a finger against the brass, at its exact center "—there."

Ash placed a finger against the test piece. He thought: *small*. He thought: *less*. He pushed.

The whole disc heated. Not dramatically (it would cool in a minute) but uniformly, the heat spreading from his touch to the edges the way heat always spread, indifferent to his intention.

He pulled his hand back.

"Again," Haran said. No impatience in it.

Again. The reservoir, when Ash reached for it, came in volumes. Even the smallest reach was still an ocean pouring through a crack in a dam—not the surgical thread Haran was describing, but something that spread and filled and exceeded the container.

"I can't—" Ash started.

"You can." Haran picked up the test piece, now cooled, and set it in his own palm. He extended his hand toward Ash. "Watch."

A small warmth bloomed in the exact center of the brass. A circle, barely larger than a thumbnail, a few degrees of difference. Ash could see it in the way the light caught the surface, the faint difference in reflection where the metal had accepted heat. Then it cooled. A second later, another brief, precise bloom, slightly off-center. Then another.

The movement of Haran's channeling was invisible. No glow, no effort showing in his face or hands. Just the warm spot appearing and vanishing, appearing and vanishing, a series of controlled, surgical inputs that asked only what they needed from the day's pool and received exactly that.

Ash watched.

After a moment, Haran turned the test piece over and set it on the bench.

"The valve work," he said. "When you calibrated it by hand, before. What were you feeling for?"

Ash thought. "Back-pressure. The taper seating. There's a specific resistance when it's right—you can feel the whole load through the stem."

"What else?"

"Temperature. The brass warms slightly when the seat makes full contact—friction from the lapping. You feel for the point where the warmth stops increasing."

Haran's hand rested on the bench. "And your hands felt that."

"Yes."

"So your hands already know what precision feels like." He set the test piece between them. "The center only."

Ash tried again. Reached for the smallest thread he could find, the thinnest imaginable connection between the Wellspring and his fingertip. Placed his finger on the disc. Pushed.

The heat came in less than before, not the full bloom, not the edge-to-edge spread, but still more than the thumbnail-circle Haran had produced. Three or four times that. Ash held his finger there and tried to narrow it further, to concentrate the flow, and felt the power resist concentration the way a river resisted being narrowed by hands: not refusing, just indifferent to the demand, flowing at whatever volume it found natural.

He pulled his hand back.

"Better," Haran said. Which was true, technically. Still not useful.

"Much better," Davel said from the window, without looking up from the gear blank he was filing. Ash couldn't tell if he was being generous or dry. Probably both.

* * *

The afternoon lengthened. Petra finished her component and left, raising a hand to Ash as she went, easy and natural, the gesture of a colleague. Davel moved from the window bench to the lathe and began setting up a cut. The workshop's sounds changed register: the lathe's higher voice, the chip tray catching metal, the smell of cutting fluid added to the existing workshop smell.

Ash worked with his hands. He held components. He measured. He fetched from the rack when Haran needed something and put back what Haran was done with. He did not channel. The pump progressed slowly, as the pump would always progress slowly—there was no rushing the calibrations, no skipping the steps, and Haran didn't attempt to rush them. This was not urgency. This was work.

After a while, Ash found himself watching Haran. Not the pump, but the man. The way he moved around the bench. The small, unremarkable gestures that populated the space between tasks: the hand that reached out and touched the workshop wall as he passed, the heat that bloomed briefly under his palm and then settled, not heating the whole wall, just taking the edge off the stone's cold where he'd been standing. A lamp in the corner had dimmed as the afternoon shifted; Haran passed it on his way to the storage rack and brushed the base with two fingers. The wick brightened.

And then the fitting that Ash had fused at the outlet flange—Haran's hand passed near it as he reached for a tool, and Ash saw the old man's fingers pause a fraction of an inch from the brass, not touching, just—registering. Reading.

The small gestures accumulated.

*He's not being careful with his pool. He's been doing this all day.*

Ash had been watching Haran for years. He had assumed, without examining the assumption, the way you didn't examine the furniture in a room you'd lived in since childhood, that the warmth Haran kept in the workshop was modest because modest was all he had. That the lamp trick was a small habit. That the casual applications were what he could afford, scattered across a working day to make it comfortable.

He turned and looked at the workshop. Really looked—at the even warmth that had nothing to do with the small stove in the corner, which hadn't been lit in hours. At the steady lamp Haran had brightened in passing. At the way the air moved, slightly, as if the workshop were breathing in some comfortable rhythm that had nothing to do with the door Ash had come through.

None of it was large. All of it was constant.

He thought about the test piece—the thumbnail of heat in its exact center, the micro-pulse that appeared and vanished and appeared again with the unhurried rhythm of breathing. He thought about Haran's hand on the valve seat, the eight minutes and the even contact pattern and *for this? Not useful.* He thought about the lamp and the wall and the fitting and the decades between them.

*He's not doing a little. He's doing everything at the smallest possible scale.*

The warmth in the workshop wasn't Haran's leftover pool. It was Haran's technique, accumulated over a working day in a hundred tiny applications that cost almost nothing individually and added up to a room that felt like a room where someone had spent a lifetime making things work.

He had a strong pool; Ash had known this, filed it away, never followed the implication. What he hadn't understood was what it meant to have a strong pool and decades of training and the discipline to never flood. To run at a fraction of a fraction, always. Not because there wasn't more. Because more wasn't what the task needed.

He looked at Haran.

Haran was fitting a spring into the pump's pressure chamber. His hands were unhurried, the spring seated carefully, testing the compression with the pad of his thumb. He looked up and found Ash's eyes and held them for a moment.

The old man's face did something, small, almost nothing, the shift of a man recognizing that something he'd kept quiet for a long time had been seen. Not quite a smile. The quality that a smile might be made from. His eyes held warmth and distance in the same moment, the expression of someone who had been waiting, without urgency, to be correctly understood.

He looked back down at the spring. His thumb tested the compression again. He nodded once—to himself, or to the spring, or to the afternoon—and picked up the retaining clip that would hold it in place.

Ash turned back to the bench. His hands found the component Haran would need next, the outlet valve body, waiting on the rack. He fetched it. He set it where Haran could reach it.

They worked.

* * *

The light through the windows had gone orange by the time Ash straightened and pressed his hands to the small of his back. The pump was further along, not by much, not dramatically, the work of an afternoon measured in components seated and calibrations completed and the slow, honest progress of things done correctly. Still less than half built. Still weeks from finished.

Davel had gone. The lathe was quiet. The chip tray needed emptying but it would wait until tomorrow, and Haran was cleaning tools at the end of the bench with the ease of a man closing down a day he was satisfied with.

"I'll come back," Ash said. "Help you finish it."

Haran looked at him. Not a long look. "Your hands would be useful."

Ash began pulling on his coat. He looked at the pump, the half-built housing, the fittings laid in sequence, the work that remained. He looked at the outlet flange he'd channeled into place, permanent and solid and exactly what the design had needed it to be, and the pipe he'd bent with force, rigid now in a way it hadn't been designed to be, and the seal he'd fused in the joint that was supposed to come apart.

Done. Real. Each of it useful in the moment.

"How's the building's water system until you finish?" he asked.

"Residents are managing. Two taps from the terrace, bucket brigades, the neighbors sharing." He didn't make it sound like heroism. Just logistics. "They've been doing it for six weeks. A few more won't break them."

Ash thought about offering to accelerate the work. About the hours this pump would take versus what he could do in an afternoon if he stopped being careful—if he shaped the housing and sealed the joints and set the valves with the Wellspring instead of with hands and compound and a bending jig. The pump would be done tomorrow. Running by the day after.

He didn't say it. He put his arms through the coat.

Haran was threading a new fitting as he spoke, the die turning in steady quarter-rotations, his hands working while his words came between the turns.

"How much did that cost you?" he said. "The fittings. The pipe."

Ash thought about it honestly. He had not paid attention, which was itself information. "Not much. Essentially nothing."

Haran nodded. The die turned. "Good," he said. "Remember what the answer is." Another quarter-turn, the curl of metal coming away clean. "You might want to check it again later."

Ash waited. There was more.

"You're learning what you can do," Haran said. The threading handle turned, steady, unhurried. "Good. Don't forget what you don't need to do."

Ash thought about the valve seat. About the two attempts and what they'd cost the brass. About the eight minutes of Haran's hands and the even contact pattern and the truth that Haran's hands hadn't needed any help and hadn't wanted any.

He thought about the outlet flange, solid and permanent, and the bayonet mount that could no longer be detached for cleaning, and the pipe that would never flex to accommodate a fitting that needed adjustment. He thought about the Velden building's residents and their bucket brigades, and the pump that would run without anyone topping up the channeling, and the person in three years' time who needed to clear a blockage on the inlet side.

Not a catastrophe. Still useful work, still genuine progress. He hadn't broken anything, exactly.

He had just closed some doors.

"See you next week," he said.

"Door's always open," Haran said. The threading handle turned.

Ash pulled on his gloves and went out into the early evening. The street outside the workshop was in shadow, the building's upper floors blocking the sun, which had dropped behind the district's skyline, but the sky above was still that deep autumn blue that came in the last hour before dark. The district smelled like cooking and coal smoke and the mineral undertone of close-packed stone buildings.

He walked. The flagstone that always tipped was three steps from the door, and he stepped over it without thinking. The reservoir hummed, vast and patient. Essentially nothing, he had told Haran. The fused joint. The rigid pipe. The sealed bayonet mount. Essentially nothing, and the Wellspring hadn't noticed.

*Remember what the answer is.*

He thought about the lamp in the workshop corner—the warm, even light that Haran had brightened in passing, two fingers on the base, nothing more. He thought about the thumbnail of heat in the center of the test piece, precise and costing what it cost and no more. He thought about eight minutes and a valve seat and the contact pattern that told the truth about pressure and patience and a lifetime of working with your hands.

He kept walking. The street opened into a broader way, the terrace where the workshop sat giving way to the cross-route that ran toward the Third Terrace lifts, and the crowd thickened: people heading home, a produce cart blocking the corner, a knot of children playing some game in the angle of a building. He moved through them, and a few people glanced at him with that particular attention he'd been learning to expect—not yet familiar, still startling each time.

His hand found the gear casing in his coat pocket, cracked along the hub, scrap, worthless. He'd dropped it in without thinking, a piece from the practice pile. His thumb traced the crack.

The evening settled around him. Above the workshop two districts behind him, the lamp burned on, even and warm, tended by hands that knew exactly what they were doing.
