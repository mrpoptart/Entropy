# Chapter 19

The candle on the desk was guttering when Ash sat down.

He'd brought it from the supply room at the end of the hall—a plain tallow candle, the kind the command post kept in stacks for the crews who worked the lower districts where the light channeling had failed. It smelled like rendered fat and gave off a yellowish light that made the stone walls look closer than they were. He could have warmed the room with a thought, lit it with a pulse, made the quarters feel like somewhere a person lived. He didn't. The cold felt appropriate. The dim light felt honest.

His cot was pushed against the far wall, the blanket folded at its foot with the precision of someone who had learned tidiness as a form of apology—a habit from the years when the least he could do was keep his space small and neat. The desk was a slab of channeled stone, built into the wall like everything in the command post, designed for someone who could warm the surface with a touch. He'd set Dorenne's papers on it: the deployment lists, the crew chief reports, the attachment he'd pulled from the operations room chart that morning.

The attachment. A single sheet of heavy paper, clipped neatly, covered in Dorenne's staff's careful handwriting. Weekly expenditure estimates. Cumulative totals. The sum at the bottom, circled once in dark ink.

He smoothed the paper flat with both hands and looked at the number.

Then he pulled a blank sheet from the desk's single drawer—standard supply paper, the kind he'd used for years in Dorenne's office, back when his job was filing reports about infrastructure inspections he couldn't fix. He'd carried a pencil in his pocket since he was fourteen. The habit of a boy who wrote things down because he couldn't channel them into memory the way everyone else did. He pulled it out now and set it beside the paper.

He began.

* * *

The early days first.

He closed his eyes and reached for the memories—not the reservoir, not the warmth in his chest, but the other thing. The recollection of what it had been like when the power was new.

The activation itself he couldn't calculate. That had been chaos—the building collapsing, the force pouring out of him without direction or control, the heat that had blistered paint off a wall six meters away. Whatever that cost, it was lost in the emergency. Call it unknown. Set it aside.

But the days after. The testing period, when Dorenne's examiners had brought him to the assessment hall and asked him to do things—heat water, move stone, channel force against a calibrated resistance plate. He remembered the resistance plate. A slab of granite mounted in a wooden frame, wired to instruments that measured displacement and thermal bleed. They'd asked him to push against it, and he'd pushed, and the granite had cracked and the frame had splintered and the instruments had pegged and broken.

He'd laughed. The examiner—a thin woman with an elder's pool and a master's precision—had not laughed. She'd looked at her broken instruments and then at him and then at the cracked granite and said, very quietly, "We're going to need a bigger plate."

The waste from that session. He tried to remember. The room had been warm when they started—normal warm, the ambient temperature of a channeled building in early autumn. By the time they finished, the examiners had opened the windows and the stone floor was hot enough to feel through boot leather. All of that heat was waste. Energy that left his reservoir, passed through his body, missed the target, and bled into the air and stone and walls. Running hot, as channelers said. He'd been running hot for hours.

He wrote the first number on the blank paper. An estimate—rough, conservative. The assessment session: three hours of continuous channeling at untrained efficiency. At that level, maybe one unit in five reached the target. The rest was waste heat. If the useful output had been—he thought about the cracked granite, the broken instruments, the way the examiners had revised their estimates upward three times—maybe 200,000 kJ of useful work, generously. At twenty percent efficiency, that meant he'd spent a million. One million kJ, gone in an afternoon of testing.

He stared at the number. It looked small on the page. It was more than an elder channeler produced in three months.

The next days. The training period—two weeks of guided practice under the examiners' supervision, learning the fundamentals that every child learned by age seven. How to direct the flow. How to control the output. How to push energy into a specific target rather than flooding everything around it. He'd improved fast—his mind was good at this, at optimizing, at finding the efficient path through a system. But fast was relative. A child learning to channel wasted a few hundred kJ over the course of a year. Ash, learning with a Wellspring, wasted more in an hour than most people spent in a month.

He calculated the training period line by line. Day one: terrible efficiency, flooding everything, the practice room so warm the examiners took shifts. Maybe 300,000 kJ spent for 60,000 kJ of useful work. Day two: slightly better. Day three: better still. By the end of the first week, he was channeling at something like basic competence—forty percent of his output reaching the target, sixty percent lost as heat. By the end of the second week, approaching competent. Sixty percent. Still wasting almost half of everything he spent.

He wrote the numbers. Added them. The training period alone: roughly 4,000,000 kJ. Four million. The examiners' daily pool, combined, was maybe 25,000 kJ. He'd burned through the equivalent of 160 elder-days learning what children learned while playing.

The pencil point was wearing flat. He turned it, found a sharper edge, kept writing.

* * *

The first deployments.

Dorenne had sent him out within days of the training period ending. The need was real—the city's infrastructure was failing faster than the maintenance crews could patch it, and here was a resource that could do in minutes what took teams of channelers days. She'd started him on water systems. Junction repairs, pipe reinforcements, the mechanical work of keeping a city's circulation moving.

He remembered the first deployment. A water junction in the Thornwall district, where three pipes converged at a channeled stone manifold that had been weakening for years. The maintenance crew had been nursing it with daily patches—a few hundred kJ of reinforcement from two middle-generation channelers who spent their morning pools just to keep the junction from splitting. He'd walked in, knelt, pressed his palms to the stone, and reinforced the entire manifold in ten minutes. The crew chief had stared. One of the channelers—a woman in her thirties, tired-eyed, her hands chapped from the daily contact with cold stone—had turned away and left without speaking.

He hadn't understood that, at the time. The turning away. He understood it better now.

The junction repair: maybe 50,000 kJ of useful output, at his competent-level efficiency of sixty percent. Call it 83,000 kJ spent. Significant—but the reservoir was so large that the spending hadn't registered. Like dropping a coin into a lake and listening for the splash. You knew, intellectually, that the water level changed. But you couldn't hear it. You couldn't feel it. The lake was too big and the coin was too small and the distance between your hand and the surface was too far.

He listed the deployments. Week by week, site by site, drawing from memory and from Dorenne's records spread on the desk beside him. The water junctions. The retaining walls. The transit shaft reinforcements. The heating grid restorations in the lower districts, where the elderly shivered through autumn mornings because the building lattices hadn't been recharged in months and nobody had the pool to spare.

He'd done good work. He knew that. Every deployment was a real problem solved, a real improvement in real people's lives. Water that flowed. Buildings that stood. Warmth in rooms that had gone cold. He'd walked through districts where people recognized him and called his name and thanked him, and the gratitude had been genuine, and he'd felt it in his body like sunlight—the lightness, the expansion, the strange disorientation of being worth something for the first time in his life.

He wrote the numbers and the gratitude didn't change them.

The first deployment phase—three weeks of district work, averaging three sites per day, each one a withdrawal from the reservoir. His efficiency improved across the period. He'd started at roughly sixty percent and ended somewhere near seventy—approaching skilled. The improvement meant each deployment cost less, but there were more deployments as Dorenne expanded the schedule. More sites. More districts. The work growing to fill the capacity, the way work always did.

He calculated the total for the first deployment phase. The number came out to roughly 6,000,000 kJ. Six million. He'd been channeling for less than six weeks at that point, and he'd already spent six million kilojoules.

He set the pencil down and pressed his fingertips against his eyes.

The candle flame wavered. A draft from somewhere—the window seam, probably, the old channeling around the frame starting to thin. He could feel the cool air on the back of his neck, and he let it sit there.

Six million kilojoules in six weeks of routine deployments. Not emergencies. Not heroics. Just the daily work of keeping a city standing, directed through one person because one person was easier to deploy than twenty.

He picked up the pencil.

* * *

The earthquake.

He didn't want to calculate this. The memory of those days lived in his body differently than the deployment memories—not as a sequence of tasks but as a single sustained effort, continuous and overwhelming, the kind of exertion that blurred individual moments into a wash of sensation. Heat and stone and the deep vibration of a city trying to shake itself apart. His palms pressed to the ground. The reservoir pouring through him like a river through a broken dam.

But the numbers didn't care what he wanted. The numbers were the point.

He spread Dorenne's crew chief reports across the desk. They'd tracked him during the earthquake response—not precisely, because nothing was precise during those days, but thoroughly. Duration of channeling at each site. Observable outputs. Thermal readings from the surrounding area, which gave a rough measure of waste heat. The reports were meticulous in the way that Dorenne's operation was always meticulous: not because precision mattered in the moment, but because Dorenne knew it would matter later.

She'd been counting. All this time, she'd been counting.

Day one of the earthquake. The initial response—stabilizing buildings in the upper districts, where the oldest and most channeling-dependent structures had cracked first. He'd moved from building to building with a crew of six, pressing his hands to foundations and walls and load-bearing columns, channeling reinforcement into stone that was shaking itself to dust. Six hours of continuous work. The crew chief's report noted the duration and the number of structures stabilized: fourteen buildings, three retaining walls, a bridge support that had cracked along its full span.

He tried to estimate the cost. Each building stabilization during active seismic stress—not routine reinforcement but counter-vibration work, damping the shaking at the point of contact while simultaneously reinforcing the structure's integrity. The output cost for a single building: maybe 500,000 kJ minimum. More for the larger structures. The retaining walls: 1,000,000 to 2,000,000 each. The bridge: somewhere in between.

At his efficiency during the earthquake—which had been worse than his training baseline, because precision collapsed when you were channeling at maximum output for hours, because fear and exhaustion degraded control the way they degraded everything—maybe fifty percent. Maybe less. He'd been flooding. He knew that. The reports noted the thermal bleed: ambient temperatures around his work sites had risen by fifteen degrees. The stone he knelt on blistered his knees through his trousers.

Day one alone: he wrote the number and it was larger than the entire first deployment phase. Eight to ten million kJ, spent in six hours. The reservoir had given it without complaint—had poured and poured, and he'd felt nothing but the work, the effort, the necessity. He hadn't known to feel anything else. You didn't check the level of the ocean while you were trying to keep people from drowning in it.

Day two. Worse. The aftershocks were stronger than the initial event—unusual, the geologists had said, but then everything about the earthquake was unusual, the weakened Kharren Fault reinforcements failing in ways the ancient channelers had never anticipated because the ancient channelers had never imagined a world where their work wouldn't be maintained. He'd moved to the lower districts. The dam. The retaining walls that held the hillside above four thousand homes. The transit shafts that carried water and waste and people through the city's bones.

The dam alone. He closed his eyes and felt the memory of it—his body pressed flat against the face of the dam wall, both palms spread, the reservoir emptying through him in a sustained torrent of force that sealed a fracture running the wall's full height. Hours. He didn't know how many. The crew chief's report said four. It had felt like days.

The dam: 5,000,000 kJ of output. Maybe more. At degraded efficiency—forty percent by then, he was sure of it, running so hot that the mist off the reservoir face was turning to steam where it touched the stone around him—that meant 12,000,000 kJ spent. Twelve million. From a single act of repair.

Day three. Day four. The aftershocks diminishing but the structural damage compounding—each tremor revealing new weaknesses in the old channeling, new fractures in foundations that had been sound for generations. He'd worked until he couldn't see straight. Until the crew chiefs physically pulled him away from a wall and made him eat and drink and rest for three hours before sending him back.

He calculated day by day. The numbers climbed. Each one a building held, a wall sealed, a fracture stopped. Each one a life or a hundred lives or a thousand, the arithmetic of heroism tallied in the same column as the arithmetic of loss.

When he added the earthquake response—all four days, all the deployments, all the damage stabilized and all the energy bled as heat into the stones of Solathis—the number sat on the paper and looked back at him with the blank face of a fact.

Roughly 20,000,000 kJ. Twenty million kilojoules. Forty percent of the Wellspring's total capacity, spent in four days of holding up a city.

His hand was shaking. He set the pencil down again and watched it shake—a fine tremor, not dramatic, the kind you might not notice if you weren't looking at your own hand on a desk in a dim room at whatever hour this was. He pressed his palm flat against the stone surface and held it there until the tremor stopped.

*Twenty million.*

He hadn't known. He hadn't checked. He hadn't wanted to know, because knowing would have changed what he did during those days, and what he did during those days was the right thing. Every building he held, every wall he sealed, every life behind those walls—he would do it again. He would do all of it again, every kJ, every hour of flooding heat and cracking stone. There was no version of this where he looked at the number and wished he'd spent less. The people were alive. The district stood. The dam held.

But the reservoir felt bottomless because he'd never bothered to check. And now he'd checked.

* * *

He pulled all the numbers together.

The assessment session. The training period. The first deployment phase. The earthquake. The post-earthquake reconstruction—three weeks of daily deployments, three to six sites per day, his efficiency improved to seventy or seventy-five percent but the workload doubled and then tripled as Dorenne expanded the schedule.

He wrote each total on a fresh sheet of paper, one line per phase, the numbers stacked in a column. The handwriting was precise—the particular neatness of a person who had been writing things down his whole life because he couldn't afford to lose track.

Assessment: ~1,000,000 kJ
Training: ~4,000,000 kJ
First deployments: ~6,000,000 kJ
Earthquake response: ~20,000,000 kJ
Post-earthquake reconstruction: ~4,000,000 kJ

He drew a line beneath the column and added.

The sum was 35,000,000 kJ.

He stared at it.

Checked the addition. Checked it again. Went back through each phase estimate, adjusting for optimism, rounding up where he'd rounded down, accounting for the small expenditures he hadn't tracked—the casual uses, the warming of rooms, the demonstrations for examiners and Council members, the moments when he'd channeled just because he could, just because it was there, just because for the first time in his life the world responded when he pushed.

The adjusted total: closer to 26,000,000. Twenty-six million, not thirty-five—his first pass had been too rough on the earthquake, overlapping some numbers. He recalculated the earthquake days more carefully, pulling apart the crew reports, separating the sites he'd double-counted. The dam, the retaining walls, the buildings—distinct events, distinct expenditures. He was more careful now. More precise.

The earthquake settled at roughly 15,000,000 kJ. Still enormous. Still forty percent of the work he could have done with a normal deployment schedule over a year. But fifteen, not twenty.

He recalculated the total.

Assessment: ~1,000,000 kJ
Training: ~4,000,000 kJ
First deployments: ~6,000,000 kJ
Earthquake: ~15,000,000 kJ
Reconstruction: ~4,000,000 kJ

Total: ~30,000,000 kJ

*No.*

He went back through the early numbers. The training period—had he really spent four million? He reconstructed it day by day. The first days were the worst: massive waste, untrained flooding, the practice room turned into an oven. But the later days were better. The curve was steep. He revised downward: 3,000,000, maybe. The first deployments—he cross-referenced with Dorenne's records, her staff's careful estimates. Their numbers were slightly lower than his for the routine work, slightly higher for the complex structural jobs. He split the difference. 5,500,000.

The reconstruction phase. Three weeks of steady work, improved efficiency, but more sites per day. Dorenne's cumulative total—the number circled at the bottom of the attachment—covered weeks one through six. He'd been working for three weeks since the earthquake. Her number was 3,800,000 for the reconstruction alone.

He trusted her number. She'd been counting when he hadn't.

New total:

Assessment: ~1,000,000 kJ
Training: ~3,000,000 kJ
First deployments: ~5,500,000 kJ
Earthquake: ~15,000,000 kJ
Reconstruction: ~3,800,000 kJ

Total: ~28,300,000 kJ

Twenty-eight million. He could feel the precision tightening, the range narrowing. Some of these numbers could be off by ten or twenty percent, but the order of magnitude was right. He wasn't wrong by half. He wasn't wrong by a third. The total was somewhere between 25,000,000 and 32,000,000 kJ.

Call it what it was.

He'd burned through roughly half the Wellspring in eight weeks.

The pencil sat on the paper. The candle guttered again—lower now, the tallow pooling, the flame leaning sideways in the draft. He should replace it. He didn't move.

*Half.*

Half of everything he would ever have. Not half of this year's supply. Not half of a renewable budget that would refill while he slept. Half of the total. The Wellspring didn't recharge. It wasn't a pool—it was a deposit, laid down in some geological process he didn't understand, cracked open by accident, draining through him into the stones and walls and water systems of a city that needed more than he could give. Every kJ that left the reservoir was gone. Every deployment, every repair, every moment of heroism was a withdrawal from an account that would never see a deposit again.

And the early waste. He looked at the numbers and his jaw tightened, the muscles along the side of his face locking down the way they did when something hurt that he wasn't allowed to show.

The training period. Three million kJ—of which less than a third reached any useful target. Two million kJ of waste heat, dumped into practice room walls and stone floors and the air of assessment halls, accomplishing nothing. Gone. He'd spent the equivalent of two hundred elder-days of useful work just learning not to be terrible. The cracked granite. The broken instruments. The examiners opening windows to vent the heat of his incompetence.

And before that, the assessment session itself. A million kJ spent proving something that could have been proved with a tenth as much, if anyone had known how to test a Wellspring, if anyone had ever needed to.

Four million kJ of waste. Four million, out of fifty million total. Eight percent of everything he would ever have, gone in the first week because he was a novice with an ocean and nobody had taught him how to use a cup.

*If I'd been trained first. If someone had—*

But who? Who would have trained him? No one had ever been a Wellspring before. The examiners had tested him with elder-grade instruments because they didn't have anything larger. The training program was improvised from first principles by people who'd never seen a reservoir that didn't refill every night. There was no curriculum for this. There was no precedent.

He could see it now—the bitter geometry of it. The waste wasn't anyone's fault. The waste was built into the situation, the inevitable cost of being the first and only person to hold this particular burden. Someone had to pay the learning tax. Someone always did. He'd paid it in kJ instead of coin, and the exchange rate was four million units of irreplaceable energy for the privilege of learning what a child learned for free.

He pushed the chair back from the desk and stood. Crossed to the window. The glass was cold under his fingertips, and beyond it, the city was dark—not fully dark, not the dark of a dead city, but the particular dim of a city at night where the light channeling was unevenly maintained. Some windows glowed. Some streets held faint warmth from the heating lattices in their paving stones. Some didn't. The gradient was visible from here, the way it was visible from every high point in Solathis: the upper districts bright and warm, the lower districts fading into the ambient dark, the terraces stepping down into dimness like a staircase into a basement.

He pressed his forehead against the glass and let the cold settle into his skin.

*Half. In eight weeks.*

* * *

He went back to the desk.

The second calculation was worse. Not because the numbers were larger—they weren't—but because the numbers had a direction.

He wrote it at the top of a fresh sheet: FORWARD.

Dorenne's deployment schedule stretched across months. He'd seen it on the chart—the columns marching forward, the hash marks waiting to be filled, the work expanding as the reconstruction moved from surface repairs to deep infrastructure. Retaining walls. Terrace anchoring. Transit shaft reinforcement. The bones of the city, the old channeling that the ancient builders had laid down centuries ago, now thinning and cracking and failing in ways that no living crew could repair. Except him.

He wrote the weekly expenditure rate. At his current efficiency—call it seventy-five percent, skilled level, improving but not yet approaching the mastery that took years of deliberate practice—and at Dorenne's current deployment rate of three to six sites per day, each one a withdrawal calibrated to the task:

Average weekly expenditure: approximately 1,200,000 kJ.

He stared at the number. Checked it against Dorenne's records for the most recent reconstruction weeks. Her staff's estimates put it at roughly 1,000,000 to 1,400,000 per week, depending on the complexity of the sites. His estimate sat in the middle. Call it 1,200,000.

The remaining reservoir: approximately 22,000,000 kJ. Maybe more, maybe less—the edges he could feel were imprecise, the shape of the reservoir a sensation rather than a measurement. But the sensation agreed with the math. Half was gone. Call what remained 22,000,000 and accept the uncertainty.

22,000,000 divided by 1,200,000 per week.

Eighteen weeks.

He wrote the number and the pencil pressed hard enough to indent the paper.

*Eighteen weeks.*

Not someday. Not eventually. Not in the abstract future where bad things lived because they were too far away to be real. Eighteen weeks was a season and a half. Eighteen weeks was the span between now and the end of winter. Eighteen weeks was a number of days he could count on his fingers if he used both hands twice.

And that was at current rates—the reconstruction rate, the three-to-six sites per day. Dorenne's schedule was expanding. The columns on her chart were getting denser. More sites, more work, the demand growing as the scope of the city's degradation became clearer. The earthquake hadn't caused the infrastructure crisis. It had revealed it. Every tremor had shaken loose decades of deferred maintenance, exposed foundations that had been quietly failing for years, cracked open the fiction that the city's bones were sound. The work wouldn't shrink. It would grow.

At an expanded rate—six to ten sites per day, the pace Dorenne's forward-planning columns implied—weekly expenditure rose to 1,800,000 or 2,000,000 kJ.

22,000,000 divided by 2,000,000.

Eleven weeks.

He felt it in his stomach—a dropping sensation, physical, as if the floor had shifted. Not the reservoir. Not the edges. His body, responding to a number the way bodies responded to a fall, with the sudden absence of ground.

Even at improved efficiency—say he reached master level, ninety percent, which would take years of practice he didn't have—the savings were meaningful but not transformative. Master efficiency meant spending roughly fifteen percent less for the same output. Eleven weeks became thirteen. Eighteen became twenty-one.

Twenty-one weeks. Five months. He could improve his technique from now until the reservoir emptied and the improvement would buy him a month.

The draft from the window was cold on his neck. The candle flame leaned. He watched his own hand, holding the pencil, steady now. The tremor from earlier was gone. What replaced it was something quieter—a stillness in his body that felt like the stillness of a room after a door has closed.

He wrote the projection on the paper, below the weekly rate and the division:

At current deployment rate: ~18 weeks
At projected expanded rate: ~11 weeks
With master-level efficiency (unlikely in timeframe): ~21 weeks
Range: 11 to 21 weeks.

He drew a box around the range. The box was small. It fit neatly on the page, a rectangle of pencil marks containing the span of his remaining usefulness.

*That's all there is.*

He sat with it. The reservoir was there—warm in his chest, present, familiar. He reached for it the way he always did, the unconscious check, the reassurance. It was still vast. Still more than anyone alive had ever held. Still enormous by any measure except the one on the paper in front of him.

But the paper was the measure that mattered. Not the feeling—the math.

* * *

He should have stopped there.

The calculation was done. The number was found. The box on the page held the answer, neat and final, and there was nothing left to add that would change the shape of it. He should have folded the papers, blown out the candle, lain down on the cot, and tried to sleep. That was the reasonable thing. The manageable thing.

But his mind was the mind it had always been—the mind that saw systems and followed them, that pulled threads and couldn't stop pulling until the whole weave was visible. The mind that Dorenne had hired for its ability to see patterns in infrastructure reports, to find the fracture line in a building before it cracked. The mind that Haran had trained, without either of them knowing it, in the long afternoons of the workshop where every mechanism was a system and every system had a logic and every logic could be followed to its conclusion.

The mind wouldn't stop.

He turned to a fresh sheet. Wrote at the top, in smaller letters, as if the smaller writing could make the thought less real:

THE WORLD.

The planetary reservoir. The source of all magic, declining across generations, measurable in the weakening pools of each successive generation born. He'd known this his whole life—everyone knew it, the way everyone knew that winter was cold and the mountains were tall. The decline was a fact of the world, debated in its particulars but undeniable in its trend. Elder pools at 10,000 kJ. Middle pools at 2,500. Young adult pools at 1,000. Adolescent pools at 400. The numbers falling like steps on a staircase, each generation lower than the last, and at the bottom of the stairs—

Nobody talked about what was at the bottom of the stairs.

He'd never thought about it in these terms before. The decline had always been someone else's problem—a political argument, a Council debate, a thing that concerned the infrastructure planners and the Preservationists and the people who wrote broadsheets about generational injustice. It hadn't concerned him because he'd had no pool, no stake, no place in the system. The decline was a river flowing past him on both banks while he stood on dry ground.

Now he was in the river. And the river was going somewhere.

He wrote the generational numbers. Not from any reference—he'd heard them a thousand times, in Council reports and dinner conversations and the matter-of-fact way people described their daily budgets. Everyone knew approximately what their generation's pool was. The numbers were as familiar as currency denominations.

Elder: ~10,000 kJ/day
Established: ~5,000 kJ/day
Middle: ~2,500 kJ/day
Young adult: ~1,000 kJ/day
Adolescent: ~400 kJ/day

The rate of decline between generations was roughly fifty percent. Each generation born with half the daily pool of the one before it. Not exactly—the data was noisy, the measurements imprecise, the boundaries between generations blurry. But the trend line cut through the noise like a blade.

Fifty percent per generation. A generation was roughly twenty-five years.

He traced the trajectory forward. The adolescents of today—the children growing up with 400 kJ, barely enough to warm their own hands and make a cup of tea—would have children of their own. Those children would be born with 200 kJ. Maybe less. Enough to light a candle. Not enough to heat a room.

And their children. 100 kJ. The flicker of a pool that could accomplish almost nothing—a warm handshake, a lit match, the faintest glow in the dark before it went out.

And after that—

He put the pencil down.

The world's reservoir was doing what his reservoir was doing. Draining. Not in weeks but in generations, not catastrophically but steadily, the long slow exhalation of a planet letting go of something it had held for eons. The staircase had a bottom. Everyone knew the staircase had a bottom. Nobody looked at the bottom because looking at the bottom meant accepting that the world they lived in—the world built on magic, shaped by magic, dependent on magic for every warmth and light and healing and connection—was a world with an expiration.

He was the staircase, compressed. His reservoir was the planetary reservoir in miniature—a finite deposit, drawn down by use, declining toward zero. He was going where the world was going. The only difference was speed.

He'd arrive first.

The thought should have been terrifying. It was—but underneath the terror was something else, something harder to name. Recognition. The sense of a pattern completing itself, a shape he should have seen weeks ago, months ago, the day the Wellspring cracked open inside him. He was the world in small. The world was him in slow. The same math, the same decline, the same trajectory from abundance to scarcity to—

He reached for the reservoir. Felt it. Warm. Present. Finite.

The way everyone's was finite. The way everyone's had always been.

* * *

And then—the thought he didn't want to have.

Maren.

It arrived the way the worst thoughts always did—not as a decision to think about something, but as a presence that was suddenly there, like a person standing in a doorway you hadn't noticed was open. He hadn't been thinking about her. He'd been thinking about generational decline, about the abstract mathematics of a world running down, about numbers and trends and the impersonal geometry of entropy.

But the numbers weren't impersonal. The numbers had a face.

Maren's pool was approximately 1,000 kJ per day. Young adult generation, on the weaker end—she'd never said so directly, but he knew. He'd watched her his whole life, the way you watched a sibling, noticing everything and nothing, the accumulation of observation so constant it felt like knowledge rather than attention. He'd seen her channel with a precision that made her pool look larger than it was. He'd seen her come home tired from shifts that an elder would have finished by lunch. He'd seen her, years ago, calculate whether she could heat the bath and still have enough left for her homework—the small daily arithmetic of a life lived inside constraints that he, ironically, had never faced. He'd had no pool at all. She'd had one just large enough to make every expenditure a choice.

And her pool was weakening.

He'd noticed it in passing, weeks ago—a conversation they hadn't quite had, a moment where he'd sensed the thinning. Her channeling was as disciplined as ever, her technique as clean, but the raw capacity behind it was less than it had been. Not dramatically. Not catastrophically. The way a river was less in late summer than in spring—still flowing, still functional, but lower. Slower. The rocks starting to show.

She was doing in years what he was doing in weeks.

He wrote it down. Not because he needed to—the numbers were simple, obvious, the kind of calculation that barely qualified as calculation. But he wrote it because writing made it real, and this needed to be real.

Maren's daily pool: ~1,000 kJ (declining)
Decline rate: unmeasured, but consistent with generational trend
At current decline: functional capacity reduces ~2-3% per year
Projected loss of functional viability: 15-20 years

He looked at the number and the pencil was still in his hand and his hand was still steady and the candle was still burning and the room was still cold and Maren was somewhere in the middle districts right now, covering a maintenance shift for a crew that was shorthanded, spending her small and precise and diminishing pool on work that used to take half the effort.

Fifteen to twenty years. That was her number. Not eighteen weeks—eighteen years, maybe fifteen, the time it would take for her pool to decline to the point where the skilled work she'd built her life around became impossible. The healing, or the infrastructure maintenance, or whatever she did with her days—the work that demanded a certain minimum output, below which the channeler simply couldn't perform. She'd reach that threshold. Every young channeler would reach it. The only question was when.

She'd been living with this math her whole life.

He stood up. Sat down. Stood up again. Walked to the window and back. The quarters were too small for pacing—four steps in any direction before a wall stopped him—so the movement was a kind of contained restlessness, a body that wanted to go somewhere and had nowhere to go.

She'd never had the luxury of thinking the well was infinite.

Every morning of her life, Maren had woken up with approximately 1,000 kJ in her pool. Every morning, she'd calculated—not consciously, not with paper and pencil, but with the bone-deep awareness of a person who'd grown up inside constraints. Can I heat the water and still have enough for work? Can I take the extra shift and still channel for dinner? Can I heal this, fix this, warm this, do this—and still have something left for tomorrow, and the day after, and the year after that?

He'd never done that calculation. Not when he was poolless—there was nothing to calculate—and not after the Wellspring, when the reservoir seemed infinite. He'd gone from nothing to everything without ever passing through the territory of *enough*, the territory where Maren had lived every day of her life.

And now she was watching that territory shrink.

He thought of her at the kitchen table, years ago—twelve, thirteen, the age when the pool settled into its adult range and you knew what you'd have to work with. He remembered her face the day the school assessment came back. She'd read the number and folded the paper and put it in her pocket and set the table for dinner. She hadn't mentioned it. Their mother had asked, and Maren had said "fine," and the conversation had moved on to Ash—to his lack of a pool, to the latest specialist their mother had found, to the ongoing project of understanding what was wrong with her son.

What had Maren's number been? He didn't know. She'd never told him. She'd folded the paper and put it away and become, over the following years, the most disciplined channeler he'd ever seen—getting more out of her pool than anyone, squeezing every kJ, making sufficiency look like abundance through sheer technique and will. She'd become exceptional because she couldn't afford not to be. Because the number on the folded paper in her pocket was lower than she wanted, and she'd decided, at twelve or thirteen, that she would make up the difference with skill.

The way Haran did. The way every careful channeler did. The difference between enough and not-enough bridged by discipline, practice, the refinement of control that turned waste into output. Maren had learned this. Haran had mastered it. And neither of them talked about it, because talking about it meant admitting that the bridge existed, and admitting the bridge existed meant admitting the gap beneath it.

Ash had been calculating his own decline as though it were unique. Sitting at this desk with his papers and his pencil, adding up his expenditures, projecting his timeline, drawing boxes around ranges—as if he were the only person in the world facing the end of a reservoir. As if the math were his alone.

It wasn't.

It was Maren's. It was the tired woman who'd turned away from him at the Thornwall water junction, whose daily pool couldn't accomplish in a year what he'd done in ten minutes. It was every young channeler picking up extra shifts, stretching their pools, learning to make less do more because more wasn't coming. It was the adolescents with 400 kJ, watching elders do casually what they'd never afford. It was the children who would be born with 200. With 100. With nothing.

It was the world's life, measured out in declining increments, the staircase descending one generation at a time into a dark that nobody had prepared for.

He sat down at the desk. The papers were spread in front of him—his numbers, his projections, his careful columns. The box drawn around his range: 11 to 21 weeks. His math. His decline. His countdown.

He looked at the numbers and they were still his numbers and they were still true and they still mattered. But they were smaller than he'd thought. Not in magnitude—in scope. He'd been treating the Wellspring as a unique phenomenon, a singular event, a story about one man and his extraordinary problem. And it was that. It was also the most common story in the world, told at a scale that made it obvious and a speed that made it undeniable.

He was Maren. Maren was the world. The world was him. The same math, the same finitude, the same long or short walk toward the same dark.

The candle had burned to a stub. The flame was low—a blue flicker barely clearing the rim of the holder, the last of the tallow pooling around the wick. He watched it. The flame leaned, straightened, leaned again. The draft from the window touched it and it shivered.

He didn't replace it. He sat in the dimming light and looked at his numbers and thought about his sister, out there in the city, channeling with her precise and careful hands, spending what she had because the work needed doing and she had always been the one who did the work. She'd never mentioned the cost. She'd never drawn a box around her range. She'd just lived inside it—quiet, disciplined, finite—and he'd never noticed because he'd been too busy counting his own coins.

The flame went out.

He sat in the dark for a long time. The reservoir was warm in his chest. He could feel its edges—not distant anymore, not the ghost of geometry he'd sensed weeks ago on the terrace overlook. Closer now. More defined. The shape of what remained, held inside a body that was otherwise unremarkable, otherwise ordinary, otherwise exactly like every other body in Solathis that woke each morning and spent what it had and hoped it was enough.

He pressed his palms together. The warmth of the reservoir met the warmth of his own skin and they were the same temperature, and he couldn't tell where one ended and the other began, and for the first time since the Wellspring cracked open inside him, that inability to find the boundary felt not like abundance but like loss.

The room was dark. The city was dark. The papers on the desk held numbers that would still be there in the morning.

He lay down on the cot without undressing and pulled the blanket over himself and closed his eyes and the dark behind his eyelids was the same dark as the room and the same dark as the city and the same dark, eventually, as sleep.
