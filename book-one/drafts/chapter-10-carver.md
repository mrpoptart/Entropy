# Chapter 10

Dorenne's office smelled like cold tea and old paper.

She was standing at the map when Ash arrived—a wide-format rendering of Solathis pinned to the wall behind her desk, districts outlined in different colors, annotations in her tight handwriting clustered around the eastern and southern edges. She didn't look up. She was tracing a line with her finger from the eastern heating grid to the southern water junction, and the line kept stopping where her finger stopped, as if the city's problems were a sentence she couldn't finish.

"Sit," she said.

He sat. The chair was the same one he'd used as her aide—sturdy, slightly too low, positioned so she could look down at whoever occupied it. He'd never noticed the angle before. Now he noticed everything about Dorenne's office: the calibrated distances, the way the light from the high window fell across her desk and left visitors in partial shadow. A room built for a person who managed things.

She turned from the map.

"The eastern district's heating grid failed four months ago," she said. "You know this."

He did. He'd filed the reports himself, back before the Wellspring—the maintenance requests, the crew dispatches that kept getting delayed, the follow-up letters from residents that accumulated in the eastern district's folder until the folder needed a second folder. Four months of cold in homes built to be warmed by channeling, where the walls were designed as thermal interfaces and without power they conducted cold as efficiently as they'd once conducted heat.

"Three crews have been assigned," Dorenne said. "Each one ran dry before completing a quarter of the grid. The lattice requires sustained output at a level our maintenance channelers can't provide—not anymore. The grid was built for elder-strength pools. The crews we have are middle-generation at best, and most of them are pulling double shifts covering other districts."

She pulled a sheet from her desk—figures, names, dates. The particular organization of information that was Dorenne at her most precise.

"Four hundred and twelve households. Roughly sixteen hundred people. They've been hauling water from the market district for bathing. Wearing layers indoors. The children are getting sick—not from anything specific, just from cold. The kind of slow, grinding illness that comes from four months of never being warm enough." She set the sheet down. "Two elderly residents have died. Not from cold directly. From the complications of cold—pneumonia, falls on icy interior floors. The kind of deaths that get listed as natural causes because nobody tracks what cold does to people who are already fragile."

Ash looked at the sheet. The names were listed in Dorenne's handwriting—neat, impersonal, the same hand that wrote budget projections and crew schedules. Two names circled at the bottom.

"The grid needs approximately three thousand kilojoules per day to maintain full function," Dorenne said. "That's the estimate from my engineers. The initial reactivation will cost significantly more—the lattice has degraded during the shutdown and will need to be recharged from zero. Call it ten thousand to bring it back online. Fifteen to be safe."

She was watching him. Not the way she watched her staff—checking for competence, for compliance. Something more careful. The way you watched a tool you were about to use for the first time at scale.

"That's a fraction of what you spent at the quarry last week," she said.

He looked up. She held his gaze.

"You threw boulders, Ash. You melted stone. You spent—I don't know what you spent. No one knows what you spent, because you don't track it and neither does anyone else. But whatever it was, it didn't do anything for anyone except entertain you and your friend."

The words were calm. Measured. Dorenne didn't raise her voice. She didn't need to.

"There are sixteen hundred people in the eastern district who have been cold for four months. You can fix that. Today. This afternoon." She picked up the sheet again, held it so he could see the names. "The question is whether you will."

It wasn't a question. They both knew it wasn't a question.

"When do we go?" he said.

Something crossed her face—brief, controlled. Not a smile. Satisfaction, maybe. Or relief. The expression of a person who had solved a logistics problem that had been sitting on her desk for months.

"Now," she said.

* * *

The eastern district clung to the lower terraces like an afterthought.

Ash had walked these streets before—as Dorenne's aide, carrying reports, taking notes on cracked foundations and failing systems. He'd catalogued the problems with the detached competence of someone who could identify decay but couldn't do anything about it. Now he walked the same streets and the catalogue was the same, but the feeling behind it had changed. He wasn't here to document. He was here to fix.

The streets were narrow. Buildings leaned into each other across alleys barely wide enough for two people abreast. The architecture had the particular look of lower-district construction—competent but not elegant, built in an era when strong channelers had power to spare but not the inclination to waste it on aesthetics for the poor. The walls were thick, the windows small, the heating lattices embedded deep in the stone where they could radiate warmth through the structure's mass. Good design. The kind of design that worked beautifully when someone fed it power and turned into a trap when no one did.

Dorenne walked ahead of him with two engineers—middle-aged women in the grey coats of the Infrastructure Bureau, both carrying assessment tools and the particular expression of people who had been fighting a losing battle for years and had stopped expecting reinforcements. One of them kept glancing back at Ash. The other didn't look at him at all.

The first junction box was set into a wall at the intersection of two residential blocks. A metal-faced panel with a channeling interface—a smooth stone disc the size of a palm, designed to accept energy and distribute it through the lattice network behind the walls. The disc was cold. Ash could feel it from a meter away—not the absence of warmth but the active presence of cold, the thermal mass of stone that had been draining heat from the surrounding air for months.

"This feeds blocks twelve through nineteen," said the engineer who'd been glancing at him. Verant, her name was. "Eight buildings. Hundred and twenty households."

"The lattice is intact?" Ash asked.

"Structurally, yes. The channels are clear—we checked last month. It's just empty. Like a pipe with no water."

He stepped up to the interface. Put his palm on the disc.

Cold. The stone bit into his skin. Behind it, through it, he could feel the lattice—a network of channels cut into the building's walls, running through floors and ceilings, connecting rooms and corridors in a vascular system designed to carry warmth the way veins carried blood. Empty veins. Cold walls conducting cold into homes where people slept in layers and children coughed through the night.

He pushed.

The reservoir responded the way it always did—instantly, enormously, with a force that had nothing to do with his intention. He wanted a stream. He got a river. Energy poured through his palm into the interface disc and the disc heated under his hand in seconds, the stone going from cold to warm to hot. He felt the lattice behind it drinking—pulling the energy inward, distributing it through the network, the channels filling like dry riverbeds in a sudden rain.

Too fast. The disc under his palm was scorching. He pulled back on the output—clamped down the way he'd practiced at the quarry, pressing the reservoir's surface flat—and the flow reduced. Not to a trickle. To a heavy pour. The disc glowed faintly at its edges, heat shimmering in the air above it.

Waste. He could feel it. The heat bleeding from his hand into the air, radiating from the disc in all directions, warming the street instead of the buildings. At the quarry, the waste hadn't mattered. Here it was energy that could have gone into walls, into homes, into the hands of children. He clamped down harder. The output narrowed.

The lattice was filling. He could feel it through the interface—the network coming alive section by section, channels warming in sequence as the energy propagated through the stone. A rumble in the walls, barely audible. The deep sound of stone expanding as it heated, the building's structure adjusting to a temperature it hadn't held in months.

Verant put her hand on the wall of the nearest building. Her eyes widened.

"It's warm," she said. She looked at her colleague. "The wall is warm."

Ash kept channeling. The lattice was greedy—four months of cold had left the stone depleted, and the network was absorbing everything he gave it, pulling the energy deeper into the structure, distributing it through floors and walls and ceilings. He could feel the propagation—room by room, the buildings warming from the interface outward, heat moving through stone the way color spread through water.

He held the output for five minutes. Ten. The waste heat was constant—his hand was hot, the air around the disc shimmered, and the pavement at his feet was warm enough that the frost in the cracks had melted. Inefficient. Wildly, extravagantly inefficient. A trained channeler would have done this with a fraction of the energy, the way Haran would have threaded a needle where Ash was using a hammer.

He didn't care. The buildings were warming. He could feel it through the lattice—room after room, wall after wall, the cold retreating as the energy pushed through. Sixteen hundred people were about to be warm.

He stepped back. The interface disc glowed faintly, residual heat dissipating into the lattice. The air around it was tropical—waste energy radiating in all directions, a pocket of warmth in the cold street.

Verant was staring at him. Her colleague had put both hands on the wall now and was pressing her cheek against the stone, eyes closed, the way you pressed your face to a warm pillow.

"How much did that cost you?" Verant asked.

*Essentially nothing.*

He heard Haran's voice saying it. The workshop. The pipe fitting. The question that was always the same question.

"I'm fine," he said. "Where's the next junction?"

* * *

The eastern district had fourteen junction boxes.

He fed them all. One by one, working through the residential blocks in a circuit that Dorenne's engineers had mapped years ago and never had the resources to run. Each junction was the same—cold disc, empty lattice, the greedy pull of depleted stone—and each one responded the same way. He pushed power through the interface and the buildings drank it and the walls went warm.

By the fourth junction, people were coming out.

Not crowds. Not immediately. A door opened in block fifteen and a woman stood in the frame, one hand on the wall, looking at the stone as if it had spoken to her. She pressed her palm flat. Held it there. Her head tilted.

She looked down the street at Ash. At Dorenne standing beside him with her engineers. At the faint shimmer of waste heat hanging in the air.

She went back inside. A minute later, a child appeared in the doorway—small, maybe six, wearing three layers of clothing that were too big for her body. The child put her hand on the wall the way her mother had. Then she pressed her whole body against it, arms spread, face turned sideways, the way a cat lay in a patch of sunlight.

Ash moved to the next junction.

By the seventh, the doors were opening before he arrived. Word moved through the district faster than he did—passed from building to building, from window to hallway to street. People were gathering at the intersections, watching him work. Not cheering. Not yet. Something more careful—the wary attention of people who had been let down by the system too many times to celebrate a repair they hadn't confirmed yet.

He fed junction eight. The lattice sang—a resonance he hadn't noticed before, a harmonic in the stone that emerged when the network was more than half-charged. The buildings hummed.

A man stepped out of a doorway in block twenty-two. Old—elder generation, but the careful posture of someone whose body had started to negotiate with gravity. He walked to the nearest wall and stood with his hand on it for a long time. He didn't look at Ash. He looked at his hand on the warm stone.

Then he went back inside. When Ash passed the window a few minutes later, the man was sitting in a chair by the wall with his eyes closed, his face tilted toward the warmth the way you tilted toward the sun in winter.

Junction eleven. Junction twelve. The circuit was taking longer now—not because the work was harder, but because people kept stopping him. Not to thank him, not exactly. They stopped him to tell him things. Which floor had been coldest. Which family had a new baby. Which building had lost its heating first and had been waiting longest. They told him these things the way you told a doctor your symptoms—precisely, urgently, with the compressed emotion of people who had been enduring something and had finally found someone who might understand why it mattered.

He listened. Dorenne waited.

Junction thirteen. Fourteen.

He stepped back from the last interface and flexed his hands. The waste heat was considerable—the street was ten degrees warmer than it should have been, a pocket of artificial summer in the district's cold. His shirt was damp with sweat, though the sweating had nothing to do with effort. The reservoir hadn't noticed. He checked it the way he'd learned to—the brief internal inventory, a glance at the ocean—and found it exactly where it had been that morning. The fourteen junctions, the hours of sustained channeling, the raw force he'd poured into the lattice—all of it had cost him less than he could measure.

Dorenne was standing at the end of the street with her engineers, making notes. She looked up as he approached.

"How do you feel?"

"Fine."

She wrote something down. The pen moved without hesitation—she'd been making marks all afternoon, a notation system he didn't recognize, recording his output and the time at each junction. She was building a dataset.

"The southern quarter's water system has been on my list for six weeks," she said. She didn't look up from the page. "Tomorrow?"

He looked back down the street. Doors were open. Windows that had been dark were lit. The district had the particular quality of a place coming back to life—not the sharp animation of celebration but the slow, deep breath of relief. People moved differently in warm buildings. Their shoulders dropped. Their voices carried.

A child was laughing somewhere. Not the giddy laughter of play—the involuntary, startled laughter of someone who had just pressed their bare feet to warm stone for the first time in four months.

"Tomorrow," he said.

* * *

He walked the district afterward.

Dorenne had gone—back to the Hall, back to her office, back to the map on the wall where the eastern district would be updated from red to something else. Her engineers lingered, checking lattice readings, confirming output levels, doing the careful technical work that translated his raw force into documented infrastructure. They'd have numbers by morning. Dorenne would have her dataset.

Ash walked alone.

The streets were different now. Not architecturally—the buildings still leaned, the alleys were still narrow, the pavement was still cracked where maintenance crews hadn't reached. But the walls were warm. He could feel it as he passed—radiant heat coming through the stone, the lattice network doing what it had been designed to do, distributing energy evenly through the district's structures. The air in the street was warmer too, ambient heat leaking through windows and doorways, the district exhaling months of accumulated cold.

He walked past a row of residential buildings and heard sounds he recognized. Cooking. Water running. The particular percussion of daily life—pots on stoves, doors opening, the low murmur of conversation that happened when people occupied their kitchens instead of huddling in their warmest room. These were sounds he'd grown up with. Sounds that meant a building was alive.

A woman was hanging laundry on a line strung between two windows. The fabric steamed in the warm air rising from the wall. She saw him looking and nodded—not a greeting, not really. An acknowledgment. The kind of nod that passed between two people who shared an understanding too specific for words.

He kept walking. Block nineteen. Block twenty. The buildings here were older, the lattice work deeper in the walls, and the heat came through more slowly—a gradual warming that would take hours to reach the upper floors. But it was reaching. He could feel it through the stone when he put his hand on a wall. Layer by layer. Room by room.

A door opened as he passed. An old woman—older than the man he'd seen earlier, small and bent, her hands knotted with the particular thickness of someone who'd spent decades channeling and was now too weak to warm her own tea. She stood in the doorway and looked at the warm wall beside her. She put her hand on it. She looked at him.

Her eyes were wet. She didn't speak. She didn't need to. She stood in her doorway with her hand on warm stone and her eyes full and her mouth pressed thin, and Ash stood in the street and looked at her and felt something in his chest that had nothing to do with the reservoir.

He nodded. She nodded. He walked on.

Twenty-three years. Twenty-three years of being the person who couldn't contribute. Who ate the food and used the water and occupied the space and gave nothing back. Who smiled and helped and worked with his hands and tried—tried so hard—to be worth the resources he consumed, to offset the cost of his existence with warmth and humor and willingness. Twenty-three years of the careful performance, the cheerful mask, the private accounting of every meal he didn't pay for with channeling and every maintenance shift he couldn't take and every heating cycle someone else spent on his behalf.

He had fixed a neighborhood in an afternoon.

The thought arrived without drama. It settled into him the way the heat settled into stone—quietly, deeply, reaching places that had been cold for so long he'd stopped noticing the temperature. He walked the warm streets and the thought expanded. He'd fixed it. Not partially, not temporarily, not with the half-measures of a crew running on empty pools. He'd filled the lattice to capacity. The district would stay warm for days on the charge he'd given it—weeks, if the engineers were right about the stone's retention. And when it faded, he could come back and fill it again. And again. And again.

His hands were open at his sides. The reservoir pressed behind them—patient, enormous. He'd poured hours of sustained channeling into fourteen junction boxes and the ocean hadn't noticed.

He stopped at a intersection where three streets met. The buildings around him were warm. The air was warm. Somewhere above him, a window opened—the sound of a latch releasing, the creak of hinges that had been frozen shut for months. A face appeared, looked up at the sky, disappeared. The window stayed open. The first open window in the eastern district in four months.

He stood in the intersection and breathed.

* * *

The southern quarter's water system was worse than the heating grid.

Ash went the next morning. Dorenne met him at the junction of the Third and Fourth Terraces with a different pair of engineers and a map of the water network that was more annotation than map—red marks covering the original lines, each one a failure point, a blockage, a junction where the channeled pressure had dropped below the minimum needed to push water uphill through stone pipes.

"The system was built two hundred years ago," said the senior engineer, a grey-haired man named Torath. "The original channelers could maintain pressure across the entire network from three master junctions. We've been running it with twelve, and as of last month, we can't staff more than eight."

The water system didn't use heating lattices. It used pressure channels—stone pipes with channeling interfaces that maintained the force needed to push water through the network against gravity. The quarter sat on a slope, the lower buildings fed easily by natural pressure, the upper ones dependent entirely on channeled force. Without it, water stopped at the fourth floor. Everything above went dry.

"How many people above the fourth floor?" Ash asked.

"Six thousand."

He worked the junction boxes the way he'd worked the eastern district—one by one, feeding energy into the interfaces, feeling the system respond. The water network was different from the heating lattice—less forgiving, more mechanical. Heat distributed evenly through stone. Water required sustained pressure, constant force, the channeled equivalent of a pump running at full capacity. When he pushed too hard, a pipe in block thirty-one groaned and Torath grabbed his arm.

"Less," Torath said. "The joints are old. If you blow a seal, we lose the section for a week."

He pulled back. Adjusted. Found the ceiling the system could handle and held the output just below it. The water moved—he could hear it, the gurgle and rush of flow returning to dry pipes, the particular music of a system coming back to life. Above him, in the upper floors, he heard a different sound. Taps opening. The hollow thunder of water filling empty tanks.

Someone shouted. A window opened and a woman leaned out, wet to the elbows, grinning.

"It's running!" she called. Not to him—to the street, to anyone. "The water's running!"

* * *

The crumbling residential tower was called Grayspire.

It stood at the western edge of the middle districts—twelve stories of elder-era architecture, graceful and doomed. The structural reinforcement that held it together had been degrading for a decade. Maintenance crews patched it seasonally, but each season the patches held less and the gaps between them grew. Two months ago, a section of the eighth floor had dropped thirty centimeters. The residents above had been evacuated. The residents below had been told the building was under review, which was the Infrastructure Bureau's way of saying they didn't have the resources to fix it and didn't have the authority to condemn it.

Dorenne brought him three days after the water system.

He stood at the base and looked up. The tower's beauty was still visible—the carved stonework, the sweeping lines of a structure designed by people who could shape rock like clay. But the beauty had a particular quality that Ash recognized from years of cataloguing infrastructure decline. It was the beauty of a thing that had been left too long. Hairline fractures in the facade. A slight lean he could only see from ground level. The eighth floor, where the drop had occurred, was visible as a discontinuity in the stone—a horizontal line where the wall above didn't quite meet the wall below.

He pressed his palms to the foundation. Pushed.

This was different from heating or water. Structural reinforcement required force distributed through the building's skeleton—the load-bearing walls, the floor plates, the columns and arches that carried weight from top to bottom. He couldn't just flood it. The building had a specific architecture, a logic of forces, and his energy had to follow that logic or it would do more harm than good.

He listened. Through his palms, through the stone, he could feel the building—its weight, its stresses, the places where the structure was sound and the places where it was failing. The eighth floor was obvious: a void in the force network, a section where the channeled reinforcement had thinned to nothing. The stone there was carrying its own weight without magical assistance, and it was losing.

He channeled into the foundation and pushed upward, following the load-bearing walls. The energy rose through the structure—he could feel it climbing, filling the reinforcement network the way water filled pipes. At the eighth floor, the void drank. The stone there was starving—starved of the energy that had held it rigid for two centuries. His force poured in and the building settled. Not dropped—settled. A deep, structural exhalation, the sound of stone relaxing into support it had been missing.

Above him, something clinked. He looked up. A window on the ninth floor had been propped open with a wooden wedge, and the wedge had fallen—knocked loose by the building's shift. A face appeared. Looked down.

"What was that?" the face called.

He didn't answer. He was still channeling. The building was telling him things—through the stone, through his hands, a language of pressures and stresses that his body was translating without instruction. Here: a column that needed more. There: a floor plate that had started to bow. The eighth floor wanted the most, but the seventh and ninth were compensating in ways that would fail next if he didn't reach them too.

He worked for an hour. Dorenne's engineers monitored from inside—measurement tools pressed to walls and floors, tracking the reinforcement as it spread. When he finally stepped back, his arms ached. Not from the channeling—from pressing against the foundation stone for an hour, a physical posture his body wasn't accustomed to.

"The eighth floor has stabilized," Verant reported. She'd joined the rotation. "Reinforcement is at approximately sixty percent of original specification."

"Can you bring it to full?" Dorenne asked. She was looking at Ash.

He checked the reservoir. The morning's work—the tower, the walk here, the sustained structural channeling—sat against the total like a coin against a vault.

"Yes," he said.

He went back. Pressed his palms to the stone. Pushed until the engineers said stop.

* * *

The transit shaft on Meridian Street had been dead for two years.

It was a vertical shaft—a stone tube three meters wide, running from the base of the Fourth Terrace to the top of the Seventh. A channeled platform sat at the bottom, designed to rise through the shaft on sustained force. In the high-magic era, a single operator could power the platform's ascent—channel downward through their feet, the reactive force pushing the platform up. Now it took three, working in concert, and even then the platform rose in lurches that rattled the shaft walls.

This shaft had no operators. The last crew had been reassigned to the Northridge Terrace shaft, which served a wealthier district and had political priority. Meridian Street's residents used the stairs. Nine hundred steps from bottom to top. The elderly used the stairs. Parents with children used the stairs. Workers carrying loads used the stairs. For two years, nine hundred steps, every day.

Ash stood on the platform. It was stone—heavy, solid, a disc carved to fit the shaft with a finger's width of clearance on all sides. The channeling interface was in the center: a worn circle of smooth stone, polished by years of feet.

He stepped onto the interface. Planted his feet. Channeled downward.

The platform lurched. Rose a meter, stopped. His output was uneven—too much force on the left side, the platform tilting until it scraped the shaft wall. He adjusted, shifted his weight, tried to equalize the pressure. The platform rose another meter. Tilted the other direction.

*Smaller. Even. Steady.*

He breathed. Let the reservoir settle. Pushed—not hard, not fast, but evenly, a sustained column of force through the platform's mass. The platform rose. Smoothly this time. The shaft walls slid past—stone, shadow, the occasional window cut for light. He rose through the Fourth Terrace. The Fifth. The platform didn't lurch. The force held.

At the top, he stepped off. The Seventh Terrace opened around him—a wide street, market stalls, people. The shaft opening was marked with a metal railing and a sign that read OUT OF SERVICE.

He pulled the sign off the railing. Dropped it on the platform. Rode it back down.

At the bottom, a small crowd had gathered. People who had watched him ascend through the shaft's lower windows. They stood at the platform's edge and looked at the sign lying on the stone.

"It works?" a man asked.

"It works."

The man stepped onto the platform. Then a woman. Then two children who held their mother's hands and looked at the shaft above them with the particular expression of children who had been told something was broken and were now being asked to stand on it.

Ash stood in the center and channeled. The platform rose. The children laughed—the sound bouncing off the shaft walls, amplifying, filling the vertical space with an echo that hadn't been heard in two years.

He ran the platform for an hour. Up and down. Loading passengers at the bottom and unloading them at the top. The people stood on the platform and rose through the shaft and stepped off at the Seventh Terrace and looked at the city from the height they'd been climbing to for two years, and some of them stood for a moment at the railing and breathed, and some of them walked away without stopping, and one woman sat down on a bench at the top and didn't move and didn't speak and stared at the stairs she hadn't had to climb.

Dorenne arrived at the end of the hour. She stood at the bottom of the shaft and watched him descend.

"I have four more shafts on the priority list," she said. "And the northern aqueduct. And the Third Terrace foundation."

She had a folder in her hands. He could see the papers inside—maps, diagrams, lists. The infrastructure of a city's failure, organized into deployable packages.

"When?" he said.

"We'll build a schedule." She opened the folder. "The northern aqueduct serves the agricultural terraces—food production has dropped twelve percent since the pressure failed. That should be next. Then the Third Terrace foundation—the retaining wall is showing stress fractures. After that—"

She stopped. Looked at him.

"You're smiling," she said.

He was. He hadn't decided to. It was the other kind of smile—the one that happened to his face without his permission, the involuntary response of a body that had found the thing it was looking for.

"Build the schedule," he said.

* * *

The days took on a rhythm.

Dorenne scheduled. Ash deployed. The pattern established itself with the efficiency of a system designed by someone who understood systems—which Dorenne did, better than anyone he'd ever met. She identified the need. She briefed him on the technical requirements. She arranged engineering support, provided maps, coordinated with district managers. He arrived, channeled, and the broken thing worked.

The pattern had a name, though no one used it: need, deployment, gratitude, next need.

The northern aqueduct took half a day. The pressure channels were simpler than the residential water network—larger pipes, fewer junctions, a system designed for volume rather than precision. He fed force into the master junction and the water moved, a great hydraulic sigh as months of dry pipe filled and the agricultural terraces received the irrigation pressure they'd been supplementing with bucket chains and prayer.

The Third Terrace retaining wall took longer. The wall was massive—fifty meters long, eight meters tall, holding back the weight of the terrace above and everything built on it. The stress fractures Dorenne had mentioned were visible from the street: a web of hairline cracks radiating from the wall's midpoint, each one a failure waiting to cascade. He pressed himself against the stone and channeled for three hours. The cracks didn't close—that would have required reshaping the stone, a precision he didn't have—but the reinforcement held them stable. The wall stopped moving. The engineers confirmed: fracture propagation halted.

A residential block in the southern quarter. A water junction in the market district. A heating grid in the lower terraces that had been running at half capacity for a year.

Each one was a genuine crisis. Each one was real people in real difficulty. And each one, when he'd finished, left him standing in a place where things worked that hadn't worked before, surrounded by people who looked at him the way the old woman in the eastern district had looked at her warm wall—with something too large and too immediate for gratitude.

He was getting better.

Not at the precision work—that was still beyond him, the micro-pulse technique still three or four times too much output for anything delicate. But at the brute channeling, the sustained heavy work that Dorenne's deployments required, he was measurably improving. The waste heat at each junction was lower than the last. His output was more consistent—fewer surges, fewer corrections. He learned to read the systems through the interface stones, to feel the lattice or the pipe network or the structural skeleton before he pushed, to adjust his output to what the system could absorb rather than what the reservoir wanted to give.

Raw practice. Hour after hour, junction after junction, building after building. His hands learned the feel of different materials the way a carpenter's hands learned wood—the particular resistance of heating lattice stone, the flex of pressurized pipe, the deep structural vocabulary of a load-bearing wall. He wasn't clean. He wasn't efficient. A trained channeler with a fraction of his power would have done each job with less waste, less ambient heat, less force bleeding uselessly into the air.

But no trained channeler had the power to do what he was doing. That was the math that made everything else irrelevant.

He checked the reservoir each evening, the way he'd learned to—the internal inventory, the brief glance at the ocean. Each day's work sat against the total like a drop against a sea. The infrastructure jobs were expensive by any standard—thousands of kilojoules at his efficiency rate, each deployment costing more than a young channeler's annual pool. By any rational measure, he was spending extravagantly.

The ocean didn't notice. Day after day after day, and the level hadn't moved in any way he could perceive. The reservoir sat behind his ribs—vast, patient, undiminished by the morning's work or the afternoon's or the week's. Infinite wasn't the right word. He knew it wasn't infinite. Haran's voice lived in some careful corner of his mind, asking the question that was always the same question.

But knowing something wasn't infinite and *feeling* it as infinite were different things. And the feeling won. Every morning the reservoir was there, full and vast, and every evening it was still there, and the days of hard channeling left no mark he could find.

He stopped checking.

* * *

Dorenne's office wall changed.

The map was the same—Solathis, districts outlined, annotations clustered—but the annotations were different now. Beside the red marks, she'd added green. Each green mark was a deployment: date, location, a brief description of the work. Eastern district heating grid. Southern quarter water system. Grayspire tower. Meridian Street transit shaft. Northern aqueduct. Third Terrace retaining wall. The green marks multiplied as the days passed, spreading across the map like a second city overlaid on the first.

He noticed. He didn't think about what it meant—not yet, not in the way that would matter later. He noticed the way you noticed weather: as a fact of the environment, neither good nor bad.

What he noticed more—what he felt in his body, in his posture, in the particular quality of his step when he left the Hall each morning—was the way the deployments felt. Each one was a door opened in a building where the doors had been closed. Each one was the sound of water running in pipes that had been dry. Each one was the moment when a building settled under his hands and the people inside felt the walls stop moving.

He carried the feeling the way you carried warmth from a good fire—not in his hands but in his body, in the deep places where temperature lived. He walked through the city and saw things he'd seen before—the cracked pavements, the dim district lighting, the transit shafts that lurched or didn't run—and instead of the old inventory of things that were broken, he saw a list of things he could fix. The shift was total. Where he'd once catalogued failure as a witness, he now catalogued it as a repairman.

There were so many things to fix. The city was full of them—more than he'd known, more than Dorenne's maps showed, more than the reports he'd filed as her aide had captured. Solathis was held together by people whose pools were too small for the systems their grandparents had built, and the gap between what the city needed and what its residents could provide was growing wider every year. He'd known this. He'd filed the reports. But knowing it as data and knowing it as the particular weight of a failing retaining wall under his hands were different things, the way knowing the word for hunger and being hungry were different things.

He could feel the city's need through the interface stones. When he pressed his palms to a junction box, the system behind it told him how long it had been starving. The eastern district's lattice had been a four-month fast. The northern aqueduct had been longer—slow degradation over a year, the kind of failure that happened in increments too small to trigger emergency response. Grayspire had been a decade of neglect.

All of it fixable. All of it within his reach.

Dorenne brought him a new list every morning. He worked through it every afternoon. The green marks on her map spread.

He stopped visiting the family as often. Not a decision—a consequence. The deployments started early and finished late. He was tired in the evenings—not from the channeling but from the walking, the standing, the physical posture of pressing his body against stone for hours. He sent messages. He stopped by when the schedule allowed. His mother asked him to stay for dinner and he said next time, and the next time came and went, and the time after that.

He stopped thinking about the workshop.

Not a decision.

A consequence.

* * *

Three weeks into the deployment schedule, Dorenne called him into her office.

She was sitting behind her desk this time—not standing at the map, not holding reports. Sitting, with her hands folded, in the particular posture she adopted when she was about to say something she'd been thinking about for longer than it would take to say it.

"The Council's quarterly review is in two days," she said. "I'd like you to attend."

He'd sat in on Council sessions before—as her aide, in the back row, taking notes. The Council governed Solathis—twelve elected members, each representing a district or interest group, meeting in the grand chamber at the top of the Hall of Infrastructure. He'd found the sessions tedious. Long speeches, procedural arguments, the particular theater of people who wielded institutional power performing that power for each other.

"What for?"

"The infrastructure report. My department's allocation for the next quarter." She picked up a document and set it down, a gesture that had nothing to do with the document and everything to do with the rhythm of what she was saying. "The eastern district deployment alone has reduced emergency maintenance requests by sixty percent. The water system repairs saved an estimated forty thousand labor-hours that would have been spent on bucket chains. The transit shaft—"

"I know what the deployments did."

"The Council doesn't." She looked at him. "They know the Wellspring exists. They know I've been directing deployments. What they don't know—what I'd like them to see—is the scope of what's possible. Not the spectacle. The infrastructure."

He understood. She wanted him in the room so the Council could see the asset she was managing. She wanted the green marks on her map translated into political capital—funding, authority, the institutional power that came from being the person who controlled the city's most significant resource.

He understood, and it didn't bother him. The deployments were real. The people he'd helped were real. If Dorenne's political position strengthened because she'd pointed him at genuine problems and he'd solved them, then her political position *should* strengthen. That was how the system was supposed to work.

"I'll be there," he said.

She nodded. The slight relaxation—not in her posture, which was always controlled, but in the quality of her stillness. The tension of an ask, resolved.

"One more thing," she said. "The Thornwall aqueduct. It feeds the lower eastern district—three thousand households. It's been losing pressure for eight months. I've had it on the critical list since before the heating grid."

She slid a map across the desk. The aqueduct was marked in red—a long line running from the upper terraces to the lower, with failure points annotated at every junction.

"After the Council session," she said.

He took the map. Folded it. Put it in his pocket, where it sat against the cracked gear casing from Haran's workshop that he still carried and hadn't looked at in weeks.

"After the Council session," he said.

Dorenne went back to her work. He went back to his.

The map of the aqueduct pressed against the gear casing in his pocket. Two objects from two worlds, separated by a layer of fabric. He didn't notice the weight of either one.

Outside, the city waited. Its walls were cracking and its pipes were dry and its transit shafts were silent, and somewhere in the eastern district a child was pressing her bare feet against warm stone. He would go there. He would go everywhere. The reservoir was vast and the need was vast and between the two of them, they filled the space where twenty-three years of uselessness had lived.

He walked through the streets and the streets knew him now—the infrastructure crews nodded, the district managers waved, the people in the buildings he'd repaired looked at him from their windows and their doorways and their warm, working homes. He was the person who fixed things. He was the person who could fix anything. After a lifetime of being worth less than the resources he consumed, he was finally—finally—worth more.

The feeling settled into his bones. Not joy—something deeper. Something structural. The particular peace of a load-bearing wall that had found its weight, a heating lattice that had found its warmth, a transit shaft that had found its purpose.

He was useful.

He would do anything to stay this way.
