# Chapter 14

The market on Candle Street was closing when Ash saw her.

He was cutting through the lower market ring on his way back from the Millward retaining wall — a deployment that had taken most of the afternoon, three hours of channeling into cracked stone while Dorenne's structural assessment team measured and documented and checked their numbers against his output. Good work. Necessary work. The wall held forty residential towers above the gorge, and the channeled reinforcement in its base had been thinning for years, the old stone whispering under the weight of everything it carried. He'd poured force into it until the whisper stopped, until the stone was solid and whole and warm beneath his palms, and the sensation had been — not satisfying. Bigger than satisfying. The sensation of being the reason forty buildings wouldn't fall.

He came down through the market ring because it was faster than the transit corridor, and the transit corridor was running on reduced service again, the shaft operators pulling half-shifts because their pools didn't stretch the way they used to. He moved through the stalls — half of them already shuttered, the awnings folded, the last vendors packing fish and cloth and oil into crates — and the movement felt good, the downhill momentum of a body in motion, a body with somewhere to be and something to have done.

He almost walked past her.

Ryn was standing at the coppersmith's stall at the bottom of the market ring, her back to him, examining something small she held up to the fading light. Her hair was tied back the way she wore it when she'd been working — a single knot, practical, already coming loose. Her coat was the same coat she'd worn for two years, the grey one with the frayed cuffs, and her boots were caked with the reddish dust that came from the transit maintenance tunnels where she'd been working since he — since before.

He stopped. The market flowed around him — a woman carrying a basket of flatbread, two children chasing something, a man dragging a handcart over the cobblestones — and in the moving crowd Ryn was still, examining her small copper thing, and the stillness caught him the way unexpected silence caught him, the way a pause in music caught him. A break in the rhythm.

He should keep walking. He had the morning briefing to prepare for. Dalla's deployment report needed review. Dorenne wanted his assessment of the Millward wall's channeling density before the strategy meeting, and the assessment was half-formed in his head, a collection of structural impressions he needed to translate into the language her planning apparatus understood.

He should keep walking.

"Ryn."

She turned. The small copper thing was a hinge — he could see it now, a cabinet hinge with a decorative curl at the top, the kind of thing you'd buy to replace one that had broken. She looked at him, and the looking lasted a beat longer than it should have, the way you looked at someone you hadn't expected to see in a place you hadn't expected to see them.

"Ash." A smile. Not wide — careful. The smile of someone navigating the distance between what they wanted to say and what was safe to say. "I didn't know you came down to this market."

"I was cutting through. The transit's running half-shifts."

"I know. I maintain the transit." A pause. Not pointed, not accusing. Just the pause of someone who'd stated a fact that happened to illuminate a gap. "The shaft bearings need relining. We've been pulling double rotations."

"Is it holding?"

"For now." She tucked the hinge into her coat pocket. Her hands were rough — working hands, the knuckles reddened from cold and effort. He remembered those hands. He remembered the way they'd warmed his cup without thinking about it, the casual pulse of channeling that was as natural to her as breathing, as unremarkable as her heartbeat. "How are you?"

Not *what have you been doing*. Not *tell me about the deployments*. She asked the question the way she'd always asked it — aimed at the person, not the performance.

"Good," he said. "Busy. We're running a six-day deployment schedule now — Dorenne got the Council funding approved. The Millward wall today. The southern cistern network next week. There's a structural assessment queue backed up to—" He heard himself. The words coming out in the cadence of a briefing, organized by system and schedule and the priorities Dorenne had ranked on her wall. He stopped. Started again. "I'm good. Things are moving."

Ryn watched him. Her expression didn't change, exactly. Something behind it shifted — a recalculation, a small adjustment in the architecture of her face that he knew well enough to read and didn't know well enough to name.

"Things are moving," she repeated. Not mocking. Testing the phrase, holding it up the way she'd held up the hinge, examining its shape.

"You should see the eastern district. The heating grid — I reinforced it three weeks ago, and it's still holding. Full output. Warm water in four residential blocks that haven't had warm water in eight months. There's a family on the third tier who—" The story was already forming, the narrative of impact he'd built in three weeks of deployment, the proof that the work mattered, that *he* mattered. "I'm helping, Ryn. Really helping. Not the way I used to help — not carrying tools and taking notes. I'm doing the actual work."

"I know," she said. "Everyone knows."

The words landed simply. Everyone knows. His name in every district. His power on everyone's lips. The Hero of Nothing and Everything — that's what someone in the middle tiers had called him, and he'd laughed at the name and carried it inside him like a coal, warm and bright.

"That's — it's good, right? That's what I always wanted. To be useful. To not be—" He stopped. The sentence had a destination he didn't want to visit. *To not be dead weight.* The old word. The word that had lived in other people's mouths and in his own chest for twenty-three years. "To actually contribute."

"You always contributed."

"Not like this."

"No. Not like this."

The silence between them was the wrong shape. Not the comfortable silence of two people who knew each other so well that words were optional. This was a silence with edges, a silence you could cut yourself on if you leaned into it wrong. She was looking at him with an expression that was somehow both open and closed — her eyes meeting his, her posture relaxed, and underneath both of those things a watchfulness that he recognized from the years before, the specific watchfulness of Ryn deciding whether to say the thing she was thinking or let it pass.

She said the thing.

"You talk about yourself differently now."

The words arrived with the unhurried directness that was Ryn's signature — no preamble, no softening, just the observation laid down like a brick. He felt his shoulders tighten. A small, involuntary contraction. The body registering something before the mind could catch up.

"What do you mean?"

"I mean you talk about yourself differently." She wasn't accusing. She was describing. The way she'd describe a change in the grain of a piece of wood, or a shift in the resonance of a bearing she was testing. Technical. Precise. "Everything is about what you can do. What you reinforced. What you stabilized. How many people, how many buildings, how much power. It's all — capacity. Output. Like you're reading from one of Dorenne's reports."

The heat came fast. Up through his chest, into his jaw. Not anger — something sharper and more confused, the reflex of a wound being touched before he knew there was a wound.

"That's not fair."

"I didn't say it was unfair. I said it's different."

"I have a lot to talk about. A lot is happening. For the first time in my life, I have things to talk about that aren't—" He stopped again. The sentence kept pointing toward the same place, the place where the old Ash lived, the Ash who sat in rooms while other people channeled, who smiled while other people built, who performed his own okayness like a one-man show he couldn't stop staging. "Ryn, you've had magic your whole life. You can't — you don't know what this is like. To go from nothing to this. To be able to *do* things."

She didn't flinch. She didn't step back. She stood in the closing market with the hinge in her pocket and the dust on her boots and looked at him the way she'd always looked at him — directly, without flinching, without performing anything for his benefit.

"I know."

"Then why are you—"

"I'm not picking at it, Ash."

"It sounds like you're picking at it."

"I'm telling you what I see." Her voice was steady. Not cold. The particular temperature of Ryn when she was being honest, which was the temperature of deep water — cool enough to feel, warm enough to stay in. "I see my friend. I see him doing incredible things. I also see him disappearing into the things he's doing, and I don't—" A pause. The first crack in the steadiness, so brief he almost missed it. "I don't know where you are in there. When you talk. I can hear the deployments. I can hear Dorenne's schedule. I can't hear you."

The market was nearly empty now. The last vendor — the coppersmith, an old woman with an elder's easy warmth radiating from her hands — was pulling a canvas cover over her stall. The light was going. The evening settled over the market ring the way evenings settled over the lower tiers, gently, with the patience of a city that had been settling into darkness for centuries.

Ash stood in the settling dark and felt something in his chest that he didn't want to feel. Something that lived in the space between what Ryn was saying and what he wanted to hear, and the space was the space where the old Ash lived, the Ash without power, the Ash who needed things he could never get, and he didn't want to go to that space. Not now. Not when the Millward wall was reinforced and the eastern grid was holding and the deployment schedule was running and the city needed him, actually needed him, needed *him* specifically, not a charity case in a workshop but the Wellspring, the only one, the one who could hold the city together with his hands.

"I'm right here," he said.

She looked at him. The look lasted longer than a look should last, and in its duration something passed between them — not a message, not a meaning, something older and less articulable. The thing that had always been between them, the unnamed current that ran beneath the friendship, the thing they'd both agreed not to examine. He couldn't read her expression. Or he could read it and didn't want to.

"I am happy for you," she said.

Five words. She said them the way she said everything — plainly, without decoration, without the warmth that would have made them easy to receive. She was happy for him. She meant it. And the meaning was a door she was closing, gently, because the other thing she wanted to say — the thing behind the observation, behind the concern, behind the look that lasted too long — was something she'd decided not to say. Not tonight. Maybe not ever.

The silence after was the wedge.

He felt it. Not in his mind, where he could have argued with it, but in his body — in his sternum, in the muscles of his jaw, in the place behind his ribs where the Wellspring hummed its constant patient hum. The silence was wrong. It was the wrong silence. It was the silence of two people standing on opposite sides of a gap that hadn't been there a month ago, a gap that he'd opened by becoming something and she'd opened by staying the same, and neither of them had the tools to build across it.

"I should—" He gestured vaguely uphill. Toward the upper tiers. Toward Dorenne's office. Toward the room near the Hall where his deployment brief waited and the morning was already assembling itself in his schedule. "Early briefing tomorrow."

"Yeah." Ryn pulled her coat tighter. The evening was cooling, and she didn't channel to warm herself — or she did, but so subtly that he couldn't tell, the difference between using her pool and simply existing as a person with a pool invisible in the casual economy of her body. "Good to see you."

"You too."

He walked away. Uphill. The stairs rose through the market ring toward the middle tiers, and his legs took them easily, the body that had climbed these stairs a thousand times before the Wellspring climbing them now with the thoughtless efficiency of a machine, and behind him Ryn stood in the empty market, and he didn't look back, and the not-looking-back was its own kind of statement — the statement of someone moving forward too fast to see what he was leaving behind.

* * *

The house smelled like spiced lamb.

Ash stood in the doorway and the smell hit him before anything else — dense, warm, layered with the rosemary and black pepper and slow-cooked fat that meant his mother had spent the entire afternoon at the stove. Not the channeling stove, the one that heated at a touch. The old stove. The iron one that ran on wood and patience, the one she'd used when he was small because the channeling interface was too expensive for daily cooking, and then kept using after they could afford the channeling interface because the old stove made the food taste the way she wanted it to taste. The stove was a choice. The food on it was a statement.

His favorite meal. She'd made his favorite meal.

"Ashlyn." His mother appeared from the kitchen, wiping her hands on a cloth, and the use of his full name was the use of a woman who hadn't seen her son in ten days and was pretending that ten days was normal, that ten days wasn't a chasm, that she hadn't counted every single one. Her face did three things in rapid succession: relief, hunger, restraint. She wanted to touch his face. He could see it in the way her hands rose and then didn't, the way they found the cloth instead, the way the cloth became a prop for the performance of not needing to touch him. She'd learned not to hover. He'd taught her that, without meaning to, in the weeks since the Wellspring — taught her by pulling away, by being busy, by being important, by being too large for the arms that had held him.

"Ma. It smells incredible."

"Your father brought the lamb from the market this morning. The good vendor, the one on the east terrace." She was already moving, already organizing — pulling him into the house by the force of her preparation, the gravitational pull of a meal that had been waiting for him all day. "Sit. It's nearly ready. Maren's washing up."

The table was set for four. The good plates — the blue ones with the chipped edges, the ones his grandmother had made, the ones his mother brought out for occasions she wanted to matter. A candle in the center, lit by hand, the wax pooling in the clay holder he'd made when he was twelve, a lopsided thing that his father had praised and his mother had kept and that sat on the shelf between uses with the patient dignity of objects that have been loved beyond their quality.

He sat. The chair was his chair — the one by the window, the one with the worn armrest where his elbow had ground the wood smooth over two decades of dinners. The house was warm. Not channeling-warm — stove-warm, the dry, gentle heat of burning wood, the kind of warmth that came with sound and smell and the small crackle of flame. His mother had heated the house the old way. For him. Because the old way was the way the house had smelled when he was a boy, when the house was his world and the world was small enough to hold.

*She's trying.*

The thought arrived unbidden, and he pushed it aside because it carried a weight he didn't want to carry tonight. He was here. He'd come. That was enough. He'd looked at the deployment schedule and found a gap — an evening between the Millward assessment and the southern cistern survey — and he'd walked down through the tiers to the house where he'd grown up, and he was here, sitting in his chair, and the lamb was cooking and the candle was lit and this was enough.

His father came in from the back room, and the room changed shape.

Haren Torren was a broad man, built for the physical labor that had been his life — masonry, mostly, the manual kind that didn't require channeling, the kind that used his back and his arms and the weight of his body against stone. His pool was modest — middling for his generation, enough for daily life, not enough for the infrastructure work that paid well. He'd spent his career doing the work that didn't need magic, the carrying and lifting and placement that happened after the channelers had shaped the stone. He'd made a living at it. He'd made a good living at it. He'd never made his son feel that the living was diminished by its lack of magic.

"Ash." His father's hand found his shoulder. The grip was firm — not the tentative grip of a man handling something fragile, but the sure grip of a man who understood physical contact, who communicated through pressure and warmth and the particular duration of a held shoulder. "Good to see you."

"Good to see you, Da."

"You've been busy." Not a question. Not a complaint. A statement that occupied the space where a complaint might have lived, if his father were a different kind of man. "I saw the work on the eastern terraces. The retaining wall."

"The Millward wall. Today, actually. Three hours."

"Three hours." His father sat down across from him. The candlelight caught the lines in his face — deeper lines than Ash remembered, or deeper lines than he'd noticed. His father was aging the way manual laborers aged: steadily, visibly, the body recording its use in the ledger of its surface. "That wall's been failing since before you were born. Edrath's crew had six channelers on it for a month, the year your mother and I moved here. Couldn't hold it. Too much stone, too little pool." A pause. "You did it in three hours."

"The technique's getting better. I waste less than I did at the start."

"I imagine you do."

There was something in the way his father said *I imagine you do* — a gentleness, or the ghost of one, that lived underneath the pride. The pride was real. Ash could see it in his father's eyes, in the way the lines around them shifted when he talked about the wall, in the brightness that came from watching your son accomplish something that six men couldn't. But underneath the pride, in the place where fathers kept the things they couldn't say, there was something else. A question. An old question, the same question Ash had seen flickering in his father's face at dinner two weeks ago and the dinner before that — a question his father kept almost asking and then not asking, the question retreating into the lines of his face like water into dry ground.

_Did you know I was proud of you before this?_

Ash heard the question without it being spoken. He heard it and moved past it the way he moved past the transit protesters and the tinkerer pamphlets and the small signs of a world asking questions he didn't have time for. His father's pride was a fact. His father's love was a fact. These facts existed, solid and reliable, and he could lean on them without examining their architecture.

"The funding came through," Ash said. "Dorenne's plan — the six-month reinforcement schedule. The Council approved it."

"I heard. Your mother's friend at the Hall — Serada — she told us."

"It means we can accelerate. Hit the critical systems faster. The water networks, the structural foundations—"

"That's wonderful, Ash."

"It is." The energy of the deployment was still in him, the forward momentum of a day spent doing meaningful work at meaningful scale. He could feel the words wanting to come — the numbers, the systems, the districts served, the lives made better. The briefing that lived inside him now, the narrative of impact that was the language he spoke most fluently. "The Millward wall alone protects forty residential towers. Eight thousand people. And that's just one wall. If we can hit the schedule Dorenne's projecting—"

Maren appeared in the doorway.

She was drying her hands on a towel, her hair damp at the temples, and the sight of her — the specific sight of his sister in the doorway of the house they'd grown up in, wearing the dark work clothes she wore to her shifts, her shoulders carrying the particular set that meant she'd been channeling all day — brought the room into a focus that the deployment numbers couldn't.

"You came," she said.

"I came."

"Ma's been cooking since noon."

"I can smell."

She sat down beside him. Close. The way they'd always sat — siblings who'd grown up sharing a table too small for the family's needs, their elbows finding the choreography of long practice. She smelled like stone dust and the faint metallic tang of channeling residue — the scent that lingered on your hands and clothes after a long day of structural work. She'd been on a maintenance shift. He could tell by the way she held her shoulders, the slight forward hunch of someone who'd spent hours pushing energy into stone, the posture of controlled depletion.

"How was the shift?" he asked.

"Fine. The Threadneedle junction's cracking again — we sealed it last month, but the substrate's degrading faster than the reinforcement can hold." She flexed her fingers. A small movement, almost unconscious — the flex of a channeler checking what she had left. "We're running double crews, but it's — it takes what it takes."

"I could look at the Threadneedle junction. If Dorenne adds it to the schedule—"

"It's on the list. Fourth tier priority." Maren's voice was even. Neutral. The voice of a professional discussing work with another professional. But there was something in the way she said *fourth tier* — a precision that had weight behind it, the precision of someone who knew exactly where her work ranked on someone else's chart.

Their mother brought the food. The lamb came to the table on the blue plate, surrounded by roasted root vegetables and a sauce that was dark with wine and slow time. She set it down and stood for a moment with her hands on the plate's edges, looking at the table — the four seats filled, the candle burning, the food steaming — and her expression was the expression of a woman standing in a painting she'd composed, a still life of a family dinner that she'd assembled with the same fierce attention she'd once given to protecting her son from a world that wouldn't accommodate him.

"Eat," she said. "Before it gets cold."

They ate.

The lamb was extraordinary. Of course it was. His mother's cooking had always been extraordinary — not technically, not the way a professional chef's cooking was extraordinary, but in the way that food made by someone who knows exactly what you love and makes it with that knowledge is always extraordinary. The spices were the spices of his childhood. The fat was rendered the way she'd always rendered it — slow, patient, the technique of a woman who couldn't waste ingredients and had turned economy into art. He ate and the eating was a kind of return, a brief homecoming conducted through the mouth, through taste and warmth and the memory encoded in flavor.

"This is—" He swallowed. "Ma, this is perfect."

She nodded. The nod was quick, pleased, the nod of someone receiving exactly the praise she'd worked for. But her eyes didn't leave his face. She watched him eat the way she'd always watched him eat — with the particular attention of a mother who'd spent years ensuring her child was fed, was warm, was safe. The attention had a quality now that it hadn't had before, or that he hadn't noticed before: a hunger. She was watching him the way a person watched something they were afraid of losing. Not the boy. The moment. The dinner itself, the four chairs filled, the ordinary impossible fact of her family at a table.

"Tell us about the work," his father said.

Ash talked. The deployment schedule. The Millward wall. The Council vote. The strategy meetings with Dorenne's team — Dalla and Verren and Orett and the careful machinery of institutional planning. He talked about the engineering challenges, the way different stone types responded to channeling reinforcement, the satisfaction of feeling a cracked foundation seal itself under his palms. He talked about the scale of it — the thousands of people served, the systems stabilized, the slow steady push against the city's decline.

He talked, and the talking was good. The words came easily. He was fluent in this language now — the language of capacity and impact, the language of Dorenne's charts translated into dinner conversation. He could feel his parents listening, could see his father's eyes bright with pride, could sense his mother's attention sharpening and softening as the narrative rose and fell.

"The technique's the thing," he said, leaning forward, his fork forgotten beside his plate. "I'm still wasting more than I should — way more than a trained channeler would. But I'm getting better. The structural work especially. There's a — it's like a conversation with the stone. You push, and the stone pushes back, and if you listen to the pushback you can find the natural lines of force and work with them instead of against them. It cuts the expenditure by—"

He looked at Maren. She was watching him. Her plate was half-finished, her fork resting against the edge, and she was watching him with an expression he couldn't quite parse — attentive, engaged, and underneath both of those things something else. Something careful.

"You know what I mean," he said. "The pushback. The stone's resonance structure."

"I know what you mean."

"Is that how you were taught? To listen for it?"

"Not exactly." She picked up her fork. Took a bite. Chewed. The chewing was deliberate — the deliberateness of someone buying time. "We learn it differently. With a smaller pool you have to find the resonance early, because you can't afford to push through it. It's — efficiency isn't optional, for us."

"Right. Right, exactly. And I'm starting to get there. Not like you — you've had years. But the principle's the same. The resonance structure, the natural lines of force. Dorenne's structural engineer, Orett, she showed me how to read the grain patterns in the old stone, and once you can read the grain you can—"

He was talking faster now. The momentum of the idea pulling him forward, the excitement of discovery, the pleasure of understanding something that had been opaque becoming clear. This was the thing he loved about the power — not just the capacity, the scale, the thousands of people helped. The *learning*. The daily revelation that magic was not just force but craft, that channeling was not just pushing but listening, that the Wellspring was not just an ocean of energy but a tool that responded to skill and knowledge and attention.

"—and the efficiency improvement is measurable. Dalla's been tracking it. Week one, I was running at maybe twenty percent efficiency on structural work. Now I'm at thirty-five, maybe forty on a good day. That's still terrible by any trained standard, but the rate of improvement is—"

He stopped.

Maren's fork was on the table. Her hands were in her lap. She was looking at him with an expression that had resolved, in the time he'd been talking, into something he could read. Something clear and quiet and sad.

She was listening to him describe — with enthusiasm, with wonder, with the innocent delight of someone discovering water — the basic principles she'd spent fifteen years mastering. The resonance structure. The pushback. The efficiency gains. The craft of making a small pool do large work. The things she'd learned not because they were fascinating but because they were necessary, because her pool was a fraction of his, because her body couldn't afford the waste his body spent like loose change.

He'd been explaining her own expertise to her. At forty percent efficiency. With an ocean of power that made his waste irrelevant. Describing the struggle of learning to channel the way a tourist describes the struggle of hiking a mountain that the locals cross every day to get to work.

The silence that followed was not the wedge. The wedge had been driven in the market with Ryn. This silence was something else — a gap, a breath, the small terrible space between a person who is learning what the world costs and a person who has always known.

"That's really good, Ash," Maren said. Her voice was even. Warm, even. She meant it. She was genuinely pleased that her brother was learning to channel with something approaching skill, the way she'd be pleased if he'd learned to cook or play an instrument or do anything well. But the pleasure was wrapped in something she couldn't show him, something she'd tucked away with the practiced efficiency of a woman who'd been tucking things away her whole life: the knowledge that his forty percent efficiency, applied to his Wellspring, produced more raw output than her ninety percent efficiency would produce in a decade.

Their father cleared his throat.

"Maren, how's the new crew?" The question was easy, natural — the question of a man who knew how to fill silences, who'd spent a lifetime filling the silences that gathered around his family like weather. "You mentioned they were bringing in people from the western district."

"Three new channelers. Two young, one middle-gen." Maren's voice shifted — back to the neutral ground of professional talk, the safe territory of work and logistics. "The middle-gen's good. Experienced. The two young ones are — they're trying. It's a lot of stone, and their pools don't stretch the way they need to."

"Are they being trained?"

"I'm training them." A pause. A flicker of something — pride, or the memory of pride, or the habit of pride in a context that was shifting. "The efficiency techniques. How to read the grain. How to find the resonance lines. All the things Ash is discovering." She smiled. The smile was small and precise and cost her something he couldn't see. "I've been teaching it for three years."

Their mother's hand found Maren's shoulder. A brief touch — quick, light, the touch of a woman who understood what was happening at her table and couldn't fix it and was offering the only thing she could offer: the physical fact of presence. Maren's smile didn't change. Their mother's hand withdrew.

"The lamb is wonderful, Ma," Maren said.

"There's more. Ash, more?"

"I'm fine. Thank you."

He was watching his sister. He was watching her and seeing something he hadn't seen, or hadn't let himself see — the way her hands lay in her lap with a particular stillness, the stillness of hands that had been working all day and were now conserving what they had left. The way her shoulders held their forward set, the posture of depletion she hadn't fully released. The way she'd flexed her fingers earlier — checking her pool, measuring what remained, the unconscious inventory of a person who lived inside a budget every single day.

He saw it, and the seeing was a door he didn't walk through. He noted it. He filed it. He moved on, the way he moved on from the transit protesters and the tinkerer pamphlets and Ryn's silence in the empty market — the way he moved on from everything that suggested the world he was building had cracks in it.

"I should talk to Dorenne about the Threadneedle junction," he said. "If the substrate's degrading that fast, it might need a full deep-reinforcement rather than surface work. Your crews are doing surface patches, right?"

"Surface is what we can afford."

"But if I came in and did the deep structure — got into the bedrock, resealed the foundation layer — your surface work would hold. You wouldn't need the double crews."

"That's one junction," Maren said. "There are forty-seven in the district."

"Then I'll do forty-seven."

"With what schedule? You just said Dorenne has you running six days a week."

"There's room. There's always room. The reservoir—" He stopped. Not because there wasn't more to say but because the word *reservoir* had a weight in this room that it didn't have in Dorenne's office. In Dorenne's office, the reservoir was a resource to be allocated — a column on a chart, a variable in a projection. In this room, at this table, with his sister's depleted hands in her lap and his mother watching him like a painting she was afraid would be taken from the wall, the reservoir was the thing that had changed everything. The thing that had made him extraordinary and made his sister ordinary and made his mother purposeless and made his father proud in a way that carried a question he didn't want to answer.

"The reservoir's fine," he said.

His father nodded. His mother watched. Maren said nothing.

The rest of the dinner happened around him. His father asked about the family of the girl who'd been rescued from the eastern residential collapse — the one from the early deployments, the one whose name Ash couldn't remember but whose father had gripped his hand and wept. His mother brought out a sweet — spiced pears in honey, the dessert she'd made for every special occasion since he was a child. She served the pears with the particular care of a woman performing a ritual, each plate arranged, each portion measured, each movement a small act of love made physical.

Ash ate the pears and tasted his childhood.

But the taste was distant. Not wrong — the pears were perfect, the honey dark and warm, the spice a whisper at the edges. Everything was exactly right. And the exactness was the problem. His mother had recreated the meal with the precision of a woman trying to reconstruct something — a moment, a version of her son, a family dinner that existed in a time before the Wellspring, before the deployments, before the charts on Dorenne's wall. She'd built a little room out of food and candlelight and the blue plates with the chipped edges, a room that smelled like the past, and she'd set a place for him in it, and he was sitting in it, and he was already somewhere else.

His mind was in the Millward wall. In the stone's resonance structure. In Dorenne's deployment schedule and the southern cistern survey and the fourteen systems at critical status and the four-month timeline and the morning briefing where Dalla would have the numbers and Orett would have the assessments and the machinery of the institution would click into its next rotation.

His body was at the table. His mind was in the wall.

"—and Serada said the market prices are going up again," his mother was saying. "The heating oil. Three coppers more than last month. I don't understand how they expect—"

"I can channel the heating for the house," Ash said. "If the oil's getting expensive. One pass would last — I don't know. Weeks. Months, maybe. The walls have good insulation. I could lay a reinforcement into the stone that would hold heat for—"

"We don't need you to heat the house, Ash."

His mother said it quietly. Without force. The sentence arrived with the weight of something that had been forming for days, weeks — the slowly crystallizing knowledge that her son's power, which could reshape districts and reinforce walls and hold up buildings, was also the thing that was taking him away from the table where she'd set his place.

"I know you don't need me to. I'm saying I could. So you don't have to spend on the oil."

"The oil is fine."

"Ma—"

"The oil is *fine*, Ashlyn."

The full name again. But different this time. Not the relieved Ashlyn of the doorway. This Ashlyn was the Ashlyn of a mother drawing a line — a small, careful line between the son who could fix things and the things she didn't want fixed. The heating oil was hers. The old stove was hers. The blue plates and the spiced pears and the dented kettle on the shelf — these were the things she'd kept from the time before, the artifacts of a life she'd built around a son who needed her, and she was not ready to have them replaced by a pulse of channeling that would last for months.

He let it go. The way he let everything go tonight — by moving on, by not looking back, by letting the current of his own momentum carry him past the moment and into the next one.

"I should go," he said.

The table was still half-full. The pears were still on the plates. The candle was still burning in the lopsided holder he'd made when he was twelve. His mother's hands were on the table's edge, and her knuckles were white.

"It's early," his father said. The words were casual. The tone was not. His father's voice had a quality that Ash recognized from childhood — the quality of a man choosing his words with care, placing each one the way he placed stones, with attention to where the weight would fall. "Stay for tea. Your mother got the cedar blend you like."

"I have the morning briefing. Dalla needs the Millward assessment before—"

"One cup," his father said. "Ten minutes."

Ash looked at his father. His father looked back. The candlelight moved across Haren Torren's face, and in the moving light the lines were deep and the eyes were bright and the expression was the expression of a man who was not asking for much. One cup. Ten minutes. A father's request, made gently, from a chair across a table in a house that used to be enough.

"I—" The morning briefing. The assessment. Dorenne's schedule. The momentum of the institution, the pull of the work, the gravity of the systems that needed him. He could feel it — the pull, the forward lean of his life, the constant acceleration toward the next deployment, the next district, the next crisis. The pull was not external. It was him. He wanted to go. He wanted to be in the Millward wall's numbers and the deployment schedule's columns and the strategy meeting's maps. He wanted to be where his power mattered, where his capacity was the variable that made the equation work, where the answer to the question *who are you?* was the answer he'd been waiting his entire life to give.

*I am the person who holds the city up.*

"I really can't, Da. Early start. But I'll come back. This weekend, maybe."

"This weekend."

"I'll try."

His father nodded. The nod was the nod of a man accepting something — not a refusal, not exactly, but the shape of a refusal, the architecture of a son leaving early from a dinner his mother had spent the afternoon preparing. He accepted it the way he accepted the things he couldn't change: quietly, with dignity, without letting the acceptance cost him the thing he was trying to hold onto.

Ash stood. His chair scraped the floor. The sound was loud in the quiet room — the scrape of wood on stone, the small ugly noise of departure.

His mother didn't stand. She sat at the table with the pears and the candle and the blue plates and her hands on the table's edge, and she watched him the way she'd watched him from the window two weeks ago — with an expression that was no longer readable as a single thing, that had become a landscape, a terrain of feeling too complex for a single word. Pride. Loss. Fear. Love. The fierce animal certainty that her son was hers and the equally fierce knowledge that he wasn't, not anymore, not in the way that mattered, not in the way that put him at her table on an evening when the lamb was perfect and the candle was lit and the house smelled like everything she'd built for him.

"Maren," he said. "I'll talk to Dorenne about Threadneedle. The deep-reinforcement option."

"Sure." His sister was clearing plates. Hands steady, movements efficient. The practiced choreography of a woman who knew how to perform normalcy — who had been performing normalcy her entire life, who had built herself into the person who didn't need things and didn't cause problems and didn't ask her brother to stay for ten more minutes because the asking would be a need and needs were not something Maren Torren allowed herself.

"Thank you for dinner, Ma."

"Be safe," his mother said. Not *come back soon*. Not *we miss you*. Be safe. The words of a protector — the only words she had left, the last remnant of her purpose, the thing she could still give him even though he didn't need protection anymore, even though the thing she'd spent twenty-three years protecting him from — the world's indifference to his weakness — no longer existed. Be safe. As though safety were something the most powerful person in Solathis required. As though the word could bridge the distance between a mother at a table and a son already climbing stairs in his mind.

Ash hugged his father at the door. His father held on. Held on for a beat longer than a hug should last — one extra second, two, the grip firm and warm and communicating something that words couldn't, something that lived in the pressure of his arms and the steadiness of his breathing and the particular way his hand rested on the back of Ash's neck, the way it had rested there when Ash was small and frightened and needed to know that someone larger was holding on.

"I'm proud of you," his father said. Into his shoulder. Quiet. The way he'd said it a hundred times — at the workshop when Ash built something with his hands, at the dinner table when Ash made his mother laugh, in the dark hallway of the old house when Ash was thirteen and crying and couldn't channel and couldn't stop crying and his father had sat on the floor beside him and said *I'm proud of you* and meant it — meant it with the total, terrible sincerity of a man who knew that his pride was not enough and offered it anyway because it was everything he had.

"Thanks, Da."

He left.

The stairs rose through the tiers toward the upper city. The evening was cool, the air carrying the wood-smoke from the lower districts and the faint mineral smell of the gorges below and the cleaner, colder scent of the elder districts above. The city spread around him — a vertical landscape of lit windows and dark stone, heating lattices glowing orange in the buildings that still had them, candles flickering in the buildings that didn't. The transit shafts were dark. The walkways were quiet. The city was settling into its nightly rhythm, the rhythm of a place that had less energy than it needed and parceled what it had with increasing care.

Ash climbed. The Wellspring hummed behind his ribs. The reservoir was there — vast, warm, patient, the ocean of power that was his answer to every question, his solution to every problem, the thing that had rewritten his life from the ground up. He could feel it the way you felt your own heartbeat — constant, reliable, the baseline rhythm of his existence. The power was him. He was the power. There was no gap between the two, no daylight between Ash and the Wellspring, and the seamlessness was the seamlessness of a new identity settling into place, smooth and total, the way water fills a container and becomes its shape.

Behind him, in the house on the lower tier, his mother was putting away the blue plates. His father was banking the fire in the old stove. Maren was washing dishes with hands that had channeled all day and would channel again tomorrow and would channel a little less the year after that and a little less the year after that, the slow quiet arithmetic of a declining pool that nobody in Dorenne's office was tracking because it was ordinary, because it was happening to everyone, because it was the background condition of a world that was running down.

He didn't think about any of this. He was thinking about the Millward wall. About the stone's resonance structure. About the morning briefing and the deployment schedule and the four-month timeline and the fourteen systems at critical status.

The city needed him.

The city needed him, and the need was a river, and the river was flowing, and he was swimming with it, fast and strong and sure, and the swimming felt like purpose and the purpose felt like identity and the identity felt like the only true thing in a life that had been false for twenty-three years.

He climbed faster.

Behind him, the light in the kitchen window went dark.

He didn't see it go.
