# Chapter 2

The ramp was longer than he remembered.

Ash had walked it once before, the day Dorenne hired him, and it had seemed almost gentle then—a corridor curving up through the outer wall of the Council annex, wide enough for equipment carts, sloped at an angle designed for loads heavier than a man. Now, in the dark before dawn, with his breath making small clouds in the unheated air and his legs already warm from the forty flights below, the ramp felt like the building's way of reminding him that he was here on a technicality. The transit lifts hummed in their shafts somewhere deeper in the structure, carrying people upward on pulses of channeled force, smooth and fast and routine. He climbed.

The annex was quiet at this hour. The stone walls held the faint residual hum of old channeling—the work of architects three generations dead, their craft still holding the building together the way a song holds in a room after the singer has left. Ash's footsteps echoed. He'd left the apartment two hours before dawn, dressing in the dark by touch, two shirts and the quilted vest and the good trousers his mother had pressed the night before, laid out on the chair beside his bed with the care of a woman preparing an offering. He'd eaten standing up, cold porridge from the pot, and slipped out without waking anyone, pulling the door closed with the particular slow pressure that kept the latch from clicking.

He reached the upper level as the sky through the corridor windows was beginning to thin from black to gray. The infrastructure office was at the end of the hall, its doors closed, a strip of warm light visible at the threshold. Someone was already inside.

He stood in front of the doors for a moment. Smoothed his collar. Pressed his palms flat against his trousers to dry them.

Then he pushed through.

The office was larger than he'd expected from the hallway. A high-ceilinged chamber with tall windows along one wall, still dark at this hour, and every surface buried under the working disorder of a place handling more than it was built for. Charts pinned over other charts on the walls. Reports stacked on desks, on chairs, on a table near the window where three separate briefings' worth of documents sat with their edges curling. The room smelled like ink and stone dust and the faint metallic tang of channeling soaked into the walls by decades of concentrated work.

The woman at the main desk looked up. Not Dorenne. Younger, maybe forty, with the particular expression of someone who had been working since before the light-stones were lit and expected to keep working long after they dimmed. She had a stylus behind one ear and a report open in front of her, and she looked at Ash the way she might look at a delivery that had arrived on the wrong day.

"You're the new aide."

"Ash. Yes."

"Tessaly. I manage the schedule." She looked him over once, efficiently, the way someone inventories a tool. "Dorenne's not in yet. She comes at first light. You can start with these."

She pushed a stack of folders across the desk toward him. "Briefing summaries from last week. Read them. Don't take notes yet—just read. If you have questions, write them down and ask me later. Not during the briefings."

He took the folders. They were heavy. "Where should I—"

"There's a desk in the corner. It was Parren's." A pause, brief but specific. "The last aide."

The desk was small, tucked against the wall near the door, positioned so that whoever sat there could see the whole office without being in the way. A good desk for watching. Ash set the folders down and opened the first one.

* * *

The briefing summaries were dense and assumed he knew things he didn't.

He read them anyway. Slowly, carefully, the way he'd taught himself to read anything that mattered—pen in hand, marking passages he'd need to come back to, building the architecture of understanding one piece at a time. The reports covered the previous week's maintenance operations across four districts: crew deployments, channeling expenditure estimates, structural assessments, priority rankings. The language was technical, studded with abbreviations he didn't recognize and references to systems he'd only read about in public notices. He wrote the abbreviations down. He'd learn them.

What he did understand was the shape of the problem. Even without the technical vocabulary, the numbers told a story. The maintenance crews were understaffed. The requests outnumbered the deployments by a ratio that made his stomach tighten when he calculated it in the margin of his notebook—roughly three to one. For every building that got a crew this week, two buildings waited. And the waiting buildings weren't standing still; they were degrading, the old channeling in their bones fading a little more each day, which meant that when a crew finally reached them, the work would be harder, more expensive, more draining on pools that were already stretched.

He was partway through the third folder when the office began to fill. People arrived in ones and twos, hanging coats on hooks by the door, activating the heating lattice in the wall with casual touches as they passed—a palm pressed flat, a brief pulse, the stone warming under their hands the way it never warmed under his. Ash watched the gesture repeat itself half a dozen times in half an hour. Nobody thought about it. Nobody noticed the ease of it. The lattice was just another surface in the room, and they were just living in it, the way fish don't notice water.

He noticed. He always noticed. But today the noticing had a different quality—less the old ache of exclusion and more the focused attention of someone cataloguing a workplace. These were his colleagues now. The way they channeled the wall was information. The way they moved through the room was information. The way they greeted each other—who spoke first, who waited, who looked tired, who didn't—all of it went into the same place in his mind where stairwell patterns and district maps lived, filed automatically, waiting to be useful.

A man about his age dropped into the desk opposite Tessaly's and began sorting documents without looking up. Broad-shouldered, with the particular kind of tiredness that lives in the face of someone who's been running maintenance shifts on a pool that doesn't stretch as far as it needs to. His hands moved automatically through the papers—sorting, stamping, stacking—while his eyes stayed fixed on something inside his own head.

"That's Caelen," Tessaly said, not looking up from her own work. "District liaison, southern quarter. If he looks through you, don't take it personally. He looks through everyone before his second cup of tea."

"Noted."

"And that's Voss." She tilted her head toward a gray-haired man settling into a chair by the window with the care of someone whose joints had opinions about the cold. "Senior engineer. He's been here longer than the furniture. He'll explain things to you if you ask nicely, but only once, so listen the first time."

"Also noted."

"And that"—she glanced toward the door as it opened—"is Dorenne."

Dorenne Kharren came through the door the way weather comes through a window—he felt the change before he understood it. She wasn't rushing. Her stride was the same measured pace he remembered from the ramp: fast enough to set the rhythm, slow enough that keeping up felt like a choice. She wore the Infrastructure seal on her collar, dark fabric against the gray of her hair, and she carried a leather case under one arm that was thicker than any of the briefing folders on his desk.

Her eyes found him immediately. A nod—brief, acknowledging, carrying the particular weight of a person confirming that something they arranged has in fact materialized. Then she was past him, moving through the room, and the office organized itself around her the way iron filings organize around a magnet. Tessaly was on her feet with a schedule. Caelen looked up. Voss straightened in his chair.

"Morning. Eastern district update first—Tessaly, where are we on the Northridge heating grid?"

The day began.

* * *

Ash spent the morning carrying things.

Not metaphorically. Physically carrying things—folders from one end of the office to another, a crate of rolled charts from the storage room on the lower level (the ramp again, twice, his legs starting to feel the repetition), a stack of priority reports to the Council records office three corridors away. The work was unglamorous and constant, and he did it without complaint, because it was work, and work was what he was here for, and every trip down the ramp and back was a trip he was making as someone who belonged in this building.

Between deliveries, he sat at his desk and read. The briefing folders gave way to the current week's schedule—a document so complex it might have been a military campaign. Dorenne's days were partitioned into blocks measured in quarter-hours: briefings, inspections, Council meetings, crew reviews, district assessments. Every block was labeled with a location, a contact, a subject, and a priority ranking that used a color system he hadn't learned yet. Tessaly managed the schedule the way his father managed load-bearing walls—with an awareness of stress points and the understanding that everything was connected to everything else, and moving one block would shift six others.

He was memorizing the color system when Dorenne called him in for the first briefing.

Her office—the inner office, separated from the main room by a heavy door that someone had reinforced with channeling so old the stone around the frame had fused—was smaller than the outer chamber but no less cluttered. A broad desk. Two chairs for visitors. A window that looked east over the city, where the morning sun was just clearing the ridgeline and turning the grand districts to gold. She sat behind the desk with her leather case open and three reports spread before her, and she didn't look up when he entered.

"Sit. Listen. Don't talk unless I ask you something."

He sat. He listened.

The briefing was between Dorenne and two team leaders—a man and a woman, both middle-generation, both tired in the way the whole office seemed tired. They were discussing the weekly maintenance deployment for the central districts. The conversation moved fast, loaded with technical language and the shorthand of people who'd been having the same argument for years. He caught maybe half of it. He understood the shape of even less.

But he watched. He watched the way Dorenne listened—her eyes moving between the two team leaders with the steady tracking of someone assembling a picture from pieces that didn't quite fit. He watched the way the woman, the lead on the northern crew, leaned forward when she made a point and sat back when the man spoke, ceding ground she'd have to reclaim later. He watched the way the man—southern crew—kept returning to the same number, a figure he clearly thought was more important than the woman did, circling back to it the way you circle back to a sore tooth.

"The overlap on Thornwall," the woman said. "Sorren's crew and mine both have it on the schedule for next week. We can't both work the same section without—"

"Sorren's crew has priority," the man said. "The assessment ranked it—"

"The assessment ranked it before the Ridgewall report came in. The northern section has degraded faster than—"

Dorenne held up one hand. The room went quiet.

"Ash."

He straightened. The two team leaders looked at him with the mild confusion of people who seemed to be noticing him for the first time.

"You've been reading the briefings from last week. The Thornwall scheduling conflict—what did you see?"

His throat tightened. He'd been reading the briefings for three hours. He'd seen the conflict in the deployment schedules—it was right there in the overlap, two crews assigned to adjacent sections on the same day, which meant they'd be drawing on the same district's channeling infrastructure to power their work. Competing demands on a system that could barely handle one. But he didn't know enough to know the answer. He barely knew enough to frame the question.

"Both crews are on the same day," he said. His voice came out thinner than he wanted. "Is the conduit—can it handle both at once?"

Silence. The woman looked at the man. The man looked at the scheduling chart on Dorenne's desk.

"The conduit recovery window," the woman said slowly. She leaned forward and traced the junction diagram with her finger. "If they're both drawing on the same section, the conduit needs—what, three days between major operations?"

"At minimum," the man said. His hand was already moving to the chart, pulling it closer. "So if the northern crew shifts to the beginning of the week and we take the end..."

"The infrastructure recovers between them. They don't compete." The woman sat back. "That's been sitting in the conduit specs this whole time."

Dorenne's expression didn't change. "Work the revised timing and have it on Tessaly's desk by end of day." She turned back to her reports, and the briefing continued, and Ash sat very still in his chair and made himself breathe evenly because his hands were trembling under the desk and he didn't want anyone to see.

He hadn't solved anything. He'd asked a question—the right question, maybe, but still just a question. The team leaders had done the actual work, pulled the answer from expertise he didn't have. But his chest was doing something unfamiliar—a loosening, a release of pressure he hadn't known was there—and he sat with it for the rest of the briefing, letting it settle, not trusting it enough to name it.

* * *

The rest of the morning was harder.

Tessaly sent him to the records office for a filed assessment. "Third corridor, second left, past the archive doors."

He took the third corridor. He took the second left. The corridor branched into two passages that both looked the same—pale stone, lattice lighting, no signage—and he chose the one on the right because it seemed wider, and it led to a storage room full of empty crates. He backtracked. Took the other passage. It curved, joined a stairwell he didn't recognize, and deposited him on a landing where the air was colder and the light-stones had gone dark. He stood there for a moment, his hand on the wall, feeling the stone's silence where everyone else in this building would feel its resonance—the channeled structure humming directions into their palms, the building itself telling them where they were.

His palm felt nothing. Just stone.

He retraced his steps to the main corridor, found a woman carrying a stack of folios, and asked her where the records office was. She pointed back the way he'd come—past the archive doors, she said, not past them. He'd walked right by it. The doors were unmarked, sealed with a channeled lock that glowed faintly at the edges, the kind he couldn't feel and therefore hadn't registered as a destination.

He knocked. Someone opened. He got the assessment. He came back to the office seven minutes late, his face warm, his breathing controlled.

Tessaly didn't look up. "You got lost."

"Yes."

"Everyone gets lost the first week. The building's navigational channeling is designed for people who can feel it." She turned a page. "You'll need a different system."

An hour later, he misread a priority code. The reports used a color-and-number system—red for urgent, amber for standard, green for informational—but the codes were stamped small on the upper corner, and the stamp on the Thornwall crew assessment was smudged. He read the amber-3 as an amber-1, which changed the routing. Amber-3 went to the district liaison's desk for filing. Amber-1 went directly to Dorenne.

He carried the report to Dorenne's stack. He set it down. He walked back to his desk.

Tessaly appeared beside him forty seconds later. She held the report between two fingers, the way you hold something that's ended up where it shouldn't be.

"This is a three," she said. She pointed to the stamp. "See the tail on the number? Threes curve left. Ones don't."

He looked. The smudge resolved itself into a three. It was obvious now. It had not been obvious a minute ago.

"Where does it go?"

"Caelen's desk. District filing." She set the report down in front of him. She didn't say anything else. She went back to her desk, and Ash sat with the misrouted report and the particular heat that climbed the back of his neck when he made a mistake in front of someone whose opinion of him was still being formed. He picked up the report. He walked it to Caelen's desk. Caelen took it without looking up.

He sat through a second briefing—this one about water system maintenance in the lower districts—and understood even less than the first. The engineering terminology was a wall, and he stood on the wrong side of it, writing down words he'd have to look up later, nodding at intervals that he hoped corresponded to reasonable moments for nodding.

At midday, a visiting official arrived for a meeting with Dorenne—a Council liaison from the finance office, a lean man with the bearing of someone who believed his time was the scarcest resource in any room. Dorenne was still in a briefing. The official stood in the outer office with the barely contained energy of a person whose schedule had just been violated, and his gaze swept the room and landed on Ash with the precision of someone looking for someone to be dissatisfied at.

"I was told the Director would be available at noon. It is noon."

Ash stood. His first instinct was to apologize—the reflexive sorry of a person who'd spent years absorbing other people's discomfort as his own fault. He swallowed it.

"She's finishing a briefing on the southern district water main. Can I get you anything while you wait? Tea?"

"I don't want tea." The man's voice carried the flat edge of someone who'd been dismissed by proxy and wasn't going to accept it gracefully. "I want the meeting I was promised."

"Of course. I'll let her know you're here." He turned toward Dorenne's door, but the door was closed and the channeled seal was engaged—the faint glow at the frame that meant she'd locked it from inside, a signal he couldn't override and couldn't even knock through. He stood in front of the sealed door for a beat too long, aware of the official watching him, aware that he couldn't do the simple thing the moment required.

He turned back. "She's in a sealed briefing. I can't interrupt, but she should be out shortly—she keeps her commitments."

"Sealed." The official repeated the word as though tasting it for quality. "And you can't—"

"No." Ash said it without the usual cushioning, the joke or the deflection. Just the fact, bare and quick, before the man could finish the sentence and make it worse.

A silence. Tessaly looked up from her desk, assessing, and Ash felt the moment tipping—the official's irritation looking for somewhere to land, the sealed door behind him like an accusation.

He pulled a chair from the wall—not Dorenne's visitor chair, which was inside the inner office, but a serviceable one near the window—and positioned it where the midday light caught the view of the eastern gorge.

"This spot has the best light, if you're going to be sitting for a few minutes."

The official looked at the chair. He looked at Ash. For a moment the irritation held—the set of his jaw said he wasn't finished being angry. Then something shifted, not softened, but redirected, the energy finding a channel that wasn't confrontation. He sat down.

"Thank you," he said, and the word came out grudging and real.

When Dorenne emerged three minutes later, the official was sitting by the window looking at the gorge, and the worst of the confrontation had drained from the room. Dorenne's eyes moved from the official to Ash and then to the sealed door behind her—the door Ash couldn't have knocked on—and something moved across her face—too quick to read, gone before it fully formed. She didn't comment on it. She turned to the official and the meeting began.

* * *

In the early afternoon, during the second briefing—the one about water systems that he mostly couldn't follow—Voss mentioned the Kharren Fault.

It was a passing reference, part of a longer sentence about inspection schedules that Ash didn't fully understand. But the name snagged on something. He'd seen it in the briefing folders that morning, a reference to seismic reinforcement beneath the eastern districts, and the word *fault* had stayed with him the way certain words did—the ones that carried weight you couldn't yet measure.

After the briefing, he went back to his desk and found the reference in the folder. Not a full report—a summary, a few paragraphs in dense engineering language, attached to a quarterly assessment of the city's deep-structure maintenance. He understood maybe a third of it. The Kharren Fault was a seismic fracture in the bedrock beneath Solathis, stabilized generations ago by channelers whose pools dwarfed anything alive today. The stabilization was maintained by quarterly crews. The system had held for over a century.

The numbers attached to the summary were mostly opaque to him—channeling coefficients, resonance decay rates, terms he'd need weeks to learn. But two columns were simple enough: a projected maintenance requirement, and an actual maintenance capacity. The projected number held steady. The actual number went down, year by year, entry by entry.

He didn't have the training to know what the gap between those numbers meant. He didn't know if it was urgent or routine, whether someone in this office was already tracking it, whether the numbers were cause for concern or the normal background noise of a city that was declining everywhere. But the gap bothered him. It sat in his mind the way the three-to-one maintenance ratio had sat there that morning—a shape that felt wrong, even if he couldn't yet say why.

He wrote a question in his notebook: *Kharren Fault—is the gap between projected and actual getting wider? Who tracks this?* He closed the notebook and put it in his pocket and went back to carrying folders.

* * *

The day ended the way days in offices end—not with a clean edge but with a gradual thinning, people leaving in ones and twos, the light-stones dimming as the last channeler to leave stopped maintaining them. Ash stayed until Dorenne left, which was well after everyone else, and he used the time to re-read the briefing folders he'd gotten through that morning, filling in the gaps in his understanding, looking up the abbreviations he'd written down, building the map.

Dorenne paused at his desk on her way out. She looked at the stack of folders, then at the notebook open beside them, then at him.

"You're still here."

"I wanted to finish the briefings."

"They'll be here tomorrow."

"I know. I wanted to have them finished before tomorrow."

Something moved across her face—an assessment, quick and practiced, the way she assessed a structural report or a crew's efficiency numbers. Reading him the way she read everything: for what it could do, for what it was worth, for where it fit.

"First day," she said. It wasn't a question.

"First day."

"Go home, Ash. Eat something. Sleep. The ramp will be here in the morning."

She left. He sat for another minute in the empty office, in the dimming light, with the charts on the walls and the reports on every surface and the faint hum of old channeling in the stone around him. His legs ached from the ramp. His eyes ached from the reading. His hand, when he flexed it, was cramped from writing.

He gathered his things and headed for the ramp, and then the stairs, and then the long walk down through a city that was cooling in the evening air, its districts dimming from the edges inward the way they always did—the elder heights holding their warmth, the middle districts fading, the lower districts letting go first.

* * *

Ryn was sitting on the wall when he got there.

The wall ran along the edge of a terrace in their home district, overlooking the gorge where the river cut through the plateau's eastern face. It was a low wall, knee-high, made of the same pale stone as everything else in Solathis, and it had been their spot since they were fourteen—the place they came when the world was too much or too little, the place that belonged to the time before either of them had to be anything in particular. The stone was cold. It was always cold here; the gorge funneled wind up from the river, and nobody in this district had the pool to spare on warming a public wall.

She had two cups beside her. Steam rising from both.

"You look terrible," she said.

"Thank you. I worked hard on it."

He dropped onto the wall beside her, and the cold of the stone came up through his trousers like a greeting. She handed him a cup. It was warm in his hands—she'd channeled it recently, the ceramic still holding the heat of her touch. A small thing. The kind of small thing she'd been doing for him since they were children, so automatic it had its own grammar.

"Tell me," she said.

He told her. Not the performance version—not the version he'd give his mother, all brightness and triumph, every difficulty polished smooth. He told her the real version: the folders he couldn't fully understand, the corridor where he'd stood with his hand on silent stone while everyone else navigated by resonance, the priority code he'd misread and Tessaly correcting it without looking at him. The briefing where he'd sat nodding at things he couldn't follow. The scheduling conflict where he'd asked a question—just a question—and his hands had shaken afterward. The sealed door he couldn't knock on. The report on the Kharren Fault that he'd understood just enough to find unsettling.

Ryn listened the way she always listened—with her whole body angled toward him, her cup held in both hands, her eyes steady on his face. She didn't interrupt. She didn't reassure. She let him talk until the talking ran out, and then she sat with the silence for a moment, the way a person sits with someone's coat in their lap—holding it, not wearing it.

"The scheduling thing," she said. "That was good."

"It was obvious."

"It wasn't obvious to the people who've been doing the job for years. That's different from obvious."

He looked at his cup. The tea was some kind of herb—sharp, slightly bitter, the kind Ryn favored because it was cheap and grew wild on the terraces below the market district. She'd picked it herself, probably. She did things like that.

"I don't know what I'm doing," he said. It came out quieter than he intended.

"You're doing what you always do. You're watching, and you're figuring it out, and you're working harder than anyone notices." She took a sip. "You've been doing that your entire life. The only difference is someone's finally paying you for it."

He looked at her. In the evening light, with the gorge wind pulling at her hair and the steam curling between her hands, she looked exactly like what she was—a person who'd known him since before any of this, who'd watched him climb every flight of stairs in this city without once suggesting he take the lift, who'd warmed his cup a thousand times without making it mean anything more than warmth.

"How was your day?" he asked.

"I replaced a water junction in the Northridge district. The fitting was corroded and the whole line was leaking into the substructure. Took me four hours." She said this the way she said most things about her work—plainly, without emphasis, the report of a person who did practical things and didn't need them to be remarkable. "The homeowner watched me the whole time. She kept asking if I needed more tea. I think she was lonely."

"Did you have the tea?"

"Three cups. Terrible tea. She was lovely."

They sat. The gorge deepened in color below them as the light pulled back, the river at the bottom catching the last of the sky. Somewhere in the district behind them, someone was channeling a heating lattice—he could feel it not as a sensation but as a change in the air, a warming at the edges. The evening sounds of a neighborhood settling into itself: doors closing, voices carrying through open windows, the distant hum of a transit lift cycling through its shaft.

"I'll buy the next round," Ash said. "There's a stall at the bottom of the terrace. The one with the lanterns."

"The one that overcharges."

"The one that has the good cider."

She looked at him, and something moved in her expression—quick, there and gone, the shadow of a recognition she chose not to speak. He had money now. Not much. But the first installment of his wages had come with the position, a small advance against the first month, and it sat in his pocket like a stone from a country he'd never visited.

"All right," she said. "The good cider."

He went to the stall. He ordered two cups. He paid without counting the coins first—without the familiar calculation of what this cost against tomorrow's breakfast, against the contribution to his parents' household, against the running tally of what he owed the world for the space he occupied in it. He just paid. The vendor handed him the cups and moved on to the next customer, and the transaction was ordinary, which was the whole point, which was everything.

He brought the cups back. Ryn took hers and wrapped both hands around it, and her fingers were cold because she'd been sitting on the wall waiting for him, and the cider was warm, and the warmth moved from the cup into her hands into the air between them.

She didn't comment on the fact that he'd paid. She didn't say anything about what it meant—the first time he'd bought something without the weight of that calculation behind it. But her eyes stayed on his face for a beat longer than usual, and the corner of her mouth moved in a way that wasn't quite a smile but lived in the same country as one. She saw it. She always saw everything.

"So," she said. "Day one."

"Day one."

"How does it feel?"

He thought about it. The honest answer was complicated—layered with the exhaustion and the uncertainty and the ache in his legs and the briefings he hadn't understood and the corridors he'd gotten lost in. But underneath all of that, under the day's full weight of fumbling and watching and carrying and trying, there was something else. A loosening he couldn't quite name—the same one he'd felt after the briefing, when his hands were shaking and the question had turned out to be the right one.

"It feels like something I could get good at," he said.

Ryn nodded. She didn't say *you will be*. She didn't tell him it would get easier, or that he was brilliant, or that the office was lucky to have him. She just nodded, and drank her cider, and leaned back on her hands so that the gorge wind caught her full in the face and she closed her eyes against it with the expression of a person who was exactly where she wanted to be.

They sat on the wall until the cider was gone and the air was too cold for sitting. They talked about nothing important—a building in her district that was settling, a vendor in the market who'd been shortchanging on flour, whether the transit lift at the end of their street would ever run on time again. The conversation had the texture of a garment worn soft by years of washing: easy, shapeless, fitting them both without effort.

And underneath it—underneath the talk and the cider and the cold stone and the evening pulling dark across the gorge—something hummed. Not spoken. Not examined. The particular quality of warmth that exists between two people who have known each other long enough to stop performing, in a world where intimacy was built on shared channeling, on the vulnerability of letting another person feel your pool, on the domestic magic of warming shared spaces together. He couldn't offer that. He had never been able to offer that. And she had never once made it feel like something missing.

Tonight he didn't have to be cheerful. The scaffolding that held up his public self—the smiles, the ease, the jokes that covered the grinding exhaustion of living by hand in a world built for magic—none of it was deployed. Not because he was letting it down, the way he did on stairwell landings when no one was watching. Because he didn't need it. The feeling in his chest wasn't a performance he was maintaining. It was just there—small, real, slightly bewildering in its persistence.

Ryn opened her eyes and looked at him, and he was already looking at her, and for a moment neither of them said anything, and the moment was warm, and the warmth had nothing to do with channeling.

"Same time tomorrow?" he said.

"If you survive."

"I'll survive."

"Then same time tomorrow."

She slid off the wall and landed lightly, the way she did everything—practical, efficient, no motion wasted. She raised her cup in a gesture that meant goodbye or thank you or something that lived between the two, and then she walked into the evening, her silhouette sharpening against the last light at the terrace's edge before she turned the corner and was gone.

Ash sat on the wall a little longer. The stone was cold beneath him and the air was cold around him and the cup in his hands had gone cold too, and none of it mattered as much as it usually did. He looked out over the gorge, where the river was a dark line at the bottom of the dark, and the city rose behind him in its layers of fading warmth—the elder heights still glowing, the middle districts dimming, his own district already letting go.

Tomorrow he would climb the ramp again. He would carry folders and misread codes and sit in briefings he didn't understand and try to learn the shape of a job that was bigger than he'd realized and harder than he'd expected. He would get things wrong. He would get some things right. He would come home tired, and his legs would ache, and the office would still be there the next morning, waiting for him, the way it waited for everyone who worked there—not because he was special, but because he showed up.

He finished the last cold mouthful of cider and climbed down from the wall and walked home through the darkening streets, and his legs knew the way, and the city cooled around him, and in his chest the small warm thing kept its shape against the cold, unreasonable and real, and he let it stay.
