# Chapter 5

The basement of the Hall of Infrastructure smelled like chalk and old stone and the faint copper tang of instruments that had been calibrated too many times.

Ash followed Dorenne down a staircase he hadn't known existed — narrow, tucked behind a supply closet on the ground floor, the steps worn smooth in the center by decades of feet. The walls were unfinished stone down here, rough-cut, without the polished facing that covered the upper floors. No one had bothered to make this place presentable. It was a working space, functional and forgotten, the kind of room that existed because someone needed to put things somewhere and the basement was where things went.

Three days. Three days since the apartment. Three days since the beam and the wall and his mother's blood on the rubble and the power that had come through him like a river breaking its banks.

His mother was healing. The ribs were mending — the healers had done two sessions, careful work, the kind that cost them a significant portion of their daily trickle and left them with the hollow-eyed fatigue he recognized from watching healers work on other people his entire life. Her leg was slower. The nerve damage required a specialist, someone from the elder generation whose understanding of biological systems went deeper than accelerating what the body already wanted to do. Dorenne had arranged it. Of course she had.

The temporary housing was a flat in a mid-district building — two bedrooms, a working heating lattice, water that ran warm from the tap without anyone channeling it. His father slept in a chair beside his mother's bed. Maren came every morning and every evening, her schedule reorganized around the family's new geography with the silent efficiency she brought to everything. Nobody talked about the apartment. Nobody talked about what Ash had done. The ruins of the building sat in the lower-middle band like a broken tooth, and the silence around it was so complete it had its own weight.

Dorenne stopped at a door at the bottom of the stairs. Iron-bound, heavy, with a channeling lock that she pressed her palm to without breaking stride. The lock clicked. The door swung inward.

"The assessment chamber," she said. "Standard procedure for any new channeling manifestation. Usually children — twelve, thirteen years old, coming into their first awareness."

She said this without emphasis, but the word *children* sat in the air between them with a specific gravity that Ash felt in his sternum.

"The examiners are expecting us. I'll be in the observation alcove." She looked at him — a quick, assessing look, the kind she gave to assessment reports before signing off on them. "Follow their instructions. Don't volunteer anything about the activation itself. If they ask questions outside the scope of the calibration, refer them to me."

"What's the scope of the calibration?"

"They'll tell you." She held the door. "Go ahead."

* * *

The room was larger than the staircase suggested. Low-ceilinged but wide, the stone floor scored with lines and circles that had been cut into the surface — geometric patterns, some filled with a metalite substance that caught the lamplight, others worn nearly invisible by years of feet and scuffed chairs. Along the far wall, a wooden bench held a row of instruments Ash couldn't name: glass cylinders mounted on brass stands, a set of nested metal rings suspended from a frame, something that looked like an hourglass filled with fine blue sand. The lamps were channeled — steady, even light, the kind that came from lattice strips maintained by the building's infrastructure team.

Three people sat at a table near the instruments. Two men and a woman, all middle-aged, all holding clipboards. The woman had a pen behind her ear and an expression that suggested she'd been doing this job long enough to have opinions about it and none of them were enthusiastic. One of the men was eating an apple. He didn't stop when Ash came in.

"Name?" the woman said.

"Ash. Ashlyn Carro."

She wrote it down. "Age?"

"Twenty-three."

"Previous channeling assessment?"

"None."

She looked up. The pen paused over the clipboard. "None? You've never been assessed?"

"I couldn't channel. Until three days ago, I couldn't—" He stopped. The sentence had nowhere to go that didn't feel like standing naked in a room full of strangers. "No. No previous assessment."

She wrote something. The man with the apple took another bite. The other man — thin, balding, with the cautious posture of someone who'd spent his career avoiding attention — studied Ash with an interest that seemed involuntary, as if he'd been told something about this case and was trying to reconcile the information with the young man standing in front of him.

"Standard trickle calibration," the woman said. "Four stages. We'll walk you through each one. Questions at the end." She set the clipboard down and stood. "First instrument is the calibration stone. This way."

She led him to the bench. The calibration stone sat in the center of the instrument row — a block of pale mineral, roughly the size of a man's fist, mounted on a brass cradle. Its surface was smooth, almost translucent, with a faint milky luminescence that came from within the crystal structure itself. Along the side of the cradle, a graduated scale was etched into the brass: numbers one through ten, each paired with a color swatch painted on a small tile. White at one. Pale blue at two. Deeper blue, green, amber, through to a deep red at ten.

"The stone responds to channeled energy," the woman said. "Place your palm on it and channel. Gently. The mineral lattice will change color to indicate the strength of your trickle on a one-to-ten scale. Most first-timers register between two and four. Children, usually lower." She glanced at her clipboard. "Given your age and the — circumstances — we're expecting something in the upper range. Five or six, maybe. Take your time."

Ash looked at the stone. It sat on its cradle with the patient stillness of an object that had been touched by hundreds of hands and had never been surprised by any of them.

He placed his palm on it.

The channeling was — he didn't know what channeling was. He'd watched other people do it his entire life, watched the focused stillness, the slight tension in the jaw, the way hands pressed flat to a surface and held. He'd mimicked the gesture a thousand times in private, pressing his palms to walls and stones and table surfaces, waiting for the response that never came. Now the response was there. The power was a pressure behind his ribs, a fullness in his blood, and when his hand touched the stone, it moved — not because he directed it, not because he understood what he was doing, but because the stone was a channel and the pressure was enormous and contact was all it took.

The stone turned white. Then blue. Then green.

The color changes happened so fast they were almost simultaneous — a ripple of transformation sweeping through the mineral lattice from the point of contact outward, each color blooming and being overtaken by the next. Amber. Orange. Red. Deep red, the darkest swatch on the scale, the ten — the theoretical maximum that the examiner had not even mentioned as a possibility.

The stone went past red.

It went somewhere beyond the scale — a color Ash had never seen in mineral, a deep violet that pulsed from within the crystal structure with a light that cast shadows on the wall behind the bench. The brass cradle rattled against the tabletop. The stone was vibrating — not with sound but with the force of energy being pushed through a structure that was not built for this much.

The crack was sharp. A single, clean line split the stone from crown to base, and the two halves fell apart on the cradle with a sound like a cup dropped on tile. The violet light guttered and died. A wisp of heat rose from the broken halves — waste energy, the bleed-off of channeling pushed through mineral at a rate the lattice couldn't convert.

Ash pulled his hand back.

The room was very quiet.

The woman with the clipboard was standing three feet away. Her pen had stopped moving at some point during the color progression and had not resumed. The man with the apple was holding it an inch from his mouth, mid-bite, frozen. The thin man had both hands flat on the table and was leaning forward with an expression that Ash could only describe as the face of someone watching a mechanism do something it had never been designed to do.

"I'm sorry," Ash said. "I didn't mean to—"

"Don't apologize," the woman said. Her voice was careful. Measured. The voice of a person recalibrating in real time. She picked up one half of the stone and turned it in her fingers. The break was clean — crystalline, the internal structure exposed like a cross-section in a geology text. She set it down. She looked at her colleagues. A conversation passed between them in the language of people who had worked together long enough to communicate in glances: the man with the apple set it down. The thin man straightened his papers. The woman picked up her clipboard.

"Second instrument," she said.

* * *

The second test was the field rings — the set of nested metal rings suspended from the frame on the bench. The examiner explained the procedure with the same brisk efficiency, though something had shifted in her tone. A layer of routine had been peeled away. Beneath it was attention.

"Channel into the inner ring. Hold the energy steady. The rings are calibrated to measure field stability — how evenly you can sustain a channeled output over time. We're looking for thirty seconds of stable field. The instruments" — she indicated a glass cylinder beside the frame, filled with a liquid that shifted color in response to the rings' output — "will record the readings."

Ash reached for the inner ring. It was brass, cool to the touch, smaller than his wrist. He wrapped his fingers around it and pushed.

The power moved. Again — not with precision, not with any conscious direction, but with the sheer force of a reservoir that seemed to have no interest in moderation. The ring hummed. Then all the rings hummed. The nested set vibrated against each other, the metal singing with a frequency that climbed until it was less a sound than a pressure in the teeth.

The liquid in the glass cylinder surged upward and hit the stopper at the top. It stayed there.

"Hold it steady," the woman said. She was watching the cylinder. "Just — maintain what you're doing."

He tried. He was doing the same thing he'd been doing, which was nothing, which was everything — he was touching the ring and the power was moving and he had no way to adjust what he couldn't describe. The liquid stayed pegged. The rings sang. Thirty seconds passed, and then a minute, and the readings didn't change because they'd maxed out the instant he'd made contact and there was nowhere higher for them to go.

The woman set her clipboard on the table. She excused herself. She walked out of the room without explaining where she was going.

The man with the apple — he'd abandoned it entirely now, the fruit sitting on the table with one bite taken, browning — cleared his throat. "That was, ah. That was good."

The thin man said nothing. He was writing. He'd been writing since the stone cracked, small, fast notes in a hand too tight for Ash to read from where he stood.

Ash let go of the ring. The humming faded. The liquid in the cylinder dropped, slowly, settling back to its resting level with the reluctance of something that had been forced to a height it found uncomfortable.

His hand was warm. Not painfully warm — just warm, the way his skin had been warm since the apartment, a persistent radiant heat that came from somewhere inside and couldn't be explained by the temperature of the room. He flexed his fingers. The warmth was steady, constant, like a pulse he couldn't turn off.

The woman came back. With her was an older woman — sixty, maybe sixty-five, with close-cropped gray hair and the particular bearing of someone who had spent decades in rooms where knowledge was currency and she held more of it than anyone else. She wore no insignia, carried no clipboard. Her hands were empty and her eyes were sharp and she looked at Ash the way Haran looked at a mechanism he hadn't seen before: with an interest that was pure, technical, and entirely unconcerned with his comfort.

"This is Scholar Veradenne," the examiner said. "She's the Hall's senior theorist on channeling mechanics."

Scholar Veradenne didn't offer her hand. She walked past Ash to the bench, picked up one half of the broken calibration stone, and held it to the light. She turned it. Set it down. Picked up the other half. Turned it. Set it down.

Then she looked at the glass cylinder, still settling.

Then she looked at Ash.

"Do that again," she said.

* * *

The tests multiplied.

Scholar Veradenne produced instruments from a locked cabinet in the corner of the room — instruments the standard examiners had never touched, judging by the dust on the cases. A resonance plate: a disc of hammered copper that vibrated at frequencies corresponding to the channeler's output, producing visible standing-wave patterns in a thin layer of sand spread across its surface. A thermal gradient array: a row of metal rods of varying composition, designed to measure waste heat dispersion. A force target: a stone block on a calibrated spring mount, marked with lines indicating displacement in precise increments.

Each test was designed for a range. Each test had a range Ash exceeded.

The resonance plate: he was supposed to produce a simple standing-wave pattern — concentric circles, maybe a star shape if his output was particularly even. His touch scattered the sand off the plate entirely. The copper disc bent upward at the edges, warped by the vibration, and the sand ended up on the floor, the wall, and in the thin man's hair.

"I'm sorry," Ash said. "I can try—"

"Don't try anything differently," Veradenne said. She was on her knees, collecting sand from the floor. Not annoyed. Fascinated. "Just do what you're doing."

"I'm not doing anything. I'm just — touching it."

"Yes. That's what's interesting."

The thermal gradient array: he placed his hand near the first rod — near, not on, because Veradenne wanted to test his output at a distance. The first three rods changed color within seconds, the heat-sensitive paint shifting from blue through green to yellow. The fourth rod changed color. The fifth. The examiner with the apple — who had gravitated toward the far wall over the course of the last hour — took another step back. The sixth rod's paint bubbled.

"That's enough," Veradenne said.

Ash pulled his hand away. The rods cooled. The sixth rod's paint was ruined — a bubbled, blackened strip where the thermal limit had been exceeded by a margin the instrument was never designed to encounter.

"The waste heat," Veradenne murmured. She was writing in a small leather notebook, not a clipboard — her own notes, private, in a shorthand Ash couldn't decipher. "Your efficiency is approximately..."

She trailed off. Did a calculation in the margin. Did it again.

"Approximately five percent," she said. "Maybe less."

She said this to the room, not to Ash. The three examiners looked at her. The woman with the clipboard made a small sound — not a word, more like the sound of a number failing to fit inside the space she'd allocated for it.

"Five percent efficiency means ninety-five percent waste," Veradenne said. She was still writing. "An untrained channeler averages twenty percent efficiency. This is worse. Significantly worse. You are channeling with less precision than a five-year-old."

The words landed in Ash's chest like stones dropped into water. His jaw tightened.

"But the five percent you are delivering," Veradenne continued, still writing, still not looking at him, "exceeds the total output of anyone I have ever tested." She looked up. "That includes three elders and a theoretical peak model I built last year that I believed could not exist in practice." She closed the notebook. "The force is there. The control is not. You are heating the entire room when you should be heating a cup."

The room was warmer. Ash noticed it now — had been noticing it, peripherally, for the last twenty minutes. The ambient temperature had climbed steadily with each test. The examiners had shed their outer layers. The thin man's forehead was damp. The waste heat from Ash's channeling — the ninety-five percent that wasn't going where it was supposed to go — had been bleeding into the stone walls and the air and the instruments themselves, warming everything indiscriminately, turning the basement chamber into something approaching uncomfortable.

"Sorry," Ash said again.

Veradenne studied him. The assessment was clinical, thorough, and utterly devoid of the social performance that usually accompanied human interaction. She looked at him the way she'd looked at the broken calibration stone — with interest in the mechanism, not the person.

"Don't apologize for magnitude," she said. "Apologize for accuracy. One is a gift. The other is your responsibility." She opened the locked cabinet again. "There is one more test I'd like to run."

* * *

The force target was the simplest instrument in the room. A block of dense stone — granite, Ash guessed, from the grain and the weight of it — mounted on a spring-loaded platform. Push the stone with channeled force, and the spring compressed, and the displacement was readable on a graduated scale etched into the mount. The scale went from zero to a hundred units. Ash didn't know what the units represented.

"Standard output for a full-trickle young adult is eight to twelve units," Veradenne said. She stood beside the target with her notebook open, her pen ready. "A strong elder might register thirty. The scale goes to a hundred because the instrument was built with margins, not because anyone was expected to reach that range."

Ash stood three paces from the target. The stone sat on its platform with the solidity of something that had been pushed by hundreds of hands and had never moved more than an inch.

"Channel force into the stone. Don't touch it — project from your hands. Distance channeling is less efficient, but I want to see what happens without direct contact."

He lifted his hands. Palms out, facing the stone. He pushed.

The stone didn't move. It vanished.

Not literally — it launched off the platform with a force that exceeded the spring's capacity, tore the mounting bolts from the base, and hit the far wall of the chamber hard enough to crack the stone facing. The spring, decompressed beyond its design limit, sang a high, warbling note and then snapped. Pieces of the mount scattered across the floor. The granite block bounced off the wall, skidded across the scored stone floor, and came to rest against the table leg where the three examiners had been sitting.

They were no longer sitting. They were standing, pressed against the wall behind the table, their clipboards held in front of them with the instinctive defensiveness of people shielding themselves with whatever was closest.

The silence was long.

"I can pay for that," Ash said.

Veradenne hadn't moved. She stood beside the empty platform mount — the bolts sheared clean, the spring a coiled ruin on the floor — and she was writing in her notebook. She wrote for a long time.

Ash's chest was doing something complicated. The power hummed in his blood, steady and enormous, and the room was warm and the stone was across the floor and the examiners were staring at him and the humor of it — the absurdity of a man who had spent twenty-three years unable to warm a cup of water now accidentally demolishing institutional testing equipment — rose in his throat like a laugh he didn't quite release.

He'd broken the stone. He'd maxed the rings. He'd melted the paint. He'd launched the target through its own mounting.

And the power felt exactly the same as it had when he walked in. Not diminished. Not drawn down. The pressure behind his ribs was steady, oceanic, unchanged by anything he'd expended. Like a lake that didn't notice a cupped handful taken from its surface.

*Is this what it feels like?* he thought. *Is this what everyone feels, all the time?*

No. He knew the answer. He'd watched channelers his entire life — watched them ration, conserve, calculate the cost of every application. He'd watched Maren parse her trickle like a household budget, apportioning fractions to tasks that Ash had just accomplished with a force that broke the instruments designed to measure it. He'd watched his mother spend half her daily energy warming the stove and arrive at evening with nothing left.

This wasn't what they felt.

This was something else.

* * *

Veradenne spoke to the examiners in a low voice near the door. Ash stood beside the ruined force target and waited, his hands in his pockets because he didn't know what else to do with them. They were warm. They were always warm now, and the warmth was steady, unremarkable from the inside, impossible to ignore from the outside. When he'd helped his mother sit up yesterday — gently, carefully, his hands under her shoulders — she'd closed her eyes and said, "Like a heating stone." She'd meant it as wonder. He'd heard it as something he couldn't name.

The thin man left. The man with the apple — he'd retrieved it from the table, as if normalcy could be restored through snacking — followed. The woman with the clipboard spoke briefly to Dorenne in the observation alcove. Ash couldn't hear what was said, but he could see Dorenne's face through the window: composed, attentive, making notes of her own on a small pad. Not surprised. She'd felt the activation. She knew the scale, at least roughly. This testing was confirmation, not discovery — for her.

For the examiners, it was something else entirely.

Veradenne approached him. She'd closed her notebook and placed it in the pocket of her overcoat, and her hands were clasped behind her back in the posture of a scholar approaching a conclusion she'd built from evidence and was now preparing to deliver.

"Sit down," she said.

There was one chair left at the table. He sat. She remained standing, which put her eyes slightly above his, and the geometry of it — the standing authority, the seated subject — was so precisely the arrangement of every institutional interaction he'd ever endured that his shoulders tightened without his permission.

"Your reservoir," she said, "is not measurable with any instrument in this facility."

He waited.

"The calibration stone is rated for a range that encompasses every recorded trickle in the current population, including the strongest living elders. You exceeded its maximum within the first second of contact. The field-stability instrument pegged at maximum and stayed there for the duration of your channeling, which suggests that your sustained output exceeds the instrument's ceiling by an unknown margin. The force target — you saw what happened to the force target."

"I saw."

"The thermal gradient array suggests your waste-heat profile is consistent with an untrained channeler operating at approximately five percent efficiency. This is poor. Historically poor. It means that for every unit of energy you direct toward a task, nineteen units are lost as ambient heat." She paused. "In a normal channeler, this would be crippling. A young adult operating at five percent efficiency would burn through their daily trickle heating a single room and accomplish nothing of use."

She stopped. Her eyes were steady on his, and the assessment in them was not unkind but was entirely without softness. This was a person who worked in data, in measurement, in the language of quantifiable phenomena, and what she was describing was a phenomenon that had broken every quantification she could apply to it.

"In your case," she said, "five percent of what you carry appears to be more than most people carry in total."

The room was very quiet. Somewhere above them, the Hall of Infrastructure went about its morning — staff arriving, reports filing, Tessaly's brisk voice carrying down a corridor. Down here, in the basement, a woman Ash had never met was telling him something that didn't fit inside any shape he knew.

"How much?" he asked. His voice was steady. He was surprised by how steady it was. "How much am I carrying?"

"I don't know." She said this without embarrassment. "The instruments can't measure it. They can tell me you exceed their maximums, but they can't tell me by how much. The analogy I keep arriving at" — she paused, and something shifted in her voice, a scholar reaching for precision in a place where precision had failed her — "is depth. A trickle is a surface phenomenon. A daily allotment drawn from a shallow well. What you have is not a trickle. It's a wellspring — a deep source that feeds from below. The surface is what everyone else has. You have what's underneath."

*Wellspring.* The word landed in the room and stayed. It had the weight of something coined in the moment — not rehearsed, not borrowed, just the right word arriving at the right time because the person speaking it had spent decades developing the vocabulary to recognize what she was seeing.

Veradenne seemed to hear the word settle. She nodded, once, to herself.

"I'll use that in my report," she said. "Wellspring. It's more accurate than anything else I've been calling it."

Ash sat with the word. It fit. It fit the way the power felt inside him — not a ration, not a budget, not the careful trickle that everyone he loved spent their lives managing. A source. Something deep and continuous, something that pressed upward against the container of his body with a force that had nowhere to go but out.

"The tests you ran," he said. "All of them. How much of the — how much of the wellspring did they use?"

Veradenne looked at him. The question had landed somewhere interesting, judging by the sharpness of her attention.

"Given your waste-heat profile, I'd estimate the total energy expended across all tests — including the force target — at roughly the equivalent of a strong elder's full daily trickle. Perhaps more."

He felt the reservoir. It was right there — the pressure, the fullness, the oceanic weight of it. He felt it the way you feel your own heartbeat when you press your fingers to your wrist. Present. Unchanged.

"I don't feel any different," he said. "I don't feel like I spent anything."

She nodded. "That's consistent with a reservoir of significant depth. If the well is deep enough, a full day's expenditure at the rate you've been demonstrating might be — functionally — a rounding error."

Something expanded in his chest. Not the power — something else, something that had been compressed inside him for twenty-three years and was now, tentatively, allowing itself to take up space. A lightness he didn't trust.

"Can you tell me," he said, and the question was harder than the others, harder than *how much* or *how deep*, the question that had been sitting in his throat since the apartment, since the beam, since the moment the power broke through and he became something he didn't recognize, "does it refill?"

Veradenne's expression didn't change. But something behind it shifted — a door closing, gently, on a room she didn't want him to enter.

"I don't know," she said.

"But trickles refill. Everyone's trickle refills overnight."

"Everyone's trickle is drawn from the planetary reservoir through a personal channel. The trickle depletes during the day and replenishes during sleep, yes. But a trickle is a daily allotment — a fixed draw rate from a larger source. What you have doesn't appear to follow the same pattern."

He waited. The door was closed but he could feel the room behind it, the space where the answer lived.

"The working assumption," Veradenne said, and her voice had shifted into a register he recognized from Dorenne's most careful briefings — the tone of someone delivering information they wished they could frame differently, "is that what you carry is a fixed reservoir. A sealed well. Your personal channel to the planetary source may be different from a standard trickle — we don't know, nobody has seen this before — but the most consistent model, given what we know about channeling mechanics, is that you are carrying a large but finite store. Not renewable. Not replenishing. Just... vastly larger than anything on record."

The words arranged themselves in the air. *Fixed. Sealed. Finite. Not renewable. Not replenishing.*

*Vastly larger.*

He heard them all. He understood them all. They sat in his mind like objects on a shelf — each one visible, each one comprehensible, each one carrying a specific weight and a specific implication.

He picked up *vastly larger*. He held it. He turned it.

The others stayed on the shelf.

"But you're not certain," he said.

"No. I'm not certain about any of this. Nobody has ever seen a wellspring before. The model I'm describing is extrapolation from known mechanics applied to an unknown phenomenon. It could be wrong. You might replenish. You might draw from a deeper source. You might operate on principles we haven't identified." She looked at him with the careful honesty of a scholar who understood the difference between what she knew and what she guessed. "I am giving you the best framework I have. It is incomplete."

"Okay," he said.

He heard himself say it — the easy, accommodating *okay* that had been his default response to institutional information his entire life. The sound of a person accepting what he was told. The sound of a person filing something away.

*Vastly larger.*

The ocean didn't need a bottom. That was the thing about an ocean — it was big enough that the question of depth became abstract, theoretical, a problem for someone with instruments that could reach that far. You didn't stand on the shore of an ocean and wonder about the bottom. You stood on the shore and felt the immensity of it and the bottom didn't matter because the surface was enough, the surface was more than enough, the surface was everything.

He was standing on the shore.

Behind him, on the shelf, *fixed* and *finite* and *not renewable* sat where he'd left them. Small words. Careful words. The words of a woman who measured things and understood limits and had the professional integrity to describe uncertainty as uncertainty instead of dressing it up as knowledge.

He would come back for those words later. He would pick them up and turn them and understand what they meant.

Later. Not now.

Now, the ocean.

* * *

Dorenne was waiting in the corridor outside the assessment chamber. She'd been talking with the woman examiner — clipboards, low voices, the choreography of institutional information transfer — and she broke off when Ash emerged.

"Scholar Veradenne will file a formal report," Dorenne said. She fell into step beside him, walking toward the stairs. "I'll have a summary by evening. The key findings match what we expected — scale beyond measurement, efficiency that needs significant work, no precedent in the records."

He nodded. His body was walking up the stairs, following Dorenne's brisk pace, his legs carrying him through the motions of a person proceeding to the next item on a schedule. Inside, the word was still ringing.

*Wellspring.*

"The examiners will need to be managed," Dorenne was saying. "They've signed confidentiality agreements as standard procedure, but Scholar Veradenne will want to publish. I'll need to coordinate with her on timing — we control the narrative, not the academic cycle."

"She called it a wellspring," Ash said.

Dorenne paused on the stairs. Just a half-step, a hitch in her rhythm. Then she continued.

"A wellspring," she repeated. The word moved through her voice the way light moves through a prism — entering as one thing, emerging as something broken into its component parts, each one useful, each one manageable. "That's good. That's — we can work with that." She looked at him. The warmth was there, genuine, threaded through the calculation. "How are you feeling?"

The question was real. He could hear it — the part of Dorenne that had taken him on when nobody else would, the part that remembered him as a young man with no magic and a smile that worked too hard, the part that had watched him nearly destroy a building three days ago and had responded by arranging healers and housing and a testing schedule because arranging things was how she cared.

"I feel the same," he said. "After everything they tested — the stone, the rings, the target I broke — I feel exactly the same. Like nothing was used."

Dorenne stopped on the landing. She turned to face him fully, and the stairwell was narrow enough that they were close — close enough for him to see the fine lines at the corners of her eyes, the way her jaw held a tension she probably didn't know was visible.

"That's remarkable," she said.

"Veradenne said it might be fixed. A finite reservoir."

"She told me."

"She also said she's not certain."

Something moved behind Dorenne's eyes. Not the calculation — something older, something that predated the strategy and the management and the institutional reflexes. Something that looked, for a moment, like a woman standing in front of a problem she genuinely didn't know how to solve.

"Nobody is certain," she said. "That's what we're working with."

She held his gaze for a beat longer than necessary. Then she turned and continued up the stairs, and her voice resumed its professional cadence, and the moment folded itself into the space between them where things that mattered too much were stored without being named.

"I'm arranging a meeting with the Council's resource committee for end of week. They'll want a briefing. Before that, we need to establish a baseline — regular testing, controlled conditions, documented. Veradenne's framework is a start. We build on it."

"A baseline for what?"

"For understanding what you can do. And what it costs." She reached the top of the stairs and held the door. "Knowledge is management, Ash. We can't use what we don't understand."

He stepped through the door into the ground-floor corridor. The Hall of Infrastructure surrounded him — high ceilings, polished stone, the morning bustle of staff moving between offices with the purposeful energy of people who kept a city running. He'd been working here for weeks. He knew the corridors, the shortcuts, the way the light came through the western windows in the mid-morning and turned the stone floors gold.

It looked different now. Not the building — the building was the same. He was different. The power sat in him like a held note, constant, enormous, and the building's stone hummed faintly with the ambient channeling of the infrastructure it supported, and for the first time he could feel that hum. Not see it, not observe it from the outside the way he'd observed channeling his entire life. Feel it. In his hands, in the soles of his feet, in the bones of his face. The building was alive with energy, and he was alive with energy, and the two were the same substance.

He walked to his desk. The aide's desk, the smallest one, pushed into the corner near the door. His blotter. His jar of pens. The surface he'd cleaned on his first morning, his hands moving across it with the diligence of someone earning his presence through effort.

He sat down. He straightened the jar. He placed his hands on the desktop.

The wood was warm where he touched it. Warmer than it should have been. The waste heat — five percent efficiency, ninety-five percent bleed, the numbers Veradenne had described with the precise disapproval of someone who valued craft — radiated from his palms into the surface, and the desktop absorbed it the way stone absorbed sunlight, passively, without effort.

He lifted his hands. Two faint marks on the blotter — darker spots where the heat had been concentrated, the ghost of his palms visible in the material like a signature he couldn't control.

*Wellspring.*

The word was his now. Not Veradenne's clinical coinage, not Dorenne's strategic asset. His. The word that described the thing inside him that had been sealed for twenty-three years and was now open, flowing, pressing against every surface he touched with a force he couldn't modulate and a scale he couldn't comprehend.

Three days ago, he'd been a man who couldn't warm a cup of water. Today, he'd cracked a calibration stone, maxed every instrument in the Hall's testing facility, launched a granite block through its own mounting, and raised the ambient temperature of a basement room by several degrees just by standing in it.

And he felt the same. The reservoir hadn't moved. The ocean was still there — vast, steady, a pressure against the inside of his skin that was becoming, moment by moment, less frightening and more familiar. Less like something that had happened to him and more like something he was.

*Vastly larger.*

The other words — *fixed, finite, not renewable* — were on the shelf where he'd left them. He could see them if he looked. He didn't look.

The morning light came through the western windows and turned the stone floors gold, and Ash sat at his desk with warm hands and a word that fit and an ocean inside him that didn't need a bottom, and for the first time in three days the guilt and the wonder stopped wrestling and settled into something that felt, however briefly, like a shape he could carry.

He picked up a pen. The ink jar was full. The deployment schedule for the week was on his desk, waiting, the same work he'd been doing before the world changed.

He started working. The pen moved. The power hummed.

Outside, in the corridor, he heard Dorenne's voice — measured, strategic, already speaking to someone about the briefing, the committee, the framework, the plan. She was building something around him. He could feel it the way you feel a house being constructed around a foundation — the weight of the walls going up, the ceiling coming closer, the structure taking shape.

He should have minded. Maybe later he would.

For now, the pen moved and the work was familiar and the power was inside him and the word was *wellspring* and the ocean was vast and the day was bright and somewhere in the Hall a scholar was writing a report that would change the language the city used to describe what he was, and the language would spread — through offices and markets and dinner tables and the careful conversations of people who hadn't decided yet what to make of him — and by the time the language settled, the word would feel as permanent as the power itself.

*Wellspring.*

He let it in.
