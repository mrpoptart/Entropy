# Chapter 8

The district smelled like charcoal and wet stone, and Ash arrived buzzing.

He couldn't help it. The press event had been three hours ago—a hall full of officials and representatives and people with good posture and better politics, and somewhere in the middle of it all, someone had pointed at him and said his name and the room had shifted the way rooms shift when the person being named is suddenly the person everyone is looking at. He'd smiled. He'd answered questions. He'd shaken hands with a grip calibrated carefully to not break anything, and he'd walked out into the afternoon with his blood still humming like a plucked string.

The Wellspring didn't care that he was trying to walk normally. It didn't care that he was moving through a neighborhood where the gutters were cracked and the heating lattices in the walls threw off an uneven warmth—too hot near the joins, cold between them, the signature of infrastructure that had been mended and mended and mended until the mendings outnumbered the original stone. The reservoir behind his ribs was full and restless, the way a held breath was restless. He wanted to *use* it. Not for anything important. Just to feel the edges of the ocean again, to know it was still there.

He turned off the main street and down the narrower lane that cut toward the workshop, and the pressure eased a little—the familiar drop in ambient noise, the way a place that didn't pretend to be grand had its own kind of quiet. The buildings here leaned slightly, each into the next, sharing load the way families shared load when nobody had extra. He'd walked this lane so many times he knew which flagstone would tip under his left boot, and he stepped over it without thinking.

Haran's workshop was at the end of a short run of buildings whose ground floors were all occupied by people who made things—a cobbler, a woman who repaired channeling objects that couldn't afford proper guild restoration, a man who sold components that had no function Ash had ever identified. Then the workshop. The shutters on the single small window were open, and warm light pushed through the gap along with the smell of fresh-cut wood and oil and a sharper mineral tang that meant someone had been working a fitting.

He pushed the door.

* * *

Inside, the disorder was the same disorder it had always been—not chaos, but the layered accumulation of work in progress, each project stacked atop or beside or behind the last without ceremony. Shelves ran the walls to the ceiling, crowded with components in various states of completion: gear trains wrapped in cloth to keep the grit out, coils of copper tubing in three diameters, lengths of pipe racked like arrows, a row of small mechanical assemblies that Ash recognized as valve bodies and did not recognize as anything finished.

Two tinkerers were working at the secondary bench—a young man Ash knew slightly, called Perren, who had a talent for precision metalwork and a tendency to hum, and a woman Ash didn't recognize who was grinding something at the bench vise with careful half-strokes. Neither of them looked up when he came in. That was how the workshop was: you arrived, you got to work, the door was where conversation happened on the way out.

Haran was at the main bench.

He had his back to the door—always, always at the bench when Ash arrived, like the man had been placed there by some prior arrangement with the universe. He was working on something large. Ash came around the corner of the shelving unit and saw it: a water pump, half-assembled, its body a cylinder of good iron about the diameter of Ash's thigh, the piston housing open, valves laid out in order beside it on a cloth. The intake and output flanges were roughed in place but not fitted yet. Beside the pump, a schematic drawn on large paper in Haran's dense, economical hand.

"Press event run long?" Haran said, without turning.

"Three hours."

"Hm."

Ash came to the bench. He looked at the pump. He knew the type—a mechanical lifter for buildings whose district water system had developed pressure drop. The kind of infrastructure problem that used to be solved with a channeler pushing energy into the mains, which used to be simple, which was becoming less simple every year as fewer channelers had enough trickle to spare and more buildings needed the boost. A mechanical pump cost more upfront and required no channeling to operate. This was the point. This was exactly the kind of thing the workshop built.

"Whose is it?"

"Tenement on Morrow Street. The district mains can't supply the upper two floors without backup."

"How long have they been without reliable water?"

Haran picked up a pipe fitting and turned it in his hands, examining the threading. "Since spring."

Ash looked at the schematic. He knew the design—he'd helped spec a similar one two years ago. His eyes moved to the pump body, the half-fitted flanges, the valve bodies laid out like a surgical field. His hands itched.

The fitting Haran was holding needed to be seated in the intake port. Ash could see it—the port's lip was slightly swollen from the threading process, the fitting's collar binding at the shoulder. Under normal circumstances, you'd use a drift and a mallet, careful work, seat it without damaging the seat. Ash looked at the port. He looked at the fitting. He reached out with a thread of the Wellspring, pushed it through the port lip, *eased*—

The fitting dropped into place with a sound like a heavy door shutting.

Haran turned it. It did not move. He turned it the other way. It did not move.

"Fused," Ash said, understanding approximately a half-second after the fact.

"Fused," Haran agreed. He set the fitting down. He looked at it with the expression of a man cataloguing information rather than forming an opinion.

* * *

The second thing was the output pipe.

It needed a bend—thirty degrees, smooth radius, nothing aggressive. The copper was annealed, soft enough to work by hand with the right form. Perren had started it, got it close, and left it for Haran to check before they went any further. Ash picked it up. The radius was almost right, one side of the curve flattening slightly where the initial bend had gone slightly fast.

He could see exactly what it needed. A tiny correction—less than five degrees, coaxing the copper back toward round.

He sent a thread through the pipe wall and pressed.

The copper straightened. Then it straightened further. Then it locked—not curved, not flexible, but rigid, the grain of the metal rearranged by a pulse that had intended to coax and delivered something considerably more emphatic. Ash held a pipe that was now essentially an iron rod with delusions of copper.

Perren looked up from the vise. Looked at the pipe. Looked at Ash. Looked back at the pipe. Went back to his work.

"I can do the correction," Ash said.

"The pipe is fine," Haran said.

"It's—I stiffened it."

"I can see that." Haran took the pipe from him. He ran his thumb along the bend, testing the new rigidity, the altered grain. He set it aside without expression and reached for the backup pipe on the rack. "The bend is correct. We'll use this for the rigid section and make the flexible connection downstream."

Ash opened his mouth. Closed it.

The thing was, it had *worked*. The pipe was correctly bent. That it was now brittle at the curve, that replacing it downstream with a flexible joint added cost and complexity, that Haran had now rerouted his design around a fitting that should have been interchangeable—these were the things Ash registered in the following silence, one by one, the way you registered a smell that had been there all along.

* * *

The third thing was the joint at the discharge flange.

This one was supposed to be removable. It was designed that way—Ash could see it in the schematic, a cleanable joint, accessible for maintenance. The pump would accumulate sediment at the discharge point, it always did, and the joint was there so someone could break it every six months and clear the housing without disassembling the full pump body.

The joint was leaking. Not badly—a seep, the ghost of a drip where the faces weren't mated cleanly. Ash saw it. He reached out with the Wellspring—just a thread, just the fine needle of it—and sealed the gap.

Permanent.

He knew it as he felt it set. The faces weren't just mated now, they were *fused*, the iron between them remade at the crystal level into a continuous surface that would hold against any pressure the pump could generate and would need a cold chisel to break open and would never, under any circumstances, allow a maintenance worker to clean the sediment housing without destroying the fitting.

He stood back. He looked at what he'd done.

The woman at the secondary bench had stopped grinding. Ash didn't look at her. He looked at the joint, sealed and permanent and structurally sound and practically useless, and then he looked at his hands.

Haran came and looked at the joint. He ran his finger along the seam. He pressed where the gap had been.

"Hm," Haran said. He reached for his notebook and made a brief annotation on the schematic—a mark at the discharge flange, a small arrow, a notation in his shorthand that Ash couldn't read.

The workshop was very quiet.

* * *

The valve seat was a precision job.

Haran had said so when he pulled it from the cloth—a brass disc, ground to flatness within tolerances that couldn't be measured by eye, meant to seat against its counterpart in the valve body with an interference fit that was simultaneously tight enough to hold pressure and light enough to allow the valve to cycle freely. Too tight and it would stick. Too loose and it would leak. The calibration was a feel thing, the kind Ash had done dozens of times before the activation, fingers learning the relationship between materials through minute adjustments.

"Give it a go," Haran said. No instruction. No setup. Just the valve body on the bench and the seat disc in Ash's hand and the implicit invitation of a man who believed you learned things by attempting them.

Ash picked up the disc. He could feel the brass between his fingers—the weight, the flatness, the slight tackiness of the ground surface. His hands knew this. He'd calibrated valve seats with screwdrivers and lapping compound and patience, checking and rechecking, making small improvements. The knowledge was in his palms.

He set the disc in the seat and pushed gently—just his hands, no channeling, the way he'd always done it.

The disc seated. He checked the fit by feel—drag, resistance, the way the disc resisted rotation without sticking. Too much drag. He lifted it, made a tiny mental adjustment to the angle of approach, set it again.

And felt the Wellspring stir.

He hadn't reached for it. But the moment his attention sharpened on the fit, the power was there—at his fingertips, against the brass, pressing into the valve body through his skin because the Wellspring had learned that focus meant action, that precision meant channeling, that the impulse to do the thing right meant an application of force. A battering ram wearing gloves and trying very hard to be careful.

The disc slipped sideways. He felt it—wrong angle, micrometers of displacement—and his hands corrected automatically and the Wellspring corrected behind them, faster, amplifying the correction into an overcorrection. The disc shifted. He steadied it. The power steadied with him, a step behind, a step too large.

He held still. The power held still.

He tried again.

The disc set. He felt the resistance—too high, the seating too aggressive. He tried to ease it back by fractions and felt the reservoir respond like someone had connected his proprioception to a waterfall. Too much. Again too much. The disc jammed—not hard, not broken, but out of true, the ground face no longer parallel to its counterpart.

He put the disc down.

He picked it up again.

On the second attempt, he got further. The disc seated cleanly, drag about right, and he held it there and tried to feel whether the fit was good without triggering another cascade. The Wellspring simmered. He held his breath. He pushed the disc fractionally—a test—and the power pushed with him, always with him, always this fraction too eager, and the disc moved two micrometers further than it had needed to and now it was seated too deep and the seating angle was wrong.

He picked up the disc again. His hands were steady. His hands had always been steady.

The brass housing was not broken. But on his third pass, trying to compensate in the other direction, he pushed too hard against the housing's rim—not with his hands but with the thing behind his hands—and felt a give that should not have been there.

He stopped. He set the disc down. He stepped back from the bench.

The housing was not ruined. The deformation was small—a slight ovaling of the rim, visible only if you knew to look. But the interference fit was gone. The valve wouldn't seal. They'd need to recut the housing.

Perren had stopped humming.

* * *

Haran looked at the housing for a long moment. Then he picked up the disc and turned it between his fingers.

"Your hands still work," he said. Not to the housing. To Ash.

Ash said nothing.

Haran set the disc down on the cloth. He moved to the bench stool and sat—unhurriedly, the movement of a man settling in, not giving up—and he picked up the housing and turned it under the light. "The power doesn't replace them. It's another tool." He paused. "You don't use a hammer on glass."

"I wasn't—I tried to be careful."

"I know. You were careful with your hands." He set the housing back on the cloth. "The power isn't listening to careful yet."

He lifted the disc. He positioned it in the housing—the same housing, the ovaled one, working with what he had. His hands moved with the unhurried certainty of a man who had done this specific task so many times that the motions had become their own language, a vocabulary of micro-adjustments that lived below conscious thought. Ash watched him work. The disc went in. Came back out. In again, fractionally different angle. A pause—Haran's fingertip resting against the disc's face, not pressing, just feeling. Then a slight rotation. A fractional push. His thumb tested the drag.

He set the disc down. He reached for the lapping compound—a tin from the shelf, old and dented and familiar. He applied a thin line to the housing seat. Disc in again. A slow circular motion, six passes, the same pressure throughout, a consistency that was neither effort nor ease but the third thing between them.

He lifted the disc. Cleaned the compound from the seat. Checked the surface with his fingertip, reading the uniformity by touch.

"There," he said. Not to Ash. Just said it.

The ovaling hadn't disappeared. But the seat was true—he'd worked with the housing's new shape instead of against it, compensated for the deformation in the angle of approach, arrived at a fit that would hold. Ash stared at it. He didn't know how. He would not have thought it possible.

"How much formal channeling training did you have?" Haran asked.

"None. I didn't—" Ash stopped. Obvious. "None."

"That's the difficulty, then." Haran set the housing aside. "Everyone else who receives this amount of power has trained for it. They've been learning control at the scale of their trickle, and the trickle grows slowly. Your hands know precision." He tapped the bench—once, a demonstration. "The power doesn't know anything yet."

* * *

What Haran showed him next was not what Ash expected.

He expected a lesson in restraint. A technique for pulling back, for damping the reservoir, for getting the Wellspring under control by pushing less of it through everything. He was braced for it—the mental exercise, the image of a valve, the deliberate withholding.

What Haran did instead was channel something.

It was a small thing. He picked up a pipe fitting from the rack—a compression fitting, threaded interior, the kind that needed careful seating—and he held it up between two fingers and let a thread of his trickle flow into the fitting's collar.

Ash felt the hair on his arms rise.

It wasn't the amount. It was the quality—the surgical exactness of it, the way Haran's trickle moved into the fitting like water finding a level, no more than needed, shaped to the task. There was no waste. No ambient warmth, no spillover, no bleed into the surrounding air. The thread went in and the thread did its work and the thread stopped.

"Micro-pulse," Haran said. "Short. Targeted. You stop before the effect completes and assess." He handed Ash the fitting. "The fit you're trying to achieve exists at the end of seven such pulses, not one." He paused. "Or forty. The number doesn't matter. What matters is that you can feel where you are after each one."

Ash took the fitting. He reached for the next fitting on the rack—a matching piece. He held them together, the way you'd mate a compression fitting, and tried to send a thread—a small thread, a micro-thread—into the collar.

What came out was not a thread.

The heat flare was brief—a pulse of warmth that radiated outward from his hands and made the nearest tin on the shelf tick with thermal expansion. Not violent. But visible. The fitting in his hands was now seated—permanently, fused, the same mistake as the intake port. He'd been aiming for a fraction. He'd delivered the full thought.

"Again," Haran said.

Ash tried again. The second attempt was worse—too much conscious effort in the withholding, the reservoir pushing back against restraint with a pressure that found its way through in a single spike. Another fuse. Another permanent connection. He set it down. His palms were warm and unhelpful.

"The problem," Haran said, "is that you're trying to hold back the ocean. You can't. Not yet." He picked up a different fitting—a smaller one, lighter, less consequence if it went wrong. "Don't hold back. Send less. There is a difference."

He demonstrated. Ash felt the micro-pulse again—smaller this time, a half-second of channeling that warmed the fitting by barely perceptible degrees. The fitting wasn't mated. Nothing was fused. The purpose of the pulse was only to touch, to assess, to understand the material. A listening rather than a doing.

Ash tried.

A thread went out. Not a river—a thread. It lasted perhaps a quarter of a second before the reservoir, feeling the channel open, poured through it like something grateful for release. But a quarter of a second was more than nothing. Ash felt it before it ran away from him—the briefest tactile impression of the fitting's surface, the material's response to the energy, the shape of what he was trying to do.

Then the fitting heated. Then it fused with its neighbor.

"Better," Haran said.

"I wrecked it again."

"The fitting is worth nothing. What you felt in the first quarter-second—" Haran tapped his temple. "That's worth a great deal."

* * *

They worked for a while. Ash went through the scrap pile—Haran produced it from behind the secondary bench with the air of a man who had anticipated this need, a wooden crate of parts too worn or damaged to use in real work but still good for learning the temperature of things. Ash picked pieces up and sent micro-pulses into them. He fused many of them. He heated several of them. He managed, twice, to stop before the effect ran away—a thread, a quarter-second, a breath of contact, and then his hand going still and the reservoir pulling back from the edge like a dog recalled.

Not clean. Not controlled. But not entirely involuntary, either.

The third time he stopped in time, he looked up. Haran was watching him with an expression Ash recognized from years of work in this room—the look Haran wore when something was going roughly the way it was supposed to go, which was different from the look he wore when something was going well. Progress was not the same as success. Haran's face understood the difference.

"Your hands are the right instrument," Haran said. "Steady. Practiced. Your hands know what they're doing."

"My hands keep getting overruled."

"Yes." Haran picked up a piece from his own bench—the housing they'd set aside, the one with the ovaling, and began re-examining it. "You already know precision. You've known it for years, in your hands, in the work. You just need to teach the power what your hands already know." He set the housing back down. "That takes time."

"How much time?"

A pause. The kind Haran allowed without filling. "Longer than you want. Less than you think."

Ash went back to the scrap pile. He picked up a gear casing—old, cracked along the hub, not worth anything. He held it and sent a thread. Quarter-second. Stop.

The casing didn't fuse. The thread had touched, pulled back, returned nothing permanent.

He held it up to the light. The crack was still there. He hadn't helped the gear casing and he hadn't worsened it and he had, for one-quarter of a second, understood something about the material with a precision he hadn't had yesterday.

He set it back in the pile. He reached for the next piece.

* * *

He realized it slowly, the way you realize something that has been true all along.

He'd been watching Haran work—it was hard not to, working beside him in the same space, catching the periphery of his movements. Haran had been at the pump the whole afternoon, continuing the assembly around the problems Ash had created, designing through and past the fused fittings, the rigid pipe, the permanent joint. His hands moved at the pace of someone who didn't need to think about what his hands were doing.

And there it was. Ash saw it. He'd been looking at the wrong thing.

When Haran finished a section of the pump and set a fitting with a soft, precise click, a thread of channeling passed through his fingers. Brief. Natural. The thread calibrated the seating in the same motion as his hands—not instead of his hands, *alongside* them, the two so integrated that they were one action. When Haran picked up the schematic to consult it and then reached back without looking to find the fitting he needed, a short pulse of energy confirmed the fitting's mass and position before his fingers closed around it. Not a reach. A certainty.

Every lamp in the workshop that Haran had lit that afternoon—and he'd lit three, as the light shifted—lit with the same casual thread. No pause. No expenditure of attention. Just a hand raised and a lamp answering.

Ash had seen this as Haran conserving. Being careful. Doling out a meager trickle in miserly portions because he didn't have much to work with.

*He has a strong trickle.*

The understanding came in and rearranged everything around it the way a key rearranged a lock.

Not a weak trickle, handled efficiently. A *strong* trickle, trained to such radical precision over so many decades that efficiency had become invisible—not a choice made in each moment, but a habit so deep it had become reflex. Every small act Haran performed was the output of fifty years of practice. The warmth in the workshop was not the ambient temperature of a winter afternoon—Haran had been channeling, constantly, in micro-pulses so controlled they registered as room temperature. The lamps were not lit because the lattice happened to be working. They were lit because Haran had lit them with the part of his attention you used to breathe.

Ash stood at the bench with a gear casing in his hand and looked at Haran—really looked at him—and what he saw was a man who had spent thirty years learning to channel the way a musician spent thirty years learning to breathe: so thoroughly that the technique had become the body, and the body had forgotten what it had been before.

Not abstinence. Not restraint. Mastery wearing the clothing of ordinary.

Haran looked up from the pump. He met Ash's eyes. He read the expression on Ash's face—Ash felt the reading of it, felt how precisely Haran catalogued what he was seeing—and something passed through the old man's face in return. A small thing. A warmth that was not the warmth of the workshop. The expression of a man who had not been seen for a long time and had stopped minding, and was now minding slightly, and was letting himself.

"The schematic," Haran said, looking back down at the pump. "Bring it here. I need to show you where the discharge flange will need remounting."

Ash brought the schematic. His hands were very steady.

* * *

They worked for another hour. Ash used his hands—just his hands, the old way, the way he'd worked for years. He held pipes while Haran fitted them. He passed tools when Haran's hands were occupied. He swept metal shavings from around the vise. Small tasks. Background tasks. The kind of work that was invisible when it was done and visible only when it wasn't.

He didn't reach for the Wellspring. Not to help, not to try again, not to prove to himself that the quarter-second improvement was real. He just worked.

The pump remained half-built. It would be half-built for weeks. Haran had the threading to complete on the discharge section, and the float calibration, and the intake filter housing which was still being sourced from a supplier on the Fifth Terrace, and the fitting for the mains connection which depended on Morrow Street's building manager answering a letter he'd apparently been sitting on for two weeks. Half-built was where it was. Next week it would be somewhere further along. The week after that, further still.

Perren packed up at some point and left without ceremony. The woman Ash didn't know packed up shortly after. The workshop went quieter.

Ash watched Haran thread a fitting by hand—no channeling, just good technique, a die and a handle and the patience to let the thread cut itself rather than forcing it. The curl of metal came away clean. Haran checked the thread with his thumb, running it along the ridge, and nodded.

"Come back Thursday," he said. "Perren is going to need help with the float housing."

"I'll come back."

"Bring your hands."

Ash smiled. He couldn't help it.

He put on his coat. He gathered the nothing he'd brought with him—no tools, no materials, he'd arrived buzzing and empty-handed and that was how he would leave, except for the gear casing in his coat pocket that he'd dropped in without thinking, a piece of scrap that he intended to practice on later. Quarter-second pulses. The stopped thread. He'd do it at home until the stopping became something he trusted.

He was nearly to the door when Haran spoke.

"How much did that cost you?"

Ash turned. Haran was looking at the intake fitting—the first one, the fused one, permanent now, part of the pump's new geometry.

"The fitting?"

"All of it. The fittings. The pipe."

Ash tried to feel where the reservoir sat. It was like trying to measure the depth of an ocean by looking at the surface. "Nothing, really. I can't feel any difference."

Haran nodded. A slow nod, the kind that was less agreement than confirmation—as if the answer had completed a thought rather than introduced one. He turned back to the pump. He picked up the threading handle and began the next section.

"Good," he said. "Remember what the answer is." A pause, the length of one clean thread-turn. "You might want to check it later."

Ash waited. But Haran was threading now, the handle turning in steady quarter-rotations, and the conversation had the quality of something finished.

Ash opened the door. Cold air from the lane—charcoal and wet stone and the distant metal smell of a district where things were built rather than bought. The light was going out of the afternoon, the sky above the close-packed rooftops a pale grey fading toward dark.

"You're learning what you can do," Haran said, behind him. "Good." The threading handle turned. "Don't forget what you don't need to do."

Ash stepped into the lane. He closed the door.

He stood for a moment with his hand still on the latch. The wood was cool and unresponsive—no lattice, no channeling interface, just wood worn smooth by years of hands. He let it go.

The lane was quiet. The flagstone that would tip under his boot was three steps ahead, and he stepped over it without thinking about it, habit so old it was automatic. The reservoir behind his ribs was the same size it had always been since the activation—vast, unhurried, indifferent to what he'd done with it this afternoon.

He thought about Haran's hands on the valve disc. The six passes with the lapping compound. The feel of a ground surface read by a fingertip. The way mastery looked from the outside: unremarkable, unhurried, the same movements a novice would make, except that every movement arrived.

He put his hands in his coat pockets. The gear casing was there, the cracked hub, the scrap piece. He found it by touch without looking.

He started home.

The reservoir pulsed behind his ribs—steady, vast, patient. He'd spent nothing today that he could feel. That was the fact. The pump would take weeks. The joint was fused. The fitting was permanent. The pipe would need a flexible connection downstream that hadn't been in the original design.

He turned off the lane and into the main street. The heating lattices in the walls sent their uneven warmth into the air. He moved through it and thought about what it cost to do a thing, and what it cost to do a thing wrong, and whether those two costs were necessarily the same.

They weren't, of course.

They weren't at all the same.

He thought about that all the way home.
