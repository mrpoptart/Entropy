# Chapter 20

The workshop was dark from the street.

Ash stood at the bottom of the hill and looked up at the building—low-roofed, stone-walled, the wooden sign above the door that Haran had never replaced hanging slightly crooked the way it had hung for the entire decade Ash had known the place. The windows were shuttered. No light leaked from the seams. From out here, at this hour, it could have been abandoned.

But the chimney was warm. He couldn't see it in the dark—the night was overcast and moonless, the kind of deep autumn dark that pressed against the skin—but he could feel it. A faint thermal presence in the air above the roofline, the residual heat of a stove that had been burning low for hours. Not the stove itself—Haran's stove hadn't been lit in months, he was sure of that. The warmth was channeled. Tiny, sustained, invisible. The signature of a man who kept his workspace at a temperature that didn't cost anything to anyone who wasn't paying the closest possible attention.

Ash's legs ached. He'd walked here from the command post—downhill through the middle districts, past the darkened transit shafts and the maintenance crews working by lantern light, past the shuttered storefronts and the fading heat in the paving stones. An hour's walk, maybe more. He hadn't kept track. The numbers in his head—the ones on the papers still spread across the desk in his quarters—left no room for tracking anything as mundane as minutes.

He climbed the hill. The flagstone that always tipped was still three steps from the door, and his body stepped over it without consulting him, the way it stepped over everything familiar—the motion of a person returning to a place his feet remembered better than his mind.

He knocked.

No answer. He waited. Knocked again, harder, the sound of his knuckles against the wood sharp in the quiet street. From somewhere deeper in the building, a chair scraped.

The door opened. Haran stood in the frame—sleeves rolled, an apron still tied at his waist, his hands carrying the faint sheen of cutting oil. Behind him, the workshop was lit by a single lamp that threw long shadows across the benches and the rack of tools along the south wall. The air that escaped past him was warm and smelled like home—oil and sawdust and the mineral bite of cut metal and the particular warmth that Haran's space had always held, not because of insulation or fire but because of decades of tiny, invisible applications of heat, layered day after day until the walls themselves seemed to remember the temperature.

Haran looked at him.

Ash didn't know what his own face was doing. He could feel the muscles around his jaw holding something—tension, exhaustion, the locked-down expression of a man who had been sitting alone with numbers for hours and hadn't spoken to anyone since. Whatever it was, it was apparently legible, because Haran's gaze rested on it for a long moment, reading, and something shifted behind the old man's eyes. Not alarm. Not the careful concern that Ash had learned to dread from people who noticed him struggling. Just recognition—the same quality he'd seen on Haran's face the day in the workshop when Ash had nearly ruined the valve seat, the day Haran had watched him flood a fitting with more force than the task required. The look of a man noting a fact and choosing, with great deliberateness, what to do with it.

"Come in," Haran said. He stepped aside.

Ash came in.

* * *

The workshop had changed and hadn't.

He stood just inside the door and let the warmth settle around him—the particular temperature of this room, always the same, always a few degrees above the ambient air outside, held steady by the accumulated micro-channeling of a man who didn't believe in wasting energy or in being cold. The lamp on the central bench threw its circle of light across the work surface, and in that circle, Ash's tools were laid out.

His tools. The set he'd built up over years of working here—the small brass-handled screwdrivers, the needle files, the calipers he'd bought secondhand from a retired watchmaker in the Third Terrace market. They were arranged on the bench in the configuration he'd left them in. Not piled, not pushed to the side, not redistributed to other stations. Set out. Ready. The screwdrivers in their leather roll, unrolled, each one in its sleeve. The files in the wooden block, points up. The calipers laid parallel to the bench edge, jaws closed, the vernier scale reading zero.

He hadn't been here in weeks. Since before the earthquake—since the deployment schedule had consumed his days and the identity of being useful had consumed the rest. Weeks of not visiting, not sending word, not even thinking about the workshop except in the abstract way you thought about places you'd outgrown. He'd let it go. He'd let all of it go—the pump project, the community, the part of himself that had lived here—because the Wellspring was louder and larger and more important than anything a workbench could offer.

And Haran had kept his tools ready. Not as a rebuke—there was nothing reproachful in the arrangement, no pointed display of abandonment. As a statement. As an act of faith so quiet it barely registered as a statement at all. *You'll come back. This will still be here. I'll keep it warm.*

Ash's throat did something he didn't give it permission to do. He swallowed against it and looked away from the bench.

The rest of the workshop told its own story. Petra's station near the door was cluttered with a new project—some kind of geared mechanism, half-assembled, the components laid out in her particular style of organized chaos. Davel's lathe in the corner had new marks on the chip tray, fresh cuts in the guide rails, the signs of heavy recent use. The racks along the south wall had been rearranged—new tools, unfamiliar shapes, things that hadn't been there the last time Ash stood in this room.

The workshop had been busy. The workshop had continued.

And in the far corner, where the pump project had lived—where Ash had spent an afternoon with Haran bending pipe and seating fittings and learning the difference between what his hands could do and what the Wellspring wanted to do—

The pump was gone.

The workspace where it had been was clean. The jigs were stored. The bending forms were hung on their hooks. The half-assembled housing, the inlet fittings, the outlet flanges—all of it, gone. In its place was a single sheet of paper tacked to the wall above the bench, covered in Haran's careful handwriting, with what looked like a schematic and a list of names.

"It's installed," Haran said. He had moved to the far end of the workshop, to the small stove—the iron box he kept for boiling water, one of the few things in the room that ran on fuel rather than channeling. Except it didn't run on fuel anymore. Ash watched as Haran filled a kettle from the tap, set it on the stove, and rested his hand briefly on the iron surface. A warmth bloomed—not visible, not dramatic, just the faintest change in the quality of the air above the stove top. The water in the kettle began, quietly, to heat.

A micro-pulse. The kind of channeling that cost less than the energy it took to light a match, applied with a precision that wasted nothing, that heated the water and only the water and not a single particle of air or iron or stone around it. The kind of channeling that Ash had tried to learn in this room and failed at, because his reservoir came in volumes and Haran's came in droplets, and the droplets were more useful than the volumes for everything that mattered at this scale.

"The pump?" Ash said. His voice came out rough. The walk had dried his throat, or the numbers had, or the hours of sitting alone with what the numbers meant.

"Finished two weeks ago. Installed in the Velden building the next day." Haran didn't turn from the stove. His hand rested on the iron, patient, the heat moving from his palm into the metal and from the metal into the water with the unhurried efficiency of a process that had been perfected over decades. "The district's water pressure is back to normal. Better than normal—the old channeled pump was failing every six weeks and nobody had the pool to maintain it. This one runs on physics. Spring-loaded piston, gravity return, check valves machined by Davel on the lathe." A pause. "Petra did the seals. She's better than either of us at seals."

The kettle was beginning to murmur. The faintest sound—not a boil, not yet, just the quiet conversation of water accepting heat.

"The residents contribute," Haran said. "Tiny pulses to keep the springs from fatiguing. A child could do it. Anyone with a pool—any pool at all—can give what the pump needs for a week." He took his hand from the stove. The kettle continued heating, the iron holding the warmth he'd given it, the water climbing toward temperature on the residual energy of a single casual touch. "It doesn't need a Wellspring. It never did."

The words landed in the warm air of the workshop and sat there, and Ash stood with his hands at his sides and looked at the empty corner where the pump project had been, and the sheet of paper on the wall with its schematic and its list of names—Haran, Petra, Davel, and half a dozen others he recognized from the tinkerer community, people who'd built a working water system while he was out reshaping the city with an ocean of power that was eating itself alive.

He didn't say anything. There was nothing to say that the silence wasn't already saying.

* * *

Haran made tea.

He had a way of doing this that Ash had watched a hundred times and never fully understood until the afternoon of the micro-pulse lesson—the afternoon that now felt like it belonged to another life, another person, a version of himself who'd stood in this room with the Wellspring humming behind his ribs and thought that the ocean was the interesting thing about himself.

The kettle reached temperature. Haran lifted it from the stove—no mitt, no cloth, just his bare hand on the handle, because the handle wasn't hot. The iron around it was, but Haran had heated the stove surface and the water and nothing else. His hand wrapped around the handle with the ease of a man who knew exactly where the heat was and wasn't.

He poured into two cups. Earthenware, chipped at the rim, the dark brown glaze that Ash associated with this workshop the way he associated sawdust and oil and the mineral smell of cut metal. Haran set one cup on the central bench, near Ash's tools. He carried the other to his own stool at the far end and sat down.

He didn't ask what had happened. He didn't ask why Ash was here, at this hour, with that expression. He sat on his stool with his cup between his hands and his eyes on Ash's face and he waited with the patience of a man who understood that some things arrived when they were ready and not before.

Ash picked up the cup. The warmth of it soaked into his palms—not channeled warmth, just the ordinary heat of hot water through fired clay, and the difference was something he could feel in his body now, the distinction between energy that came from a reservoir and energy that came from a kettle. He couldn't have felt that difference three months ago. He couldn't have felt it before the numbers.

He drank. The tea was plain—dried herbs, probably from the vendor on the terrace two streets over, the one who sold in bulk to people who didn't care about presentation. It tasted like the workshop. Like evenings spent at this bench with components in his hands and nowhere else to be.

He sat down on his stool. The wood was worn smooth where his weight had rested a thousand times before, the particular contour of this seat memorized by a body that had spent years here—years before the Wellspring, years of being the person whose hands were his only tools and whose tools were enough, in this room at least, to matter.

The silence stretched. It was not an uncomfortable silence—not the loaded quiet of a conversation being avoided, but the particular quality of silence that existed between two people who had spent enough time together that the absence of words was itself a kind of language. Haran drank his tea. Ash held his. The lamp burned. The workshop breathed its warm breath.

"I did the math," Ash said.

Haran's eyes came to his face. Steady. Unsurprised.

"The reservoir. The Wellspring." He set the cup down. His hands needed to be empty for this—needed to be holding nothing, gripping nothing, so that what came next could move through him without catching on anything. "I've been tracking the expenditures. Dorenne's records, the crew reports, everything. And I—I calculated what's left."

He stopped. Took a breath. The air was warm and smelled like the workshop and the reservoir was warm in his chest and he could feel its edges now, feel them the way you felt the walls of a room you'd been sitting in long enough to know exactly how far you could stretch your arms.

"Half," he said. "Roughly half is gone. In eight weeks."

The word sat in the air between them. Haran didn't move. His face held the same expression it had held since Ash started talking—attentive, level, the look of a man listening to the full shape of something before responding to any piece of it.

"The earthquake was the worst of it. Fifteen million kilojoules in four days—maybe more, maybe less, the estimates are rough. But the routine work was bad too. The training period, the deployments, everything. Six million on deployments alone, before the earthquake even hit. At the rate Dorenne has me working—" He heard his own voice, heard the precision in it, the mechanical recitation of numbers he'd spent hours calculating. He sounded like Dorenne's staff. He sounded like a report. "At the current rate, I've got eighteen weeks. Maybe eleven, if the schedule expands. Twenty-one if I hit master efficiency, which—" He stopped again. His jaw tightened, the muscles along his face locking the way they did when something was trying to come through that he didn't want to let through. "Which would take years."

He looked at his hands. They were resting on the bench, on either side of his cup, and they were steady. He was grateful for that. The steadiness was a small gift from a body that had spent a lifetime performing composure.

"I went through the whole thing. Phase by phase. The waste from the training period—four million kJ, learning what children learn for free. The assessment session. Every deployment. The earthquake." He could hear himself speeding up, the words coming faster, the careful structure of the recitation starting to crack at the seams. "And the world's pools are declining too. The same trajectory. The same math. Every generation smaller. Maren—" His sister's name snagged on something in his throat. He pushed past it. "Maren's pool has been weakening. She's doing in years what I'm doing in weeks. She's been living inside this math her whole life. She never had the—"

He stopped.

The workshop was quiet. The kettle ticked as it cooled. Somewhere outside, a distant sound—a voice, or a cart wheel, or the ambient noise of a city that continued to exist regardless of what the numbers said.

Haran sat with his cup. His face was still.

"I sat with the numbers for a long time," Ash said. Quieter now. The urgency had drained out of the words, leaving something flatter behind—the tone of a person who had reached the end of what the numbers could tell him and found nothing waiting there but the dark. "I drew a box around the range. Eleven to twenty-one weeks. That's the box. That's what's left."

He picked up his cup again. The tea had cooled, the temperature dropping steadily in the workshop air, the warmth seeping out through the clay into his palms and then into the surrounding nothing. He drank it anyway.

Haran was quiet for a long time.

Not the performative quiet of a man composing a response—not the loaded silence of someone deciding what to say. The genuine quiet of a man who had heard something important and was giving it the space it needed to settle. Haran's hands rested on his knees, the cup held loosely between them, and his eyes were on the middle distance—not on Ash, not on the bench, but on some point in the warm air between them where the words had landed and were still arriving.

A minute. Two. The lamp flame wavered and steadied. The walls of the workshop held their warmth, the accumulated heat of a day's worth of micro-pulses radiating gently into the room, sustaining a temperature that required nothing to sustain it except the hours already invested.

Ash waited. He'd learned, years ago, that Haran's silences were not empty. They were the part of the conversation where the most important work was being done—the sorting, the weighing, the careful assessment of what mattered and what didn't, performed with the same precision Haran brought to a valve seat or a spring calibration. You didn't rush it. You sat with it. The answer would be the right size when it came.

When Haran spoke, his voice was the same voice it always was—level, unhurried, the voice of a man who had decided what to say and saw no reason to say it louder or faster than the words required.

"So. How much is left, and what do you want to build with it?"

* * *

The words passed through the air between them and arrived somewhere inside Ash's chest—not in the reservoir, not in the warm vast space where the Wellspring hummed and diminished, but somewhere else. Somewhere older. Somewhere that had been his before any of the power had been his.

He opened his mouth. Closed it. Opened it again.

"I—" He stopped. The sentence he'd been forming—some version of the numbers, some repetition of the range, the box, the countdown—dissolved before it reached his tongue, because it wasn't what Haran had asked. Haran hadn't asked how much was left in the way Ash had been asking it. How much was left in terms of time. How much was left in terms of weeks, deployments, the columns on Dorenne's chart stretching forward into a diminishing future. Haran had asked *how much is left* the way a man at a workbench asked how much material was in the stock: not as a countdown, but as an inventory. Not *how long until this runs out* but *what can we make with what's here*.

And the second half of the question—*what do you want to build*—

He stared at Haran. The old man's face was patient. Not expectant—he wasn't waiting for an answer the way a teacher waited for a correct response. He was waiting the way he waited for a fitting to seat, for a spring to settle, for the natural conclusion of a process that had its own timing.

"I don't know," Ash said. The words came out honest, and the honesty of them was a relief that moved through his body like a physical sensation—a loosening in his shoulders, a softening of the locked muscles along his jaw. He didn't know. He genuinely didn't know. For the first time since he'd sat down at the desk with the candle and the pencil and Dorenne's reports, the not-knowing didn't feel like failure. It felt like the right answer to the right question.

"I don't know what to build," he said again. "I've been—the whole time, since the Wellspring, I've been spending. Just spending. Because the need was there and the power was there and it seemed like—" He stopped. Pushed his hands through his hair, the gesture of a man trying to find words for something he'd only understood as numbers. "Like there was no reason not to. Like the only question was *how much* and never *what for*. Dorenne had the schedule and the schedule was full and every deployment was a real problem with real people and I just—poured."

Haran nodded. The nod was small, contained—not agreement, not judgment, just acknowledgment. *Yes. That's what happened. Go on.*

"And then the earthquake." Ash's hands came to rest on the bench, palms flat, the same position they'd been in at the desk in his quarters, the same posture he'd held while the numbers accumulated on the page. "Fifteen million kJ. Maybe more. Four days. I would do it again—all of it, every hour—but I did it without knowing what it cost because I'd never checked. Because it felt bottomless. Because checking would have meant admitting it wasn't."

The workshop was very quiet. The sounds of the city outside—distant, muffled by stone walls and shuttered windows—were barely audible. In the warm circle of the lamp's light, the two of them sat with the shape of what Ash had said, and the shape was not the numbers. The numbers were the scaffolding. The shape was something underneath—the realization, still fresh, still raw, that the power he'd built his new self around was a resource with an end, and the end was visible, and the question of what to do about it was not a question he'd ever asked.

"The Wellspring isn't—" Ash started, and then stopped, because the sentence was trying to be something larger than he could manage. He tried again. "I've been treating it like it's me. Like the reservoir is who I am. Like when it empties, I—"

He couldn't finish. His jaw locked down on the rest of it—the fear, the shape of the dark at the end of the numbers, the thing he hadn't been able to look at in the room with the guttering candle.

Haran set his cup on the bench. The small sound of earthenware against wood was precise, deliberate—the sound of a man creating a punctuation mark in a conversation.

"When I was your age," Haran said, "I could heat a forge."

Ash looked at him.

"Full forge temperature. I held it for eight hours a day during my apprenticeship—metalwork, structural shaping, the big channeling that builds cities. I had the pool for it. I had the pool for anything." His hands rested on his knees, broad and knuckled and steady, the hands of a man who'd done ten thousand hours of precise work and could still do ten thousand more. "I spent twenty years learning to use less."

The lamp flame shifted in a draft, and the shadows on the wall behind Haran moved, and for a moment the old man's face was all light and hollows—the deep lines around his eyes, the set of a jaw that had been holding patience for decades.

"Not because my pool shrank. Because I watched what happened when people relied on force." He picked up his cup again, held it between his palms. Not drinking—holding. Feeling the warmth. "A building channeled with brute force needs brute force forever. A building channeled with precision needs almost nothing. The force becomes a dependency. The precision becomes a gift."

He looked at Ash. The look was steady and held something that Ash recognized from years of working beside this man—not warmth exactly, though there was warmth in it. Respect. The particular respect of someone who had chosen, a long time ago, to take you seriously.

"Your reservoir is a resource," Haran said. "It's an extraordinary resource. I've never seen anything like it and I never will again." A pause. The cup turning slowly in his hands. "But it's not you. It was never you. You were the boy who could diagnose a pump failure by sound before you could tie your boots. You were the person who walked into this workshop at fourteen with a cracked gear casing in your pocket and asked me what was wrong with it, and when I told you, you went home and figured out why. The reservoir didn't give you that. It can't take it away."

Ash's throat closed. He looked at the bench—at his tools, laid out and waiting, the screwdrivers in their roll and the files in their block and the calipers reading zero. He looked at them and his eyes burned, and the burning was a physical sensation that lived in the heat behind his sinuses and the pressure in his temples, and he didn't try to stop it.

"I abandoned the pump," he said. His voice was thick. "I walked away from it. From all of this. And you finished it without me."

"We finished it without you," Haran said. The words were matter-of-fact—no reproach, no satisfaction, no performance of forgiveness. Just the truth. "Petra, Davel, the Velden residents, two adolescents from the building next door who wanted to learn to thread pipe. We finished it." He paused. "It took longer. Not as long as you'd think."

"I could have done it in an afternoon. The whole pump. With the Wellspring."

"Yes." Haran nodded. "And it would have worked until something broke, and when something broke, who would fix it?"

The question was not rhetorical. Haran's eyes held his, waiting for the answer with the patience of a man who had time—who had always had time, who had built his life on the understanding that the right answer, arrived at slowly, was worth more than the fast answer, arrived at with force.

"Not me," Ash said quietly. "I'd be somewhere else. Fixing something else. And the pump would need someone who understood it. Someone who'd built it."

"Someone who knew where the seals went and why the springs were sized the way they were and what to do when the check valve fouled in winter." Haran reached over and tapped the sheet of paper on the wall—the schematic, the list of names. "Six people know this pump. Six people can maintain it. When I'm gone, when Petra moves, when Davel's hands finally give out—" a small, dry half-smile "—there will still be someone in that building who can take the pump apart and put it back together and understand what they're looking at."

He turned back to Ash. The half-smile faded into something quieter.

"That's building," he said. "That's what building looks like. Not the object. The knowledge. The capability that stays when the person who started it leaves."

* * *

Ash sat with it.

The workshop held its warmth. The tea cooled in his cup, and he didn't drink it, and the cooling was its own small lesson—heat leaving, entropy winning, the natural direction of all things moving toward equilibrium unless someone spent energy to hold them where they were. Haran's micro-pulses could keep this room warm for a day on the energy it would take Ash to heat a single building. The difference wasn't power. The difference was precision. The difference was decades of learning that the smallest possible input, applied at exactly the right moment, was more than the largest possible force applied without care.

"What do you want to build?" Haran asked again. Not pressing—returning to the question the way a craftsman returned to a workpiece, picking it up and turning it, examining it from a new angle.

Ash looked at the schematic on the wall. The pump. Six names. A building with water pressure that didn't depend on anyone's pool, maintained by the people who lived there, designed to survive the absence of the person who'd designed it.

He thought about the city above—the districts he'd reinforced, the walls he'd channeled, the water systems and heating grids he'd poured the Wellspring into over weeks of deployment. Good work. Necessary work. Every building warmer, every wall stronger, every system functional because he'd been there and spent what he had. And every one of them dependent on ongoing channeling that the city's diminishing workforce couldn't sustain. Every repair a stopgap. Every reinforcement a temporary fix that bought time but didn't change the trajectory.

He thought about the math—not his math, the world's math. The generational decline. The staircase descending. The children who would be born with 200 kJ, then 100, then nothing. The world where all of this—every channeled wall, every heated building, every magically maintained system—would eventually stop working because there would be no one left with the pool to maintain it.

And he thought about the pump. Working. Running on physics and tiny contributions. Running without him.

"Something that lasts," he said. The words came slowly—not because he was uncertain, but because they were building themselves as he spoke, each one finding its footing on the one before it. "Something that works after I'm gone. After the reservoir is empty. After—" He looked at Haran. "After all of it. Something that doesn't need a Wellspring."

Haran's face did something—the same small, almost-nothing expression Ash had seen months ago, the day he'd watched the old man's micro-channeling and understood, for the first time, what he was looking at. Not a smile. The material a smile was made of. Recognition, and warmth, and the quiet satisfaction of something arriving on schedule.

"Good," Haran said. He drained the last of his tea and set the cup down. "Then we should talk about what that looks like."

* * *

They talked.

Not in the way Ash talked with Dorenne—the brisk, structured, agenda-driven conversation of a meeting with a Director, where every word served a function and every function served a plan. This was the other kind of conversation. The kind he'd had with Haran a hundred times at this bench—circling, unhurried, the ideas arriving in their own order and at their own speed, each one examined and set down and picked up again from a different angle.

Haran stood and moved to the storage racks along the back wall. He pulled a roll of paper from the middle shelf—large, heavy stock, the kind used for technical drawings—and spread it across the central bench, weighting the corners with tools. The paper was covered in diagrams. Not the pump schematic—something larger. Something that showed streets, buildings, the contour lines of a district's terrain. Notes in Haran's handwriting crowded the margins, dense with figures and arrows and the particular shorthand that Ash recognized from years of reading Haran's working notes.

"The pump was a proof," Haran said. He leaned over the drawing, his hands flat on the paper, and Ash saw the district laid out—the Velden building at the center, the surrounding blocks radiating outward, each one annotated with numbers and symbols. "One building's water supply. Self-maintaining. Distributed labor. The next question is: what else can you build that way?"

He pointed to a cluster of buildings at the edge of the drawing. "Heating. These six buildings share a retaining wall along the eastern face. The wall was channeled sixty years ago—elder-level work, thick, designed to last. It's thinning now. The maintenance crews shore it up every three months, and every three months the shoring takes more energy than the last time because the original lattice is degrading."

Ash looked at the drawing. He could see the wall—a heavy line along the eastern edge, with notation marks showing the channeling density at various points. The density was uneven, thick at the base where the original work had been strongest, thinning toward the top where age and thermal cycling had worn it down.

"What if the wall didn't need channeling?" Haran said. "What if you buttressed it mechanically—iron strapping, tension cables, compression braces at the stress points—and used tiny channeled inputs only at the critical junctions? The way the pump uses micro-contributions to keep the springs from fatiguing."

"You'd need someone to design the bracing," Ash said. His mind was moving—not the way it moved when he calculated expenditures, the grim arithmetic of depletion, but the other way. The way it moved when it saw a system and began pulling threads. "The stress analysis. Where the load concentrates, where the original channeling is thinnest. You'd need to match the mechanical support to the specific failure pattern of the wall."

"You'd need someone who can look at a structure and diagnose its failures," Haran said. He was watching Ash's face. "Someone who spent years reading infrastructure reports and understanding where buildings break."

"That's—" Ash stopped. He'd been about to say *that's what I did for Dorenne*, and the sentence reoriented itself in his mind as he spoke, tilting from past to present, from what he'd been to what he could be. "I can do that. I've been doing that since I was her aide. Before the Wellspring."

"Before the Wellspring," Haran repeated. Letting the words sit.

The drawing on the bench was dense with possibility. Ash leaned closer, tracing the annotations with his eyes—the numbers that represented not magical expenditure but mechanical specification, the dimensions of bolts and cables and compression points, the engineering of structures that would stand without being held up by anyone's pool.

"The Wellspring could accelerate it," he said. Not the way he'd said it before—not the eager, careless offer of unlimited power thrown at a problem. Slowly. Feeling the edges of the idea as he built it. "Not replace the mechanical work. Accelerate it. Set the anchoring points—fuse the iron into the stone, compress the bracing, the heavy structural work that would take a crew weeks. Do it once. Do it right. Then the mechanical system takes over and the channeling isn't needed anymore."

Haran nodded. The nod was the same small, contained acknowledgment he gave to a properly seated fitting—the confirmation of something that had arrived at the correct tolerance.

"Catalytic," Haran said. "One large expenditure that eliminates the need for ongoing small ones. Like setting a fire to melt the wax that will burn for a month."

"One fire," Ash said. "Not the candle."

"Not the candle."

They looked at each other across the bench, and the drawing between them was covered in the plans for a different kind of work—not the heroic, spectacular, history-making work of holding up a city during an earthquake, but the quiet, structural, invisible work of making sure the city could hold itself up after the person who'd held it was gone. The work Haran had been doing his whole life. The work the pump represented. The work that looked like patience because it was, and that lasted because it was designed to.

* * *

The night deepened around the workshop. Somewhere in the conversation, Haran had made more tea—the same casual micro-pulse, the kettle warming under his hand, the water reaching temperature in the time it took to continue a sentence. Ash drank this cup. The warmth of it moved through his chest, and the warmth was not the reservoir, and the distinction between the two was no longer a loss. It was a fact. The tea was warm because Haran had warmed it. The reservoir was warm because—

Because it was there. Because it was a deposit, laid down in some process he didn't understand, cracked open by accident, draining through him. A resource. Not an identity. A resource, and resources were for building.

The thought settled into him the way the workshop's warmth had settled into him when he walked through the door—gradually, then all at once, then as something that had always been true and he'd simply been too close to see. The Wellspring wasn't who he was. It had never been who he was. It was what he had. And what he had was—he looked at the drawing, at the plans, at the tools on his bench waiting for his hands—what he had was enough to build something that would outlast it.

Not happiness. The situation hadn't changed. The numbers were the same numbers. The box on the page in his quarters still held the range: eleven to twenty-one weeks. The reservoir was still draining, the world's pools were still declining, and somewhere in the middle districts his sister was still channeling with her precise and diminishing pool, and none of that was different than it had been an hour ago.

But the panic—the suffocating, wall-closing, floor-dropping panic that had driven him out of his quarters and down the hill and through the darkened streets to the only door that had ever been unconditionally open—the panic had a shape now. It had edges he could work with. It was no longer the formless dark at the bottom of a well. It was a workbench. It was a set of specifications. It was a question he could answer.

*What do you want to build?*

Haran had begun sketching on a fresh sheet—modifications to the wall bracing, a variant design that accounted for the specific geology of the lower districts where the retaining walls were oldest and the stone was softest. His pencil moved with the same unhurried precision as his hands on a pump fitting, and watching him draw was like watching him channel—each line placed where it needed to be, no wasted motion, no extra force.

"The efficiency technique," Ash said. "The micro-pulses. I couldn't do it before. Not even close."

Haran looked up from the sketch.

"I flooded every time. The reservoir comes in volumes—I couldn't narrow it down to the kind of precision you use. But if I'm going to do catalytic work, I need to. I need to set anchoring points without flooding the entire wall. I need to fuse iron into stone without stiffening the stone. I need—" He paused. Heard himself. The list of needs was not the frantic list of a man trying to preserve a diminishing resource. It was the practical list of a man trying to learn a skill. "I need to learn to do what you do."

"You need to learn to use a cup instead of a river," Haran said. "Yes." The pencil tapped the bench twice—an old habit, the small percussion that meant he was thinking. "Your hands already know precision. They knew it before any of this. The problem was never your hands." He set the pencil down. "The problem was that the reservoir made precision feel unnecessary. When you have an ocean, why learn to pour?"

"Because the ocean is shrinking."

"Because the pour is what reaches the cup." Haran's eyes were steady. "The ocean was always going to be the wrong tool for this. You could heat this room in a second. You could heat the whole district. But you'd heat everything, and you'd heat it once, and tomorrow it would be cold again and you'd be a day's worth of Wellspring poorer. Or—"

"Or I can heat the water in the kettle and only the water in the kettle and the heat goes exactly where it's needed and nowhere else."

"And the room stays warm because I've been doing that all day, a hundred times, each time costing less than a breath."

The silence that followed was different from the earlier silences. Not the heavy quiet of waiting, not the patient quiet of listening. The quiet of two people who had arrived, together, at the same place—a workbench, a set of plans, a question with a shape.

"It will take time," Haran said. "The technique. You won't learn it in a week."

"I have eleven to twenty-one weeks."

A pause. The faintest movement at the corner of Haran's mouth—not a smile, not quite, but the closest thing to one that Ash had ever seen from a man who smiled the way other people channeled: sparingly, with precision, wasting nothing.

"Then we should start tomorrow," Haran said.

* * *

Ash stayed.

Not because there was more to discuss—the plans on the bench were preliminary, the sketches rough, the conversation about technique only a beginning that would need dozens of hours to become anything real. He stayed because the workshop was warm and his tools were on the bench and the stool beneath him was shaped to his weight and the room smelled the way it had always smelled, and being here was like pressing his palms to the ground after weeks of falling.

Haran cleaned up. He washed the cups at the small basin in the corner—by hand, with cold water, the way he did everything that didn't require channeling and most things that did. He rolled the drawings and placed them on the shelf with the care of a man who knew he'd need them again soon. He wiped the bench surface, hung his apron on the hook by the door, checked the lamp's oil level with a practiced glance.

The small ceremonies of closing a workshop for the night. Ash had watched them a thousand times. Had performed them himself, years ago, on the evenings when he stayed later than Haran and locked up. The rhythm of them was as familiar as the staircase in his parents' building, as the flagstone that tipped three steps from the door—knowledge held in the body, in the hands, in the part of a person that predated the Wellspring and would outlast it.

"Ash," Haran said.

Ash looked up from the bench. He'd been looking at his tools—not touching them, just looking, the way you looked at something you'd forgotten you owned and found in a drawer, the recognition that was also a kind of grief for the time you'd spent without it.

"You'll come back to this," Haran said. He was standing by the door, his coat over his arm, ready to walk home through the dark streets to wherever he lived—Ash had never been to Haran's home, he realized. In all the years. He knew the workshop and the man inside it and the precise arrangement of every tool on every rack, and he had never once been invited to the place where Haran slept. The workshop was what Haran shared. The rest was his.

"The work. The bench. The technique." Haran's voice was quiet—the same level, unhurried voice, but with something in it that Ash hadn't heard before. Or hadn't noticed. Or had noticed a hundred times and never identified because it was so constant it had become invisible, like the warmth in the walls. "You were the best I've had in this workshop. Not the most talented. The most patient. Patience is rarer."

Ash's eyes burned again. He blinked. Looked at the bench.

"Go home," Haran said. "Sleep. Come back tomorrow and we'll start."

He opened the door. The night air came in—cold, carrying the mineral smell of stone and the distant sound of the city breathing in its sleep. Haran stepped out.

"Thank you," Ash said. The words were not enough. They were all he had.

Haran paused on the threshold. He didn't turn around. His hand rested on the doorframe—a brief touch, the faintest warmth passing from his palm into the wood, so subtle it was almost invisible. The workshop's doorframe, kept warm. The door that was always open.

"Lock up when you leave," he said. "The key's where it always is."

He walked into the dark. His footsteps faded on the stone, unhurried, the pace of a man who had been walking this street for decades and would walk it again tomorrow.

* * *

Ash sat alone in the workshop.

The lamp was low. The warmth in the walls was fading—gradually, the way it always faded when Haran left, the accumulated heat of a day's micro-pulses slowly radiating into the night air, spending itself, moving toward equilibrium. Tomorrow Haran would come back and lay down another day's worth of tiny inputs, and the cycle would repeat, and the workshop would be warm again. Not because anyone spent a fortune. Because someone spent a little, every day, with care.

He looked at his tools. He reached out and touched the calipers—ran his finger along the beam, felt the smooth steel under his fingertip, the precise graduation marks etched into the metal. His hands knew these tools. His hands had known them before the Wellspring and they knew them still, and the knowing was not contingent on anything except the years he'd spent learning.

He picked up the calipers. Opened the jaws. Measured the edge of the bench—an absurd measurement, meaningless, the calipers telling him the thickness of a wooden surface he'd leaned on ten thousand times. The number on the scale was precise and small and completely useless and his hands were steady and the tool was in them and the workshop was warm and for the first time since the candle guttered in his quarters, the feeling in his chest was not the reservoir and not the absence of the reservoir.

It was relief.

Not happiness. Not the bright, spilling, too-large-for-the-shelf feeling he'd felt the day Dorenne hired him, or the day the Wellspring cracked open and the world flooded in. Something smaller. Something that fit. The particular relief of a man who has been falling and has not yet stopped falling but has, in the middle of the fall, caught sight of something solid—a handhold, a ledge, a surface that might bear weight. He hadn't landed. The fall was still in progress. But the question had changed. Not *how far until the bottom* but *what can I reach from here*.

*What do you want to build?*

The workshop held its warmth. The lamp burned. Outside, the city slept, its districts cooling in the dark, its systems fading, its magic draining at the speed of generations—the slow, irreversible work of entropy, which took everything, eventually, back to cold. And inside the workshop, on a bench that smelled like oil and sawdust and the accumulated patience of a man who had spent his life making things that worked without him, Ash sat with the question and the question had a shape he could hold.

He stayed a long time. Then he banked the lamp, found the key where it always was—on the nail behind the tool rack, the third nail from the left—and let himself out into the dark.

The flagstone tipped under his foot. He barely noticed.

He walked home through the quiet streets, and the reservoir hummed in his chest, finite and warm, and his hands were empty, and the empty hands remembered what they were for.
