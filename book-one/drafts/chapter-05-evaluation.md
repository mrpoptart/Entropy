## Criterion-by-Criterion Analysis

### 1. Voice & Style

**Carver (9/10).** The strongest voice match to chapters 1-3. Clean, declarative prose that trusts the reader completely. The too-small chair detail carries displacement without commentary. Narrative distance shifts organically — medium for the bureaucratic material, deep close for the moments of wonder. The "what to avoid" list is honored throughout. The final passage — the kettle-as-prayer metaphor — is the kind of controlled lyrical peak the style guide calls for: earned by pages of restraint.

**Hearthkeeper (8/10).** Improved from the Chapter 4 draft. The "hands in pockets for three days" opening is the single best behavioral detail across all three drafts — it shows Ash's fear without naming it. Some residual over-explanation: the Vara sections occasionally tell us what she's thinking rather than showing what Ash observes. The warmth motif is well-handled. Vara's parting line about aquifers is excellent prose and perfect seeding.

**Lyricist (8/10).** Strong rhythmic control and the most propulsive read. The force target scene — the granite block launching through its mounting — is the best-paced action moment across all drafts. The closing section runs slightly long, with the "shelf" metaphor repeated enough times to lose its edge. The stairwell scene with Dorenne is the best Dorenne moment across all three drafts. Minor issue: Ash's surname varies from the other drafts ("Carro" vs. "Torren"/"Verada") — needs standardization.

### 2. Emotional Impact

**Carver (8/10).** The emotional arc is correct: nervousness → wonder → uncertainty buried → identity fusion beginning. The "ocean doesn't need a bottom" line lands. The closing kettle metaphor is devastating. Slightly weaker on the *wonder* — the comedy of breaking everything doesn't fully land because the Carver's restraint keeps the temperature low. The chapter is effective but could use one moment of pure physical joy.

**Hearthkeeper (8/10).** Strongest on the three-day gap material. The mother saying "Like a heating stone" is a perfect small beat — tender, uncomfortable, the mother seeing her son as something new. The "they would never be cold again" closing is powerful but slightly on-the-nose for the style guide's restraint mandate. The chapter earns its emotion through accumulation.

**Lyricist (9/10).** The most emotionally complete arc. The force target scene generates genuine laughter (the examiner with the apple is a perfect comic detail), and the tonal shift to Veradenne's assessment is expert. The stairwell scene with Dorenne — her moment of genuine uncertainty — is the only draft where Dorenne feels fully human. The closing is the weakest element: it oversells the identity fusion in the final paragraphs, telling us what the power means instead of showing Ash living with it.

### 3. Pacing & Structure

**Carver (9/10).** The tightest draft at ~4,500 words. Every scene earns its length. The progression from bureaucratic boredom to escalating astonishment to Vasra's quiet authority is well-architected. The Dorenne coda is efficient — seeds the information-management thread without overstaying. One weakness: the opening is abrupt. Three days have passed since the most traumatic event of Ash's life, and the Carver drops us into the basement corridor with minimal transition. Two or three sentences about the intervening days would ground the reader.

**Hearthkeeper (7/10).** At ~5,800 words, the longest draft but not excessively so. The three-day transition material at the top is the best-paced opening across all drafts. The tests section is well-handled. The weakness is Vara's explanation — it runs about 30% too long, with the lake-and-ruler analogy and the "aquifer" speech both landing on the same point. Pick one and cut the other. The chapter also lacks a strong Dorenne beat, which the Carver and Lyricist both provide.

**Lyricist (8/10).** At ~6,200 words, well-paced for its length. The force target scene is the highlight — the escalation through tests builds genuine momentum. The Dorenne stairwell scene adds a dimension the other drafts lack. Weakness: the final section — Ash at his desk — runs long. The "shelf" metaphor for the deferred words is clever on first use and diminishing on the third. The chapter would be stronger ending at "He let it in" with 200 fewer words preceding it.

### 4. Character

**Carver (8/10).** Ash is rendered through behavior: the flinch, the apology reflex, the careful attention to the too-small chair. Vasra is given exactly enough presence — the knees that creak, the dark stone from her pocket, the forty years compressed into her assessment. The three examiners feel real through minimal detail (the clipboard, the pen that stops). Dorenne is present at the end but could use one moment of genuine unguarded emotion.

**Hearthkeeper (8/10).** The examiners are the most individuated here — Tessaril has a name and a personality. Vara Solenne is well-drawn. The apple-eating examiner from the Lyricist is absent, which is a loss. The mother detail ("Like a heating stone") is the best family-thread seed across drafts.

**Lyricist (9/10).** The strongest character work. The apple-eating examiner is a brilliant small detail — a person who expected a boring morning, made human by a snack. Veradenne has the most authority and presence. The stairwell Dorenne — the moment where her mask slips — is the best single character moment across all three drafts. It shows us the person beneath the strategist. Ash's own character comes through the "I can pay for that" line — comic, endearing, the reflex of a person accustomed to being a burden.

### 5. Worldbuilding Integration

**Carver (9/10).** Magic shown entirely through use. Calibration stone, field ring, targeting disc — each demonstrated in action. Waste heat as the physical consequence of inefficiency is rendered through ambient temperature rising. The waste-heat detail is the most physically convincing across all drafts.

**Hearthkeeper (9/10).** The granite-saturation exercise is the most technically detailed worldbuilding moment and the most convincing. Vara's instruments from the locked cabinet add texture. The five-percent efficiency figure is lower than the other drafts (Carver uses 15-20%, Lyricist uses 5%) — needs standardization. The Hearthkeeper's version of the efficiency explanation is the clearest.

**Lyricist (9/10).** The resonance plate (sand scattering), thermal gradient array (paint bubbling), and force target (granite through the wall) are the most inventive instrument designs. Each test feels like a distinct experience. The building's ambient hum that Ash can now feel is a strong worldbuilding detail that neither other draft provides.

### 6. Continuity

**Carver (9/10).** Consistent with chapters 1-3. The stair-climbing callback, the Haran comparison, the too-small chair echoing the children's-test theme. The ramp detail connects to Chapters 1-2. Names Vasra as the scholar. One issue: Ash's surname "Torren" may need checking against other chapters (Chapter 1 doesn't give a surname, but the name should be consistent going forward).

**Hearthkeeper (7/10).** The three-day transition is the best bridge from Chapter 4. "Hands in pockets for three days" is continuity gold. However, the surname "Verada" differs from other drafts. Tessaril feels like an established name that could conflict with Tessaly from Chapter 2 (too similar). Vara Solenne is a good scholar name.

**Lyricist (8/10).** Good continuity with the established world. The desk scene at the end directly connects to Ash's role from Chapters 2-3. The Dorenne stairwell scene builds on the Chapter 1 ramp dynamic. Surname "Carro" differs from other drafts. Veradenne is the most distinctive scholar name.

### 7. Seeds & Setup

**Carver (9/10).** Thread 1 (Reservoir Countdown) perfectly planted: the refill question, Vasra's honest uncertainty, Ash burying "fixed" beneath "vastly larger." The kettle-prayer metaphor in the closing — holding certainty without examining it — is the most elegant planting of the willful blindness motif. Thread 12 (Identity Fusion) progressed through the transit-lift moment: he *could* use it now but chooses the ramp. Dorenne's information management seeds the control thread. The sealed document routing through her office is a subtle, perfect Dorenne detail.

**Hearthkeeper (9/10).** Thread 1 planted with the strongest explicit warning: Vara's aquifer speech is the clearest statement of finitude across all drafts. "Springs are not infinite. They draw from aquifers. The aquifer draws from rainfall." The reader will remember this. Thread 12 progressed through the "never cold again" beat. The "hands in pockets" motif seeds both fear and the eventual identity shift. The ramp-instead-of-stairs closing inverts Chapter 1's stairclimbing to show identity already shifting.

**Lyricist (8/10).** Thread 1 planted through Veradenne's careful framing and the "shelf" metaphor for deferred words. The planting is effective but slightly over-explained in the Lyricist's characteristic way — the shelf metaphor tells us Ash is choosing not to engage with "fixed" rather than showing him do it instinctively. Thread 12 progressed through the desk scene — Ash returning to his old role but experiencing it differently. The Dorenne "we can work with that" beat is the strongest single seed for the patron-control arc. Forward momentum into Chapter 6 is strong.

## Best Passages

### Carver

**The kettle-prayer closing:**
> He held it the way his mother held the old kettle she'd packed from the apartment—without looking at it, without explaining, without admitting that the holding was a kind of prayer.

The best closing passage across all three drafts. Connects to the mother's old-kettle motif from the outline, renders Ash's willful blindness through physical metaphor, and plants the denial without naming it.

**The buoy metaphor:**
> But they registered the way a buoy registers in an ocean: present, visible, too small to change the shape of the water.

### Hearthkeeper

**Hands in pockets:**
> He'd kept his hands in his pockets for three days.

The single best behavioral line across all three drafts. Nine words that tell us everything about Ash's relationship to the new power: fear, restraint, the inability to trust his own body. Should be transplanted into any final version.

**Vara's aquifer warning:**
> "The word *wellspring* — I used it because it's accurate. A deep source that feeds from below. But springs, in nature, are not infinite. They draw from aquifers. The aquifer draws from rainfall. There is always a source, and the source is always finite. Remember that."

The strongest Thread 1 planting across all drafts. The reader will remember this when Ash doesn't.

### Lyricist

**The force target:**
> The stone didn't move. It vanished.

The best single-sentence moment across all three drafts. Comic, terrifying, and perfectly paced.

**Dorenne on the stairwell:**
> Something moved behind Dorenne's eyes. Not the calculation — something older, something that predated the strategy and the management and the institutional reflexes. Something that looked, for a moment, like a woman standing in front of a problem she genuinely didn't know how to solve.

The best Dorenne moment across all drafts. Shows the human beneath the strategist.

**"I can pay for that":**
> "I can pay for that," Ash said.

The most Ash line in any draft. Comic, endearing, the reflex of someone who's spent his life calculating his cost to others.

## Weakest Passages

### Carver
- Cold open: drops us into the basement with no transition from the three-day gap. Needs 3-5 sentences of context (the Hearthkeeper and Lyricist both handle this better).
- Vasra's explanation runs slightly long — the explanation of trickle mechanics could lose 2-3 sentences without losing clarity.

### Hearthkeeper
- Tessaril's name is too close to Tessaly from Chapter 2 — will create confusion.
- Vara's explanation section uses both the lake/ruler analogy AND the aquifer speech — both land on the same point. Keep the aquifer speech (it's stronger), cut or compress the lake/ruler.
- The "never cold again" line is powerful but edges toward telling. Consider cutting or making it more ambiguous.
- No Dorenne scene — a significant gap given that she arranged the testing and is managing the aftermath.

### Lyricist
- The "shelf" metaphor for deferred words works on first use but is revisited three times. Once is enough.
- The final section (Ash at his desk) oversells the identity fusion. The pen-and-deployment-schedule ending is good, but the preceding paragraph ("the word would spread...") is exposition about future events that the style guide would flag.
- Surname "Carro" needs standardization.
- At 6,200 words, the chapter could lose 400-500 words in the final section without losing anything.

## Recommended Path

### Primary Recommendation: Build from the Carver

The Carver should serve as the foundation draft. It has the strongest voice match to chapters 1-3, the most disciplined POV, the best pacing, and the most devastating closing passage (the kettle-as-prayer metaphor). Its restraint makes the wonder land harder.

### Passages to Transplant

1. **From the Hearthkeeper: the three-day transition.** The Carver's opening needs 3-5 sentences bridging the gap. The Hearthkeeper's opening material — mother healing, temporary housing, "hands in pockets for three days" — should be condensed and woven into the Carver's first scene.

2. **From the Hearthkeeper: Vara's aquifer warning.** Replace or supplement the Carver's Vasra closing with the Hearthkeeper's aquifer speech. It's the strongest Thread 1 seed across all drafts and gives the reader something concrete to remember.

3. **From the Lyricist: the Dorenne stairwell moment.** Add the Lyricist's stairwell beat to the Carver's Dorenne coda. The moment where her mask slips — "something that predated the strategy" — adds a dimension the Carver's version lacks.

4. **From the Lyricist: "I can pay for that."** Transplant this line into the Carver's force-target moment (the weight in the ceiling). It's too good and too Ash to leave out.

5. **From the Lyricist: "The stone didn't move. It vanished."** If the Carver's force-target moment can accommodate this beat, it adds comic punch.

### What Not to Pull

Do not pull the Hearthkeeper's full Vara explanation (the lake/ruler analogy — it duplicates the aquifer point). Do not pull the Lyricist's shelf metaphor (the Carver's buoy metaphor is more elegant). Do not pull the Lyricist's desk-scene ending (the Carver's walking-into-the-city ending has better momentum).

## Revision Notes

1. **Add three-day transition.** The Carver needs 3-5 sentences at the opening or just after the first scene break covering: mother healing, temporary housing, the warmth in his hands, the fear of touching things. Draw from the Hearthkeeper.

2. **Standardize names.** Scholar: pick one (Vasra, Vara Solenne, or Veradenne). Ash's surname: establish one and use it going forward. Avoid "Tessaril" (too close to Tessaly).

3. **Standardize efficiency figure.** The Carver uses 15-20%, the Lyricist uses 5%. The magic system doc suggests ~20% for untrained. Use 20% (untrained baseline) for consistency.

4. **Strengthen the wonder beat.** The Carver's restraint is a strength, but the chapter needs one moment of pure physical joy — the sensation of power moving through him that feels *good*. Not narrated as joy, but rendered physically: the expansion in his chest, the lightness, the feeling of being full instead of empty. The Lyricist's "something that had been compressed inside him for twenty-three years" approaches this.

5. **Keep the kettle closing.** This is the chapter's best passage. Do not replace it.
