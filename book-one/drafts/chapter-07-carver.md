# Chapter 7

The new apartment smelled like fresh plaster and someone else's channeling.

Ash stood in the doorway with a crate of his father's tools braced against his hip. The hall behind him was wide enough for two people to pass without turning sideways—wider than the old stairwell by half. The walls were smooth, the mortar lines invisible, the stone fused so cleanly that the surface caught the light from the channeling strips overhead and held it in a pale, even glow. No cracks. No water stains. No curtain hung across the landing to trap whatever warmth bled through from above.

He set the crate down inside the door. The floor didn't groan.

"Kitchen's through there," his mother said from somewhere deeper in the apartment. Her voice carried easily—the rooms were larger, the ceilings higher, the stone dense enough to hold sound without swallowing it. "There's a proper larder. A cold shelf with its own lattice."

She appeared in the hallway. She was using the cane the healer had given her—a smooth-handled thing, channeled lightweight, nothing like the knotted walking stick old Verath had used on the stairs of the old building. Her leg was healing well. The elder-generation healer Dorenne had sent came every second day, and Ash could see the improvement in the way his mother moved: less guarding, more weight on the mending leg, the careful steps becoming merely careful instead of cautious.

She looked at the crate. "Those go in the back room. Your father wants them by the window."

"Where is he?"

"Downstairs. Checking the foundation joints." She said it with a particular flatness—the sound of a woman whose husband had been walking the perimeter of the building since they arrived, running his hands along the base stones, pressing his thumb into mortar lines the way Ash used to press his into the crack on the old stairwell. Some habits survived the move.

Ash carried the crate through to the back room. The window faced east, toward the ridge, and the morning light came through unobstructed—no adjacent building close enough to block it, no overhang, no laundry line strung between walls. The glass was intact. Both panes.

He set the tools beneath the sill and looked out. From here he could see the rooftops of the Third Terrace descending toward the gorge, the pale stone catching the sun, the walkways clean and maintained. A transit lift hummed in its shaft two buildings over—he could hear the low vibration through the wall, steady, functional. Someone was using it. Someone was always using it.

The old building had been on the Fifth Terrace. The transit lift there had worked on a schedule that got less reliable every year, and Ash had never used it anyway.

He turned from the window and went back for the next crate.

* * *

The old apartment was half a ruin.

Sorren's team had stabilized the building after the activation—the foundations held, the main structure sound—but the fifth floor was gutted. Ash's family's apartment was open to the sky through two floors of fractured stone, the exterior wall gone, the interior walls cracked and leaning. The city had roped it off. Nobody lived on the fifth floor anymore. The Merrat family had relocated to a cousin's place on the Fourth Terrace. Old Verath had moved in with her daughter. The building still stood, but it stood the way a body stands after a fever: upright, diminished, not the same.

His mother had been going back every morning for a week. She climbed to the fourth-floor landing—as high as the city's safety cordon allowed—and sorted through what the neighbors had carried down from the apartment before the access was sealed. Boxes and bundles, stacked against the stairwell wall, covered in plaster dust. The salvage of twenty-three years.

Ash found her there on the third morning of the move, kneeling on the landing beside an open crate. Her cane lay across her knees. Her hands moved through the contents with a slowness that had nothing to do with her leg.

"Mum, I can carry those."

"I'm sorting."

He looked at what she'd laid out on the stone floor. A curtain—the one from the bedroom window, singed along one edge where the wall had come apart. A bowl, ceramic, cracked clean across the base. His father's measuring tape, the brass housing dented but the tape still coiling. A kettle—the kettle, the iron one with the wooden handle his mother had wrapped in cord when the original grip split, years ago. The side was dented. The spout was bent.

"We don't need those," he said. "The new place has everything. The kitchen—"

"I know what the new place has."

She picked up the bowl. Turned it in her hands. The crack ran from the rim to the base, a clean fracture that would leak if she filled it with anything. She wrapped it in the singed curtain and set it back in the crate.

"Mum."

"Go bring the other boxes down. The ones by the second-floor landing."

He went. The stairwell was the same stairwell—every riser, every turn, the railing that wobbled on the third-floor landing. But the plaster was gone from the walls in long strips, exposing the stone underneath, and the crack he'd measured with his thumb every morning had widened into a gap he could fit his fingers through. The heating lattice on the third-floor landing was shattered. Someone had swept the pieces against the wall.

He carried boxes. His mother sorted. She kept the curtain, the bowl, the measuring tape, the kettle, a set of cooking spoons with scorched handles, a wooden frame with no picture in it. She discarded clothes that were torn beyond mending, a rug too heavy with stone dust to clean, broken crockery that even she couldn't justify. But the ratio troubled him. Too much kept. Too little discarded. Objects that served no purpose in a kitchen with a cold shelf and a proper larder and channeling strips that worked.

He didn't say anything else about it. His mother's jaw had the set it took when she'd decided something, and twenty-three years had taught him what that set meant.

On the way out, he passed the front entrance. His father's wooden door wedge was still there, sitting on the threshold where it had landed when the building shook. The one thing that hadn't needed channeling to hold. He picked it up and put it in his pocket.

* * *

The copper kettle cost him a day's wages from the stipend Dorenne had arranged.

It was well-made—a smith on the Second Terrace, the kind of shop that served the better households. The copper was heavy-gauge, the handle riveted, the spout shaped with a precision that would pour clean and thin. He'd chosen it because it was better than anything his mother had ever owned, and because the old kettle was dented and bent and cracked along the base where it had hit the rubble, and because replacing broken things with better things was what you did when you could finally afford to.

He brought it home in the evening. His mother was in the kitchen—the new kitchen, with its cold shelf and its channeling-powered stove and its windows that faced the morning sun. She was making dinner. The smell was different here—the same onions, the same cumin, but the ventilation worked, and the scent dispersed instead of settling into the walls the way it had in the old apartment, where twenty years of cooking had soaked into the stone until the kitchen smelled like cumin even when nothing was on the stove.

"I brought you something."

He set the kettle on the counter. The copper caught the channeling light and threw it back warm and bright. His mother looked at it. Her hands were wet from washing vegetables, and she dried them on her apron before she touched it—lifted it, tested the weight, tilted it to see the spout.

"It's beautiful," she said.

"Copper holds heat better than iron. And the handle won't split."

She set it down. She looked at it for a moment longer—the kind of look that had layers he couldn't read, that said something about the kettle and something about the son who'd bought it and something else entirely that lived in the space between the two.

"Thank you, Ashlyn."

She put the old kettle in the cabinet above the stove. Not in the waste bin. Not with the things to discard. In the cabinet, behind the new dishes Ash had bought last week, where it sat in the dark on a shelf meant for things you didn't use but couldn't part with.

He didn't notice where she put it. He was already unwrapping the blankets—new ones, thick wool, dyed a deep blue that matched nothing in the apartment but felt like a color a family should have. And the tools for his father: a new set of chisels, a spirit level with brass fittings, a leather roll to keep them in. Good tools. The kind his father would appreciate.

His father came in from the back room, where he'd been fitting a shelf bracket with his old tools—the dented measuring tape, a hand drill, a set of screws he'd brought from the salvage. He saw the leather roll on the table and opened it. His hands moved over the chisels the way they moved over any tool—testing the edge, the balance, the fit of handle to blade. He knew tools. These were good ones.

"You didn't have to do this," he said.

"I wanted to."

His father looked at him. The look held pride—enormous, genuine, the kind that lived in the eyes and the set of the mouth and the way a man stood when his son had done something worth standing for. But underneath the pride, something else. A question that lived in the pause before he spoke, in the hand that almost reached for Ash's shoulder and stopped an inch short, in the way he glanced at the old tools on the shelf behind him before turning back to the new ones.

"These are fine work," his father said. He rolled the leather closed and tied it with the cord. He set it on the table beside the old measuring tape and did not put the old tools away.

Ash's mother served dinner. The new dishes, the new table—Dorenne's furnishing allocation, standard for relocated families, but nicer than standard because the Wellspring's family got the version of standard that came with attention. His mother ladled soup from the old pot, the one she'd carried down from the fifth floor herself, the one with the handle she'd re-riveted twice. She could have used the new pot. She used the old one.

They ate. The food was the same. The kitchen was different. The windows let in the last of the evening light, and the heating lattice in the wall hummed at a steady, comfortable warmth that nobody in the family had provided—it came from the building's shared channeling system, maintained by the district's infrastructure crew. His mother's hands rested on the table between bites. They weren't channeling. They didn't need to be. Someone else's pool was keeping the walls warm.

* * *

The neighbors introduced themselves on the third day.

A woman from the apartment across the hall—middle-aged, well-dressed in the understated way of the Third Terrace—knocked and offered a small cake on a plate. She was polite. She asked how they were settling in. She did not ask about the activation, or the old building, or the blown-out wall on the Fifth Terrace that half the city had seen. She asked about the cake plate when she left—"just return it whenever"—and her smile was courteous and measured and landed nowhere.

A man from the floor below introduced himself in the stairwell. He was an administrator in the Commerce Bureau—he mentioned this early, the way people on the Third Terrace mentioned their positions, not as boasting but as coordinates. He knew who Ash was. Everyone on the Third Terrace knew who Ash was. He shook Ash's hand and said the building was well-maintained, the district quiet, the transit lifts reliable. He did not linger.

In the old building, Ash had known every family by sound. The Merrats arguing about whose turn it was to channel the landing lattice. Old Verath's cat yowling at the second-floor window. The family on the first floor whose youngest child sang the same three notes every evening while her mother cooked. The particular creak of each apartment door, the rhythm of footsteps on the stairs, the way the building breathed at night—settling, groaning, the sounds of a structure and its people aging together.

Here, the walls were thick enough to hold silence. The transit lift hummed in its shaft, but the hum was mechanical, impersonal—the sound of a system, not a neighbor. No one's cooking smelled through the walls. No one knocked to borrow salt or return a pan or ask if Ash's father could look at a hinge. The building was full of people. The hallways were empty.

His mother felt it first. Or she showed it first—Ash couldn't tell the difference. She stood at the kitchen window on the fourth evening and looked out at the walkway below, where two women passed each other with a nod and kept walking.

"It's quiet," she said.

"It's nice," Ash said.

She didn't answer. She turned from the window and went to make tea, and she reached for the old kettle before she remembered it was in the cabinet, and she used the new copper one instead. The water boiled fast—the stove's channeling interface was well-maintained, responsive, nothing like the temperamental lattice in the old kitchen that his mother had nursed with her own pool for years, learning its moods, compensating for its dead spots with the patient precision of a woman who knew that the only warmth her family could count on was the warmth she provided.

The copper kettle whistled. She poured. She sat at the table with her tea and her cane propped against the chair and the quiet of the Third Terrace settling around her like something that was almost, but not quite, peace.

* * *

He broke the heating lattice on the fifth day.

He'd been trying to adjust the temperature in the back room—his father's workroom, where the morning sun heated the space unevenly and the far corner stayed cold. The lattice interface was set into the wall beside the door: a smooth stone disc, palm-sized, connected to the network of channeling-reactive stone that ran through the walls. A standard residential system. Touch it, push a thread of energy, and the lattice warmed.

Everyone in Solathis knew how to do this. Four-year-olds did this.

Ash placed his palm on the disc. He tried to push gently—the same attempt he'd made with the calibration stones in the Hall basement, the same search for a thread in the ocean. He could feel the interface under his hand, the stone's readiness, the lattice network branching through the walls like veins. He pushed.

The disc turned white.

Heat erupted from the walls. Not warmth—heat, the searing, airless oppression of stone pushed past its design tolerance. The lattice strips in the wall glowed from dull amber to bright orange to a white that hurt to look at. The air in the room thickened. Ash pulled his hand back. The heat kept coming—the lattice was saturated, overloaded, still releasing the energy he'd poured into it in a single uncontrolled pulse.

A crack ran up the wall from the interface disc. Then another. The stone was expanding—heated beyond its tolerances, the channeling-reactive mineral fracturing along its crystal structure the way the calibration stones had fractured in the basement of the Hall.

"Ash." Maren's voice from the hallway. Flat. Not a question.

She appeared in the doorway. She assessed the room in a glance—the glowing walls, the cracked lattice, the air shimmering with waste heat. She crossed to the wall and placed both palms flat against the stone, flanking the interface disc. She closed her eyes.

Her channeling was invisible. No glow, no surge, no dramatic display. Just the slow, steady withdrawal of excess energy from the lattice—drawing it out of the overloaded stone, dispersing it through her own body, bleeding it off as the controlled, even warmth that radiated from her palms and forearms. Her jaw tightened. A vein stood out on her temple. The lattice dimmed from white to orange to amber to its normal resting state.

She held her hands there for a long time. The cracks in the stone didn't heal—she couldn't undo structural damage—but the lattice stabilized. The temperature dropped from searing to warm to comfortable. The air cleared.

Maren pulled her hands away. She flexed her fingers. The wince was small—a brief compression of her lips, the kind of expression that appeared and vanished in the same breath. She'd spent a significant portion of her daily pool on the repair. More than she'd planned to spend.

"Sorry," Ash said.

"The interface has a limiter," she said. "It's designed for residential-grade input. If you push past the limiter, the lattice absorbs the excess and has nowhere to put it."

"I didn't mean to—"

"I know." She looked at the cracks in the wall. "I'll ask the building's maintenance crew about the stone. The lattice itself should be fine now."

She left. Her footsteps in the hallway were even, unhurried, the steps of a person who had somewhere else to be and was already recalculating her day around the pool she'd just spent. She didn't close the door harder than necessary. She didn't sigh. She didn't look back.

Ash stood in the back room. The walls were warm. The cracks in the stone ran from the interface disc to the ceiling in thin, branching lines—a map of his excess, his overflow, the distance between what he intended and what his hands delivered. The room smelled like hot stone and ozone.

He looked at his palms. Still warm. The reservoir behind his ribs hadn't shifted. The amount of energy that had overloaded a residential heating lattice and cracked the walls of the back room—enough to heat the entire apartment for a month, probably—had cost him nothing he could feel.

* * *

He broke the lighting strip in the hallway the next morning.

He'd been reaching for the interface—a narrow stone bar set into the wall at shoulder height—and his hand had brushed it on the way to the coat hook. Not even a deliberate push. A glancing contact, his palm sliding across the surface for less than a second.

The strip flared. Every channeling strip in the hallway—seven of them, running the length of the apartment from the front door to the kitchen—went from their ambient glow to a blinding, actinic white. The light was physical, a pressure against his eyes. He threw his arm up. The strips held their overloaded state for two seconds, three, and then the nearest one cracked with a sound like a knuckle popping, and the light cut out.

Darkness. Then the remaining strips flickered back to half their normal brightness, the damaged one dark and split along its length.

Maren was already in the hallway. She'd been putting on her coat to leave for work. She stood with one arm in a sleeve, her face lit by the uneven glow of the surviving strips, and she looked at the cracked one the way a carpenter looked at a joint that needed redoing—assessing the damage, estimating the cost, already planning the repair.

She didn't say anything. She took off her coat, hung it back on the hook, and spent twenty minutes coaxing the damaged strip back to function. When she finished, the light was dimmer than the others—a visible scar in the hallway's even glow—but it worked.

She put her coat on again. She left for work. She was late.

The third time was the kitchen faucet. The water system in the new apartment ran through channeling-reactive pipes—standard Third Terrace infrastructure, the water pushed by district-level channelers through a network that served the whole building. The faucet interface was a small stone valve set into the counter beside the basin. Ash turned it. The pipe behind the wall groaned, and water erupted from the faucet with enough force to hit the opposite wall.

He scrambled to close the valve. The stone was slippery under his wet hands, and the pressure from his grip fed back into the interface—more energy, more force, the water hammering against the basin in a white sheet that sprayed across the counter and onto the floor. He let go entirely. Stepped back. The faucet continued at full pressure for several seconds before the building's system compensated and the flow dropped to normal.

The kitchen floor was flooded. Water dripped from the counter, the wall, the window above the basin. The copper kettle sat in a puddle.

His father appeared with a cloth. He dropped it on the floor and pushed it around with his foot, mopping without comment, the way he'd mopped spills in the old apartment—practically, without fuss. He wrung the cloth into the basin and mopped again.

"The valve's sensitive," his father said.

"I barely touched it."

His father looked at him. The look was not accusatory. It was the look of a man who had spent his life working with materials—who understood tolerances, who knew what happened when force exceeded design, who had spent decades fitting doors and shimming floors and compensating for a building that was slowly coming apart. He understood the problem. He just didn't know how to say what he understood without saying something else underneath it.

"You'll get the feel for it," his father said.

He went back to the workroom. Ash heard him pick up a chisel—one of the old ones, from the salvage, not the new set. The sound of metal on wood. His father, working. The same sound in a different room.

* * *

Maren came by every evening that first week. She didn't announce these visits as repairs. She came for dinner, or to check on their mother's leg, or to bring something she'd baked—small acts of sibling presence that fit naturally into the family's rhythms. But each visit included a circuit of the apartment: a hand brushed along the hallway wall, a palm pressed to the heating lattice interface, a quick check of the lighting strips and the kitchen valve. She found what Ash had broken and she fixed it.

The heating lattice in the front room—overloaded again, not as badly this time, but the stone was stressed and the temperature had crept above comfortable. She drew the excess out and let the lattice settle.

The lock on the front door. Ash had touched it on his way in and the channeling mechanism had seized—too much energy forced through a precision interface designed for the lightest contact. Maren spent ten minutes coaxing it back into alignment, her fingers barely touching the stone housing, her channeling so fine that Ash couldn't detect it even standing beside her.

A lighting strip in the bedroom that had developed a flicker—not from Ash's direct touch but from the ambient bleed of his proximity. His body radiated waste heat, a background hum of energy that the apartment's sensitive Third Terrace systems picked up and responded to. The strip was reacting to him. Maren dampened it with a careful, calibrated touch that left the stone slightly less responsive—enough to filter out his ambient noise.

She did this without being asked. Without complaining. Without discussing it with him or their parents. She came. She ate dinner. She checked the apartment. She fixed what needed fixing. She left.

On the seventh evening, Ash walked her to the door. The hallway was warm—properly warm, the lattice holding steady at the temperature Maren had set it to, not the temperature Ash had accidentally forced it to.

"Thanks for coming by," he said.

"Of course."

She buttoned her coat. The same coat, buttoned to the throat. Her face was composed—the habitual composure, the surface she wore so naturally it was almost invisible.

"I'll get better at it," he said. "The lattice. The—all of it."

"I know."

She opened the door. The hallway beyond was wide and smooth and quiet.

"Maren."

She paused. Half-turned.

He wanted to say something about the repairs. About her pool, spent each evening on his mistakes. About the work she did that nobody mentioned over dinner, that their parents didn't see or chose not to comment on, that he recognized only in the aggregate—the apartment functioning, the systems holding, the cracks not spreading. He wanted to say he saw it. That he knew what it cost her.

"See you tomorrow?"

"Tomorrow," she said.

She left. The door closed. The lock engaged with a soft click—the mechanism she'd repaired, responding to the lightest touch, working exactly as it was designed to work.

* * *

The maintenance crew came on the ninth day. Two of them—young, professional, wearing the district's service livery. They'd been called about the cracks in the back room wall. They assessed the damage, replaced the fractured stone around the interface disc, and re-seated the lattice connections. The work took an hour. They were polite. They did not ask how a residential heating lattice had been overloaded badly enough to crack the wall stone.

They knew who lived here. The whole building knew.

Ash watched them work from the doorway. Their channeling was practiced, efficient—the kind of quiet competence that came from doing the same repairs hundreds of times. They spoke to each other in the shorthand of professionals, and they handled the stone with a familiarity that reminded him of Sorren's team at the old building. Different scale, same craft.

When they finished, one of them—the younger one, barely older than Ash—caught his eye in the hallway.

"If the lattice gives you trouble again," he said, "there's a diffuser plate we can install. It sits over the interface and spreads the input across a wider surface area. Meant for—" He stopped. Recalculated. "Meant for high-output users."

"Thank you."

The maintenance worker nodded and left. Ash stood in the hallway and listened to their footsteps recede down the stairwell—the stairwell he hadn't climbed yet, the one with the transit lift that worked.

He looked at the front door. The lock Maren had repaired. The lighting strip she'd dampened. The hallway walls, smooth and uncracked, holding the warmth that the district's channelers provided and his sister maintained and his hands disrupted every time he reached for something without thinking.

He went to the kitchen. His mother was at the table, her leg propped on a cushioned stool, a cup of tea in front of her. The copper kettle sat on the stove, still warm. She was looking out the window—not at anything, the way people looked out windows when the thing they were looking at wasn't outside.

"The maintenance crew fixed the back room," he said.

"I heard them."

He sat across from her. The table was new—Dorenne's furnishing allocation, smooth-planed wood, no wobble. The old table had wobbled. His mother had compensated by folding a piece of cloth under one leg, and she'd adjusted the fold every few months as the floor settled, and the cloth had been there so long it had become part of the table's identity—not broken, just particular.

"Do you like it here?" he asked.

His mother looked at him. Her eyes held something he couldn't parse—not sadness, not gratitude, not the complicated expression she'd worn when he showed her the copper kettle. Something quieter. The look of a woman sitting in a room where everything worked and nothing was hers.

"It's a good apartment," she said.

"Better than the old one."

"Better," she agreed. She picked up her tea. The copper kettle gleamed on the stove behind her. In the cabinet above, invisible, the old iron kettle sat on its shelf with its dented side and its bent spout and its handle wrapped in cord, holding nothing, keeping nothing warm.

"You've done well, Ash," his mother said. "You've done so well."

She said it the way she said things that were true. And underneath the truth, in the space between the words and the way she held her cup—both hands, the way she'd always held a cup when the warmth mattered, when the heat coming through the ceramic was the heat she'd put there herself, her own pool, her own effort, her own daily act of making a home warm—underneath the truth, something she would not say and he would not hear.

The evening settled. The heating lattice hummed. The copper kettle cooled on the stove. Outside, the Third Terrace was quiet in the way the Third Terrace was always quiet—maintained, orderly, the silence of a place where everything worked and nobody needed to borrow anything from anyone.

His mother finished her tea and rinsed the cup and put it on the rack and picked up her cane and went to bed. She paused at the kitchen doorway.

"Goodnight, Ashlyn."

"Night, Mum."

She went. Her footsteps in the hallway were uneven—the cane, the healing leg, the rhythm of a woman still learning to walk in a new way through a new place. The bedroom door closed.

Ash sat at the table. The apartment was quiet. The walls were warm. Everything in it was new or nearly new—the dishes, the table, the blankets, the copper kettle, the tools in the leather roll. Everything except the dented measuring tape on the shelf in the back room, and the cooking spoons with scorched handles in the drawer by the stove, and the old iron kettle in the cabinet, and the wooden door wedge in his pocket that he'd carried from the old building and set on the shelf beside his bed without knowing why.

He didn't notice the pattern. He noticed the improvement. The working lattices, the reliable transit lift, the thick walls that held warmth without his mother spending her pool each morning and evening to keep the cold out. The kitchen that smelled like dinner instead of twenty years of dinners. The windows with both panes intact.

This was what he'd wanted. A home that worked. A family that was safe. A life where the things that had been broken were replaced with things that weren't.

He placed his palm flat on the table. The wood was cool and smooth under his hand. The reservoir behind his ribs pulsed—steady, vast, unchanged by a week of overloaded lattices and cracked stone and flooded kitchens. Unchanged by anything. The ocean inside him, patient and full, waiting for him to learn to use it.

He would learn. He would get better. He would stop breaking things and start fixing them, the way Dorenne fixed districts, the way Sorren's team fixed buildings, the way his mother had fixed the cold in the old apartment one pool at a time for twenty-three years. Except he wouldn't need twenty-three years. He wouldn't need patience or precision or the slow daily expenditure of a body's small gift. He had the ocean. He had enough.

He lifted his hand from the table. The wood held no mark, no warmth, no trace of the power that pressed against his palms. The apartment hummed around him—the lattice, the lighting, the water system, the building's quiet mechanical life—and he sat in the center of it and felt, for the first time since the activation, something close to home.

It wasn't home. Not yet. But it was better.

He went to bed. The sheets were new. The pillow was new. The blankets were the deep blue ones he'd bought, thick and warm and carrying no one's smell but the faint mineral scent of the shop where they'd been made. He lay in the dark and listened to the building—not groaning, not settling, not speaking in the language of stress and compression he'd learned in twenty-three years of stairwells. Just the hum. Even and low and impersonal. The sound of a place that didn't need him.

He closed his eyes. The reservoir pulsed behind his ribs. The copper kettle sat on the stove. The old kettle sat in the cabinet. His mother slept in the next room, in a bed she hadn't chosen, in a building she hadn't warmed, in a neighborhood where nobody knew the sound of her footsteps on the stairs.

Ash slept. He dreamed of nothing. The apartment held its warmth without him, and the silence was the kind that nobody had made.
