# Chapter 5

The basement of the Hall of Infrastructure smelled like dust and lamp oil and the faint mineral sharpness of stone that had been channeled so many times the rock itself seemed to hum. Ash followed a clerk down a corridor he'd never seen before — below the offices, below the ramp, below the level where the building's foundations met the plateau's bedrock. The walls were rougher here. Unfinished. The kind of stone that architects left raw because nobody important was meant to see it.

Three days. Three days since the building collapsed, since his mother's legs broke, since something inside him had detonated and taken the outer wall of his childhood home with it. Three days of temporary housing in the Merival district — three clean rooms that smelled like other people's soap, a kitchen his mother hadn't learned yet, a view of a street where nobody knew their names. Three days of his father carrying meals to the bedroom where his mother lay with her legs splinted and elevated, the healer visiting twice a day, the sound of channeled warmth moving through his mother's bones audible as a low, sustained hum that Ash could hear through the wall if he stood very still.

He could hear things now. Feel things. The warmth in his hands hadn't gone away. It had settled — less urgent than the first eruption, less terrifying, but present. A pressure behind his palms that responded to proximity the way a compass responded to north. When he passed a wall with active channeling in it, the pressure shifted. When he walked near the heating lattices in the Merival apartment, he could feel them pulling at something inside him, and the something wanted to answer.

He'd kept his hands in his pockets for three days.

The clerk — a young woman with a clipboard and the harried expression of someone managing too many appointments — stopped at a door at the end of the corridor. It was heavy, iron-banded, the kind of door that suggested the room behind it had been designed to contain things.

"Assessment room four," she said. "They're expecting you."

She opened the door and stepped aside and did not follow him in.

* * *

The room was larger than he'd expected. Low-ceilinged, wide, lit by lattice strips along the walls that gave off a cool, even glow — the institutional lighting of a space designed for precision rather than comfort. Stone floor, swept clean. Three long tables arranged in a U-shape around a central area that was marked with a circle of inlaid copper, the metal flush with the stone, worn to a dull sheen by years of feet and hands and channeled energy passing through it.

Three people sat behind the tables. Two men and a woman, all middle-aged, all wearing the plain half-cloaks of administrative staff. They had clipboards. They had instruments laid out before them — devices Ash recognized from Dorenne's office: calibration stones, resonance gauges, graduated cylinders filled with a pale liquid that responded to channeled energy by changing color. The tools of measurement. The bureaucratic apparatus of a city that processed magical ability the way other systems processed tax returns.

The woman in the center — she seemed to be in charge, or at least in possession of the largest clipboard — looked up when he entered. Her expression was the particular flatness of someone who had performed this task many times and expected nothing from this iteration to be different.

"Ashlyn Verada?"

"Ash."

She made a note. "Age?"

"Twenty-three."

Another note. A glance at her colleagues — brief, automatic, the kind of look that confirmed they were all starting from the same page. One of the men was adjusting an instrument on the table, a device with a crystal face and a needle that rested at zero. The other was arranging a set of stones in a row, each one a different size, graduating from a pebble to something the size of his fist.

"This is a standard trickle assessment," the woman said. "We'll run through a series of channeling exercises designed to measure the strength and stability of your reservoir. The process takes approximately forty minutes. Do you have any questions?"

"I've never channeled before. I mean — before three days ago. I don't know how to—"

"We're aware of your situation." She said it with the clipped efficiency of someone who had been briefed and would have preferred not to have been. "Director Kharren's office sent the preliminary report. We'll start with basic contact channeling and proceed from there."

She gestured toward the copper circle in the center of the room. "Step into the assessment area, please."

He stepped into the circle. The copper under his boots felt different from the stone — warmer, or denser, or something his feet couldn't quite name. The pressure in his hands responded. A gentle pull, like standing near a fire on a cold day, the body's automatic orientation toward warmth.

The woman set a stone on a small pedestal in front of him. It was the size of an egg, pale gray, polished smooth, with a lattice of fine lines running through it like veins in a leaf. The lines were colorless.

"This is a calibration stone," she said. "Place your hand on it and channel. Any amount. The mineral lattice will respond with a color change that corresponds to the strength of the input. Level one is faint blue. Level ten is deep red. Most young adults register between three and five on their first assessment."

She picked up her pen. The two men watched with the attentive boredom of people who had seen this particular stone change color several thousand times.

Ash looked at the stone. He looked at his hand.

Three days ago, his hands had blown out the wall of his parents' apartment. Three days ago, force had poured through his palms with a violence that had shattered stone and scattered timber and nearly brought the ceiling down on his family. He hadn't known how to start it and he hadn't known how to stop it and the memory of that helplessness sat in his body like a bruise he kept pressing.

He reached out and touched the stone.

He didn't know how to channel. Not really — not the way channelers knew, the ones who'd been doing it since childhood, the muscle memory built from years of casual use. He knew what it looked like from the outside. He'd watched his mother channel the stove every morning of his life. He'd watched Maren's hands shimmer with focused force. He'd watched strangers warm walls and seal cracks and activate transit lifts with the absent ease of people performing an act as natural as breathing.

He'd just never breathed.

He touched the stone and tried to — push. The way he'd pushed the beam. Not with his muscles but with the thing behind his palms, the warmth, the pressure. He thought about the stove. About his mother's hand on the interface. About the gesture so basic it wasn't even called channeling. Just living.

The stone cracked.

Not dramatically — not an explosion, not the violent detonation of three days ago. A clean split, right down the center, the two halves falling away from each other on the pedestal with a soft click. The mineral lattice in both halves had gone — not blue, not red. White. A blazing, crystalline white that pulsed once and then faded as the stone's structure failed and the energy he'd pushed into it found no lattice left to illuminate.

The room was quiet.

The woman with the clipboard looked at the stone. She looked at her colleagues. The man on the left had taken his hand off the instrument he'd been adjusting. The man on the right was still holding one of the graduated stones, frozen in the act of placing it on the table.

"That was—" the woman started.

"I'm sorry," Ash said. "I didn't mean to — I don't know how much to—"

"That was a level-ten calibration stone," she said. Her voice had changed. The flatness was gone, replaced by something more alert, more careful. "It's rated for inputs up to ten thousand kilojoules equivalent. Nobody breaks them."

"I'm sorry."

She set her clipboard down. She picked it up again. She made a note that took longer than any of the previous notes.

"We'll proceed to the next assessment."

* * *

The second test was simpler in concept and, as it turned out, more difficult in practice. Hold a channeled field stable for thirty seconds.

The woman — her name was Tessaril, he learned, when she introduced herself properly after the calibration stone incident, as if the stone's destruction had elevated him from appointment to person — placed a metal plate on the pedestal. It was thin, circular, etched with concentric rings that Ash assumed corresponded to measurement gradations. Around the edges of the copper circle, the two men positioned three instruments on tripods — devices with crystal faces and fine needles, angled inward, pointed at the plate.

"Channel into the plate," Tessaril said. "Steady output. Try to hold the same level for thirty seconds. The instruments will measure field stability — how consistent your output is over time. Fluctuations are normal for a first assessment. We're looking for a baseline."

Ash put his hand on the plate. The metal was cool under his palm.

"Whenever you're ready."

He pushed. Gently — or what he intended as gently. The pressure behind his hands responded immediately, eager, the way water responds to a crack in a dam. Not the catastrophic break of three days ago but a steady, insistent flow that he tried to modulate by thinking about it, by imagining a smaller opening, a thinner stream.

The needles on all three instruments swung to the right and pegged.

They didn't flutter. They didn't waver. They hit the maximum reading on their scales and stayed there, pressed against the stops, trembling faintly with the force being pushed through them.

Tessaril stared at the instruments. She checked the first, then the second, then the third. All pegged. All steady. She looked at the plate under Ash's hand. The concentric rings were glowing — all of them, every ring, from the outermost to the center, a uniform white that made the metal look like it was made of light.

"You can stop," she said.

He took his hand away. The glow faded. The needles drifted back toward zero, reluctantly, like compass needles released from the pull of a magnet.

"That's maximum on all three gauges?"

"Those instruments are rated for elder-level output," the man on the left said. His boredom was entirely gone. "Full capacity. I've never seen all three peg simultaneously."

Tessaril was writing again. Her handwriting, Ash noticed, had gotten smaller, as if she were trying to fit more information into the same space.

"The field was stable," the man on the right said. He was reviewing the gauge he'd been monitoring. "Completely stable. No fluctuation at all over the thirty seconds."

"That's unusual?"

"For a first assessment?" The man looked at him. "That's unusual for anyone."

Tessaril set her clipboard down with a precision that suggested she was thinking about something larger than the next test. "Excuse me," she said. She stood, walked to the door, and left the room.

The two men looked at each other. The silence had a particular quality — the quality of people recalculating.

"You can step out of the circle if you'd like," the man on the left said. "She'll be a moment."

Ash stepped out of the circle. The copper's pull faded. He stood beside one of the tables and looked at the cracked calibration stone, still sitting in its two halves on the pedestal. The mineral lattice had gone dark. The stone looked ordinary now — just gray rock, split clean.

He'd broken it by touching it. By trying to do the smallest, gentlest thing. The most basic channeling act that existed — push energy into a stone, watch it respond — and he'd shattered it because the push that felt small to him was, apparently, catastrophic.

The warmth in his hands pulsed. He put them in his pockets.

Tessaril returned with a woman who was not what Ash had expected.

She was old. Not middle-aged-old, the way the examiners were, but genuinely old — elder generation, seventy at least, with white hair cropped short and a face that looked like it had been assembled from angles rather than curves. She wore a scholar's half-cloak, darker than the examiners', with a symbol on the collar that Ash didn't recognize — not Infrastructure, not Council. Something academic. She carried no clipboard. She carried a small leather case, the kind that held instruments, and she carried herself with the unhurried authority of a person who had stopped being impressed by things several decades ago.

She looked at Ash. Then at the cracked calibration stone. Then at the instruments still positioned around the copper circle. Then back at Ash.

"I'm Vara Solenne," she said. "I study channeling theory at the Athenaeum." She opened the leather case on the nearest table. Inside, nested in shaped velvet, were instruments Ash had never seen — smaller than the ones on the tripods, more finely made, with crystal faces that caught the light with an opalescence that suggested they were designed for a different order of measurement. "Tessaril tells me you broke her favorite stone."

"I'm sorry about the—"

"Don't be. Those stones are mass-produced and replaceable." She was arranging her instruments with the quick, efficient hands of someone who'd done this many times but not recently enough for it to be boring. "What isn't replaceable is data. I'd like to run some additional measurements, if you're willing."

"What kind of measurements?"

"The kind we don't have instruments for." She looked at him over the rim of her case. Her eyes were dark, sharp, the eyes of someone who spent her life looking at things most people couldn't see. "The assessment you've been running was designed for trickle evaluation. Graduated scales, bounded ranges. It assumes the person being measured falls somewhere on a known spectrum." She paused. "You don't appear to be on the spectrum."

"What does that mean?"

"It means my colleagues' instruments aren't large enough. It's like measuring the depth of a lake with a ruler. The ruler isn't wrong — it simply wasn't built for the job." She set the last of her instruments on the table and turned to face him fully. "I'd like to try a longer ruler."

* * *

Vara's tests were different.

She didn't use graduated stones or field-stability plates. She used what she called threshold exercises — tasks designed not to measure a specific output but to find the upper boundary of what Ash could produce. Each exercise was harder than the last. Each one involved channeling into a different medium — stone, metal, water, air — with increasing precision and force.

She was methodical. She was patient. She was, Ash realized after the third exercise, enjoying herself.

"Channel into this," she said, placing a block of granite on the pedestal. It was roughly the size of a bread loaf, dense and heavy, with a flat face polished to a mirror finish. "Structural reinforcement. Push energy into the crystalline matrix and try to make the stone harder. You'll feel resistance — that's the stone's existing structure pushing back."

He touched the granite. He pushed.

The resistance was there — a solidity in the stone that met his energy and absorbed it, the way a sponge absorbs water. But the sponge had a capacity, and he reached it almost immediately. The granite's crystalline structure saturated. He could feel it — a threshold, a fullness, the stone saying *enough* in a language his hands understood without translation.

He kept pushing. Not intentionally — the energy behind his palms didn't stop just because the stone was full. It continued flowing, looking for somewhere to go, and when the stone couldn't absorb any more, the surplus bled outward as heat.

The room got warm.

Not dangerously warm — not the explosive overflow of the apartment. A gradual increase, like someone had turned up the heating lattice in the walls. Ash felt it on his face first, then his arms, the air around him thickening with the waste heat of energy that had nowhere to go.

"Stop," Vara said.

He pulled his hands away. The granite sat on the pedestal, unchanged to the eye. Vara placed her palm on it and held it there for a moment, reading something through the contact.

"Fully saturated," she said. "The entire block. In—" She checked a small timepiece. "Seven seconds."

One of the examiners made a sound. Vara ignored it.

"The granite block exercise is designed for master-level channelers," she said, still looking at the timepiece. "A skilled structural channeler — someone with twenty years of experience — takes three to five minutes to achieve full saturation. An elder with a strong trickle can do it in about ninety seconds." She set the timepiece down. "Seven seconds, and you were barely trying."

"I was trying," Ash said. "I just don't know how to try less."

"Yes," she said. "That's precisely the problem."

She ran more tests. Channel force into a basin of water — heat it. He heated it to boiling in seconds, steam erupting from the surface so violently that Tessaril stepped back and one of the men knocked his clipboard off the table. Channel focused force at a metal target across the room — a disk mounted on a spring-loaded frame designed to measure kinetic impact. Ash pushed. The disk dented. The spring behind it compressed past its rating and stayed compressed, the frame bent, the mounting bolts in the wall cracked.

"Sorry," Ash said again.

"Stop apologizing," Vara said. She was writing in a small notebook, her script tight and rapid. "You're not breaking things because you're careless. You're breaking things because the instruments were designed for a range and you're not in the range."

She had him channel into the air itself — push warmth outward, fill the room. He did. The temperature climbed. The examiners loosened their half-cloaks. One of them wiped sweat from his forehead.

"That's — the ambient increase is approximately twelve degrees," the man with the resonance gauge said. "In thirty seconds. That's the thermal output of a full elder's trickle concentrated into—"

"It's the thermal output of waste heat," Vara corrected. "That's the ambient bleed from his channeling, not his primary output. His efficiency is..." She paused, calculating. "Extraordinary in the wrong direction. He's pushing perhaps ten to twenty times the force required for any given task, and the surplus dissipates as heat."

"Ten to twenty times," Tessaril repeated.

"At least." Vara closed her notebook. She looked at Ash with an expression he couldn't read — not awe, exactly, not the recalculating look the examiners had worn since the calibration stone cracked. Something closer to the face Haran made when he encountered a mechanism he'd never seen before — a curiosity so deep it bordered on reverence, held in check by the discipline of a mind that valued understanding over wonder.

"Sit down," she said. Not unkindly. She gestured to a stool by the nearest table. "I need to explain what I think I'm seeing."

He sat. The stool was cold after the warmth he'd pumped into the room. His hands were warm — they were always warm now — and he pressed them against his thighs, feeling the heat through the fabric.

Vara pulled a stool opposite him. The examiners had gathered behind her, their clipboards held at their sides, their professional detachment surrendered entirely to attention.

"Every person born in this city — every person born in this world — carries a reservoir of channeling energy," Vara said. "You know this. The reservoir determines your trickle: the amount you can access and spend each day. The trickle replenishes during sleep, drawing from the planetary source. The total capacity varies by generation — older generations have larger reservoirs, younger ones smaller. This is the decline. This is what we've been measuring, tracking, and arguing about for the better part of forty years."

She paused. She folded her hands in her lap.

"Your reservoir is not a trickle."

The room was quiet.

"A trickle is a channel — a narrow opening through which energy flows at a regulated rate. What I've measured in you today is not a channel. It's a source. A deep source that feeds from below. Not a trickle but a wellspring." She said the word as if she were placing it on a shelf — carefully, with awareness of its weight. "A wellspring."

The word settled into the room. Ash felt it land — not in his mind but somewhere lower, in his chest, in the place where the warmth lived. *Wellspring.* The sound of it matched something — the pressure behind his hands, the oceanic feeling of power that had been building in him for three days, the sense of something vast and deep that he couldn't see the bottom of.

"How much?" he asked. His voice came out rough. He cleared his throat. "How much can I—how large is it?"

Vara looked at her notebook. She looked at the cracked calibration stone, the bent target frame, the instruments still pegged at maximum.

"I can't tell you precisely," she said. "My instruments aren't calibrated for what you are. I can tell you that in the past two hours, you've expended energy that would represent several days' worth of output for a strong elder-generation channeler. And your reservoir hasn't measurably diminished."

The number landed. Several days' worth. In two hours. And he wasn't tired. He wasn't depleted. He felt the same as he had walking in — the same pressure, the same warmth, the same oceanic fullness pushing against the inside of his skin.

"Orders of magnitude," Tessaril said from behind Vara. Her voice was quiet. "That's — orders of magnitude beyond anything we've ever recorded."

"Yes," Vara said.

The room was too warm. Ash could feel it — the waste heat from his channeling exercises still hanging in the air, the stone walls radiating it back. He'd done that. He'd heated the room by accident, by spillover, by the simple inability to channel at a scale the room was designed for.

"Where did it come from?" he asked.

Vara's expression didn't change. "I don't know."

"Has this happened before? To anyone?"

"No. Not in any historical record I've studied. Not in any theoretical framework I'm aware of." She said this without drama, without the breathlessness that the statement seemed to warrant. She said it the way she said everything — precisely, as a fact among facts. "Trickle variance exists within generations. Some people are slightly stronger, some weaker. But the variance is small. A factor of two at most. You are not a variance. You are something we don't have a category for."

"Is it—" He stopped. Started again. The question was harder to form than he expected, as if the words themselves resisted assembly. "Does it refill? Like a trickle? Will it—come back, after I use it?"

Vara was quiet for a long time.

Not the calculating quiet of Dorenne, who went silent when she was choosing between strategies. Not the teaching quiet of Haran, who went silent when he wanted you to find the answer yourself. This was the quiet of a scholar standing at the edge of what she knew and looking out at the territory beyond it.

"I don't know," she said.

Ash waited. The warmth in his hands pulsed.

"A trickle replenishes because it draws from the planetary reservoir through a personal channel. The channel opens during sleep. The amount it draws is fixed by the channel's capacity — which is, in turn, determined by when you were born. This is the model. It's well-established. It's been consistent for every person we've ever measured."

"But."

"But you are not a trickle." She leaned forward slightly. "I don't know if your reservoir connects to the planetary source the way trickles do. I don't know if it replenishes. I don't know if it's renewable or fixed." She paused. "The working assumption — and I want to be clear that this is an assumption, not a finding — is that your reservoir functions like a trickle: fixed total capacity, daily renewal from the planetary source. Just... vastly larger."

*Vastly larger.*

The words expanded in his chest like a breath he'd been holding for days. Vastly larger. The calibration stone cracking. The instruments pegging. The granite saturating in seven seconds. Several days of elder-level output and the reservoir hadn't measurably diminished. An ocean. A wellspring.

"But it's fixed," one of the examiners said. "Right? It's still a fixed amount. Just—"

"We don't know," Vara said. She said it directly, to the examiner, but her eyes moved to Ash and held there. "We don't know. Nobody has ever seen this before. I want to be honest about the boundary between what we can measure and what we can't."

Ash heard her. He heard the careful precision of *we don't know* and the honest emphasis on *fixed* and the admission that the boundary of knowledge had been reached and that the territory beyond it was dark.

He heard all of it.

He heard *vastly larger*, and the ocean in his chest swelled, and the word *fixed* passed through him like a stone through water — present for a moment, then gone, sinking below the surface into some depth where it settled, unseen, unfelt, waiting.

* * *

Vara ran a final series of measurements. More precise this time — she used instruments from her case that responded to subtler gradations, reading not just the force of his output but its texture, its pattern, the way his energy moved through materials. She was mapping something, building a picture from data points that her existing models couldn't accommodate.

She made him channel into stone again — not granite this time but a thin slab of slate, fragile, the kind of material that would shatter under brute force. She told him to reinforce it without breaking it.

He broke it.

She gave him another slab. He broke that one too.

A third slab. He held his breath. He tried to think about smallness — about the gentlest touch, the lightest pressure, the way his mother's hand rested on the heating lattice with the ease of twenty years' practice. He pushed with everything he understood about restraint.

The slab cracked. Not shattered — cracked. A single line, corner to corner, the slate splitting along its natural grain.

"Better," Vara said.

"I broke it."

"You broke it less." She examined the crack, running her fingertip along the line. "Your control is essentially nonexistent. You have no training, no framework for modulation, no muscle memory for channeling at any scale. You're like a person who's never held a pen trying to write in calligraphy — the hand is strong enough, but the fine motor coordination doesn't exist yet."

"Yet," he said.

She looked at him. "Yet. It can be learned. Every channeler learns it. But they start learning at age five, with trickles measured in hundreds of kilojoules. You're starting at twenty-three, with a reservoir that—" She paused. The pause held something she decided not to say. "It can be learned."

She packed her instruments. The examiners were writing — all three of them now, filling their clipboards with notes that Ash suspected would be on Dorenne's desk by evening. Tessaril had regained some of her professional composure, though her notes had grown substantially longer than the standard trickle assessment form seemed designed to accommodate.

"What happens now?" Ash asked.

"Now?" Tessaril looked up. "Now we write our report. Director Kharren will receive it. The Athenaeum will want their own assessment — Vara's department, I imagine."

"I'd like to run longitudinal measurements," Vara said, closing her leather case. "Weekly, at minimum. Track the reservoir level over time. Determine the replenishment rate — if there is one."

"And in the meantime?"

"In the meantime, you should be very careful about what you touch." She said it without humor, or with a humor so dry it had been dehydrated into something resembling seriousness. "Your efficiency is approximately twenty percent — untrained baseline. That means for every unit of work you intend to do, you're producing five. The surplus comes out as heat, force, or structural damage, depending on what you're channeling into." She paused. "The room we're standing in has increased in temperature by approximately fifteen degrees since you sat down."

He hadn't noticed. He noticed now — the warmth in the air, the sweat along his spine, the way the examiners had shed their half-cloaks at some point during the last hour. He'd been doing this. Just by sitting here, just by existing with this thing inside him, he was heating the room.

"I'm sorry," he said. Again. The apology was becoming reflexive.

"Don't apologize," Vara said. She latched her case and picked it up. "Learn. That's the only useful response to a situation this unprecedented." She regarded him for a moment — that look again, the one that lived between curiosity and something deeper. "A wellspring. I've been studying channeling theory for forty-three years. I've never seen anything that made me reconsider the framework."

"And now?"

"Now I have some thinking to do." She walked toward the door, then stopped. "Young man."

"Ash."

"Ash. The word *wellspring* — I used it because it's accurate. A deep source that feeds from below. But springs, in nature, are not infinite. They draw from aquifers. The aquifer draws from rainfall. There is always a source, and the source is always finite." She held his gaze. "Remember that."

She left. The door closed behind her with a heavy, final sound.

Ash sat on the stool in the overheated room and looked at his hands. The warmth pulsed in his palms. The broken calibration stone sat on the pedestal across the room, split clean, its lattice dark. The bent target frame hung on the wall, its mounting bolts cracked, its spring compressed past recovery. Around him, the instruments were being packed away by examiners whose clipboards held more notes than they'd written in months, data points for a phenomenon that didn't fit their forms.

*A wellspring.*

He turned the word over. Felt it settle. It fit — the way a key fits a lock you didn't know existed, the click of alignment, the sense of something named that had been nameless and therefore unreal.

He was a Wellspring.

Vara had said *finite*. She'd said *aquifer* and *source* and the careful, precise hedging of a scientist who wouldn't say more than she could prove. She'd said *we don't know* and *working assumption* and *fixed*.

But the ocean was in his chest, and the ocean was vast, and three hours of channeling — of breaking stones and pegging instruments and heating rooms by accident — hadn't diminished it by any amount he could perceive. He searched inside himself for a change, a lessening, any sense that the reservoir had dropped. He found nothing. The fullness was the same. The pressure was the same. The warmth in his hands was the same.

*Vastly larger.* The words were Tessaril's, repeated from Vara's phrasing, offered with the reverent caution of someone describing something they'd never encountered. Orders of magnitude. Beyond anything on record. A deep source that feeds from below.

*Fixed,* Vara had said. *We don't know,* she'd also said. And: *the source is always finite.*

The ocean didn't need a bottom. It was an ocean.

He stood up from the stool. His legs were steady. His body felt the same as it had three days ago — strong, reliable, the body that had climbed forty flights of stairs every morning and carried loads and done everything by hand. The same body. But now the hands that had gripped timber and turned cranks and filed brass teeth were warm with something that cracked stone and bent metal and exceeded every measurement the city had to offer.

He flexed his fingers. The warmth responded — a pulse, a readiness, the power settling deeper into his bones with every hour that passed. It was becoming part of him. Three days, and it was already hard to remember what his hands had felt like before. Cold. They'd been cold, in the mornings, in the washroom, in the long walk up the stairs. His hands had been cold his entire life.

They would never be cold again.

* * *

Tessaril met him in the corridor outside the assessment room. She had a form — a standard trickle assessment form, he could tell, with fields for level rating and stability index and daily capacity estimate. She'd filled in what she could. Where the form asked for a numerical rating, she'd written a word instead. The same word, in every field.

*Unmeasurable.*

"This will go to Director Kharren's office," she said. "Along with the detailed notes. Vara — Scholar Solenne — will file a separate report with the Athenaeum. I expect you'll be hearing from both institutions."

"Thank you."

She hesitated. The professional mask slipped for a moment, and underneath it was a face that looked younger than her years, wide-eyed, the face of a person who had chosen a career in standardized measurement and had just encountered something that made the standards meaningless.

"I've been running trickle assessments for fourteen years," she said. "I've tested over four thousand channelers. Children, adults, elders. I've never—" She stopped. "I've never had to use a second form."

She handed him the form. He took it. The paper was warm where her hand had held it, and his hand was warmer, and the word *unmeasurable* looked back at him from every field like an answer to a question he'd been asking his whole life.

He walked up the corridor toward the ramp. The basement's rough stone gave way to the polished surfaces of the upper floors. The lattice strips in the walls grew brighter, the air cooler, the building transforming around him as he climbed from the raw infrastructure below to the functioning institution above. He passed clerks and administrators and channelers going about their work, none of them aware that the person walking past them had just exceeded every measurement their building could perform.

At the top of the ramp, where the corridor opened into the main hall, he stopped. The high windows let in the late afternoon light — three days past the equinox, the sun already angling toward the western edge of the plateau, the sky carrying the particular amber quality of autumn in a city that was slowly losing its warmth. The light fell across his hands.

Warm hands. Warm without channeling a heating lattice. Warm without the stove's iron grate or the friction of climbing stairs. Warm from the inside.

He thought about the word. *Wellspring.* A deep source that feeds from below. He thought about Vara's face when she said it — the careful placing of it, the awareness of weight. He thought about Tessaril's form and the word *unmeasurable* repeated in every field. He thought about the calibration stone, cracked clean, its lattice blazing white before it died.

He thought about his mother, lying in the Merival apartment with her legs in splints, the healer's channeled warmth moving through her bones. He thought about his father, carrying meals, steady-handed, carrying things the way he always had. He thought about Maren, standing in the dust of their destroyed apartment, staring at him with an expression he would carry for the rest of his life.

He thought about *fixed* and *finite* and *the source is always finite*.

The thoughts were there. They passed through him the way Vara's warning had passed through him — acknowledged, heard, present for a moment and then gone, settling below the surface of the vast, warm, singing pressure in his chest. He noted them. He filed them. He let them sink.

The ocean remained.

He walked out of the Hall of Infrastructure into the autumn light, and his hands were warm, and the word *wellspring* lived in his mouth like something he'd always known, and the city spread out before him in its tiers and terraces — the elder heights still gleaming, the lower districts already dimming, the whole vast mechanism of Solathis laid across the plateau like a problem he was only now beginning to understand he might be able to solve.

He took the ramp down. He didn't take the stairs.

For the first time in his life, he didn't need to prove he could.
