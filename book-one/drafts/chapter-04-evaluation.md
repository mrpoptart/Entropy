## Criterion-by-Criterion Analysis

### 1. Voice & Style

**Carver (9/10).** The closest match to the established voice in chapters 1-3. The prose is clean, declarative, and trusts the reader completely. Narrative distance modulates exactly as the style guide prescribes — medium baseline for the office scenes, deep close for the activation and aftermath. The "what to avoid" list is honored throughout: no named emotions, no exposition dumps, no adverb-heavy tags. The sentence rhythm matches Chapter 1's patterns precisely.

**Hearthkeeper (7/10).** Warmer and more expansive, but this warmth frequently tips into over-explanation. The narrator tells us what things mean rather than letting the images carry the weight. Sentences run longer on average, and the prose has a tendency toward explanatory apposition that the style guide specifically warns against. The draft frequently names emotions or adds interpretive glosses after physical details that were already doing the work ("the relief hit him in the chest like a fist — not sharp, but heavy, spreading"). The style guide says: show physical response, don't label.

**Lyricist (8/10).** Strong rhythmic control. The opening is the most efficient of the three — "The crack had grown three inches since Tuesday" lands harder than either competitor because of its concrete specificity. The Lyricist occasionally pushes into territory the style guide flags — some sentences are lyrically gorgeous but call attention to the writing. The closing pages are strong, with the layered "relief / guilt / strangeness" structure building cleanly. However, the Lyricist breaks POV discipline in a few spots, narrating Maren's interior with more certainty than Ash's limited perspective should allow.

### 2. Emotional Impact

**Carver (9/10).** The activation sequence is devastating because of what comes before it. The Carver earns the moment through accumulation: the thumb on the crack, the two-page brief, the mother's hands on his face, the "Good work" from his father. When the activation hits, the deep-close shift is surgical. The key passage — "He pushed, and something cracked. / Not the stone. Not the building. / Inside." — lands because the white space and sentence fragments slow the reader to Ash's own speed of comprehension. The aftermath is equally strong: "His mother was alive. His home was gone. Both of these things were because of him." No narrator interpretation. Just facts, laid side by side, and the reader does the emotional math.

**Hearthkeeper (8/10).** The parent scenes are the strongest of all three drafts. The mother saying "Let me have this" is a perfect beat that neither other draft achieves. The activation itself is powerful but slightly diluted by over-narration — the Hearthkeeper explains the emotions rather than trusting the physical rendering. The aftermath scene with the father ("His face collapsed — not dramatically, not with sound, just a settling of the features") is excellent.

**Lyricist (9/10).** The Lyricist's activation is the most visceral. The Maren moment afterward is the most emotionally complex of the three: "her face was something he would carry for the rest of his life." The closing pages are the strongest ending of the three drafts, with the rain metaphor ("the kind that waters the garden or the kind that brings the flood") providing a lyrical peak that feels earned.

### 3. Pacing & Structure

**Carver (8/10).** Five clean acts: building/crack — pitch to Dorenne — telling parents — the work day — catastrophe/aftermath. Each scene earns its length. The pre-crisis sections move briskly through three weeks of setup without feeling rushed. The crisis itself is well-timed — it arrives at the right point in the chapter and doesn't overstay. Weakness: the channeling observation section runs slightly long, with the channeling observation paragraphs repeating beats the reader already has.

**Hearthkeeper (6/10).** At approximately 7,800 words, this is the longest draft, and it feels it. The pre-crisis material is roughly 4,000 words — more than half the chapter — before the building starts to fail. The office scene is well-crafted but takes 50 lines for what the Carver accomplishes in 30. The Hearthkeeper's instinct to linger in relational warmth is a virtue in the parent scenes but a liability in the structural/technical passages.

**Lyricist (7/10).** The Lyricist makes an interesting structural choice by opening with the family scene (telling parents) rather than the office pitch. This compresses the first act but gives us the family dynamics earlier. The channeling work section is the best-paced of the three — the water-carrying detail is a perfect small beat that shows Ash's outsider position without dwelling. The crisis itself is well-paced. Weakness: the aftermath runs long, repeating emotional beats and saying "he looked at his hands" three times in the final 40 lines.

### 4. Character

**Carver (9/10).** Each character is rendered through behavior, not description. The father is captured in a single gesture: "The screwdriver went into his pocket." Dorenne's duality — the genuine care and the institutional calculation — is shown through action rather than explained. Maren is handled with perfect restraint: we get her through what Ash sees, and her interior is correctly withheld.

**Hearthkeeper (8/10).** The parents are the Hearthkeeper's greatest strength. The mother's "Let me have this" is the single best character moment across all three drafts. Dorenne is well-drawn but over-explained — the Hearthkeeper keeps telling us about Dorenne's duality when it should be showing it. Maren is slightly over-interpreted; the narrator tells us too much about her internal state for a third-person-limited POV locked to Ash.

**Lyricist (9/10).** Strong on all characters. Dorenne's "we'll figure it out" closing — "a warmth that was genuine and a weight that was strategic and a future that he could feel closing around him like a hand" — is the best single-sentence Dorenne characterization in any draft.

### 5. Worldbuilding Integration

**Carver (9/10).** Magic is shown entirely through use. The assessment team reading the building through their palms, the channelers' "particular focused absence" — all physical, observed, never explained. The structural failure is technically convincing without becoming a lecture.

**Hearthkeeper (8/10).** Generally strong. The crew leader's "stable the way a stack of books is stable — don't bump the table" is excellent worldbuilding through natural speech. However, the Hearthkeeper occasionally slides into exposition about how the magic works in a way the style guide specifically warns against.

**Lyricist (9/10).** The shear line detail — original builders fusing the building to the bedrock — is the most specific and convincing worldbuilding detail across drafts. The channelers swapping every twenty minutes when their trickles thin is magic shown through observed labor, exactly as the style guide prescribes.

### 6. Continuity

**Carver (9/10).** Consistent with chapters 1-3. The stairwell crack echoes the cold-water washing and stair-climbing from Chapter 1. The mother's warmth, the father's grip, Maren's composure — all match their established patterns. One minor issue: floor number needs standardization.

**Hearthkeeper (7/10).** Mostly consistent, but introduces some minor contradictions. The heating lattice timeframe varies from other drafts. The father's characterization is slightly warmer and more verbal than in Chapter 1, where he's established as deeply taciturn. The Hearthkeeper also has the mother using "Ashlyn" with a frequency that might dilute its emotional impact.

**Lyricist (8/10).** Good continuity with established voice and character patterns. Minor issue: uses "Tuesday" as a day name, while the Carver uses invented day-names that feel more consistent with the secondary world.

### 7. Seeds & Setup

**Carver (8/10).** Plants the "make it count" drive clearly. Seeds Dorenne's calculation without overplaying it. Correctly plants but doesn't resolve Maren's recalibration. Weakness: doesn't seed the physical residue of the power (the warmth in his hands) as strongly as the Lyricist does.

**Hearthkeeper (7/10).** Seeds Dorenne's protective/calculating duality well. However, the ending is too closed — feels like an ending rather than a hinge into Chapter 5. The chapter should propel the reader forward.

**Lyricist (8/10).** The strongest forward momentum of the three endings. "He closed his eyes. The humming didn't stop. It would never stop again" — the finality of that last sentence creates urgency to turn the page. The Dorenne "we" line is the best single seed for the patron-exploitation arc.

## Best Passages

### Carver

**The activation moment:**
> He pushed, and something cracked.
>
> Not the stone. Not the building.
>
> Inside. Deep inside, in a place he didn't have a name for — a place that had been sealed so long he'd mistaken the seal for the wall, the wall for the absence, the absence for who he was.
>
> It opened.

The best activation passage across all three drafts. The three-step metaphor — seal mistaken for wall, wall for absence, absence for identity — is the kind of controlled lyrical peak the style guide calls for: earned, inevitable, devastating.

**Dorenne's duality:**
> Ash said nothing. The arithmetic of Dorenne's world — every act of care measured against the ledger of what it displaced. He was learning the grammar. He didn't have to like all of its sentences.

### Hearthkeeper

**The mother's demand:**
> "Let me have this."

The best single line of dialogue across all three drafts. Three words that capture the mother's entire emotional history.

**The father's collapse:**
> His father's face collapsed — not dramatically, not with sound, just a settling of the features, the way a wall looks when the last of its structural channeling gives out and it returns to being simply stone.

### Lyricist

**The closing image:**
> He lay in the dark and felt the power humming in his blood and listened to it the way you listen to rain on a roof when you don't know yet whether it's the kind that waters the garden or the kind that brings the flood.

The strongest closing line across all three drafts.

**Dorenne's "we":**
> "We'll figure it out," she said. And the way she said *we* carried a warmth that was genuine and a weight that was strategic and a future that he could feel closing around him like a hand.

## Weakest Passages

### Carver
- The channeling observation section rehearses Ash's outsider position in language we've already internalized from chapters 1-3.
- The final paragraphs break POV by narrating what Dorenne is thinking and what Maren will do tonight.

### Hearthkeeper
- Explanatory glosses throughout — the prose explains emotional meaning after already showing it physically.
- The worst POV violation across all three drafts in the Maren section, narrating her interior experience with specificity Ash cannot know.
- The pre-crisis pacing loses momentum — 76 lines before trouble starts.

### Lyricist
- "He looked at his hands" repeated three times in the final 50 lines.
- Maren's interior narrated with specificity exceeding Ash's POV.
- "Tuesday" breaks secondary-world immersion.

## Recommended Path

### Primary Recommendation: Build from the Carver

The Carver should serve as the foundation draft. It is the closest to the established voice of chapters 1-3, the most disciplined in its POV, the most efficient in its pacing, and the most restrained in its emotional rendering — which, per the style guide, is where the power comes from.

### Passages to Transplant

1. **From the Hearthkeeper: the mother's "Let me have this."** Replace the Carver's parent-telling scene with the Hearthkeeper's approach. This is the single best character moment across all drafts.

2. **From the Lyricist: the closing image.** Replace the Carver's final paragraphs with the Lyricist's closing — the temporary housing, the blanket warmed by a stranger's trickle, and the rain-on-the-roof metaphor. This gives forward momentum into Chapter 5.

3. **From the Lyricist: Dorenne's "we" line.** Add the "We'll figure it out" beat with its analysis of the word "we" — seeds the patron arc more effectively.

4. **From the Lyricist: Ash bringing water to the channelers.** Replace the Carver's observation-from-outside paragraph with action rather than reflection.

5. **From the Hearthkeeper: the tea-temperature detail.** Weave into the office scene for character work.

### What Not to Pull

Do not pull the Hearthkeeper's extended pre-crisis pacing. Do not pull the Lyricist's Maren-interior passages without rewriting to stay within Ash's observational POV. Do not pull the Hearthkeeper's explanatory glosses on emotional meaning.

## Revision Notes

1. **Fix POV violations.** Every passage about what Maren "felt" or Dorenne "was already thinking" needs to be rewritten as what Ash *observes* — her face, her posture, her silence, her hands.

2. **Standardize the floor.** Pick fourth or fifth floor and be consistent.

3. **Standardize day-names.** Use invented names (Sixthday, Thirdday), not "Tuesday."

4. **Cut repeated "looked at his hands."** One instance is powerful. Choose the strongest and cut the others.

5. **Tighten the channeling-work section.** Target ~500 words of channeling observation before the groan starts. The Lyricist's water-carrying approach is the most efficient model.
