# Chapter 14

The bridge above the Thornwall market was one of the old ones — broad enough for three carts abreast, the stone railings carved with a motif of running water that had been someone's idea of poetry two centuries ago. The channeled reinforcement in its foundations was strong, elder-grade work that would hold for decades yet, and the foot traffic at this hour was light enough that the walkway felt almost spacious. Ash leaned against the eastern railing and watched the market below, the canopy stalls rippling in a wind that carried the smell of fried dough and lamp oil and the wet mineral tang of the gorge beyond.

He was early. He was always early now — not because he needed extra time for the stairs anymore, but because his days were so precisely scheduled that arriving early was the only time that belonged entirely to him. Dorenne's deployment calendar hung in his mind like a second skeleton: the Thornwall aqueduct tomorrow morning, the southern heating lattice in the afternoon, a coordination meeting with the district maintenance crews at the end of the day. He could feel the shape of the week ahead, the way it pressed against the reservoir's hum like a hand pressing into water, and the pressure was not unpleasant. It was purpose. It was direction.

Below him, a woman at a vegetable stall was warming a display case with her palm, the kind of casual, unconscious channeling that people did the way they breathed — a pulse of energy to keep the greens crisp, the root vegetables from going soft in the cold air. A young man at the next stall was doing the same thing badly, his warmth bleeding outward in a visible shimmer that heated the air a foot around his hand. Inefficient. The kind of waste that Ash would have noticed with envy three months ago and now noticed with the crisp, assessing eye of someone who thought about energy in kilojoules, in cost-per-effect, in the precise mathematics of what it took to keep a city standing.

*I could warm this entire market for an hour and not feel it.*

The thought arrived the way such thoughts did now — not as a boast, just a fact. A true thing about the world. The reservoir hummed behind his ribs, vast and patient, and the bridge beneath his feet was old work, good work, the kind of work that the Ancients had done when the world was full and power was as common as sunlight. He pressed his palm against the railing and felt the stone's channeled lattice like a distant heartbeat, steady and slow, the reinforcement holding cleanly after two hundred years. Someone had put this here and it had lasted. That was the kind of work he was doing now. That was what it meant to matter.

"You're standing like a statue."

He turned. Ryn was coming up the bridge from the western end, hands in her coat pockets, her shoulders hunched slightly against the wind that came off the gorge. She wore her work clothes — the heavy canvas jacket, the boots with the reinforced soles that she'd channeled firm herself — and her hair was shorter than the last time he'd seen her, cut close at the sides in a way that made her look sharper, older, more like the woman she was becoming and less like the girl who'd grown up three doors down from his parents' flat.

He hadn't seen her in — how long? He counted backward and the number surprised him. Two weeks. Maybe closer to three. The days had a way of collapsing into each other when every one of them was full, when every morning began with Dorenne's briefing and every evening ended with the satisfaction of systems stabilized and districts served and the warm, bone-deep knowledge that what he'd done today would still be standing tomorrow.

"I was watching the market," he said.

"You were standing on a bridge staring into the middle distance with a very serious expression." She reached the railing and leaned against it beside him, leaving a foot of space between their elbows. "That's different from watching the market."

"Is it?"

"Watching the market involves noticing that Velan's stall has moved to the east row and that he's overcharging for winter squash by about forty percent." She nodded toward the stalls below. "You didn't notice either of those things."

He looked down. She was right — Velan's stall had moved, the faded green canopy now wedged between the spice seller and the woman who repaired boots, and the squash displayed in neat rows was priced at figures that would have made his mother's mouth go tight. He hadn't noticed. He'd been looking at the channeling, the energy flows, the infrastructure of the place rather than the life happening inside it.

"I've been busy," he said.

"I know."

The wind came off the gorge and moved between them, carrying the sound of the market — the calls of vendors, the clatter of cart wheels on stone, a child somewhere laughing in the high, clear way that children laughed before they learned to moderate it. Ryn turned so her back was against the railing, her face toward the street instead of the gorge, and she looked at him.

"How are you?"

"Good," he said. "Really good. The deployment schedule is—"

"I didn't ask about the deployment schedule."

He stopped. She was watching him with the particular expression she wore when she was paying attention — not the lazy, half-focused look she gave most conversations, but the full weight of her regard, direct and steady and slightly narrowed at the edges. It was the look she'd given him when they were twelve and he'd fallen off the transit stairs and pretended his knee wasn't bleeding. It was the look she'd given him two years ago when he'd told her about the Dorenne position and she'd asked, quietly, if he was sure.

"I'm good," he said again, meaning it. "The work is — I'm doing real work, Ryn. The Thornwall aqueduct, the heating systems in the south, the retaining walls on the eastern edge — I spent three hours yesterday reinforcing a foundation that's been crumbling for a decade and a half, and when I was done, it was solid. The families in that building are going to have hot water for the first time since last spring. That's—" He caught himself gesturing. Dropped his hand. "That's what I'm doing."

"I know what you're doing," she said. "Everyone knows what you're doing. Tassa from my crew told me about the retaining wall. Her mother lives in that district."

"Then you know—"

"I know you're doing important work. I'm asking how you are."

The distinction should have landed. He heard the words and understood, somewhere behind the reservoir's hum, that she was drawing a line between the work and the person. But the line didn't hold. The work *was* how he was. The work was the first thing in his life that had ever felt like enough, and trying to separate himself from it was like trying to separate the bridge from its foundations — the structure didn't make sense without the part that held it up.

"I'm telling you how I am," he said. "I'm good because the work is good. The two aren't separate."

She looked at him for a long time. The wind moved her coat collar. Below them, the market continued its complicated business of feeding a city that didn't think much about where the warmth came from or who kept the water running.

"You talk about yourself differently now," she said.

He stiffened. "What do you mean?"

"I mean what I said. You talk about yourself differently." She pulled one hand from her pocket and gestured — a small, precise motion, the kind that in someone else might have been accompanied by channeling, a flicker of emphasis, a burst of color or light. In Ryn it was just a hand moving through the air, meaning exactly what it looked like. "Everything is about what you can do. The aqueduct. The retaining wall. The heating systems. You used to talk about — I don't know. The mechanism you were building with Haran. The thing your mother said that made you laugh. The way the light looked from the upper stairs on a clear day." She shrugged one shoulder. "Now it's deployments."

The bristle was immediate — a tightening in his chest that rose before he could think about it, the reservoir's hum sharpening for a half-second the way it did when something pressed against him and the power offered to press back. Not force. Not anything visible. Just the internal shift of a person who had spent his entire life without defenses and now had them and couldn't always tell when they were rising.

"That's not fair," he said.

"I'm not trying to be fair."

"You're saying I've changed."

"Everyone changes. I'm saying you talk about yourself differently."

"Because my life is different. Because I can do things now that I couldn't — do you know what that's like?" The question came out harder than he'd intended, and he heard it land between them like a stone dropped on the bridge. He took a breath. "You've had magic your whole life. You've never had to — you can't know what it means to finally not be—"

He stopped. The word sat in his mouth like something sharp, and he closed his teeth around it.

*Broken.*

He hadn't meant to say it. Hadn't meant to even think it, not here, not with Ryn, who had never once in their entire lives used that word about him or let anyone else use it in her hearing. She had punched Gavren Edds behind the school hall when they were thirteen because he'd called Ash "dead weight," and she'd gotten a split lip and a week's suspension and she'd told the supervising channeler that she'd do it again, and she would have. She would have done it every day for the rest of her life if that was what it took, and she had never — not once — treated him as though the absence of magic was the most important thing about him.

And now he was using it as a wall between them.

She looked at the railing. At the carved water motif, the stone worn smooth by two centuries of hands and weather. Her jaw was set in the particular way it set when she was choosing to let something pass, when the sharp thing she could have said was sitting right behind her teeth and she was making the deliberate, conscious decision not to release it.

"I am happy for you," she said.

The words were simple. They carried no edge, no irony, no undercurrent of qualification. She meant them. He could see it in the way she said them — the way her voice dropped half a register, the way her eyes came back to his and held there, steady and warm and carrying the full weight of twenty years of knowing him. She was happy for him. She had been happy for him since the moment the Wellspring activated, had felt the fierce, complicated gladness of someone who had watched the person she cared about most navigate a world that was built to exclude him, and who had wanted this for him — not the power specifically, not the Wellspring, but this: the feeling of belonging, of mattering, of not having to perform his okayness every single day.

She was happy for him. And she was also standing on a bridge in the cold, watching him become someone she didn't recognize, and the happiness and the loss existed in the same moment, in the same expression, in the same word.

"Ryn—"

"I have to get back to the crew," she said. She pushed off the railing. Her hands went back into her pockets. "Tassa's mother wanted me to thank you for the retaining wall. So — thank you."

She walked away down the bridge. Not quickly. Not slowly. The pace of a woman going back to work on a cold afternoon, her boots sounding on the old stone in a rhythm that he knew as well as he knew his own heartbeat, because he had walked beside that rhythm for most of his life and could measure its cadence the way a clockmaker measures a gear — by feel, by the frequency of a thing so familiar it had become a kind of silence.

She didn't look back.

He stood on the bridge. The market sounds rose around him. The wind came off the gorge, and the bridge held, and the reservoir hummed, and the afternoon light fell across the carved railing in a way that someone who was paying attention might have called beautiful.

He wasn't paying attention.

* * *

The house smelled like redwort stew.

It hit him at the front door — the rich, savory weight of it, the particular layering of flavors that meant his mother had started cooking hours ago and had not taken shortcuts. Redwort stew required time. You simmered the root until the fibers broke down and the broth thickened, and then you added the bone, and then the grain, and then the herbs at the end, each layer building on the one below it until the whole thing was dense and complicated and deeply, unreasonably good in the way that only food made by someone who loved you could be.

His mother made redwort stew twice a year. His birthday. And the day she decided, for no stated reason, that her son needed to come home.

He stood in the hallway and pulled off his boots. The house was warm — properly warm, the heating lattice in the walls running at a level that his mother couldn't sustain alone, which meant Maren had come early and topped it up, channeling the heat into the old stone with the discipline and efficiency that she brought to everything. The warmth wrapped around him as he stepped out of his boots and onto the smooth floor in his socks, and for a moment — a brief, unguarded moment — the reservoir's hum quieted beneath the simpler warmth of a house that had been made ready for him.

The hallway was the same as it had always been. Narrow, the walls close enough that he could touch both sides with his elbows. A hook for coats that his father had installed by hand when Ash was small, not channeled into the wall but screwed in with a metal bracket and determination. A mirror that his mother kept polished, its frame chipped in one corner where Maren had knocked it with a schoolbag twenty years ago. The floor was stone, worn in a path from the front door to the kitchen, a groove made by decades of the same feet walking the same route — father, mother, children, growing and changing and always returning to this narrow corridor that led to the room where the food was.

"In here," his mother called.

The kitchen. He crossed the threshold and the smell grew teeth, seized something in the back of his brain and pulled. His mother stood at the stove — not the channeling interface, never the channeling interface when she could help it, but the old iron stove that sat beside it like a stubborn relative who'd refused to leave. She cooked on it with wood fire when the lattice was being temperamental, and sometimes when it wasn't. The stove had been in the house longer than any of her children. She trusted it.

She turned from the pot and looked at him, and her face did the thing that faces do when love and worry are fighting for the same expression — a brightness that couldn't quite hold, a warmth that kept tilting toward something more complicated. She wiped her hands on her apron — the green one, the old one, the one she wore when she was making something important — and crossed the kitchen and put her arms around him.

He was taller than her. He'd been taller than her since he was sixteen, but the height had never changed the architecture of her embrace — she still held him the way she'd held him when he was small enough to fit entirely inside her arms, her hands flat against his back, her chin against his shoulder, the grip fierce and complete and lasting exactly one second longer than was comfortable, which was how he'd always known the difference between a greeting and a need.

"You're thin," she said, pulling back.

"I'm not thin."

"You're thin. You're not eating."

"I eat. Dorenne's office has a canteen—"

"Canteen food." She said it the way another person might say *poison* or *the decline*. "Sit down. Maren's setting the table."

The dining room was through the archway — a small room, barely large enough for the table and four chairs, the walls hung with two tapestries that his grandmother had channeled in the old way, the patterns woven and fixed in the fabric with energy so pure they hadn't faded in forty years. The table was set for four. His mother's good plates — the ones with the blue rim, the ones she only used when the occasion warranted it. A cloth on the table. Candles, lit by hand with a match, the way she always lit them, even though a flicker of channeling would have done it in a breath.

Maren was arranging the last of the flatware. She looked up when he came through the archway and smiled, and the smile was real and warm and carried the particular quality of a sister who was glad to see her brother — genuinely glad, the way you're glad to see someone you love even when the ground has shifted between you and neither of you has found the words for it yet.

"You made it," she said.

"Mother summoned me."

"Mother doesn't summon. Mother creates conditions in which refusing to appear would require a level of emotional fortitude that no human being possesses." She set the last fork in place and straightened. "It's redwort stew."

"I smelled it from the street."

"She started at noon."

His father came in from the back room, drying his hands on a cloth. He was wearing his workshop shirt — the one with the burn mark on the left sleeve from a soldering incident fifteen years ago that he'd never repaired, because he claimed it gave the shirt character. He was a quiet man, always had been, his presence in a room like the presence of furniture that was exactly the right height and depth and that you didn't think about until someone moved it. He saw Ash and his face opened in that particular way — not a smile exactly, something wider, something that started in the eyes and moved outward like light through a window.

"Ashlyn," he said.

He only used the full name in certain registers. This was the tender one — the one that meant *I am glad you are here, in this house, in this room, where I can see you*. He crossed to Ash and gripped his shoulder, and his hand stayed there for a beat, the fingers pressing once, firm and specific, a communication that passed through the bones and the muscle and the skin and said everything the man would never find words for.

"Dad."

"You look good. Tired, but good."

"Everyone keeps saying I look tired."

"You look like you're doing important work." His father released his shoulder and looked at the table, the candles, the good plates. "Your mother's been at this since morning. Don't tell her I told you she ironed the tablecloth."

"She doesn't own an iron."

"She channeled the wrinkles out. It took her forty minutes because she was determined to do it properly and the cloth kept slipping." His father's eyes crinkled. "I offered to hold it. I was told my help was not required."

They sat. His mother brought the stew to the table in the wide ceramic pot that had been in the family for three generations — handmade, its glaze crackled with age, the handles worn smooth where countless palms had gripped and lifted and carried. She set it in the center of the table with the care of someone placing something precious, and the steam rose in a column that caught the candlelight and turned it soft, golden, the smell filling the room until there was no space left for anything else.

His father served. Careful portions, the ladle dipping deep to find the chunks of root and bone. He passed the first bowl to Ash — always the first bowl, always had been, a small ritual that predated Ash's memory and that his father performed with the unselfconscious regularity of breathing. Maren received hers second. His mother last, settling into her chair with the sigh of a woman who had been on her feet for hours and was now, finally, permitted to sit.

"Tell us," she said. "Tell us everything."

He did. Or he tried to — he talked about the Thornwall aqueduct, the deployment schedule, the coordination meetings with the district maintenance crews. He described the retaining wall he'd reinforced yesterday, the way the stone had responded to the channeling, the old work underneath revealed by his power like text emerging on a heated page. He talked about the Council session, about Dorenne's proposal, about the scope of the infrastructure plan and his role in it.

He was halfway through describing the southern heating lattice — the eighteen hundred families whose homes would be warm by the end of the week — when he noticed Maren's hands.

She was holding her spoon above her bowl, both hands wrapped around it, not eating. Her thumbs were pressed together, knuckle against knuckle, and her eyes were on him with an expression that he recognized as interest — real, genuine interest — underlaid with something he couldn't name.

"How do you calibrate the channeling for the lattice?" she asked. "When you're working with an existing system that's been degraded — do you reinforce the original pathways or lay new ones?"

The question surprised him. It was a good question — a technical question, the kind that someone who worked with channeling systems would ask, specific enough to show real understanding. And something in him leaped at it, a warmth that had nothing to do with the reservoir, because this was Maren asking him about magic. This was his sister — who had been the channeler in the family, who had been the one with the pool and the discipline and the effortless competence — asking him how he did something she understood.

"Both, sometimes," he said, leaning forward. "The old pathways are usually still there — the stone remembers where the energy used to flow, and if the original work was good, you can feel the channels even when they've been dormant for years. So I start by tracing the existing paths, and if they're intact enough, I pour energy through them and let the system reactivate. But if the degradation has gone too far — if the channels are cracked or contaminated—"

"You have to lay new ones."

"Right. And new channeling in old stone is tricky because the crystalline structure has already been shaped once, and it resists being reshaped. It's like—" He searched for the analogy. "You know how a river cuts a channel, and if you try to divert it, the water keeps wanting to flow back to the old path?"

"I know exactly what that's like," she said, and for a moment — a single, bright moment — they were connected. Two people talking about the same thing, understanding it from different angles, the conversation flowing between them with the easy precision of a mechanism whose parts fit. He could see her eyes brighten, could see the mind behind them engaging with the problem, the disciplined intelligence that had made her career and her reputation turning toward something that her brother understood in a way that nobody else in this room could match.

Then he kept talking.

"The scale is the thing, though. With the southern lattice, I'm not reinforcing a single room or a single building — it's an interconnected grid across thirty-seven residential blocks, and the old pathways branch and rejoin and branch again, and holding the whole pattern in my mind while I'm channeling is—" He paused, trying to find the right frame for it. "It's like conducting an orchestra, except every instrument is made of stone and the music is heat and the conductor has to hear all thirty-seven parts simultaneously."

He meant it as description. He meant it to convey the complexity, the scope, the genuine difficulty of the work he was doing. But he heard it land across the table and understood, a half-second too late, what it sounded like from the other side — from the side of someone who maintained a single building's heating system and whose pool could sustain maybe two hours of that work on a good day before she needed to rest.

Maren's face didn't change. That was the thing about his sister — her face never changed when the ground shifted. She had been practicing that stillness since she was six years old, and the discipline ran deeper than bone, deeper than intention. She smiled. The smile was real. It was also complete, in the way that things are complete when they've closed around whatever was inside them.

"That sounds extraordinary," she said. She picked up her spoon.

The conversation moved on. Or rather, the conversation moved — it drifted sideways, the way water moves around a stone, finding a new path because the old one was blocked by something neither of them would name. His mother asked about the canteen food. His father asked about the transit systems — had Ash been using the lifts? Were the lifts working better now that the maintenance crews had additional support? His father loved the transit lifts. He was the kind of man who found genuine delight in civic systems that functioned, who noticed when the lights worked and the water ran and the streets were swept, and who attributed these small miracles to the accumulated effort of people doing their jobs well.

"The upper lifts are running on a full schedule for the first time in three years," Ash said. "The crews rebalanced the channeling loads after I reinforced the main shafts, and now the distribution is even enough that the automatic systems can handle the traffic. Dorenne's projecting that the entire eastern network will be back to full capacity within two weeks."

"That's wonderful," his father said, and he meant it — the warmth in his voice was real, the pride was real, the gladness was entirely genuine. "I remember when the lifts ran all night. You could ride them at three in the morning and they'd carry you smooth as glass. Your grandfather used to take the upper lift to the observation deck on clear nights — did I ever tell you that?"

"You've told me."

"He said you could see the Verenthi peaks from up there. The moonlight on the snow." His father's eyes went distant for a moment, the way they did when he was looking at something that wasn't in the room. "He'd ride the lift up and stand there for an hour, just looking. Said it was the best view in the city."

"He must have been proud," Ash said. "To live in a city that could build something like that."

"He was proud of many things." His father turned his glass slowly on the table — a quarter-turn, the way he turned things when he was choosing his words. "He was proud of the lifts. He was proud of the view. He was also proud of the time your grandmother spent three days fixing a fence by hand because the channeler she'd hired didn't show up, and she used wire and a borrowed hammer and the fence stood for twenty years." He took a sip. "He talked about that fence more than the lifts."

The silence that followed had a texture to it — soft, not heavy, the kind of silence that a family makes when someone has said something that everyone heard and no one is quite sure how to answer. Ash's father looked at the table. Maren looked at her stew. His mother looked at Ash.

She had been watching him. She had been watching him since he sat down, with the careful, sustained attention of a woman who had spent twenty-three years studying her son the way a navigator studies the sky — not to control it, but to understand where the weather was heading. Her eyes were dark and steady and they held a question that she would not ask and that he could not answer, because the question was not about the aqueduct or the deployment schedule or the heating lattice. The question was about the boy who had washed in cold water because he couldn't activate the heating. The boy who had woken before dawn to do chores as apology. The boy who had needed her, specifically and daily, in a way that had given her purpose and direction and a reason to get up in the morning that was fiercer and more consuming than any career or ambition or dream.

That boy was gone. The man in his place was extraordinary, was needed by a city, was important in a way that she understood was good and that she also understood had nothing to do with her. The old kettle was in the cabinet. She had bought a copper one two weeks ago — new, bright, efficient. She used the copper one for guests. When she was alone, she boiled water on the iron stove, the way she always had, because some things were not about efficiency.

She didn't say any of this. She ladled more stew into his bowl without asking if he wanted it, and the gesture was as old as he was and as familiar as his own name, and it meant *stay*.

"The stew is incredible," he said.

"Eat more."

"I've had two bowls."

"You've had one and a half. The first bowl was mostly broth. Eat the root."

He ate the root. It was perfect — soft enough to break apart on his tongue, the fibers dissolved by hours of heat into something that was more essence than vegetable, dense with the flavors of bone and herb and the particular richness that came from a pot that had been tended, checked, stirred, adjusted, tended again. His mother cooked the way Haran built mechanisms — with patience and attention and the accumulated knowledge of a thousand repetitions.

Maren refilled her own bowl. She ate with the methodical steadiness of someone restoring energy after a long day — precise bites, the spoon moving from bowl to mouth and back in an even rhythm. She was thinner than the last time he'd seen her. Not dramatically — he might not have noticed if the candlelight hadn't caught her wrists at a certain angle, showing the tendons standing more sharply than they should have in a woman her age. She was working hard. She was always working hard. But the word *hard* meant something different now than it had six months ago, because six months ago her pool had been enough for her days and now, maybe, it wasn't.

He almost asked. The question rose in him — *Are you okay? Is your pool holding? Are you sleeping enough?* — and then the Wellspring hummed, and the question felt wrong in his mouth, like a rich man asking a street vendor about the price of bread. What would he do with the answer? Offer to help? Pour some of his impossible, oceanic power into her systems to make her life easier? He could. He could reinforce her buildings, warm her home, take a hundred small burdens off her shoulders with a tenth of a percent of what he'd spent on the Thornwall aqueduct.

But Dorenne's voice was in his head — *the aqueduct serves forty thousand people* — and the math was clear, and the math was always clear, and the math said that what he spent on one person's comfort was power diverted from systems that kept thousands warm. So he didn't ask. He ate his stew and watched his sister's wrists in the candlelight and the distance between the question and the silence was a kind of failure that he did not recognize as failure, because it wore the costume of responsibility.

"More bread?" his father asked.

"Please."

His father tore a piece from the loaf — not cut, torn, the way he always served bread, a habit from his own childhood that he'd never bothered to change because it was the way his hands worked and the bread didn't care about presentation. He passed the piece to Ash and their fingers brushed, and his father held the contact for a fraction of a second, the way he held everything — doorframes, tools, his wife's elbow when they walked — with a gentleness so habitual it looked like accident.

"Your mother and I went to the rebuilt district last week," his father said. "The Saddler's Row section, where you reinforced the foundations. The family with the new baby — the Vellands — they invited us in."

"They invited you in?"

"Word gets around. People know we're your parents." His father shrugged — a small, self-effacing motion that carried more pride than he would ever voluntarily express. "They wanted to show us the walls. The baby's room. The heating works now — the lattice runs clean, they said, cleaner than it ever has." He paused. "The baby's name is Ashwin."

"I know," Ash said. "I met them."

"I know you met them. They told us. They told us you sat in their kitchen and drank tea and held the baby." His father's voice was doing the thing it did when emotion ran underneath his words like water under ice — the surface steady, the current visible only in the way the light moved. "They said you were kind. That you didn't rush."

"I wasn't—" Ash started, and then stopped, because something in his father's face told him that the story wasn't about the Vellands. It was about the boy who would have sat in that kitchen regardless — who would have held that baby and been kind and not rushed, whether he had a Wellspring or a pool or nothing at all. His father was trying to remind him of something. The reminder was gentle, oblique, filtered through a story about strangers because directness in this family had always been the province of the women, and the men said what they meant by saying something else entirely.

"They were nice," Ash said. "The baby is very small."

"Babies usually are."

His mother refilled his water glass. His father turned his bread over in his hands. Maren was looking out the window, where the evening had deepened to the particular blue that preceded full dark in Solathis — a deep, saturated color that came from the city's altitude and the way the stone of the plateau caught and held the last light long after the lower lands had gone to shadow.

They were all here. His family, in this room, around this table. The stew cooling in the pot. The candles burning at the pace candles burn — slowly, steadily, transforming wax into light without anyone needing to pour energy into them. His father's hand on the bread. His mother's eyes on him. Maren's silence, which was not the silence of someone with nothing to say but the silence of someone who had chosen, carefully and with the discipline of a lifetime, to hold what she was feeling in a place where it couldn't disrupt the evening.

He should stay. He knew he should stay, the way you know that a mechanism needs oil before it starts to grind — not because anyone has told you but because you can hear the small sound it makes when something isn't quite right. The kitchen was warm and the stew was his mother's best and his father had told him a story about a baby that was really a story about love, and every one of these details was a hand reaching for him, and every one of these hands was real.

The reservoir hummed.

Tomorrow was the Thornwall aqueduct. Seven hundred meters of failing channelwork, the joints degraded to sixty percent integrity, the flow regulators pulling water through fractures that had widened incrementally over the past decade. Dorenne's engineers estimated ten hours of sustained channeling to bring it back to operational standard. Ten hours of his time, forty thousand people's water. The numbers were clear. The numbers were always clear.

"I should go," he said.

His mother's hands stopped moving. She was holding a serving cloth — folded, the edges aligned, the way she folded everything — and her hands stopped on the fabric and stayed there, very still.

"You just got here," she said.

"I know. I have — tomorrow's a big deployment. The aqueduct. I need to rest, and the walk home takes—"

"You could stay here."

"The deployment starts at dawn. If I stay here, I have to add forty minutes to—"

"You could stay here," she said again, and the repetition carried weight that the words themselves didn't — a weight that came from the particular register of a mother who had said *stay* to this boy ten thousand times and who understood, with the precise, unarticulated knowledge of twenty-three years, that this time the word was not going to work.

"Next time," he said.

His father stood when he stood. Came to the door with him, the way he always did — a small ritual, an escort to the threshold, the last few steps of togetherness before the door opened and the world came in. Maren stayed at the table. She raised her hand — a wave, small, her fingers curling in the motion she'd used since she was a child, the little wave that had always meant *see you soon* and that now, he understood without examining the understanding, meant something less certain.

At the door, his father helped him with his coat. Not because he needed help — because his father's hands needed something to do, and the coat was there, and helping was the language this man spoke when the real language failed him.

"Thank you for dinner," Ash said. "Tell Mother—"

"I'll tell her."

His father's hand came up to his shoulder. Rested there. The grip was the same as it had always been — firm, specific, the hand of a man who had spent his life working with tools that required precision and who brought the same precision to the way he touched his children. But the hand stayed longer than usual. A beat. Two beats. The fingers pressing once, then not releasing, then pressing again, as though the hand was trying to say something that the mouth could not, something about love and pride and a question that lived underneath both: *Do you know I was proud of you before this? Do you know my love was not waiting for you to become extraordinary?*

"Dad."

"Go on." His father released him. Stepped back. "Rest well. The aqueduct needs you."

The door closed behind him. The evening air was cold — properly cold, the kind of cold that Solathis got when the season turned and the upper districts' heating lattices drew energy away from the lower atmosphere, creating a temperature gradient that you could feel in your teeth. He pulled his coat tight and started walking.

Behind him, the windows of his parents' house glowed with candlelight. Not channeled light — candles. His mother's candles, burning at the pace candles burn. Inside, he knew without seeing, his mother was clearing the plates. His father was drying them. Maren was wiping down the table with the cloth that lived in the drawer by the window, the same cloth that had been wiping that table since before any of them could remember. The stew pot would be left on the cold stove with a lid on it, and tomorrow morning his mother would reheat it for breakfast, because redwort stew was better the second day and nothing in that kitchen was wasted.

He walked. The streets were quiet at this hour — the evening traffic thinned out, the transit lifts running on their reduced nighttime schedule, the city settling into the rhythms of a world that still, despite everything, needed sleep. He passed through the market district where the stalls were closing, the vendors pulling tarps over their displays and heading home, the woman with the vegetable stall channeling her warmth into her hands now instead of the display case, conserving what she had left for the walk back to wherever she lived.

The reservoir hummed. The aqueduct waited. Forty thousand people's water.

He didn't think about the bridge. He didn't think about Ryn standing at the railing, her hands in her pockets, saying *I am happy for you* in a voice that meant it completely. He didn't think about the silence after, the space where something should have gone — an apology, a question, an acknowledgment that the person standing across from him had just told him something true and that he had responded by building a wall out of the best thing that had ever happened to him.

He didn't think about Maren's wrists in the candlelight. He didn't think about his mother's hands stopping on the folded cloth. He didn't think about his father's fingers on his shoulder, pressing and pressing, trying to say the thing.

He thought about the aqueduct. Seven hundred meters. Ten hours. Sixty percent integrity. The numbers were clean and the work was good and the reservoir was vast and tomorrow he would hold a system together that kept forty thousand people alive, and that mattered, that was real, that was the most important thing he had ever done or would ever do.

The numbers were clean.

The evening closed around him. He walked alone through a city that he was holding together, past buildings where people slept warm because of something he had done, under a sky that was vast and dark and held no opinion about the young man moving through its indifference with a reservoir full of power and a home he'd left too early and a silence in his chest where something used to live that the hum of the Wellspring had gently, thoroughly, without malice or intention, replaced.
