# Chapter 2

He arrived at the Hall of Infrastructure an hour before dawn, which meant he'd left the apartment ninety minutes before that, which meant he'd been awake since some lightless hour he'd stopped tracking after the second cup of tea. His mother had left a bowl of porridge on the stove, still warm from her channeling — a small, daily kindness measured in kilojoules she couldn't spare, performed so quietly that he sometimes wondered if she thought about it at all, or if it had simply become part of the rhythm of her body: wake, channel the stove, go back to sleep.

He'd eaten standing up. He'd washed the bowl by hand. He'd laced his boots in the dark.

The ramp Dorenne had shown him was open. A maintenance corridor that curved along the building's outer wall, wide enough for equipment carts, lit by lattice strips that glowed faintly with channeled warmth from the previous shift. He climbed it in long, easy strides — forty flights of ramp where forty flights of stairs waited on the other side of the building, available to anyone whose trickle could activate the transit lift that made the stairs unnecessary. His breath quickened by the twentieth flight. By the thirtieth, his calves were tight. By the fortieth, his vest was damp with sweat that he blotted away before anyone could see, standing in the upper corridor with his back against the wall, breathing until his heartbeat settled.

The infrastructure office was dark. He was the first one here.

He stood in the doorway and let his eyes adjust. The room was large — larger than he'd registered yesterday, when Dorenne had walked him through it with the brisk narration of someone who'd given the tour too many times. High ceilings, arched in the old style, the stonework so precise that the joins were invisible. A long central table covered in rolled charts and weighted with stones that served as paperweights and, he suspected, channeling interfaces. Desks along the walls, each buried under stacks of reports and assessment folios. A shelf of instruments he couldn't name and wouldn't know how to use. Maps pinned to a board showing the city's districts in colored overlays — green for stable, amber for stressed, red for critical.

He studied the board. He could see the pattern — the reds clustered in the lower districts, the greens holding firm in the elder quarters above — but the legend beside it was dense with abbreviations he didn't recognize. *KJ/m², RI-3, Trickle-Adj.* Numbers that meant something to the people who worked here and nothing to him. Not yet.

The room smelled like ink, stone dust, and the faint metallic tang that channeling left in the air — that same ozone sharpness he'd noticed in the market district, but older here, soaked into the walls by decades of concentrated work.

He found what he assumed was the aide's desk — the smallest one, pushed into a corner near the door, bare except for a blotter and a jar of pens. He sat down. He straightened the jar. He looked at the empty desktop and the room beyond it — the desks where people who mattered would sit in a few hours, the table where decisions that affected thousands were made daily — and thought: *This is mine.*

The thought was absurd. It was a desk. A surface. But his hands rested on it with a weight that felt like claiming something, and he let them stay there for a long moment before standing again.

Then he didn't know what to do.

He stood by his desk in the dark office and realized he had no instructions, no tasks, no orientation materials. He had arrived an hour early because he couldn't bear to be late, and now the hour stretched ahead of him, empty and silent, and the only thing he knew how to do in an empty room was make it less empty.

He started with the central table. Reports stacked without visible logic, charts curling at the edges, a cold cup of tea that someone had abandoned days ago judging by the skin floating on its surface. He didn't know the filing system yet, so he didn't reorganize — but he sorted by size, aligned the edges, cleared the cup, and wiped down the surface with a cloth he found in a cabinet. His hands knew this work. His hands had always known this work — finding the mess, making it less, earning his presence through the evidence of his effort.

He was wiping down the table for the second time when the first of the staff arrived.

* * *

Her name was Tessaly, and she was Dorenne's senior scheduler — a lean, sharp-featured woman in her forties who walked into the office carrying two leather-bound ledgers and the expression of someone who had already completed three tasks in her head before her body arrived. She stopped when she saw Ash standing at the central table with a cloth in his hand.

"New aide."

It wasn't a question. Not a greeting either — a classification. She was filing him before she'd finished walking through the door.

"Since yesterday," he said.

"The one who can't—" She caught herself with the smooth recovery of someone who'd been in institutional settings long enough to know which words landed badly. "Right. Dorenne mentioned." She set her ledgers on the central table and glanced at the cleared surface — the aligned edges, the clean wood, the absent teacup. The look was brief and evaluating, the kind that noticed everything and withheld approval on principle. "You're the fourth."

"Fourth?"

"Aide. In four years." She was already moving, pulling a chart from a shelf behind her desk. "The last one cried on day three. The one before that stopped showing up. The one before that—" She shrugged, a motion so efficient it barely qualified as a gesture. "Dorenne doesn't hire easy jobs."

She didn't ask if he was ready. She didn't welcome him. She started the orientation.

Tessaly's briefing was thorough and delivered at a pace that suggested she had learned to compress information the way a channeler compressed force — efficiently, without waste, and with the implicit expectation that you would keep up or be left behind. The office managed the systems that kept Solathis functioning: water distribution, structural reinforcement, heating grids, transit networks, and the emergency reserves that were supposed to handle crises but had been quietly drained by the ongoing demands of daily maintenance. Each system had a team. Each team submitted daily assessments. The assessments were consolidated into a brief that Dorenne reviewed every morning before her first meeting, which was always with the Council's resource committee, which was always difficult.

"Brief on her desk by seventh bell," Tessaly said, tracing the schedule with one finger. "Reports in by sixth. Team leads submit by fifth. Half will be late. You chase."

"How do I chase them if I can't use the resonance stones?"

Tessaly's finger stopped. He watched her recalculate — a brief, visible adjustment as she accounted for a limitation she wasn't used to working around. Resonance stones were the office's communication backbone: palm-sized stones attuned to each other, activated by channeling, carrying brief messages between paired sets. Every desk had one. Every team lead carried one. The entire logistics of the office flowed through them.

Her brow furrowed — not with irritation, but with the particular expression of someone encountering a constraint she hadn't budgeted for. "That's going to slow us down."

"I have legs."

"Legs." She said the word as though evaluating it as a technology. "Laris on the third level is always late. Start with him. Fourth door on the left past the archives."

She gave him a list of the team leads, their locations in the building, and the order in which they were most likely to be late. He wrote it down — all of it, in the hurried, cramped handwriting of someone who knew his memory was good but not perfect, who'd learned years ago that the cost of forgetting was always higher than the cost of a few extra seconds with a pen.

"One more thing." Tessaly was already at her desk, opening a ledger. She didn't look up. "Don't try to impress anyone on day one. Just learn where things are."

* * *

The fourth door on the left past the archives had no handle.

Ash stood in front of it, his hand half-raised in a knock that had nowhere to land. The door was smooth stone — no knocker, no bell pull, no latch. A channeled lock, of course. Everyone in the building announced their arrival via resonance stone, and the person inside opened the door with a pulse of their trickle. The system was elegant, seamless, and assumed that everyone who used it could channel. Because everyone could. Because why would you build a door for someone who couldn't?

He knocked on the wall beside the door frame. His knuckles made a dull, flat sound against the stone. Nothing happened.

He knocked again, harder. The sound didn't carry — the walls were thick, the stone channeled-dense. Inside, Laris was doing whatever Laris did when reports were due and nobody had bothered him yet.

Ash stood in the corridor. A woman passed behind him, glanced at him standing at the closed door, and kept walking. She hadn't slowed enough to offer help, but she'd slowed enough to notice, and the noticing was its own small weight.

He waited. He leaned against the wall and looked at his boots. He counted the stones in the floor. He waited longer.

After several minutes, the door opened from inside. A young man — one of Laris's assistants, carrying a stack of folios — stepped out and nearly walked into him.

"Oh — sorry. Are you—"

"The new aide. For the Director's office. I'm here for Laris's report."

The assistant looked back into the room. "Laris?"

From somewhere inside: "What?"

"Someone for the report."

A long pause. Then: "It's not ready."

The assistant gave Ash a look that was half apologetic and half *what did you expect*, and pulled the door shut behind him. The channeled lock engaged with a soft click that Ash felt more than heard.

He walked back up the ramp to Tessaly's desk. "Report's not ready."

Tessaly didn't look up from her ledger. "Noted." She turned a page. "Try Venn. Sixth level. Her door has a handle."

* * *

The morning brief was at seventh bell, and Ash had spent the intervening hour carrying documents between floors — walking the ramp, knocking on doors that sometimes opened, standing in corridors waiting for doors that didn't. He had delivered two reports successfully, failed to collect three others, and discovered that the supply closet on the fourth level had a manual latch, which he filed in his mind as the kind of information that would matter more than it should.

He sat in the corner of the briefing room with a notebook and tried to follow what was happening.

Dorenne presided from the head of the central table, her staff arrayed around her in an order that Ash recognized, after twenty minutes of observation, as a hierarchy of function rather than rank. The people closest to her weren't the most senior — they were the ones whose systems were currently in crisis. Today that meant the water team and the structural reinforcement team, whose leads sat at Dorenne's right and left like anxious bookends.

The water team's report was bad. The main aqueduct serving the western districts — the Thornwall line — had developed a channeling fracture at a junction point, reducing flow by a third. The repair required a sustained channeling effort that would pull resources from two other maintenance schedules. The structural team's report was worse: three buildings in the lower districts had been flagged for emergency reshoring, and they didn't have the channelers to cover all three before the weather turned.

Ash wrote down everything. Not because he'd been told to — Tessaly had said to listen — but because his mind worked better when his hands were moving, and the act of transcription forced him to parse the language in real time. He caught maybe half the technical vocabulary. *Channeling fracture* — he understood the words separately but not the mechanics. *Pressure tolerances* — something about how much force a pipe could handle, maybe, or how much a channeler could sustain. *Kilojoule budgets* — this one he almost understood, the idea that power was finite and had to be allocated, but the specific numbers meant nothing to him.

What he did understand was the room. Who was scared — the structural lead, Venn, whose hands gripped the edge of the table whenever she reported a number. Who was performing confidence — Laris, the water lead, whose voice carried a false steadiness that cracked when Dorenne asked a question he didn't expect. Who deferred to whom, and who avoided meeting whose eyes, and the particular silence that fell when Dorenne spoke, which wasn't fear but something closer to gravity — the way a room reorganized itself around the densest object.

When the brief ended, Dorenne's staff dispersed with the controlled urgency of people who had too much to do. Dorenne caught his eye across the room.

"Your take on the brief."

He'd been hoping for a few more days before she tested him. A few more briefings to absorb the vocabulary, understand the systems, build a frame before anyone asked him to say something into it.

"The structural team is more worried than the water team," he said. "Venn's scared. Laris is—" He searched for the right word. "Laris is performing."

Dorenne waited. The silence was an invitation to continue.

"I think the water problem might be worse than the structural one, because Laris is working harder to look calm. When the problem's manageable, people report it straight. When it's bad, they polish the language."

Dorenne looked at him for a long moment. He couldn't read the expression — it was too layered, too controlled. Then she said, "Laris has been polishing since last quarter because his wife is on the Council's resource committee and he doesn't want to create a conflict of interest. The water problem is serious, but contained. Venn's scared because she's right to be scared."

Ash's face warmed. He'd read the room correctly and the situation wrong — seen the emotional dynamics clearly but drawn the wrong conclusion because he didn't know the texture underneath. The embarrassment sat in his hands, in the way he gripped his notebook.

"Keep listening," Dorenne said. And turned back to her desk.

He sat with the notebook and the warmth in his face and thought: *That's what it feels like to be wrong in a room where being wrong matters.* It wasn't like being wrong at home, where his mother would smooth it over and his father would change the subject. It was clean and quick and didn't come with comfort afterwards. He wasn't sure yet whether that was better or worse.

* * *

The rest of Day One passed in a blur of corridors and closed doors and the particular exhaustion of navigating a building designed for people who could send a pulse ahead of them to announce their arrival, request a document, or call for a door to open.

He left the Hall of Infrastructure as the last of the light was draining from the sky. His legs ached from the ramp — the deep, cellular ache of muscles used past their capacity, different from the familiar tiredness of stairs because the ramp was steeper and longer and he'd climbed it eleven times in a single day. He walked the forty flights down in the growing dark, his hands trailing the wall for balance because his legs had stopped being reliable somewhere around the twentieth flight.

At the apartment, his mother was asleep. The stove was warm. He ate cold rice standing in the kitchen, his body too tired to sit down because sitting down meant staying down, and he still needed to unlace his boots and wash his face and study the list of team leads he'd written in his notebook. He sat on the edge of his bed and read the list by candlelight, mouthing the names. Laris — water. Venn — structural. Parren — Thornwall district. Sevin — transit. Osten — archives. He fell asleep with the notebook on his chest.

* * *

Day Two started at the same lightless hour, with the same porridge, the same ramp, the same dark office.

Tessaly arrived and handed him a stack of route sheets — the building's maintenance crews and their daily assignments, printed on thin paper in small type, organized by district. "Learn these," she said. "You're going to be carrying them."

He carried them. Between the third level and the sixth level, between Laris's locked door and Venn's open one, between the supply closet on the fourth floor (manual latch, which he now thought of as *his* door) and the records room on the third level, where the archivist held court.

Osten was a problem.

The records room was a vast, dim space lined with shelves that reached the ceiling, every surface covered in folios and bound reports and loose pages weighted with stones. Osten — elderly, white-haired, built like a man who had been sitting in the same chair for thirty years and had become part of the furniture — looked up when Ash entered and began talking.

About the filing system. About its history. About the reorganization of 1847 and the inferior reorganization of 1862 and the catastrophic attempt at modernization in 1880 that he had personally reversed over the course of fourteen months. About the difference between chronological and categorical indexing. About the western district folios and why they were stored separately from the eastern district folios, which related to a jurisdictional dispute from before the current Council was seated.

Ash stood at the counter and nodded. He tried twice to interject — "I'm here for the Thornwall assessment from—" — and was carried past both attempts on the current of Osten's monologue like a leaf on a river. Twenty minutes later, he excused himself and returned to Tessaly's desk without the document.

"Osten?" Tessaly said, without looking up.

"Osten."

"Hmm." She turned a page in her ledger. It might have been the closest thing to sympathy he'd seen from her.

At midday, he stood in the corridor where the food vendors set up their carts — three of them, their displays glowing with channeled warming plates that kept the bread hot and the cheese melted. He pulled coins from his pocket and counted them in his palm. Six quarter-marks. Enough for a full meal, but a full meal today meant no meal tomorrow, and he wouldn't be paid until the end of the week. He bought the smallest option — a piece of flatbread with oil and salt, no filling — and ate it on the stairs because the corridor had no seating and the break room required a channeled door he couldn't open.

* * *

Day Three was when the legs became a real problem.

The ramp was steeper than stairs, and the distance from top to bottom was longer. He'd been climbing it twelve to fifteen times a day — four round trips for morning reports, additional trips for afternoon deliveries, plus the twice-daily transit between his desk and the records room. By Day Three, his calves were seizing on the upper flights. He learned to pause at the halfway point, pressing his palms against the wall and stretching while pretending to study the maintenance notices posted on the corridor boards.

The pretense was useful. The maintenance notices were informational — work orders, schedule changes, system status updates, posted for any staff who passed. He started reading them because they were there and he was standing still, and then he kept reading them because they were written in plainer language than the briefing reports and he could actually understand most of what they said. Junction seven offline for repair. Junction nine depressurized for maintenance. Thornwall backup line rerouted through Bridgeway secondary.

He filed the information the way he filed everything — automatically, without deciding to, the way his hands sorted tools or his feet memorized stairwell patterns. It didn't feel important. It was just the texture of the building, absorbed through proximity.

On the sixth level, his right calf locked. He stopped in the corridor, his weight on his left leg, his jaw tight against the pain. He pressed his palm against the muscle and kneaded, hard, the way his father had taught him to work out cramps from climbing. A staffer passed him — a young woman carrying a resonance stone, walking easily in the way people walked when their bodies had never been their primary tool. She glanced at him. He straightened before she could ask.

That afternoon, Tessaly appeared at his desk with a revised errand schedule. She set it down without comment — a sheet of paper with his afternoon deliveries reorganized. The sixth-level drop-offs had been grouped together. The third-level pickups had been consolidated into a single trip. The routing was efficient in a way that his original schedule hadn't been, saving him roughly four ramp transits.

He looked at the schedule. He looked at Tessaly, who was already walking back to her desk.

"This is—" He stopped. *This is kind*, he wanted to say. But that wasn't how she'd hear it, and it wasn't how she'd meant it. She'd looked at a system, seen an inefficiency, and corrected it. That the inefficiency happened to be his body didn't make it personal. That was what made it personal.

"Thank you," he said instead.

"Mm." She sat down. She opened her ledger. The conversation was over.

* * *

On Day Three he also made a mistake.

A scheduling notice for the Thornwall district maintenance crew — a routine update about a shifted start time — had come to his desk along with six other documents that needed routing. He'd sorted them by destination and walked them to the appropriate desks. The Thornwall notice went to Parren's mailbox in the corridor outside the crew room.

Except it didn't. It went to Sevin's mailbox — transit, not Thornwall. The mailboxes were side by side, both labeled in the cramped shorthand of people who didn't expect outsiders to use their system. Ash had read the labels quickly, in a corridor lit by fading lattice strips, and his eyes had skipped.

The result: the Thornwall crew arrived at junction seven at their usual hour while a different crew was already working the same section. Two teams, one junction, not enough channeling capacity to sustain both safely. Nobody was hurt. But the dispatch coordinator spent thirty minutes untangling the overlap, and Parren — the Thornwall lead — came to the office to lodge a formal scheduling complaint, which he delivered to Tessaly in a voice that carried to every desk in the room.

Ash sat at his desk and felt the error settle into his stomach like a stone. A small mistake. A misread label. The kind of thing anyone could do and most people had done and nobody would remember in a week. But his body didn't know that. His body knew what mistakes meant for people like him — people who were already a favor, already a concession, already the person someone had to justify hiring. Every error was evidence. Every failure was confirmation of the thing everyone was already thinking: *He shouldn't be here.*

He waited for Dorenne to say something. She'd been in her office during Parren's complaint. She'd heard. She passed his desk twice that afternoon, and both times she handed him a task — a set of folios to reorganize, a draft response to file — without referencing the mistake. Without acknowledging it. Without offering comfort or correction or the kind of carefully modulated feedback that he'd braced for.

The absence of comment was worse than any reprimand would have been, and also better. It left him alone with the shame, which was terrible. It also left him alone with the next task, which meant the mistake hadn't become his identity. Dorenne treated him the same way she'd treated him yesterday: someone to give work to. Not someone to feel sorry for.

That night, he skipped dinner. His mother had left rice and a small bowl of pickled vegetables, and the smell of them was warm and familiar and made him feel, irrationally, like he didn't deserve the food. He went to bed hungry and lay in the dark thinking about mailbox labels and junction numbers and the look on Parren's face when he'd come into the office — that particular combination of annoyance and vindication, the expression of a man who hadn't expected much from the new aide and was satisfied to be proven right.

* * *

By Day Four he had a map.

Not an official one — not the kind that hung on the briefing room board with its colored overlays and technical annotations. A personal map, drawn in his notebook during the moments when he was waiting for doors to open, sitting on the ramp landing stretching his legs, or standing in the corridor outside a channeled lock he couldn't pass.

The map showed every door in the building that had a manual handle. Twelve of them, scattered across seven levels, connected by corridors and ramp sections that he'd walked enough times to know by the texture of the stone under his boots. The map also showed which doors were usually propped open, which ones had staff who arrived early and left them unlocked, and what times of day certain corridors were empty enough that he could knock on the wall without anyone watching.

It was, he realized, a map of workarounds. A route through the building that avoided the things he couldn't do, connecting the spaces where his limitations were invisible to the spaces where they were merely manageable. He'd been making maps like this his entire life — the streets where the transit stones were spaced wide enough that he could cross without activating them, the market stalls that took coins instead of channeled tokens, the stairwells in his parents' building that didn't require a trickle to light. The infrastructure of a life lived around the edges of a world built for someone else.

He didn't show it to anyone. He didn't think of it as clever. It was just the thing he did.

On Day Four he also tried Osten again. This time, instead of asking for a specific document, he asked about the records room itself — when had the current shelving been installed? Was it the original configuration or had it been modified? The question was calculated, but not cynically — he was genuinely curious, and curiosity was one of the few currencies that Osten accepted at face value.

Osten's eyes lit up. The shelving, he explained, had been installed during the reorganization of 1847, which he considered the last competent administrative act in the building's history. He talked for twelve minutes about load-bearing shelves and document preservation and the pH of the paper they'd used before the council switched suppliers.

And then — in the gap between one sentence and the next, in the small breath where Osten paused to remember a date — Ash said, "Would the Thornwall assessment from last quarter be in the 1847 section or the post-1862?"

"Forty-seven, obviously. Third shelf from the left, second row from the bottom." Osten produced the document from the stacks in under a minute, handling it with the speed and precision of a man who had organized this room for thirty years and resented anyone who implied it needed reorganizing. "Tell Tessaly she needs to send someone who knows how to ask."

Ash brought the document to Tessaly's desk. She looked at it, looked at the timestamp, looked at him. "Under a minute."

"I asked about the shelving."

A pause. Then the faintest shift at the corner of her mouth — not a smile, something smaller, something she'd deny if he pointed it out. "Good." She took the document. The conversation was over.

* * *

By the end of the first week, Ash's understanding of the office had shifted from fog to outline. Not clarity — not yet — but the shapes were emerging. He knew the rhythms: the morning brief at seventh bell, the afternoon lull when half the staff went to field sites, the late-day surge when assessment reports came in and Tessaly turned into a machine of sorting and prioritization. He knew which team leads submitted on time (Venn, always) and which ones needed chasing (Laris, always; Parren, when he was angry about something, which was often). He knew how the resonance system sounded when a new message arrived — a faint hum that everyone else processed unconsciously and that he'd learned to notice from across the room, because the hum meant information was moving and he needed to be ready to move with it, on foot, the slow way.

He'd been sitting in on the daily briefs for five days. He didn't speak unless asked, and Dorenne hadn't asked since that first morning when he'd read the room right and the situation wrong. He took notes. He studied the technical vocabulary at night, reading his own transcriptions by candlelight and cross-referencing them with the maintenance notices he'd been absorbing from the corridor boards. Slowly — very slowly, the way water wore stone — the language was becoming less opaque.

The scheduling conflict came to him on a Friday.

Not as a flash of insight. Not as a problem he set out to solve. It arrived the way most useful knowledge arrived for him — through accumulation, through proximity, through the particular advantage of being the person who walked the building instead of sending messages through it.

The Thornwall crew and the Bridgeway crew had been fighting over access to the western aqueduct for months. Both needed to work on the same line at the same time. The Thornwall crew was doing emergency repairs. The Bridgeway crew was doing routine maintenance with a standing Council authorization. Neither would yield. The conflict had been landing on coordination desks for six months, passed between staff who had more urgent fires and less patience for a problem that was more bureaucratic than technical.

Ash had been carrying documents between these offices all week. He'd been walking the ramp past the maintenance notices about junction statuses. He'd been reading the aqueduct schedules because Dorenne had told him to learn the western district maps and the aqueduct schedules were part of the maps. And he'd been standing on the ramp landing on Day Three, stretching his cramping legs, staring at a notice about junction nine being taken offline for an unrelated repair, and the number had gone into his head along with all the other numbers — junction seven, junction twelve, the gap between them, the pressure tolerances, the depressurized section.

On Friday morning, the conflict resurfaced. Tessaly was in a meeting. A message came in from Parren — another complaint about Bridgeway blocking his crew's access. Another message from the Bridgeway lead about the Council authorization. The usual cycle.

Ash looked at the messages. He pulled the aqueduct schedule from the rack on the wall — the same one he'd been reading all week. He traced the route with his finger: junction seven, eight, nine, ten, eleven, twelve. Junction nine was offline. The aqueduct was already depressurized between eight and ten. The Thornwall crew could work from seven toward nine without affecting the Bridgeway crew's pressure at twelve. The gap between them was already open. Someone just had to say so.

He wrote it up. A short note — here's the conflict, here's the schedule, here's junction nine, here's why it works. He added a line at the bottom: *I'm not sure about the pressure tolerances past junction ten. Please check.* He left it on Tessaly's desk.

When she came back from her meeting, she read the note. She read it again. Then she appeared at his desk.

"Junction nine." She set the note down and tapped it once.

"I wasn't sure about the pressure tolerances—"

"Natural break in the line. Tolerances are fine." She picked up the note and held it loosely, the way you hold a piece of paper you're going to act on. "Right."

She left. No speech. No "three senior staff couldn't sort it." The recognition was in the note going into her active folio instead of the tray. The recognition was in the scheduling adjustment that appeared on Parren's desk the next morning with Tessaly's initials, not Ash's name.

That afternoon, something else happened — something quieter. Ash was at his desk when Tessaly routed a query to him. A scheduling question from a district crew about overlapping maintenance windows. It wasn't addressed to him specifically — it was the kind of query that would normally go through Tessaly or one of the senior coordinators. She'd forwarded it to his desk with a single word written at the top: *Handle.*

He handled it. He checked the schedules, confirmed the overlap was resolvable, wrote a brief note to the crew, and walked it down to the dispatch office. It took twenty minutes. Nobody praised him for it. The query had simply been routed to his desk, as though that were a normal place for scheduling queries to go.

A gear finding its housing. Beginning to turn.

* * *

On Day Eight, Dorenne said, "Morning meeting. Sit at the table."

She said it in passing, on her way to the records room, without stopping. No speech about his performance. No assessment of his growth. Just a logistical instruction, delivered with the same economy she applied to everything: *the corner is no longer where you sit.*

He moved his notebook from the corner desk to the fourth seat from the end of the central table, and the distance between those two points — perhaps twenty feet of stone floor — contained the entire first week of his life in this building. The locked doors and the open ones. The wrong answer and the right instinct. The cramping legs and Tessaly's rerouted schedule. Osten's shelving. Parren's mailbox. The note about junction nine.

He sat at the table and nobody commented, because nobody needed to comment, because the seat was already his in every way that mattered and the formalization was just the building catching up to the fact.

* * *

The second week brought Councilor Aldren.

By now Ash had sat through eight morning briefs and three afternoon sessions with visiting officials. He'd watched Dorenne manage complaints, absorb political pressure, redirect anger, and deploy silence the way other people deployed arguments. She had a repertoire — the measured pause that let a person hear their own unreasonableness; the question that reframed the conversation without appearing to; the subtle rearrangement of seating that put the most agitated person next to the calmest staffer, changing the room's temperature through proximity.

He'd been watching the way you watched when watching was your only tool.

Aldren arrived with two aides and a grievance about the reallocation of channeling resources from his district to the Thornwall emergency repairs. His voice carried — the kind of volume that came from years of speaking in rooms where everyone else was paid to listen. Dorenne's voice did not carry, which was worse.

"Three months," Aldren was saying. "The allocation was approved three months ago, Director. My constituents were promised — and I want to be very clear about this — they were *promised* a fully operational heating grid before the first frost. Now I'm hearing, third-hand, that your office has redirected channelers to yet another emergency, and I'm left to explain to four thousand families why their homes are still cold."

"The Thornwall aqueduct is failing," Dorenne said. She didn't raise her voice. She didn't need to. "Heating grids are a hardship. Losing clean water to the western districts is a catastrophe. I don't make that distinction lightly."

"With respect, Director, you make that distinction every quarter, and it is always my district that absorbs the delay. My people are not an acceptable loss. They are not a rounding error in your triage."

The argument continued. Nobody was talking about the actual problem, which was that there weren't enough channelers to do both things. The scarcity was real. The argument was theater.

Ash watched Aldren's frustration building — the set of his shoulders, the way his hands moved when he talked, the flush rising in his neck. In another minute, he was going to say something personal. Dorenne was going to respond with something precise and devastating. And the meeting was going to end with both of them less willing to cooperate than when it started.

He thought of Dorenne's repertoire. The tools she used: silence, questions, rearrangement. She changed temperatures. He couldn't do what she did — he didn't have her authority, her position, her decades of calibrated presence. But he had hands. He had hospitality. He had the particular advantage of being nobody important, which meant his interruption would carry no political weight and therefore no political threat.

He stood up. He crossed to the side table where the tea service sat — the kettle on its channeled warming plate — and poured three cups. He brought them to the central table on a tray.

"Councilor Aldren," he said, setting a cup in front of the man. "I'm sorry to interrupt. I was looking at the scheduling rotations earlier, and I noticed something about the southern pool — the channelers assigned to your district's heating grid. They share a rotation with the lower market district, and the lower market's maintenance was completed early this quarter. There might be capacity that hasn't been reassigned."

He didn't know if this was right. He'd seen the close-out report in the records room — he'd been reading everything he could get his hands on for two weeks — and he'd seen the southern pool's rotation schedule, and the gap between them *suggested* available capacity. But he didn't have the technical knowledge to know whether those particular channelers could actually be redirected to heating work. He was guessing. The guess was educated, but it was still a guess, and his hands were not entirely steady when he set the teacup down.

Aldren looked at the tea. He looked at Ash. The interruption had broken his rhythm, which was the point — not to solve the problem but to change the temperature.

"The southern pool," Aldren said, his tone recalibrating from combative to merely cautious.

Dorenne's eyes moved to Ash. He couldn't read the expression. She didn't shut him down.

"Tessaly." Dorenne didn't look away from Aldren. "Pull the southern pool rotation and the lower market close-out. I want the numbers before end of day." She turned back to the councilor, and her voice shifted register — more open, the voice she used when she wanted someone to feel dealt with rather than managed. "Councilor. If there's capacity, I will personally ensure it's redirected to your district's grid before the week is out. If there isn't, I'll come to your office and we'll find a path together."

Aldren drank his tea. The flush was receding. "I do know what your word is worth," he said, and the concession came with just enough gravity to let his aides know that the Director owed him something now.

After Aldren left, Dorenne passed Ash's desk on her way to the records room.

"The southern pool." She stopped but didn't sit. "Were you sure?"

"No. The close-out report was real. The gap in the schedule was real. Whether the capacity was actually available — I was guessing."

"Half a squad, freed up last week. So you were right." A pause. "Did you know that?"

"No." The relief came through before he could modulate it — a visible exhale, a loosening in his shoulders that he felt and knew she could see. He'd guessed correctly. He hadn't known. The difference between being right and knowing you're right was the distance across which luck became competence, and he was standing on the luck side of the line.

"Well handled," she said. And left.

He sat at his desk and looked at his hands. They were shaking — not from fear, not from the confrontation, but from the particular cocktail of adrenaline and relief that came with having bet something he couldn't afford to lose and not losing it. He pressed his palms flat against the desk and held them there until the shaking stopped.

* * *

In the late afternoon of the second week, with the light going amber through the high windows and the office thinning as staff left for the day, Ash heard voices through a door that wasn't fully closed.

He'd been sent to retrieve a folio from the small conference room down the corridor. Two engineers — he recognized them from the morning meetings, both from Venn's structural team — were bent over a chart spread across a table. He wouldn't have stopped, except that he heard a word he'd learned: *Kharren*.

"—been stable for decades, Orin," the younger one was saying. "Literally decades. The reinforcements hold the stress. That's the whole point of them."

"Mhm." The other engineer — older, with the lined face of someone who spent too much time staring at numbers that worried her — didn't look up from the chart. She tapped a column of figures. "Look at the rate."

"I'm looking at it."

"Then you see it's not linear. The deep anchors are degrading faster than the surface structures. The gap is opening up."

"By how much?"

"Failure window moved up three years in the last assessment. Forty became thirty-seven. Next assessment, it'll be thirty-four. The one after that..." She let the arithmetic speak for itself.

"Thirty-seven years is a long time."

"It's a long time if the curve stays where it is." She straightened up and rubbed her eyes — a slow, deliberate gesture, as if pressing the numbers out of her vision. "It won't. Everything else is accelerating. Could be twenty. Could be fifteen." She rolled the chart and held it loosely, like something she was tired of carrying. "Six generations of channelers held the Kharren Fault in place. Each one was stronger than the next. The anchors were set by people we can't equal anymore, doing work we can't replicate. When those anchors go, there's nothing underneath them but geology." She tucked the chart under her arm. "And geology does not care what we think the timeline is."

Ash stood in the corridor with the folio in his hands and the conversation settling into his body — not into the filing system in his mind where technical information went to be organized, but into the lower, older part of him that recognized the shape of what he'd just heard because he'd been living inside it his whole life. A declining resource. An accelerating demand. A gap that widened with each generation. Numbers that moved in only one direction.

He knew what that felt like. He'd been the declining resource. He'd been the gap.

He brought the folio back to Dorenne's desk and said nothing about what he'd overheard. But the something that had been settling into his body didn't leave. It stayed — the way things stayed when they were too heavy to be carried consciously and too important to be put down.

* * *

He left the building in the last of the light. Two weeks of climbing had changed his legs — they still ached on the ramp, but it was an ache his body had accepted, the way his hands had accepted calluses from carrying tools. He took the forty flights down in the growing dark, his mind running through the day's residue: the Aldren meeting, the guess that turned out right, the engineers' numbers, Tessaly's "well handled" that was Dorenne's, passed down through channels.

He was so deep in his own head that he almost missed her.

"You're making a face."

Ryn was sitting on the low wall at the base of the stairwell where it opened onto the street — the stone wall that ran along the edge of the terrace, looking out over the gorge. She had her legs pulled up, her back against a support column, and a paper bag in her lap that smelled like fried bread and sharp cheese.

"What face?" he asked.

"The one where you're solving something and you don't know it yet. You've been making it since you were eight." She held up the bag. "Sit. Eat. You skipped lunch."

"How do you know I skipped lunch?"

"Because you don't know where to eat yet and you'd rather pass out on the stairs than ask somebody." She said it flat, like stating the weather. "Your mother told me when you'd probably be done. I took twenty minutes off."

This was so precisely accurate that he couldn't do anything but sit down beside her on the wall and take the bag.

They'd grown up three doors apart. Ryn's family lived in the same building as his — upper floor, better heating, her parents' trickles strong enough to maintain their apartment without the daily struggle that defined his family's existence. She'd been the girl down the hall before she'd been anything else: the first child his age who'd knocked on his door without her parents making her, the first friend who'd beaten him at a game and not apologized for it, the first person outside his family who'd learned the choreography of his limitations and performed it without performing it.

She warmed his cup when it went cold. She lit the path when they walked home late. She channeled the door open when his arms were full. Not as charity — as the automatic, unremarkable courtesy of a friend who happened to have a thing he didn't, the way you'd hold an umbrella over someone who'd forgotten theirs. She never made it an event. She never made it about her generosity or his lack. It was just what you did when you were walking next to someone and the path was dark and your hands could make light and his couldn't.

He'd loved her for that since he was twelve years old, in the quiet, unexamined way you love the person who makes the world livable. He'd never said so. The reasons were practical and terrible: romantic relationships in Solathis were built partly on shared magical intimacy — the vulnerability of letting someone feel your trickle, the domestic magic of shared channeling, the physical closeness that came with attuning to another person's energy. He couldn't offer that. The absence was a wall between them that neither acknowledged and both navigated around, the way you navigate around a piece of furniture that's too heavy to move and too familiar to notice.

Tonight, though, the wall was thin. Tonight everything was thin — the barriers between the person he performed and the person he was, the careful architecture of managed expectations and preemptive self-deprecation that he wore like a second skin. Two weeks had stripped something away. Not the armor — he wasn't ready to lose the armor — but some of the weight behind it.

"So." Ryn pulled a piece of bread apart and handed him the larger half. "Two weeks. Go."

He told her. Not the way he'd told his mother — not the raw, cracking-open version — and not the way he'd told Maren — not the polished, edited version. He told her the way he could only tell her: the full thing, with the embarrassing parts intact.

The desk in the corner. The cleared table. Tessaly classifying him before she'd finished walking through the door — *you're the fourth.* The channeled lock on Laris's door and the minutes he'd stood in the corridor like a man who'd forgotten what doors were for. The wrong answer in the first brief, where he'd read Laris's performance correctly and the situation completely wrong, and Dorenne's face — that layered, unreadable expression — and the two words that followed: *Keep listening.*

The misfiled document. Parren's complaint carrying across the office. The shame of it, the way his stomach had turned to stone. Dorenne passing his desk twice without mentioning it, the kindness of that omission.

His legs on the ramp, seizing on Day Three. Tessaly's rerouted schedule appearing on his desk without explanation. The map of manual handles in his notebook — he showed her this, turning to the page, and she looked at it for a long time without saying anything.

Osten and the shelving. The second visit, the question about the 1847 reorganization, the document produced in under a minute. The scheduling conflict and junction nine — how it hadn't been a flash of brilliance but just the slow accumulation of walking past the same maintenance notices for a week until the numbers connected themselves.

And then Aldren. The tea. The guess about the southern pool that turned out to be right but that he hadn't known was right, and the relief afterward, the shaking in his hands that he'd hidden under the desk.

"Wait." Ryn held up a hand. "Was it actually right? The thing about the rotation?"

"It turned out to be right. But I didn't know that when I said it."

She stared at him. "You bluffed a sitting Council member."

"I brought a sitting Council member tea and made a reasonable inference."

"You bluffed him. With tea."

"That's a reductive way to—"

"Tea." She was grinning now. "Ash."

She was laughing — the real laugh, the one that came from her chest and not from politeness, the one she used when something genuinely surprised her. He'd been cataloging her laughs for years the way he cataloged stairwells and district maps, automatically, without deciding to. The polite laugh was short and high. The real laugh was low and slightly uneven, as if one side of it arrived before the other.

They ate. The fried bread was from the vendor on the lower terrace — the one who worked late because her trickle was too weak to compete with the morning market channelers and their glowing displays and sealed cases. Ryn bought from her because the bread was good and because Ryn had a quiet, stubborn loyalty to people who worked harder than they had to. This was not a thing Ryn would ever say about herself. It was a thing Ash had observed over years of watching her make choices that were slightly harder than they needed to be, for reasons she'd dismiss as practical if you pointed them out.

After the food was gone and they were sitting in the last of the twilight, the gorge below them a darkness that the city's fading channeling couldn't reach, she went quiet for a while. Then, without looking at him: "Is it going to be okay? The job. All the—" She waved a hand, a gesture that encompassed everything she didn't want to list.

"It's going to be fine."

"Ash." Just his name, but the way she said it stripped the varnish off his answer and left the bare wood showing.

He looked out at the gorge. Below them, the river was a sound more than a sight — a low, constant presence, the one thing in the city that ran on its own power without channeling or maintenance or someone's daily trickle holding it together.

"I don't know," he said. "There are a hundred things I can't do. I can't use the resonance stones. I can't activate the locks. I can't read half the instruments. Every system in that office runs through interfaces I can't touch, and every time I need something done that requires even the most basic channeling, I have to ask someone. And asking costs something. Every time I ask, I remind someone that I'm the person who can't. I stood in front of Laris's door for five minutes on my first day, Ryn. Five minutes. Just standing there because I couldn't knock."

He stopped. He'd gone further than he meant to. Two weeks' exhaustion had thinned the filter between thinking and saying.

"But you figured it out," she said. Not comforting — observing.

"I drew a map. Of which doors have handles." He laughed, and it came out wrong — hollow, the sound of someone describing a coping mechanism and hearing how small it sounded. "That's my big contribution. A map of the doors I can open."

Ryn didn't fill the silence. She was good at that — at letting a pause be a pause instead of rushing to comfort or deflect. She sat with her arms around her knees and waited.

"But today someone asked me for my opinion," he said. "And I had one. And it was the right one. And nobody looked at me like I'd performed a trick."

"So they treated you like a person who had a good idea."

"Yes."

"Funny how that works."

He laughed. It came out surprised, which was the only way real laughter came from him — squeezed past the defenses before he could decide whether it was allowed. She smiled at the sound of it, and the smile had the quality of something private and carefully held, like a coin she'd been carrying in her pocket for a long time and was briefly letting catch the light.

They sat. The city dimmed around them, district by district, the heating in the stones fading as the channelers who maintained it went home and slept and let their trickles recover for tomorrow's effort. The sky above the gorge was enormous and unmanaged — no channeled lights, no lattice warmth, just stars appearing in the gaps between clouds with the patient indifference of things that predated magic and would outlast it.

"I should go," he said eventually. "I need to be there before dawn again."

"Before dawn." She pulled a face. "You're going to be one of those people. The kind the rest of us resent."

"The stairs were already worn out. I'm just maintaining the tradition."

He stood, and she stood, and there was a moment — there was always a moment — where the distance between them was exactly the distance across which you'd reach for someone if reaching were an option. He felt it in his hands, the impulse to close the gap, and she felt it too, he was almost certain, in the way she didn't step back but didn't step forward, in the slight shift of her weight that could have been the beginning of movement or the decision against it.

Neither of them moved. Neither of them named it. The moment passed the way all their moments passed — acknowledged by the silence, preserved by the silence, left where it was.

"Go sleep," she said. "You look like someone ran you through the city and back."

"Only half. The other half was paperwork."

He walked home through the dark streets, his legs tired from the ramp and his mind quiet for the first time in days. The district was shutting down around him — workshops dark, windows curtained, the faint residual warmth in the stones already fading. A man passed him on the street, heading the other direction, and reinforced the paving stone under his foot with a casual touch as he walked — the automatic civic maintenance of someone with trickle to spare, leaving the city slightly stronger behind him. Ash walked over the same stone and left it unchanged.

It didn't sting. Tonight, for the first time in two weeks, the small daily evidence of his limitation didn't carry the weight it usually did. Not because anything had changed — his hands were still his hands, empty of channeling, incapable of the basic participation that every other person in Solathis performed without thinking. The absence was the same. The difference was that today he'd found something else to put in the space where the absence lived. Not power. Function. A desk at the table, a problem he'd noticed, a director who trusted him enough to let him guess.

He climbed the five flights to the apartment. His mother had left the stove on low — he could feel the warmth when he opened the door, the particular quality of channeled heat that she maintained through the evening so the apartment would still be warm when he came home. An act of care so automatic she probably hadn't thought about it. A kindness measured in kilojoules and performed in his absence, the way he performed his own kindnesses in hers — the fixed hinges, the laid kindling, the table set before dawn.

He ate standing up in the kitchen — leftover rice, cold, the saffron still fragrant. He washed the plate by hand. He banked the stove.

In his room — his childhood room, the narrow bed, the window that looked out over the district's uneven roofline — he sat on the edge of the mattress and unlaced his boots. His hands were callused now in new places — the heel of his palm from carrying folios, his thumb and forefinger from gripping the pen. His shoulders ached from the ramp. His mind was still sorting, still filing, still building the architecture of understanding that had started as an empty desk in a dark office fourteen days ago.

He thought about the engineers and the Kharren Fault. About the numbers that moved in the wrong direction, the gap that widened, the geological patience of stone. He knew more vocabulary now than he had on Day One. Enough to understand the shape of what the engineer had said, if not the precision. Enough to be scared by it.

He thought about Dorenne's nod — the cataloging nod, the one that said *asset identified*. He'd seen his mother nod that way at a cut of meat in the market, assessing value against cost. The comparison was uncomfortable. He'd been seeing it more clearly as the weeks passed — the way Dorenne's attention tracked not just his work but his potential, the way her praise always pointed somewhere, always had a direction. *Well handled* meant *do it again.* *Sit at the table* meant *be available.* She was investing, and investments had expected returns.

He let it go. Not because it didn't matter, but because the alternative — examining it too closely, following it to its conclusion — would cost him the happiness he'd earned tonight, and he wasn't ready to pay that price.

He thought about Ryn on the wall, laughing. The paper bag. The bread from the vendor who worked late. The uneven laugh and the moment that passed and the silence that held it.

He thought about the fact that tonight, sitting on the wall with Ryn, he'd bought them food without counting the cost. He'd reached into his pocket and pulled out coins and paid without the familiar calculus — *can I afford this, does this cut into tomorrow, is this the kind of expenditure that a person who contributes nothing can justify*. He'd just paid. The way people paid. The way you did when the money was earned and the spending was yours to decide and the person sitting next to you was someone you wanted to feed.

Ryn had noticed. He'd seen her notice — the brief flicker of her eyes to his hand and then away, the thing she didn't say, the not-saying louder than any words she could have chosen. She knew what it meant. She knew what all of it meant — the money, the job, the desk, the notes, the fact that tonight he was tired from work instead of tired from performing.

For the first time in as long as he could measure, the happiness wasn't a performance. It wasn't the smile he wore like architecture, the warmth he generated as currency, the cheerfulness that paid his rent in a world that had never charged anyone else admission. It was just happiness. Unstructured. Unperformed. Sitting in his chest like a warmth that didn't require channeling or maintenance or anyone else's trickle to sustain.

The distinction was everything.

He closed his eyes. He slept.
