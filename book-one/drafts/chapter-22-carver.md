# Chapter 22

The streets were empty at this hour.

Ash descended the broad steps from the Second Terrace and turned south, away from the direct route home. The cold pressed through his coat. Above him the elder districts held their glow — warm stone, seamless facades, the quiet hum of channeling so abundant it was atmospheric. Down here, at the terrace's edge, the light thinned. A heating lattice in the paving stones flickered once and steadied, its amber pulse the color of something almost spent.

He walked.

Not toward anything. The workshop was south and below, and he supposed he'd end up there, but the route he chose was the long one — west through the upper-middle districts, then down through the reconstruction zones, then through the middle band where things worked but barely, then further down into the neighborhoods that had always been last in line. The full descent. The city as cross-section.

His boots on the stone. The sound of his own breathing. The reservoir in his chest, warm and finite, its edges precise as the walls of a room he'd measured and could never unmeasure. Twenty-two million kilojoules. He carried the number the way he'd carried the cold-water mornings, the forty flights of stairs, the smile that cost more than anyone knew — not as a burden he could set down, but as a fact that had rearranged the architecture of everything he saw.

The upper-middle district opened around him. Broad streets, solid buildings, the infrastructure he'd reinforced in the weeks after the earthquake. He recognized his own work. There — the retaining wall along the Thornwall aqueduct, where he'd pressed both hands into the cracked stone and poured heat and force until the fractures sealed and the wall hummed with a stability it hadn't known in decades. There — the residential block at the corner of Vane Street, where the foundation had shifted during the quake and he'd spent an afternoon realigning it, the stone flowing under his hands like something alive, the families watching from the street with faces he'd mistaken for gratitude and now understood were closer to awe, which was a different thing entirely.

The wall still held. The foundation still held. Warm light spilled from the windows of the residential block — five stories of families, sleeping or waking, their heating lattices fed by someone's daily channeling, their water running through pipes he'd sealed, their floors level because he'd straightened them. His work. Every warm building a piece of himself he'd never get back.

He stopped at the corner and looked up at the lit windows.

The math was simple. The retaining wall had cost him roughly three hundred thousand kJ. The foundation, another two hundred. The residential block's water system, eighty. Each number a fraction of a fraction of what he'd thought was infinite, back when infinite was the only word that fit. Each number a piece of a countdown he hadn't started yet because the concept of counting down hadn't existed in a world without walls.

He'd do it again. He knew that with the certainty of someone who'd stood in the rubble and felt the ground shake and chosen, without thinking, to hold things together. He'd do it again tomorrow if the earthquake came back. The knowledge didn't make the cost less real. It just made the cost something he could look at directly, the way you could look at a scar and know both that it hurt and that the wound it sealed had been worth sealing.

He walked on.

* * *

The Second Terrace curved west, and the civic buildings rose along its inner edge — broad stone facades, deep-set windows, the heavy architecture of an era when permanence was cheap. The Infrastructure Directorate sat at the eastern end, but its light was visible even from here. Third floor. The window he knew.

Ash slowed.

The light was steady. Warm. The particular quality of a channeled lamp maintained by someone who didn't think about the cost — an elder's casual expenditure, barely a rounding error against a pool of ten thousand kJ. Dorenne was at her desk. He couldn't see her from this distance, but he didn't need to. He knew the posture — the straight back, the hands flat on the desk or wrapped around a cup of tea she'd warmed herself, the deployment charts spread before her in their organized disorder. He knew the expression she'd be wearing at this hour: the one between the meetings and the role, the face she had when no one was performing for her and she wasn't performing for anyone. The woman who processed the end of his power as a scheduling problem and who, in a space she hadn't planned to use, had started writing his name and stopped.

She'd manage. That was what she did. The city needed managing, and Dorenne managed it — not because she was kind or cruel but because the framework that defined her had no other function. She would re-sequence the deployment charts. She would allocate the conventional crews. She would find three or four or five partial solutions that, together, covered most of what the Wellspring had been doing alone, and the coverage would be imperfect, and she would optimize the imperfection, and the optimization would be her version of grief.

He stood in the dark street and looked at the lit window and let something close.

Not with a sound. Not with the sharp click of a latch — he'd felt that in her office, the door shutting between what he'd been to her and what he'd be now. This was quieter. The slow settling of a weight into the place where it would stay. She had given him the first real thing he'd ever had, and the giving had been strategic and genuine in equal measure, and he would carry both halves for the rest of his life, and from the outside those halves would look like a single thing because they were.

He turned away from the window.

The street ahead descended toward the middle districts. He took it.

* * *

The middle districts were where the city's performance dropped.

Not collapsed — dropped. The transit shafts still ran, but the schedules posted beside them were aspirational rather than actual, and the platforms rose at intervals that depended on which maintenance channeler had enough pool left at the end of their shift to power the mechanism. The heating lattices in the streets glowed, but dimmer than the upper terraces, and unevenly — hot patches where someone had recently refreshed the stone, cool stretches where no one had bothered or no one could afford to. The buildings stood, but their facades told stories. Cracks patched and re-patched, the layered channeling visible as subtle variations in the stone's color, each repair a different hand, a different generation, the geological record of a city maintained by people whose capacity to maintain it shrank with every passing decade.

Ash walked through it and saw it differently.

Before the earthquake — before the Wellspring, before any of it — he'd walked these streets as a man without magic, and the cracks in the walls had been furniture. Background. The texture of a world that wasn't his to fix. After the Wellspring, he'd walked them as a resource, and every crack was a problem that resolved itself under his hands, and the resolution felt like purpose, and the purpose felt like identity, and the identity had a floor he hadn't known about until he'd burned through half of it in a single season.

Now he walked them as a man who could see both views at once, and the seeing changed the quality of the streets the way a change in light changes a room. The same buildings, the same cracks, the same fading lattices. But the frame had shifted. Every repair he hadn't made was a repair someone else would have to make with a fraction of what he'd spent, and the fraction was all they had, and the fraction was shrinking.

A maintenance crew worked at the base of a retaining wall three blocks down. He could see their channeling — brief flares against the stone, controlled and careful, the work of people stretching limited pools across unlimited need. Four of them, working in rotation, doing in a morning what he could have done in minutes.

He watched them the way he'd watched the crew from the overlook that morning. The lead channeler directing energy into a fracture. The second and third bracing the surrounding stone. The fourth mixing mortar by hand, filling the gap behind the magic with something physical, something that would hold when the channeling faded. The work was slow. It was methodical. It was the kind of work that kept the city standing, and it bore no resemblance to the spectacular, wasteful, intoxicating expenditures that had made him a hero and cost him half of everything he'd ever have.

He passed them without stopping. One of the crew — the hand-worker, a young man about his age, mortar dust on his sleeves — glanced up as Ash went by. No recognition. Just the brief assessment of a stranger passing in the dark and the return to work. The anonymity was a small mercy.

Further down, the street opened onto a public wall where notices and broadsheets were posted. Ash had passed this wall a hundred times. He'd never stopped to read it.

He stopped now.

The broadsheets were layered three and four deep in places, older notices buried under newer ones, the paper curling at the edges where the paste had dried. Official postings — council schedules, maintenance notices, transit disruptions. Community announcements — a healer offering reduced-rate services in the lower districts, a cooperative seeking members for a shared heating arrangement, a tinkerer advertising mechanical repairs. And, covering a full quarter of the wall's surface, printed on rough paper in ink that smelled fresh:

A DECLARATION ON THE DECLINE OF POOLS AND THE SILENCE OF THE POWERFUL

Ash read it.

The language was sharp, precise, the work of someone who knew how to construct an argument and didn't mind if the construction drew blood. The facts were plain: generational pool averages by decade, the declining curve mapped in numbers that anyone could verify. The daily cost of living in Solathis — heating, water, transit, food preparation — expressed as a percentage of pool capacity. For an elder: twelve percent. For a middle-generation adult: forty percent. For a young adult: seventy-three percent. For an adolescent: effectively all of it.

The broadsheet named no names. It didn't need to. The argument built itself: the decline was real, the decline was accelerating, and the people with the largest pools — the elders, the institutions, the powerful — were living in the same city as people whose pools couldn't get them through a day, and the distance between those two experiences was growing, and no one in authority was talking about it, and the silence was a choice.

At the bottom, in smaller type: *We are the generation that will outlive our magic. We are owed an answer. What happens when the pools run dry?*

Ash stood before the wall and read the question twice.

*We are the generation that will outlive our magic.*

His throat tightened. Not grief — or not only grief. Recognition. The shock of seeing your own face in a stranger's description, your own fear in someone else's language. These were young channelers. People with pools. People who had what he'd spent twenty-three years without, and what they had was shrinking, and the shrinking terrified them, and the terror had the same shape as the thing that lived in his chest where the reservoir's walls pressed close.

He was living at high speed what they were living in slow motion. His countdown measured in weeks and months. Theirs measured in decades and generations. The same trajectory, the same destination, the same question underneath: *Who am I when this is gone?*

He reached out and pressed his palm flat against the broadsheet. The paper was cool and slightly damp. He held it there for a moment — not channeling, not doing anything but touching the wall where someone had pasted their fear in ink and paper and hoped that the right person would stop to read it.

He let his hand drop. Walked on.

* * *

The descent continued.

Past the middle districts into the transition zone where the terraces narrowed and the buildings crowded closer and the infrastructure's decline stopped being a matter of degree and became a matter of kind. Here, the heating lattices weren't dim — they were dark. Dead stone, cold under his boots, the channeling that had sustained them exhausted or abandoned. A water tap dripped steadily into a basin that overflowed onto the walkway, the runoff tracing a dark line down the terrace steps. Someone had hung a blanket over a window where a sealing lattice had failed. Someone else had chalked a message on a wall he didn't stop to read.

He'd reinforced parts of this district during the earthquake response. He could see where. The stonework had a different quality — denser, more uniform, the slightly too-perfect surface of Wellspring-grade reinforcement that stood out against the layered, imperfect repairs surrounding it. His work, embedded in the city's fabric like a foreign thread in old cloth. It would hold for years. Decades, maybe. But the stone around it would keep cracking, and the heating lattices would keep fading, and the water taps would keep dripping, and eventually the gap between what he'd reinforced and what he hadn't would itself become a kind of fracture — the sharp line between what one extraordinary person could reach and what they couldn't, the boundary of a resource that had felt infinite and wasn't.

The street curved east, and Ash heard channeling.

Not the controlled flares of the maintenance crew on the retaining wall. Something quieter. A steady, focused pulse — the kind of energy output that came from someone working at the edge of their capacity with the discipline to keep the edge clean. He knew the quality of it before he saw the source. He'd grown up around it. He'd watched it across dinner tables and in stairwells and through the wall of his childhood bedroom, the faint warmth of someone channeling with the precision of a person who couldn't afford to waste a single kJ.

He rounded the corner and saw the water main.

The crack ran diagonally across the stone pipe where it emerged from the terrace wall — a structural fracture, the kind that happened when the original channeling weakened and the stone remembered that it was stone and stone broke under pressure. Water seeped from the crack in a thin, steady stream that had already pooled on the walkway and begun its slow descent down the terrace steps. A maintenance crew was working on it. Three people. Late shift, emergency call. Their faces were drawn with the particular exhaustion of people who'd already used most of their pool on the day's regular work and were now being asked to find more.

One of them was Maren.

She was kneeling at the base of the pipe, both hands pressed flat against the stone on either side of the fracture. Her eyes were closed. Her lips moved — not words, or not words he could hear. The slight, unconscious movement of someone directing their concentration so precisely that the body needed to participate, the way a person's hands moved when they were thinking through a difficult problem.

The crack sealed.

Not all at once. From the bottom, a slow crawl upward, the stone knitting under her hands with the careful, deliberate pace of someone who was spending exactly what the task required and not a fraction more. The water slowed. The seepage thinned. She held the channeling steady — her jaw tight, her shoulders rigid, the stillness of controlled effort that looked like calm and was anything but.

Her pool flickered.

He felt it more than saw it — a brief stutter in the energy she was directing, the way a candle flame dips when a draft passes. She'd hit a boundary. The edge of what she had left. For a moment the crack stopped sealing, the half-repaired stone holding its pattern, the fracture still visible in its upper third.

She steadied it.

A slow breath. Her hands pressed harder against the stone. The channeling resumed — thinner now, more controlled, each pulse calibrated with the excruciating precision of a person operating at their absolute limit and refusing to let the limit show. The crack closed. Millimeter by millimeter, the last of the fracture sealed, and the stone was whole, and the water stopped, and the walkway's pooled runoff was the only evidence that anything had been wrong.

She pulled her hands away.

Her fingers trembled. She flexed them — once, twice — and the trembling stopped, or she made it stop, the way she made everything stop when it threatened to be visible. She wiped her face with the back of her wrist. Stood. Picked up the tool bag at her feet. Turned to the crew leader and said something Ash couldn't hear — a report, a status update, the professional language of a person who had just spent the last of what she had and was now discussing what came next.

The crew leader pointed down the terrace. Another crack. Another pipe. Another fracture in a city built on magic that was running out.

Maren shouldered her bag and walked.

Ash stood in the shadow of a doorway thirty paces behind her and watched his sister move to the next break with the steady, unhurried pace of a person for whom this was not a crisis but a Tuesday. Not the worst night she'd had. Not the most she'd spent. Just the work. The endless, grinding, uncelebrated work of maintaining a world that took more from her every day and gave less back, performed with a discipline so complete it was invisible, the way Haran's efficiency was invisible, the way competence always looked easy from the outside.

The math from Chapter 19 — his math, the numbers that had cracked his world open — reverberated.

She'd been here the whole time.

Not here on this street, working this pipe. Here in the condition he'd discovered six days ago and reacted to as if the ground had opened under his feet. She'd been living with finitude her entire life. Every day. Every channeling. Every carefully rationed expenditure from a pool that bought less than her mother's and would buy less again tomorrow. She'd never had the luxury of thinking the word *infinite*. She'd never had the luxury of spending without counting. She'd never stood at the top of an ocean and poured it out in great, gorgeous, wasteful cascades because the pouring felt like freedom and the freedom felt like being someone who mattered.

She'd done this. Night shift after night shift. Crack after crack. Her hands on the stone, her pool flickering, her face wiped clean of whatever it cost her, and then the next one. And the next. And the next.

*This is what finitude looks like when you don't have the luxury of ignoring it.*

The thought arrived without drama. A fact settling into place beside the other facts — the reservoir's walls, Dorenne's pencil, Haran's question, the broadsheet on the wall. His sister, kneeling at a cracked pipe in the dark, channeling with the last of what she had because the pipe was cracked and she was here and the water had to stop.

He could call out.

She was thirty paces away, walking, her back to him. He could say her name. She'd turn. She'd see his face and read it the way she'd read it their entire lives — quickly, accurately, with the practiced assessment of a person who'd spent twenty years calibrating her own behavior to the weather in his eyes. She'd ask what was wrong, or she wouldn't ask, and either way she'd know, because knowing was what Maren did. Knowing and adjusting and carrying the knowledge without complaint, the way she carried everything.

His mouth stayed closed.

He didn't know what he'd say. The math? She'd lived the math. The countdown? Hers had been running since birth. The fear of becoming ordinary? She'd never been anything else, and she'd built a life on it that worked, and the life was hard and real and hers, and telling her about his own reckoning with finitude would sound like a man who'd spent a fortune discovering that money ran out.

She turned the corner. The tool bag swung at her side. Her crew followed. The sound of their boots faded down the terrace, and then the street was quiet, and the sealed pipe held, and the water stayed stopped, and the evidence of his sister's work was the absence of a problem that no one would think to credit her for fixing.

He stood in the doorway for a long time.

Then he walked.

* * *

The lower districts opened around him. Narrower streets. Lower ceilings where buildings bridged the gap between terraces. The smell of stone and cold water and the faint mineral trace of old channeling — decades of it, generations, layered into the walls like sediment. Down here the city felt its age. The architecture was older, the stonework rougher, the original construction visible beneath the centuries of magical maintenance that had kept it standing long past the point where physics would have had opinions.

He knew these streets. He'd grown up three terraces above, but the workshop was here, and he'd been walking to it since he was twelve. The route was in his legs. Down the steps at the end of Vane Street — forty-two of them, worn smooth at the center, the edges still sharp where foot traffic didn't reach. Through the covered passage that smelled of damp stone and lamp oil. Past the building where old Kessler had run a hand-tool shop until he died and his daughter converted it to a channeling supply store because the margins were better. Past the public cistern where the water came up cold and tasted of iron and the pump handle squeaked on the third stroke.

Past the cistern, he slowed.

The pump handle didn't squeak.

He stopped. Looked at it. The cistern was the same — stone basin, iron spout, the hand-operated pump that had served this block since before anyone alive could remember. But the pump mechanism was different. New. The handle was steel instead of iron, fitted with a wooden grip, and the action when he pressed it was smooth and even, the resistance steady, the water flowing with a pressure that the old pump had never managed. He pumped it twice. The water came clear and cold and strong.

He crouched and looked at the mechanism.

Gears. A spring-return system. A counterweight that stored energy from each downstroke and released it into the next cycle, amplifying the user's effort. The design was clean — functional, unadorned, the work of someone who understood materials and forces and didn't see the need to make understanding decorative. He recognized the design language. He'd worked in it himself, years ago, on a bench in a workshop that smelled like sawdust and oil.

Haran's work. Or the work of the tinkerers who'd built the pump system for the Velden building — the project he'd abandoned when the Wellspring made mechanical solutions feel quaint. Someone had installed one here too. Community-funded, probably. Distributed effort. A thing that worked without magic, maintained by the people who used it.

He stood. His hand was wet from the pump. He dried it on his coat and kept walking.

Two more blocks. A left turn at the farrier's shop — closed now, dark, the forge cold. Down a short flight of steps into the narrow street where the workshop sat between a cobbler's storefront and a building that had been empty for as long as Ash could remember.

The workshop was dark.

Past midnight. Haran had gone home — to the small apartment above the cobbler's where he'd lived for thirty years, where the tea was always warm and the tools were always clean and the rent was paid with mechanical repairs that the landlord accepted because Haran's fixes outlasted channeled ones. The workshop's front door was closed. The windows were shuttered. The street was empty.

Ash reached into his coat pocket and found the key.

It was small, iron, worn smooth at the grip from years of use. Haran had given it to him when he was fifteen — not with ceremony, not with a speech. He'd simply handed it over one afternoon and said, "For when you need to work and I'm not here." A key to a workshop. A statement that a boy with no magic was welcome in a space where things were built, and that the welcome didn't expire at closing time.

He'd stopped using it months ago. The workshop had become a place he used to go, the way the stairs had become stairs he used to climb, the way his parents' kitchen had become a room he used to sit in before the world rearranged itself around his power. He'd left without leaving — the slow drift that happened when everything in your life was suddenly less interesting than the new thing, and the new thing was being extraordinary.

The key turned in the lock. The door opened. The smell hit him first.

Sawdust. Machine oil. The sharp tang of cut metal. The ghost of Haran's tea — the particular blend he brewed in the battered pot on the shelf above his bench, the warmth long since faded but the scent held in the wood and the stone and the fabric of the workshop itself, the way a room holds the habits of the person who lives in it.

Ash stepped inside and closed the door.

* * *

The workshop was small and precise.

Two workbenches — Haran's against the far wall, Ash's beneath the high window on the east side. Tool racks on pegs, each tool in its outline, the shapes drawn in charcoal on the wood so that any absence was immediately visible. Bins of materials sorted by type — wood, metal, ceramic, salvage. The parts shelf, where half-finished mechanisms waited in various states of assembly, each one tagged with a scrap of paper noting what it needed next. The lathe in the corner, hand-powered, the belt worn thin but still functional. The drafting table where Haran drew plans in his small, exact hand — not blueprints, exactly. Ideas made visible. Solutions looking for problems.

His bench.

Ash crossed the room and stood before it. The surface was clean. Not empty — Haran had left his things where they were, the way he'd left them when the earthquake pulled him away and the Wellspring pulled him further. But the surface had been wiped down, the tools oiled to prevent rust, the work-in-progress projects arranged neatly along the bench's back edge where they wouldn't be in the way but wouldn't be forgotten.

The self-winding lamp sat at the left end of the bench. He'd started it eight months ago — a design that used a mainspring and a simple escapement to maintain a steady flame without channeling. The concept was proven. The execution was half-finished. The mainspring housing was complete, the escapement mechanism rough-cut but not fitted, the flame assembly still a sketch on a piece of paper pinned to the wall above.

Beside it, the spring-loaded pump. Smaller than the one installed at the cistern outside — a tabletop model, a proof-of-concept. He'd built the spring mechanism and the valve system before the Wellspring activated. The pump worked. It just didn't pump enough to be useful at scale, and he'd been working on the gearing ratio when the building collapsed and his chest cracked open and the world turned into something he could reshape with his hands.

And beside that, the geared mechanism he'd never finished. He couldn't remember now what it was supposed to do. The gears were cut and assembled on a mounting plate, their teeth meshing precisely, the ratios marked in his handwriting on a card beneath. Some kind of amplifier — a way to convert small, steady inputs into larger outputs. The principle was sound. The application had been lost somewhere between the evening he set it down and the morning he stopped coming back.

He pulled out the stool and sat.

The bench was the right height. It had always been the right height — Haran had adjusted it for him when he was fourteen and growing, and again when he was sixteen and done, and the surface sat at the exact level where his forearms rested flat and his hands could work without strain. Ergonomic, Haran called it. Built for the body. Built for the long hours of close work that made the difference between a mechanism that functioned and one that functioned well.

He picked up a tool.

A small file. Not the one he needed for any particular project — just the one closest to his right hand, sitting in its outline on the rack. The handle was worn to the shape of his grip. His thumb found the flat spot where years of use had compressed the wood, and his fingers wrapped around it with the automatic precision of a motion repeated ten thousand times.

He didn't channel.

The reservoir sat in his chest — warm, finite, its walls as clear as the walls of this room. He could feel it the way he'd learned to feel it in the days since the math: not as the formless ocean it had seemed before, but as a bounded thing. A measured thing. A budget, not an identity. Twenty-two million kJ, give or take. Enough to reinforce a district. Enough to seal a hundred aqueducts. Enough to do, in weeks, what crews of four would spend months accomplishing.

Enough to spend. Not enough to be.

He held the file and looked at the workbench and didn't channel and didn't plan and didn't calculate the optimal allocation of his remaining reservoir against the city's infrastructure needs. He sat with a tool in his hand in a room that smelled like sawdust and oil and let the room be what it was: a place where things were built by hands that understood materials and forces and the patient, iterative work of making something that functioned.

*What do you want to build?*

Haran's question. Six days old. Unanswered.

He turned the file over in his hand. The metal caught the faint light from the street — a sliver of moonlight through the gap in the shutters, enough to see the bench by, not enough to work by. He'd need a lamp. The oil lamp on the shelf above Haran's bench — the one that ran on a wick and a reservoir of rapeseed oil, no channeling required. He could light it with the flint and steel in the drawer. He'd done it a thousand times. The familiar sequence: steel on flint, spark on tinder, tinder to wick, the slow bloom of warm light filling the workshop from the top down, the shadows retreating into corners, the tools on the wall emerging from the dark like old friends.

He didn't light it yet.

He sat in the dark workshop with the file in his hand and the question in his chest and thought about the self-winding lamp. The spring-loaded pump. The geared mechanism he'd never finished. Projects that worked without magic. Projects that would still work when the magic was gone — when his reservoir emptied, when the pools declined past function, when the last generation of channelers woke up one morning and found that the lattices didn't respond and the water didn't warm and the world they'd built was a shell around a silence.

Projects that mattered more than he'd known when he started them. Projects that mattered more than the spectacular, heroic, intoxicating, finite acts of power that had made him someone the city celebrated and Dorenne optimized and Maren quietly orbited and Ryn watched from a distance that grew by the week.

He set the file down. Picked up the escapement mechanism for the self-winding lamp. Turned it in his fingers. The parts were rough — they needed finishing, fitting, the kind of careful work that happened at a bench with good light and steady hands and hours that didn't answer to anyone's deployment schedule.

The question that had driven the book — *who are you when you're extraordinary?* — had been the wrong question. Or not wrong. Incomplete. A question that assumed the extraordinary was the important part, when the important part had been sitting on a workbench in a dark workshop the whole time, waiting for him to come back and pick it up.

*What should you build with what's left?*

He didn't know yet.

His hands rested on the bench — broad, calloused, strong from a lifetime of doing things the hard way. The same hands that had pressed against crumbling stone and poured out an ocean. The same hands that had turned a file and cut gears and assembled mechanisms that worked on physics and patience and the understanding of materials that came from years of having no other option. The same hands that had washed in cold water every morning for twenty-three years because the heating lattice didn't work for him and never would.

Good hands. Hands that remembered the work.

He reached for the flint and steel.

The spark caught on the first strike. Tinder to wick. The oil lamp bloomed, and the workshop filled with warm light, and the tools emerged from the dark, and the bench was there, and the projects were there, and the work was there, and it was real, and it had always been real, and outside the shuttered windows the city cooled by degrees in the long, slow descent from an age of magic into whatever came after, and Ash sat at his bench with an unfinished mechanism in his hands and the question of what to build with what was left, and the question was enough, and the night was quiet, and his hands remembered, and he began.
