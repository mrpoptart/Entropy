# Chapter 10

The heating lattice ran through the walls of the eastern district like veins through a body, and every one of them was dead.

Ash stood in the corridor outside Dorenne's office with his hands at his sides and watched her unroll the maintenance map across the table. It was early—grey light through the high windows, the Hall of Infrastructure still quiet, the first shift workers not yet arrived. Dorenne had sent for him before dawn. The message had come through one of her aides, a girl barely older than Maren who'd knocked on his door at the new residence with the particular urgency of someone who'd been told to be quick about it.

"Here," Dorenne said. She tapped the map with two fingers. "The Thornwall grid. Forty-seven residential blocks, six communal halls, two schools. The heating lattice failed three weeks ago. Partial failure at first—reduced flow, uneven warming, the usual degradation. Then full collapse. The maintenance teams patched what they could, but they're running on young-generation pools. They don't have the output to sustain the repair."

She said it the way she said everything—measured, precise, each fact arriving in its place like a stone in a wall she was building. Her hands were still. Her face was composed. But there was something underneath the composure that he'd learned to recognize in the weeks since the activation: a thinness, a tautness, the quality of a rope pulled tight enough that you could hear it hum.

"How many people?" Ash asked.

"Roughly twelve hundred families."

The number landed in his chest. Twelve hundred families. Cold walls, cold water, cold mornings in a city where the heating had always worked because someone with a strong enough pool could keep it running. Until they couldn't.

"The lattice is integrated into the masonry," Dorenne continued. She traced a line on the map—a branching network that spread through the district's walls like roots through soil. "First-generation work. The channelers who built it laid the lattice directly into the stone during construction. Brilliant engineering—the heat distributes evenly, the structure reinforces itself, the system is self-moderating." She paused. "When it has enough input."

"And it doesn't."

"It hasn't for months. The assigned maintenance crew is a team of six. Three middle-generation, three young. Their combined daily output covers roughly a quarter of the lattice's demand. They've been rotating sections—heating one block for a day, then moving to the next, cycling through the district on a four-day rotation. Each block gets warm for one day and cold for three."

She let that sit. One day warm, three days cold. Not a disaster in the dramatic sense—nobody freezing to death, nobody in mortal danger. The grinding, daily arithmetic of decline. The kind of crisis that didn't make speeches or fill council chambers, just wore people down one cold morning at a time.

"What do you want me to do?" he asked.

Dorenne looked at him. Her eyes were steady—dark, sharp, the eyes of someone who'd been calculating three moves ahead since before he was born. But there was no calculation in what she said next, or if there was, it was buried so deep that the surface was indistinguishable from sincerity.

"I want you to turn it back on."

* * *

The eastern district smelled like cold stone and stale air.

Ash walked through the Thornwall streets with Dorenne two steps behind him and a maintenance crew of three trailing at a distance—watching, measuring, ready to check his work against the grid's specifications. The morning was grey and sharp, the kind of cold that found the gaps in your clothing and settled there. His breath clouded. Around him, the district showed its exhaustion.

The buildings were old, well-built, the stonework clean and precise—first-generation architecture, the kind that was meant to last centuries. And it had lasted. The walls were solid. The foundations were sound. But the heating lattice that threaded through every wall and floor and ceiling had gone dark, and without it the buildings were just stone. Just cold, heavy, indifferent stone.

He could see the lattice's traces in the walls—thin lines of darker mineral running through the masonry in branching patterns, the channels that once carried warmth from a central node to every room in every building. They looked like the tributaries of a dry river. Dead channels in living walls.

A woman stood in a doorway and watched them pass. She had a blanket around her shoulders and a child on her hip—a girl, maybe three, bundled in so many layers she looked spherical. The woman's expression was the expression of someone who'd been told help was coming and had learned to stop believing it.

Dorenne nodded to her. The woman nodded back. Nothing else passed between them.

"Here," said the lead maintenance worker—a middle-generation woman named Tirsa, with calloused hands and the particular weariness of someone who'd been solving a problem she didn't have the resources to solve. She led them to a junction point where three branches of the lattice met in a stone housing the size of a barrel, built into the base of a communal hall. The housing was cold to the touch. She pressed her palm against it and channeled—a pulse, weak, exploratory—and Ash watched the lattice lines in the surrounding wall flicker once, a dim ember that traveled two meters through the stone before dying.

"That's everything I've got for this section," Tirsa said. She said it without bitterness, without apology. A fact. "Used to be able to light a full block from this node. Now I can get maybe two rooms. Three if I skip lunch."

"Show me the intake," Ash said.

Tirsa looked at him. Then at Dorenne. Then back at him. She crouched beside the housing and pointed to a smooth section of stone at the base—a palm-shaped depression worn into the surface by decades of hands pressing against it, channeling warmth into the lattice. The stone was smooth and cold and slightly concave, shaped by use, by all the hands that had pressed here before, keeping the district alive.

Ash knelt. He placed his palm against the depression. The stone was ice-cold under his skin. He could feel the lattice through the contact—not visually, not conceptually, but *physically*, the way you could feel a pipe behind a wall by pressing your ear to the plaster. The branching network extended outward from under his hand, reaching through the masonry in every direction, a dead nervous system waiting for a signal.

He reached for the Wellspring.

The reservoir was there. Full. Oceanic. The same vast, patient pressure behind his ribs that had been there since the activation, untouched by the quarry, untouched by anything. He let it flow.

Not carefully. Not with Haran's precision or Ryn's measured control. He didn't have those things—not yet, not reliably, not at the scale this required. He opened the valve and let the Wellspring pour into the lattice the way water pours into a dry channel: flooding, crude, overwhelmingly powerful.

The stone under his palm went warm. Then hot. Then blazing.

He felt the lattice drink. The energy raced outward from the node—branching, splitting, filling every channel in every wall—and the building around him came alive. Not slowly, not in stages, but all at once, the way a light fills a room when you strike a lamp. The walls warmed. The floor warmed. The air itself began to change—the stale, cold stillness replaced by something that moved, that circulated, that pushed the cold out of corners it had occupied for weeks.

Heat. Radiating from the walls like they were breathing. The stone under his hand was almost too hot to touch—sloppy, wasteful, energy bleeding from the intake point in every direction, his output swamping the lattice's capacity to distribute evenly. He could feel the waste heat rolling off his body, the air around his hand shimmering the way it had shimmered around the boulders in the quarry.

Tirsa stepped back. Her eyes were wide.

"More," Dorenne said quietly from behind him.

He pushed. The energy poured through the lattice—out of the communal hall and into the streets, into the residential blocks, reaching through wall after wall, stone after stone. He could feel it spreading. Not precisely—he couldn't have told you which rooms were warming, which channels were full, which sections still ran cold. It was too big, too branching, too complex. He was dumping an ocean into a network of streams and trusting the water to find its own level.

But it was filling. He could feel that. The lattice was filling the way lungs fill—expanding, reaching, the dead channels coming alive in cascading sequence as energy that hadn't flowed through them in weeks suddenly flooded back.

The waste heat was enormous. The air in the corridor was tropical—heavy, thick, the kind of warmth that made you sweat in your coat. He was running hot, catastrophically hot by any trained standard, his efficiency somewhere in the range of brutal and dismal. An elder channeler with Tirsa's experience would have lit the lattice with a fraction of the energy and none of the waste. Ash was heating the neighborhood by heating *everything*—the walls, the air, his own body, the stone under his knees.

He didn't care. The reservoir was oceanic. The waste was enormous and the reservoir didn't notice. He poured and poured and the level didn't drop and the lattice drank and the district warmed and he poured more.

The first sound was small: a click, somewhere in the walls above him. Then another. Then a groan—a deep, structural sound, the stone itself expanding as heat returned to masonry that had contracted in the cold. The building was waking up. The sounds traveled through the walls—clicks and groans and a low, steady hum that he could feel through his palm, the lattice vibrating at a frequency that meant *alive*, meant *full*, meant *working*.

"That's the resonance," Tirsa said. Her voice was strange—thick, unsteady. "When the lattice is at capacity. It hums like that."

The hum. He could feel it in his bones. The whole building was humming, and through the building the street was humming, and through the street the blocks were humming—a vibration that spread outward from his palm like ripples in still water, the dead system coming back to life under the pressure of more energy than it had seen in years.

He held it. Thirty seconds, a minute, two minutes—pouring energy into the lattice until every branch was full, every channel saturated, the entire eastern grid blazing with warmth. The heat was rolling outward now in waves he could feel even at the intake point—the network distributing what he'd given it, the ancient engineering working exactly as designed, pushing warmth into every wall of every building in the district.

He pulled his hand away. The stone under his palm was glowing—a faint, ruddy light, the residual heat turning the mineral channels visible in the masonry. He flexed his fingers. They were red, almost scalded.

The hum continued without him. The lattice was full, saturated, running on the stored energy he'd dumped into it. It would last—how long? He didn't know. Days, maybe. A week. He'd put enough in to power the grid for a week, and it had cost him what felt like nothing.

The corridor was silent.

Then, from somewhere above them, through the warm stone walls, through the floors that were no longer cold, through the ceilings that were radiating heat for the first time in a month: a sound.

A child laughing.

Not at anything. Not because something funny had happened. The laugh of a child who was suddenly, unexpectedly warm—who'd been bundled in layers for weeks and could now feel the floor under their bare feet and the wall against their back and the air on their skin—and the warmth was so startling, so forgotten, so *good* that the only response the body knew was laughter.

The sound traveled through the building. It triggered other sounds—voices, footsteps, the particular commotion of people moving toward something they hadn't expected. Doors opening. A man's voice from above: "The walls are warm. Feel—feel the walls." More voices. More movement. The building coming alive the way the lattice had come alive, people emerging from their cold rooms into corridors that breathed heat, touching the walls with flat palms, looking at each other with expressions that Ash couldn't see but could hear in the quality of their voices—the particular sound of surprise tipping over into something larger.

Ash stood up. His knees were stiff. His hands were red. The corridor was sweltering—his waste heat, not the lattice's design—and he was sweating through his shirt.

Tirsa was crying.

She stood with her back against the warm wall and her calloused hands pressed flat against the stone and she was crying without sound, without performance, without any apparent intention of stopping. The tears ran down her face and she didn't wipe them. She just stood with her hands on the wall she'd been trying to keep alive for months with a pool that wasn't big enough and a crew that wasn't large enough and a system that demanded more than her generation could give.

The wall was warm under her hands. She hadn't been able to make it warm. He had.

Dorenne stepped past him. She put her hand on Tirsa's shoulder—brief, firm, the gesture of someone who acknowledged grief but would not linger in it. Then she turned to Ash.

"There are six more nodes in this district," she said. "Can you do them all today?"

He could. He could do them all today and tomorrow and the next day and the day after that. The reservoir was oceanic. The reservoir didn't care.

"Yes," he said.

She nodded. Not surprised. Expecting it. Already moving.

"Then let's go."

* * *

The second node was two blocks east, in the basement of a residential tower. The third was in the street itself—a junction box set into the paving stones, the intake worn smooth by generations of maintenance workers kneeling to press their palms against the stone. The fourth was at the back of a school where the children had been wearing their coats indoors for three weeks.

Each one was the same. Kneel, press, pour. The Wellspring flooding into dead channels. The lattice waking. The waste heat billowing off him in waves that fogged windows and made people step back. The hum returning to the walls—that deep, structural vibration that said *full*, said *alive*, said *someone finally came*.

Each one was different. At the second node, an old man was waiting—sitting on a stool in the corridor with his coat buttoned to the throat, as though he'd been sitting there since the lattice failed, as though he'd known someone would come eventually and he intended to be present when they did. When the walls warmed, he unbuttoned his coat slowly, methodically, one button at a time, and the deliberateness of the gesture was itself a statement. He didn't look at Ash. He looked at the wall. He pressed his hand against it and held it there and closed his eyes, and his expression was the expression of someone touching a face they'd been afraid they'd forgotten.

At the school, the children came out. Not in an orderly file—in a rush, a tumble, the particular stampede of children who have been confined and are suddenly free. They poured into the corridor and found the walls warm and the air warm and the floor warm under their feet, and the sound they made was not laughter exactly but something adjacent to it—a kind of collective exhalation, a release. A girl touched the wall with both hands and said, very seriously, to no one in particular: "It's *working*."

At the street junction, a woman brought him tea. He didn't know her name. She came out of the nearest door with a clay cup steaming in her hands—heated the old way, on a fire, because the lattice that should have warmed the kettle was the same lattice he was fixing—and she held it out to him without a word. He took it. The tea was bitter and too hot and he drank it standing over the junction box with his knees aching and his hands red and the district humming around him like something he'd built, and the tea was the best thing he'd ever tasted.

Not because it was good tea. Because someone had made it for him. Because someone had seen what he was doing and had gone inside and heated water over a fire and brought him a cup, and the cup was an offering, and the offering meant *you are useful*, meant *you are necessary*, meant *we needed someone and you came*.

Twenty-three years. Twenty-three years of cold water because he couldn't activate the lattice in his own bathroom. Twenty-three years of his mother heating the kettle while he stood in the doorway pretending it didn't matter. Twenty-three years of being the person who took warmth from the world and gave nothing back.

The tea burned his tongue. He didn't care. The warmth of it ran down his throat and joined the warmth of the Wellspring behind his ribs and the warmth of the walls humming around him and the warmth of the woman's expression as she watched him drink, and all of it was the same warmth, and all of it was the answer to a question he'd been asking since he was five years old and couldn't make the stove work.

*Am I worth something?*

The district said yes. The walls said yes. The children said yes and the old man said yes and the woman with the tea said yes and Tirsa's tears said yes, and the yes was so loud, so physical, so present in his body that he couldn't think about anything else. He didn't want to think about anything else. He wanted to stay here, in this moment, in this warmth, in this answer, forever.

Dorenne was already moving to the next node.

* * *

He finished the eastern district by early afternoon.

Seven nodes. Forty-seven residential blocks. Six communal halls. Two schools. The entire Thornwall heating grid, blazing to life under the pressure of more energy than it had held in a decade. He'd been crude about it—flooding rather than filling, brute force rather than precision—and the waste had been spectacular. The streets around the last few nodes were noticeably warmer than the surrounding districts, the excess heat bleeding through the stone and radiating outward like the afterglow of a fever. Tirsa and her crew would spend the rest of the day checking the lattice for stress fractures—his flooding might have damaged the finer channels, the ones designed for the gentle pressure of a maintenance worker's measured channeling, not the Wellspring's hammer.

He'd fix that next time. He'd learn to be gentler. Maybe.

But the grid was on. The grid was *on*, and the district was warm, and people were in the streets—not because they had to be, not for errands or work, but because they wanted to be, because the streets were warm for the first time in a month and the warmth itself was a destination. Children were running. Doors were open. Someone was cooking—the smell of heated oil and spices drifting from an open window, the first hot meal in a kitchen that worked.

Ash walked through it.

He walked through the district he'd fixed, and every step was a step through a world he'd made. The warm stones under his boots. The warm air against his face. The hum in the walls. A man repairing a shutter, his coat off, his sleeves rolled, working with the casual unhurriedness of someone who wasn't trying to stay warm while he worked. Two women sitting on a bench outside a communal hall, talking, their postures loose and open—the posture of people who weren't hunched against the cold. A boy carrying water from a communal tap, whistling.

*Because of me.*

The thought was simple and enormous and he let it fill him. Because of him. The lights worked because of him. The walls were warm because of him. The children were running and the woman was cooking and the man had his sleeves rolled because of him. After twenty-three years of being the person the world carried, he had carried the world back, and the weight of it was nothing. The weight of it was joy.

Dorenne walked beside him. She said nothing for a long time—just walked, and watched him walk, and let the district speak for itself. She had an eye for timing, for the beat of a moment, for when to press and when to let silence do the work. It was one of the things he was learning about her—the precision of her restraint, the way she used quiet the same way a channeler used a pause between pulses.

"Southern quarter tomorrow," she said eventually. Her voice was conversational, easy. Not a command. An observation about what came next, as natural as the next step on a path. "The water system. Fourteen hundred families rationing from communal taps. The aqueduct channelers can push water uphill for maybe four hours a day. After that, the taps go dry."

He looked at her. She was watching the district—the open doors, the children, the man with the rolled sleeves. Her expression was complicated in the way her expressions always were: genuine pleasure layered over something structural, something that was already building the next move on top of this one.

"Same as today?" he asked. "Find the intake, push?"

"The water system is different—pressure channeling, not thermal. But the principle is the same. The infrastructure exists. The design is sound. It just needs more power than the current workforce can provide."

More power. More than six maintenance workers with young-generation pools could muster. More than a career's worth of Tirsa's calloused hands pressing against cold stone.

The Wellspring could provide it. The Wellspring could provide anything.

"I'll be ready," he said.

Dorenne nodded. She touched his arm—brief, almost incidental, the kind of contact that might have been accidental if everything about Dorenne weren't deliberate. Then she turned toward the Hall of Infrastructure, already moving, already planning, the machine of her mind engaged with the southern quarter the way it had been engaged with the eastern district twelve hours ago.

Ash watched her go. Then he turned back to the Thornwall streets and walked.

The light was golden. Late afternoon, the same quality of light that had lit the quarry the day before—but this was different. The quarry had been spectacle. Boulders and craters and the private theater of power unleashed. This was use. This was purpose. This was the thing he'd been waiting for since he was old enough to understand that waiting was all he could do.

A girl ran past him—six, maybe seven, her hair flying, her bare feet slapping the warm stone. She glanced at him as she ran and he saw her see him—not with recognition, not knowing who he was or what he'd done, just a glance, a moment of a child's eyes on a stranger's face—and then she was past, running, gone around a corner with the sound of her footsteps fading into the hum of the district.

She didn't know. She didn't need to know. The walls were warm and the stone was warm under her feet and she was running because the evening was good and the world was working and someone, somewhere, had made it work.

His throat tightened. His eyes burned.

He kept walking.

* * *

The water system in the southern quarter was different from the heating lattice, and harder.

The aqueducts ran uphill—that was the fundamental problem. Solathis was built on a plateau, the water source in the gorge below, and for generations the city's channelers had pushed water upward through stone channels carved into the cliff face, maintaining a pressure that defied gravity through sheer sustained force. The system required constant input: channelers at pump stations along the route, each one maintaining a section of the uphill flow, their combined effort creating a pressure chain that delivered water from river level to the plateau's edge.

The stations closest to the top had failed first. The young-generation channelers assigned to the high stations didn't have the pool depth to maintain flow against the full weight of the water column above them. They'd compensated by shortening their shifts, running the pumps for four hours instead of eight, concentrating their output into bursts that pushed water through in waves rather than a steady flow. The taps ran for part of the day and went dry for the rest.

Ash stood at the highest pump station—a stone chamber carved into the plateau's rim, the floor vibrating with the residual pressure of the water column below—and pressed his palms against the channeling interface. It was similar to the heating lattice's intake but oriented differently: not heat into stone, but force into water, a sustained downward pressure that pushed against gravity and kept seventeen tons of water moving uphill through sixty meters of vertical channel.

He pushed.

The water moved.

It moved the way water moves when a dam breaks—not gradually, not in stages, but with the sudden, violent authority of constraint removed. He could feel it through the interface—the water column surging upward as the Wellspring's force overrode gravity, the pressure slamming through the channels with an intensity that made the stone chamber shake. Too much. Way too much. He heard a crack somewhere below him—a channel joint, probably, stressed by pressure it was never designed to handle.

He pulled back. Tried again. Gentler—or what passed for gentler when your reservoir was an ocean and the exercise called for a cup. The water column settled into a sustained flow, still too fast, still too forceful, but no longer rupturing the joints. He could hear it—the rush of water through stone, a sound like wind through a tunnel, and then, faintly, from the streets above: the sound of taps opening.

Not one tap. Dozens. The communal taps that had been dry since morning, the household connections that had been rationed for weeks—all of them suddenly, simultaneously pressurized as the Wellspring rammed water through every channel in the system. He heard a distant shout—surprise, not alarm. A cheer, maybe. Or just the sound of someone who'd been carrying buckets for a month suddenly watching water pour from their tap with the kind of pressure they remembered from childhood.

The maintenance workers at the lower stations felt it too. He learned this later—Dorenne told him, with the particular satisfaction of someone reporting a successful campaign. The channelers at stations two through six had been preparing for their afternoon shifts when the entire water column jumped, the pressure in their sections doubling and then tripling as Ash's input from the top overwhelmed every bottleneck in the system. Two of them had let go of their interfaces in shock. One had laughed. The shift supervisor had said a word that Dorenne chose not to repeat.

The taps ran all day. All night. The next morning, they were still running.

* * *

The third deployment was structural. A residential tower in the midtown corridor—six stories, first-generation construction, the foundation reinforcement failing. The building hadn't collapsed, but it had shifted. Ash could see it from the street: a lean, subtle but visible, the kind of tilt that made doors stick and windows crack and residents sleep with one ear open.

The problem was in the foundation layer—a bed of channeled stone that had been hardened and compressed by ancient channelers into something denser than natural rock, a platform that distributed the building's weight evenly across the underlying geology. The channeling had degraded. The stone was reverting to its natural state—loosening, softening, the molecular bonds that generations of maintenance had sustained finally breaking down faster than the current crew could restore them.

Ash pressed his hands flat against the foundation wall in the building's basement. He could feel the degradation—a looseness in the stone, a lack of cohesion, like pressing against sand instead of rock. He pushed.

This was different from heat or force. Structural channeling was about compression—pushing energy into the stone's molecular structure, forcing the bonds tighter, making the material denser and harder than nature intended. It required understanding, according to the maintenance manuals Dorenne had given him. Knowledge of crystalline structure. Awareness of stress patterns.

He didn't have any of that.

He had the Wellspring.

He poured energy into the foundation the way he'd poured it into the heating lattice—flooding, crude, the brute-force approach of someone who could afford to waste ninety percent of his output and still accomplish more than a trained crew could manage in a week. The stone under his hands hardened. He could feel it—a tightening, a compression, the material going from sand-like looseness to something dense and solid under the pressure of more energy than any single channeler had ever pushed into a residential foundation.

The building groaned. Not the alarming groan of failure—the deep, structural sound of something settling back into place, weight redistributing across a foundation that could suddenly bear it again. The lean corrected. Not entirely—he couldn't undo the structural shifts that had already propagated through the upper floors—but the progression stopped. The base was solid. The building would hold.

He emerged from the basement with stone dust in his hair and his palms aching. A man was standing in the lobby—middle-aged, tired-looking, a toolbox in one hand. A maintenance worker. He stared at Ash the way Tirsa had stared, the way the pump station workers had stared: the expression of someone watching a problem they'd been fighting for months get solved in minutes.

"The door," the man said. He pointed to the building's front entrance—a heavy stone door on channeled hinges, the kind that was supposed to swing smoothly and had been sticking for weeks as the building's lean pulled the frame out of alignment. "Try the door."

Ash pushed the door. It swung open. Smooth, easy, the hinges finding their original alignment as the foundation's correction propagated upward through the structure.

The man looked at the door. Looked at Ash. Opened his mouth, closed it, opened it again. Didn't speak. Just nodded—a nod that contained everything he wasn't saying, all the months of fighting a losing battle with a pool that wasn't big enough and a building that was slowly dying, compressed into a single movement of his head.

Ash nodded back.

* * *

The fourth deployment was a transit shaft.

The transit lifts ran on channeled pressure—platforms raised and lowered through vertical shafts by teams of channelers maintaining force columns beneath them. The system moved thousands of people per day between the plateau's tiers. It had been the city's pride, once. A generation ago, the lifts ran every few minutes, smooth and fast, powered by elder channelers whose pools could sustain the pressure columns for full shifts without tiring.

Now the western district's main shaft had been dark for two years.

Ash stood at the base of the shaft and looked up. Thirty meters of vertical stone, the interior smooth and featureless, a column of dead air where the pressure platform used to run. The channeling interfaces were built into the walls at three-meter intervals—palm-sized depressions where operators had once pressed their hands and pushed upward, each one sustaining their section of the lift, the combined effort creating a continuous column of force that raised the platform from bottom to top in under a minute.

Nobody had enough output to run it alone. That was the design's fundamental assumption: multiple channelers, working in concert, each one contributing their section. Distributed effort. Shared cost. The same principle as the heating lattice, the water system, the structural reinforcement—everything in the city built for a level of collective capacity that no longer existed.

The Wellspring didn't need a team.

Ash pressed his palm against the lowest interface and pushed. The force column slammed into existence—a pillar of compressed air that shot upward through the shaft with a sound like a giant's inhalation, the pressure wave hitting each interface in sequence and ringing off the stone walls in a cascading boom that echoed up and up and up until it burst from the shaft's open top thirty meters above like a cannon shot.

Too much. Again. Always too much.

He pulled back, recalibrated, pushed again. The force column rebuilt—less violent this time, a steady upward pressure that filled the shaft from floor to ceiling. He could feel it—the weight of the air itself becoming a tool, the column dense enough to support a platform, to carry weight, to do what it had been designed to do.

"Try it," he said.

The transit workers—two young women who'd been assigned to the dead shaft as a formality, maintaining equipment that hadn't run in two years—looked at each other. Then one of them stepped onto the platform.

She rose.

Not fast. Not smooth. The column was uneven—Ash's single-point input creating a pressure gradient that wobbled and surged, nothing like the even, distributed force of a full team. The platform tilted, corrected, tilted again. The woman grabbed the safety rail. She looked down at Ash with an expression that was equal parts terror and delight.

"Keep going," she called.

He kept going. He maintained the column as the platform rose—ten meters, twenty, twenty-five—and the vibration in his arm was enormous, the sustained output of holding thirty meters of air at platform-bearing pressure requiring not just force but *consistency*, and consistency was the thing he was worst at. The column surged and the platform lurched and the woman's knuckles were white on the rail, but she was rising, she was moving, the dead shaft was carrying a person for the first time in two years.

The platform reached the top. The boom of it settling into the upper dock traveled down the shaft and hit Ash like a concussion. He released the column. His arm dropped to his side. His hand was shaking—not from depletion but from the sustained effort of holding something steady, the same physical exhaustion he'd felt at the quarry when precision, not power, was the constraint.

From thirty meters above, the woman's voice echoed down the shaft:

"Send it back!"

He pressed the interface again. The column rebuilt. The platform descended—smoother this time, his second attempt learning from his first. The woman rode it down with both hands on the rail and a grin that Ash could see from twenty meters away, widening as she dropped, her hair lifting in the pressure column's updraft.

She stepped off the platform. She looked at him. She looked at the shaft—the dead shaft, the two-year-dead shaft, the shaft she'd been assigned to maintain as a make-work posting because nobody believed it would ever run again.

"Again," she said.

* * *

The deployments blurred.

Not in his memory—each one stayed distinct, vivid, a separate frame in a sequence that kept expanding. The heating lattice with Tirsa's tears. The water system with its explosive pressurization. The tower with its settling groan. The transit shaft with the woman riding the platform down, her hair in the updraft, her grin from twenty meters.

They blurred in time. Three deployments became five. Five became eight. A retaining wall in the northern quarter—fractured stone, a street that had been closed for months because the wall might come down at any time. Ash pressed his hands against the fracture and compressed and the wall held and the street opened and a market that had relocated due to the closure moved back within a week. A communal bathhouse where the heating coils had failed—similar to the Thornwall lattice but smaller, more intimate, and when the water ran hot again an old man lowered himself into the bath and sat there for a long time with his eyes closed and his hands floating on the surface and didn't say a word.

A bridge. A sewage pump. A grain storage facility where the climate channeling had degraded and three months of harvest were at risk of spoiling. Each one genuine. Each one necessary. Each one a crisis that had been building for months or years as the maintenance crews stretched thinner and the pools ran shallower and the infrastructure built for a stronger generation slowly surrendered to the arithmetic of decline.

And each one easy.

That was the thing he kept coming back to, the fact that rearranged his understanding of himself with every deployment. Each one was easy. Not technically—his channeling was still crude, still wasteful, still the brute-force approach of someone who didn't have the training to be efficient. But in terms of cost, in terms of what the Wellspring gave up to accomplish these things, each deployment was nothing. A breath. A heartbeat. A drop from an ocean that didn't drop.

He checked the reservoir after each deployment—the inward reach he'd practiced at the quarry, feeling for the edge, trying to sense whether the level had changed. And each time, the answer was the same. The ocean was the ocean. The level hadn't moved. The cliff he stood on hadn't shifted. He could no more perceive the cost of fixing a district's heating than he could perceive the weight of a single grain of sand in his pocket.

He was getting better. Not precise—not Haran's micro-pulses, not Ryn's measured warmth—but better. His flooding was less catastrophic. His waste heat was still enormous but less spectacular. By the fifth deployment, he could fill a heating lattice without fogging the windows of the adjacent building. By the eighth, he could sustain a water column without cracking the channel joints. Practice was doing what practice does—not teaching him finesse, but wearing grooves in his instincts, establishing the particular muscle memory of *this much for this task* through sheer repetition.

It wasn't precision. It was calibration by brute force. A hammer learning to hit nails instead of tables, not because it had become a screwdriver but because it had hit enough nails to know the difference.

Dorenne saw every deployment. Not always physically present—she had the city to run, council sessions to navigate, a constituency to manage—but always informed, always aware. The reports came to her office. The charts on her wall grew. Each deployment was marked on a map of Solathis—a dot, a date, a brief notation of what had been fixed and how. The dots were spreading across the city like stars appearing in a darkening sky.

She never told him what to do. That was the precision of it—the thing he wouldn't see until much later, the architecture of her influence. She presented the crises. She laid out the needs. She showed him the map and the dots and the empty spaces between them and she said *this district has been without water for six weeks* or *this building's foundation will fail before winter* or *twelve hundred families*, and she let the math do the work.

She never had to ask. He volunteered. Every time.

Because the crises were real. The needs were real. The twelve hundred families were real and the cold walls were real and the old man in the bath with his eyes closed was real, and how could he not? How could he sit in his room at the Hall of Infrastructure with a reservoir that could power the entire city's heating grid for a year and not fix the things that were broken? How could he know that people were cold and water was dry and buildings were leaning and shafts were dark and do nothing?

He couldn't. Of course he couldn't. That was the whole point. That was who he'd been for twenty-three years—the person who watched the world break and couldn't lift a finger to fix it. The person who stood in doorways while other people heated the kettle. The person whose existence was a net cost, a line item in the ledger of his family's resources, a mouth to feed and a body to house and nothing, nothing, nothing coming back the other way.

Not anymore. Now the ledger was reversed. Now every day was a deposit, every deployment a payment into an account that had been overdrawn since birth. He was useful. He was necessary. People saw him and their faces changed—from worry to relief, from resignation to hope, from the expression of someone carrying a weight to the expression of someone setting it down.

He lived for those faces. He lived for the moment when the lattice hummed and the taps ran and the lift moved and the wall held, and someone—always someone, in every district—looked at him with an expression that said *you are the answer*. He collected those expressions the way a starving man collects meals. He banked them. He held them against the twenty-three years of being no one's answer to anything.

And the deployments kept coming. Dorenne's map kept filling. The dots spread across Solathis and each dot was a neighborhood that worked, a system that ran, a crisis averted. The machine was running now—smooth, efficient, the rhythm of it as natural as breathing. Need. Deployment. Gratitude. Next need.

He didn't question the rhythm. Why would he? The rhythm was the answer. The rhythm was the proof. The rhythm was the thing that made him real.

* * *

Three weeks in, Dorenne invited him to a council briefing.

He sat in the gallery—not at the table, not yet—and watched her present the infrastructure report. Charts. Numbers. Maps. The dots he'd been planting across the city rendered in ink and parchment, each one annotated with the system restored and the population served.

"Fourteen districts have received Wellspring reinforcement in the past twenty-one days," Dorenne said. Her voice was steady, authoritative, the voice of someone presenting data that proved her right. "Heating, water, structural, transit. Combined, these interventions have restored service to approximately nineteen thousand residents."

Nineteen thousand. He'd known the number in pieces—twelve hundred here, fourteen hundred there, a building of forty families, a district of three thousand—but hearing it assembled, hearing the total spoken aloud in this room full of council members and officials and the quiet machinery of governance, the number hit him differently. Not in his chest. In his spine.

Nineteen thousand people. Warm. Watered. Housed. Moving.

Because of him.

A council member—elder generation, grey-haired, the particular bearing of someone who'd been powerful enough to be comfortable for his entire life—raised a question about sustainability. "How long will these interventions last? Is the Wellspring committing to ongoing maintenance, or are these one-time infusions?"

Dorenne fielded it with the particular fluency of someone who'd anticipated the question three days ago. "The interventions are designed as deep recharges. The Wellspring's output saturates the existing lattice—the heating grids, the pressure systems, the structural reinforcement—at a level that provides weeks or months of operational capacity. Maintenance crews continue their regular schedules. The difference is that they're maintaining a full system instead of triaging a failing one."

Weeks or months. The words registered but didn't land. Ash was thinking about the next deployment—a district in the northeast where the communal heating had been on rotation for two months. He was thinking about the intake points and the lattice structure and whether he could fill it in a single session or whether he'd need two. He was thinking about the faces.

The briefing ended. Dorenne caught his eye as he left the gallery. A small nod. Approval. Not effusive—Dorenne didn't do effusive. But warm, in the way that her warmth was always warm: precise, targeted, the exact temperature needed to keep him moving in the direction she'd mapped.

He nodded back.

Outside the council chamber, the evening light was on the city—the golden hour, the time of day when Solathis looked the way it must have looked a hundred years ago, when the elder generation was the young generation and the pools were deep and the infrastructure hummed without effort. The upper districts were lit. The lower districts were dimmer, but less dim than they'd been a month ago. Some of those lights were his.

He walked home through streets that were warm. Through a district he'd fixed. Past a transit shaft that was running—not the one he'd restarted, a different one, still operated by its regular crew, but the sight of it working, the platform rising and falling in its regular rhythm, reminded him of the woman with the white-knuckled grip and the widening grin, and the memory was warm, and the warmth joined the other warmths, and the reservoir behind his ribs was still full, still oceanic, still patient.

He wasn't counting. He didn't need to count. The reservoir was there, it would be there tomorrow, it would be there for every deployment Dorenne could devise and every crisis the city could produce, and the only question—the only question that mattered—was what he would fix next.

*What should I fix next?*

The question was exhilarating. For twenty-three years, the question had been *what can I do?* and the answer had been nothing. Now the question was *what should I fix next?* and the answer was everything. Everything, eventually. Every cold wall, every dry tap, every leaning building, every dark shaft, every broken system in this city that had never had a place for him and now needed him more than it had ever needed anyone.

The evening was cool. The stars were coming out. Somewhere behind him, in the district he'd fixed that morning, the heating lattice hummed in the walls, and the sound of it—too faint to actually hear from this distance, but present in his mind like a chord sustained—was the sweetest sound he'd ever known.

He was useful.

He was useful at last.
