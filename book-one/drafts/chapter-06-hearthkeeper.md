# Chapter 6

The jacket didn't fit.

Ash stood in front of the narrow mirror in the temporary housing and pulled at the collar, which rode up against his jaw in a way that made him look like a boy wearing his father's clothes. Which, in a sense, he was. His father had pressed it that morning—the iron heated by his mother's channeling from the bed where she sat with her splinted leg propped on a cushion, directing the temperature with small gestures of her hand while his father worked the creases out.

"Hold still," Dorenne said.

She was standing behind him, assessing. Not the jacket—him. The way he held his shoulders, the angle of his chin, the expression he defaulted to when he wasn't thinking about his face. She'd arrived at the temporary housing an hour before the event, which meant she'd left her office during the busiest part of the morning, which meant this mattered more than she was letting on.

"When Councillor Edrath asks you a question—and she will, she always does, she enjoys watching new people stammer—you answer briefly and then look at me. I'll take it from there."

"What if her question is interesting?"

"It won't be. Her questions are designed to establish that she's asking questions, not to receive answers." Dorenne adjusted something at his collar—a fold, a line—with the precise, impersonal touch of a woman who dressed other people the way she dressed buildings: for structural integrity. "You'll stand to my left. The podium has a channeling interface built into its surface—don't touch it. Don't touch anything on the stage unless I tell you to."

"Because I'll break it."

"Because you'll break it spectacularly, and spectacle is what we're managing today, not creating." She stepped back. Her eyes moved over him one more time—the assessment complete, the verdict held privately. "You look fine."

"I look like I'm about to be sick."

"You look fine. The being sick is internal and therefore not my problem until it becomes external." She picked up her own cloak from the chair—the formal half-cloak, the Infrastructure seal dark against darker fabric. "One more thing."

He waited.

"They're going to want to touch you. Handshakes, shoulder-clasps, the resonance greeting. People will reach for your hand and try to feel your pool—it's instinct, it's how they calibrate someone new. You cannot reciprocate. If you push even a thread of what you carry into a casual handshake, you'll—"

"Burn someone's hand off."

"I was going to say 'cause a scene.' But yes. That too." Something moved in her face—not quite a smile, closer to the place where a smile would be stored if the morning allowed for one. "Keep your hands at your sides unless you're shaking someone's hand, and when you shake, think of it as holding an egg."

"An egg."

"The most fragile egg you've ever held. One that's also the career of the Director of Infrastructure."

She said this lightly, and the lightness was real, and underneath the lightness was something that wasn't light at all. She needed this to go well. Not just for him—for everything her office was trying to do, for the political capital she'd been spending on infrastructure maintenance for years, for the argument she'd been making to the Council about resources and priorities and the slow fraying of systems that nobody wanted to look at directly.

He was part of that argument now. The most compelling part.

"Dorenne."

She was already moving toward the door.

"Thank you. For—" He gestured at the jacket, the preparation, the hour of her morning. "All of this."

She turned. Her eyes held him for a moment with the particular attention she gave to things she was calculating the value of. Then something underneath the calculation surfaced—brief, warm, gone before it fully arrived.

"You're going to be fine, Ash. Just don't touch the podium."

* * *

The Hall of Infrastructure looked different from the inside when it was full of people.

Ash had walked these corridors every morning for three weeks—the ramp up from the ground floor, the long hallway past the deployment office, the tall windows that caught the eastern light. He knew the building the way he knew his parents' old apartment: by texture, by temperature, by the particular quality of silence in the early hours before the staff arrived. It was the first building that had ever felt like his.

Today it was someone else's.

The main hall on the second floor—usually a working space, long tables covered in deployment maps and maintenance schedules—had been cleared and rearranged. Rows of chairs faced a raised platform at the far end, and the chairs were filling with people Ash didn't recognize. Council members in formal half-cloaks. Senior administrators from offices he'd only seen on organizational charts. Journalists—three or four of them, seated together near the front, with the focused, acquisitive expressions of people whose job was to decide what mattered. A handful of scholars, including one Ash recognized from the testing—the younger examiner, the one who'd stared at the glass dials with his assumptions quietly demolished.

And behind them, along the walls and in the doorway, the people who hadn't been invited but had come anyway. Staff from other offices. Maintenance workers who'd heard something from someone who'd heard something. A woman Ash recognized from the lower corridors—she'd nodded at him once, weeks ago, when he was carrying files down the ramp. She was standing on her toes to see over the shoulder of the man in front of her.

The room hummed with the particular energy of people waiting to be told something they already half-knew. Rumor had done most of the work. The scholars who'd tested him had told colleagues. The maintenance team who'd witnessed the activation had told their families. Dorenne's staff had told no one, officially, and everyone, inevitably. Information in Solathis moved the way heat moved through stone—outward from the source, following the paths of least resistance, arriving everywhere eventually regardless of who tried to hold it in.

Dorenne had understood this. She hadn't tried to contain the information. She'd tried to arrive ahead of it.

Ash stood in the narrow corridor behind the stage, where a curtain of dark fabric separated him from the hall. Through the gap at its edge, he could see the front row. Councillor Edrath—he recognized her from briefings, a sharp-featured woman in her sixties who sat very still and watched everything. Beside her, two men Ash didn't know, leaning toward each other in conversation. A woman with grey-streaked hair who kept glancing at the stage and then writing something in a small notebook.

His hands were at his sides. The pressure behind his ribs—the reservoir, the Wellspring, the ocean he'd been carrying for a week—pulsed with his heartbeat. Steady. Patient. Indifferent to the occasion.

He thought about the egg.

Dorenne appeared beside him. She'd been speaking with Tessaly—her senior aide, the one who managed logistics with the expressionless efficiency of a clock—and now Tessaly was moving toward the hall's entrance, positioning staff at the doors, ensuring the event had the shape Dorenne had designed for it.

"Ready?" Dorenne asked.

"No."

"Good. People who are ready for this are the ones I worry about." She adjusted her cloak. Her hands were steady. Her face had settled into the particular expression she wore for public occasions—composed, warm enough to be approachable, controlled enough to be authoritative. The face of someone who understood that politics was performance and performed it with the discipline of a master channeler threading energy through stone.

She stepped through the curtain. He heard her voice—clear, carrying, pitched for the back of the room.

"Good morning. Thank you for coming on short notice. I know your schedules are full, and I wouldn't have asked you here if this weren't worth your time."

A pause. The room settled.

"Six days ago, during a routine infrastructure assessment in the Fifth Terrace residential district, an unprecedented channeling event occurred. You've heard versions of this—some accurate, some less so. I'm here to give you the facts, and to introduce you to the person at the center of them."

Ash listened from behind the curtain. Her voice was precise and unhurried. She described the building assessment—not the full truth, not the near-collapse and the rubble and his mother's blood, but a version that was accurate in its outlines and strategic in its emphasis. An infrastructure team working on a residential building. A member of her staff present at the site. An activation event of extraordinary magnitude.

"The individual in question has been assessed by our examination team and by Scholar Vasra, who has studied channeling theory for forty years. Their findings are consistent: we are dealing with a channeling reservoir of a scale that has no precedent in recorded measurement. Scholar Vasra has used the term 'Wellspring'—a deep source, not a standard pool. The metaphor is apt."

She paused again. The silence in the hall had changed—it had weight now, the density of people holding their breath.

"I want to be clear about several things. First: this individual has been a member of my staff for three weeks. He was hired for his intelligence, his organizational skill, and his judgment—none of which required channeling, because he had none. His activation is not a product of any program or experiment. It is, as far as we can determine, a spontaneous event—the unsealing of a dormant reservoir that expressed, for twenty-three years, as complete magical absence."

Ash heard the emphasis she placed on *my staff*. The pronoun that folded him into her office, her judgment, her record of competent management. He was, in this telling, a discovery she'd already made—the magical potential was a bonus, an extraordinary validation of an investment she'd already committed to. The narrative was elegant and almost true and served purposes he could feel pressing against the walls of the room like heat.

"Second: the Wellspring represents a resource of enormous potential value to this city. I use the word 'resource' deliberately. In a time when every kilojoule matters, when our infrastructure teams are stretched beyond sustainability, when the Council itself has acknowledged that our maintenance capacity is in structural deficit—the Wellspring is not a curiosity. It is a practical opportunity."

She was brilliant at this. He could hear it—the way she built the argument in layers, each one resting on the last, each one making the next feel inevitable. She wasn't asking for permission. She was telling the room what was already true and inviting them to agree with her description of it.

"I'd like to introduce you to Ashlyn Torren."

His name in the room. The full version, formal, attached to nothing the audience knew and everything Dorenne had just told them. He stepped through the curtain.

The hall was brighter than the corridor. The channeling strips in the walls had been turned up—he could feel them, faintly, the way he'd been learning to feel ambient energy since the activation, a low warmth against his skin that had nothing to do with temperature. The faces in the rows of chairs turned toward him, and the turning was a thing he felt physically—a pressure, a gravity, the weight of collective attention settling on him like hands.

He walked to the podium. Dorenne was already there, half-turned toward him, her posture open and welcoming in the way she'd rehearsed with him—*I'm presenting you, not caging you. Stand where they can see your face.* He took his place at her left. The podium's channeling interface was a smooth disc of pale stone set into its surface, warm with residual energy. He kept his hands at his sides.

"Thank you," he said. His voice came out steadier than he expected—the performance rising, the old architecture of cheerfulness and ease rebuilding itself around the unfamiliar bones of the situation. "I'd say I'm happy to be here, but Director Kharren told me not to touch anything, so I'm mostly just trying to stand very still."

A ripple of laughter. Not much—the room was too careful for that—but enough. Dorenne's mouth moved in a way that might have been approval.

"Ash has agreed to a brief demonstration," Dorenne said. "What you're about to see is a controlled channeling display—emphasis on controlled. We've prepared a target to give you a sense of the Wellspring's scale."

Tessaly had positioned a stone block at the center of the stage. It was about the size of a man's torso—channeling-reactive mineral, the same kind they'd used in the testing, but larger, set on a reinforced metal stand. The graduated markings on its surface ran from one to ten in the same color spectrum Ash remembered from the basement room. Yellow to violet.

Ash looked at the stone. He looked at Dorenne. She gave him the smallest nod—the one that meant *proceed, but carefully.*

He placed his palm on the stone.

He'd practiced this. Two days of practice in the basement room with Vasra supervising, learning to find the difference between a whisper and a shout in the reservoir's pressure. He still couldn't find a thread—his minimum was broader than most people's maximum—but he'd learned to hold back the flood. To open the valve a crack instead of ripping it off its hinges.

He pushed.

The stone shifted through the spectrum. Yellow. Orange. Red. The colors moved smoothly this time—not the violent, instant cascade of the first testing, but a controlled progression that Ash held with his jaw tight and his breathing measured. The stone hummed. The air around his hand warmed.

Violet.

He held it there. The stone sang a single sustained note, and the channeling strips in the walls dimmed in response—the building's own energy yielding to the greater pressure, the way a candle flame bends in the presence of a furnace. The audience could feel it. He could see it in their faces—the subtle shift in posture, the unconscious leaning back, the widened eyes of channelers who could sense the field he was projecting and were recalibrating everything they knew about what a human body could hold.

The silence in the room was not the silence of an empty space. It was the silence of a full one—packed with breath and calculation and the particular quality of attention that people gave to things that frightened them and fascinated them in equal measure.

He pulled his hand back. The stone dimmed—slowly, the violet fading through the spectrum in reverse, the residual energy releasing in a gentle exhale. The channeling strips in the walls brightened again. The air cooled.

Someone in the third row let out a breath they'd been holding.

Dorenne stepped forward. "Questions will be managed through my office. Councillor Edrath, I believe you had—"

"How much?" Edrath's voice was clear and unhurried. She hadn't leaned back. She hadn't widened her eyes. She sat the way she'd sat before the demonstration—very still, very watchful, the composure of a woman who'd been in politics long enough to know that awe was a vulnerability. "How much can he hold?"

"Scholar Vasra's assessment is that the reservoir exceeds any measurement on record. The precise quantity is—"

"An estimate will do."

Dorenne paused. The pause was calibrated—long enough to suggest consideration, short enough to maintain authority. "We're still refining the measurements. What I can tell you is that the Wellspring's capacity is sufficient to address infrastructure needs at a scale our current workforce cannot match. The practical applications are what I'd like to focus on."

"The practical applications," Edrath repeated. She turned her gaze to Ash. "Mr. Torren. You had no channeling ability before last week?"

"None," he said. He remembered Dorenne's instruction—*answer briefly and then look at me*—but Edrath's eyes held him with the directness of someone who didn't allow people to look away. "I was a dead weight. Twenty-three years."

A murmur in the room. The term landed differently here than it did in daily conversation—harsher, more visible, stripped of the casual cruelty that made it ordinary. Dorenne's hand moved at her side, a small gesture that might have been reaching for the conversation and deciding against it.

"And now you're the most powerful channeler in the city," Edrath said. "Possibly the most powerful in recorded history."

"I'm also the least experienced. I broke every instrument they put in front of me during the testing." He said this with the lightness he'd practiced—the deprecation that made people comfortable, that turned the extraordinary into something they could hold. The old performance, adapted for a new stage. "Scholar Vasra had to bring out the children's practice stones."

This time the laughter was warmer. It was also shorter—cut off by the awareness, spreading through the room like the heat from his demonstration, that the joke was about a man who shattered calibration stones the way other people warmed tea.

Edrath didn't laugh. She wrote something in her notebook. "I'll expect a full briefing within the week, Director Kharren. The Council will want to discuss allocation."

"Of course," Dorenne said. The word was smooth and immediate and gave away nothing about the three meetings she'd already scheduled with Council members before Edrath could organize her own.

* * *

The questions continued for twenty minutes. Dorenne fielded most of them—turning inquiries about the Wellspring's limits into discussions of infrastructure priorities, redirecting personal questions about Ash into institutional language about assessment protocols and development timelines. She was a wall, but a graceful one—the kind of wall that looked like a doorway until you tried to walk through it.

Ash stood beside her and watched the room watch him.

He could feel it—the calculation. Not hostile, not even unkind, but present in the way that gravity is present. Every person in the hall was running a version of the same equation: what does this mean for me? The Council members were thinking about political leverage. The scholars were thinking about precedent and measurement. The journalists were thinking about the story—the dead weight who became a Wellspring, the impossible reversal, the angle that would make their readers lean forward. The maintenance workers along the walls were thinking about their districts, their buildings, the systems they poured their pools into every day for diminishing returns.

Everyone was thinking about what he could do. Nobody was thinking about him.

He knew this because he recognized the quality of the attention. He'd spent twenty-three years being invisible—a presence that registered only as absence, a person defined by what he couldn't contribute. Now the attention was the inverse: a presence that registered only as potential, a person defined by what he could provide. The invisibility and the visibility felt, in some strange and dislocating way, like the same thing.

A man in the second row—middle-aged, administrative half-cloak, the careful expression of someone who reported to someone who reported to the Council—raised his hand and asked about "containment protocols." Dorenne answered smoothly. The man nodded. His eyes stayed on Ash the way they might stay on a piece of infrastructure—assessing load capacity, structural integrity, useful life.

*A lever*, Ash thought. *They see a lever.*

But the thought didn't land the way it should have. Because underneath the calculation, underneath the assessment and the political maneuvering and the careful institutional language—underneath all of it, the room wanted him. Not him—what he carried. But the wanting felt the same. It pressed against his skin the way the reservoir pressed from within, and the two pressures met somewhere in the center of his chest, and the meeting felt like something he'd been waiting for his entire life.

He was wanted.

The distinction between *wanted for who you are* and *wanted for what you can do* was clear in theory and invisible in practice. It was the difference between warm water and warm water—the same temperature, the same sensation on the skin, indistinguishable until you learned to test for what was underneath.

He hadn't learned yet.

* * *

The event ended. The room didn't.

People lingered—clustering in groups of three and four, their conversations pitched at the particular volume that meant they wanted to be overheard by some people and not others. Councillor Edrath left first, her small notebook closed, her stride unhurried. Two men Ash didn't know intercepted Dorenne near the curtain—Council liaison staff, their half-cloaks bearing the seal of the administrative office. Dorenne spoke to them with the seamless authority of a woman who had anticipated this conversation before the event began.

Tessaly materialized at Ash's elbow. "The Director would like you to wait in the corridor. She'll join you shortly."

He waited. The corridor behind the stage was narrow and cool, and the stone walls held the faint residual warmth of the demonstration—his demonstration, his heat, bled outward through the building's structure the way waste energy always bled. He pressed his back against the wall and felt the warmth he'd put into it seeping back through his jacket.

Through the curtain, he could hear fragments.

"—allocation framework will need to be—"

"—the Preservationists are already drafting a position statement—"

"—unprecedented, yes, but that's precisely why institutional oversight—"

"—but can it be sustained? If the reservoir is finite—"

"—a weapon, Garren. Let's not pretend otherwise. The question is who controls—"

He stopped listening. Or tried to. The fragments came through the curtain the way light came through a crack—narrow, bright, impossible to ignore.

*A weapon.* Someone had called him a weapon. The word sat in the corridor with him, taking up space.

Dorenne came through the curtain. Her composure was intact—the public face still in place, the controlled warmth, the authority. But her stride was faster than her public stride, and she closed the curtain behind her with a gesture that was closer to pulling than draping.

"Well," she said.

"Well."

"Edrath will push for a Council-managed allocation framework within the week. She's fast. Councillor Darren will support her because he's looking for an issue to attach himself to before the next election cycle. The Accelerationist caucus will oppose any framework that looks like rationing, which means they'll temporarily align with my office's position, which is—" She caught herself. Recalibrated. "That's my problem, not yours. You did well."

"I stood still and didn't touch the podium."

"Exactly what I asked for. Most people can't manage it." She looked at him. The public face was still there, but thinner now—the warmth underneath it showing through, the way stone showed through plaster when the plaster wore thin. "How are you?"

"I'm fine."

"You're not. But you handled it. There's a difference, and the difference matters." She reached out and adjusted his collar—the same impersonal, structural gesture from that morning, but this time her hand rested a moment longer than the adjustment required. "Go home. Your family will want to see you. I'll send word about next steps."

"Dorenne."

"Yes?"

"Someone called me a weapon."

Her hand dropped. Her face didn't change—but something behind her face changed, the way a wall's internal structure shifted before the surface cracked.

"People will say a lot of things over the next few days. Most of them will be about leverage, about allocation, about political positioning. Very few of them will be about you." She held his gaze. "My job—one of my jobs—is to make sure the conversation stays productive. That means managing how people think about the Wellspring. Not what they feel about it. What they feel is their business. What they do with those feelings is mine."

"And what do you feel?"

The question surprised both of them. It came out before the performance could catch it—raw, young, the kind of question a person asked when they'd spent a week being assessed and displayed and measured and wanted to know if any of it mattered to anyone as a person rather than a resource.

Dorenne was quiet for a moment. Then she said: "I feel that I hired a very smart young man who has been handed something neither of us expected, and that my responsibility is to make sure the city benefits and he isn't consumed. Those two things are not in conflict." A pause. "Yet."

She left. Her footsteps echoed in the corridor—measured, purposeful, the particular rhythm of a woman who had three meetings to attend and had allocated exactly as much time for honesty as the morning could afford.

Ash stood in the corridor. The wall behind him was still warm.

*Yet.*

* * *

His parents' temporary housing was on the Third Terrace—higher than they'd ever lived, closer to the elder districts than his mother had ever been comfortable with. Dorenne had arranged it. Two rooms and a kitchen in a building maintained by a channeling crew that actually showed up on schedule, with heating lattices that worked and a transit lift that ran. His mother had said nothing about the transit lift. She'd said a great deal about the fact that the kitchen had no proper stove—only a channeling interface, sleek and efficient and useless to a woman who'd spent decades cooking on iron.

Ash took the stairs.

He heard them before he reached the landing. Not his parents—the neighbors. Two women in the corridor outside the apartment, their voices pitched in the confidential register of people sharing something too interesting to keep and too uncertain to claim.

"—the Torren boy. The one on the fifth floor—"

"The dead weight?"

"Not anymore, apparently. Garret said the whole building shook when it happened. Said the ceiling came down—"

They saw him. The conversation stopped with the abruptness of a snuffed candle. One of the women—older, grey-haired, her hands wrapped around a cup that steamed faintly with channeled warmth—looked at him with an expression that moved through three distinct phases: recognition, recalculation, and something that settled into a careful, uncertain smile.

"Ashlyn," she said. "We were just—your mother mentioned—"

"It's all right," he said. The old performance, smooth and easy, the smile that made people comfortable. "I'm sure the whole terrace knows by now."

The women looked at each other. The grey-haired one's smile solidified into something warmer—the warmth of a person who'd decided which version of this story she preferred. "Your parents must be so proud."

"They are," he said. And because the performance demanded it, and because it was also true: "I'm lucky."

He let himself into the apartment. The door closed behind him and the corridor sounds cut off and the kitchen smelled like his mother's cooking—bread, something with onions, the familiar warmth of food prepared with more intention than the occasion warranted, which was his mother's way of saying everything she couldn't say aloud.

She was at the table. Her splinted leg was propped on a cushion on a second chair, the arrangement both practical and precarious—she'd clearly been sitting here for hours, her position optimized around the injury the way she optimized everything around the constraints that life presented her. On the table in front of her: bread dough under a cloth, a cutting board with half-chopped onions, a cup of tea gone cold.

And a broadsheet.

He saw it before she could move it—the morning edition, folded to the inside page where the headline read, in the sober typeface of institutional journalism: UNPRECEDENTED CHANNELING EVENT AT HALL OF INFRASTRUCTURE. Below it, smaller: *Director Kharren announces discovery of "Wellspring" phenomenon. City Council briefing expected.*

His mother's hand was on the broadsheet. Not reading it—covering it. The gesture of a woman who'd been reading the same paragraph for an hour and didn't want anyone to see the evidence.

"You're home," she said.

"I'm home."

"Come sit down. Have you eaten? You haven't eaten. Sit."

He sat. She pushed a plate toward him—bread, cheese, pickled vegetables from a jar she'd brought from the old apartment. The jar was one of the things his father had carried out of the rubble. The pickles were his mother's recipe, made last autumn, the vinegar sharpened with a spice she bought from the same vendor every year.

He ate. She watched him eat with the particular intensity of a mother who had been watching her son eat for twenty-three years and for whom the watching was itself a form of sustenance—proof that he was here, that he was fed, that the basic architecture of care was still hers to provide.

"How was it?" she asked.

"It was fine. Dorenne handled most of the talking. I just stood there."

"You didn't just stand there. You—" She stopped. Her hand tightened on the broadsheet. "The paper says you demonstrated. In front of the Council. They watched you channel."

"It was a small demonstration. A calibration stone. I just—"

"In front of everyone." Her voice had the careful, pressed quality of a woman controlling something larger than the conversation. "They put you on a stage, Ashlyn. They put you on a stage and they watched."

The full name. Tender and worried and furious, all compressed into three syllables.

"Mum—"

"I'm not—" She pressed her lips together. Her hands were in her lap now, one wrapped around the other, the splinted leg a weight that kept her in the chair when every line of her body said she wanted to stand. "I'm not saying it was wrong. I'm saying—" She looked at the broadsheet again. "These people. The Council, the scholars, all of them. They didn't know your name a week ago. They didn't know you existed. Now they want—"

She didn't finish. She didn't need to.

Ash looked at his mother. She was smaller than he remembered—not physically, she'd always been small, but the space she occupied in the room had contracted. In the old apartment, she'd been the center of gravity—the woman who channeled the stove and maintained the heating and managed the household and kept her son's world bearable through a thousand daily acts of fierce, directed love. Here, in temporary housing with her leg in a splint and her son on the front page of a broadsheet, she was still fierce. But the fierceness had lost its target.

She couldn't protect him from this. The danger wasn't a cold morning or an inaccessible transit lift or a world that measured worth in channeling. The danger was a roomful of powerful people who wanted what he carried, and she had no weapon against that—no channeling, no institutional power, no clever framing. Just her hands in her lap and the old kettle on the counter—the battered copper one from the old apartment, the one she'd asked his father to carry out of the rubble before anything else, the one she used every morning even though the temporary housing had a perfectly functional channeling interface for heating water.

"They're going to be careful with you," she said. The way she said *they're* carried the weight of a woman who didn't believe it but needed to say it aloud—a prayer disguised as a prediction. "Dorenne is—she'll be careful."

"She will."

"Promise me you'll be careful too."

"I promise."

She looked at him. Whatever she saw—whatever version of her son was visible in this moment, sitting at the table eating her pickles with his jacket riding up at the collar—it wasn't the one she knew how to reach. Her hand came across the table and took his. She held it the way she'd always held it—tight, too tight, the grip of a woman who'd spent twenty-three years holding on to a son the world didn't want and couldn't figure out how to let go now that the world had changed its mind.

Her palm was warm. His was warmer.

She noticed. He saw her notice—the flicker in her eyes, the quick glance at their joined hands, the adjustment in her expression as she processed the fact that her son's skin carried a heat she hadn't put there. For twenty-three years, she'd warmed his hands. His food, his bath water, his room, his bed—every surface of his daily life touched by her channeling, her care made physical through magic.

He didn't need that anymore.

She let go of his hand. She reached for the cold cup of tea, pressed her palm against it, channeled warmth into it. A gesture so automatic it was almost invisible—the foundational kindness, the thing so basic it wasn't even called channeling. Just living. Just being his mother.

The front door opened.

* * *

His father came in carrying the weight of the day on his shoulders—not visibly, not dramatically, but in the particular way he closed the door and hung his coat and stood in the kitchen doorway for a moment before speaking.

"The Verath family sends congratulations," he said. "Also the Karens on the second floor. Also the woman from the grocer's, whose name I cannot remember, who stopped me on the street to ask if it was true."

"And?" his mother asked.

"And I told them yes, it's true, and he's fine, and we appreciate the well-wishes, and no, he is not available to fix their heating lattice." His father crossed to the counter and picked up the kettle—the old one, the battered one—and weighed it in his hand. Not for water. Just to hold it. "There were four people waiting outside the building when I left this morning. By the time I came back, there were eleven."

Ash's chest tightened.

"What did they want?"

"Different things. Some wanted to see you. Some wanted to ask about you. One man—I didn't recognize him, he wasn't from the terrace—wanted to know if you'd be 'available for private consultation.'" His father set the kettle down. His hands rested on the counter the way they rested on everything—broad, solid, the hands of a man who'd spent his life holding things together through physical effort. "I told him you were not available and he should direct inquiries to Director Kharren's office."

His father looked at him. The look held everything it always held—love, pride, worry, the particular weight of a man who'd watched his son navigate twenty-three years of exclusion and was now watching him navigate something equally unfamiliar and equally dangerous, and could help with neither.

"You did well today," his father said. "Your mother showed me the broadsheet."

"I didn't do much."

"You stood up in front of powerful people and let them look at you. That's not nothing." His father pulled out a chair and sat. His movements were deliberate, careful—the unhurried quality of a man who'd decided that what this evening needed was steadiness, the counterweight to everything outside the door. "People will want things from you, Ash. That's new. That's—" He searched for the word. "That's something to be careful about."

"I know."

"You don't, yet. But you will." His father's hand found his shoulder. The grip was firm and warm and lasted longer than a pat and shorter than an embrace—the exact duration of a man who expressed everything through pressure and duration and the specific angle of his palm against his son's body. "Your mother is worried."

"I know."

"So am I. But different." He squeezed once and let go. "She's worried they'll use you. I'm worried you'll let them, because it'll feel like being useful."

The words landed in the kitchen and stayed there, heavy as stone.

* * *

Maren arrived after dark.

She came through the door the way she came through every door—efficiently, purposefully, her bag slung across one shoulder and her coat already half-removed as she stepped across the threshold. She kissed their mother on the cheek, squeezed their father's arm, set her bag on the floor by the wall.

"Well," she said. "It's been a day."

She looked tired. Not dramatically—Maren never looked dramatically anything—but the particular tiredness of a person who'd spent the day being composed and was only now allowing the effort to show. Her hair was pulled back in the severe style she wore for work. Her hands, when she took off her gloves, were slightly red at the knuckles—the telltale mark of someone who'd been channeling at the edge of her capacity, squeezing efficiency out of every kilojoule.

"Colleagues?" Ash asked.

"Everyone." She sat across from him. "Lenn from the survey team asked if it was really true. So did Pareth. So did the woman in records whose name I should know by now and don't. I had six conversations that started with 'Your brother?' and ended with 'Really?' and in between I did my actual job." She picked up a piece of bread from the plate and tore it, methodically, into pieces she didn't eat. "The survey office sent a formal request to Dorenne's office asking about 'potential collaboration opportunities.'"

"Already?"

"They're not the only ones. Councillor Darren's aide came to our floor this afternoon—ostensibly about a maintenance schedule, but she asked three questions about you before she mentioned the schedule." Maren's jaw tightened—a small motion, controlled, the kind of tension that only surfaced when her discipline was holding something larger than usual. "I told her I was working and my brother's channeling capacity was not part of my portfolio."

The word *portfolio* carried the full weight of Maren's composure—professional, precise, a wall built from vocabulary.

"I'm sorry," Ash said.

"Don't be. It's not—" She stopped tearing the bread. "It's not your fault. It's just new. I'm used to being the Torren who channels." A ghost of a smile. "Now I'm the Torren who's related to the Torren who channels."

She said this lightly. The lightness cost her something—he could see it in the way her fingers pressed against the table's edge, the way her eyes moved to their mother and then away, the discipline applied to the task of appearing undisturbed.

Their mother had been watching this exchange with the stillness of a woman who could hear everything her children weren't saying. She reached across the table and covered Maren's hand with her own.

"Both of you," she said. "I need both of you to be careful."

Maren turned her hand over and squeezed their mother's fingers. The gesture was quick, controlled, and achingly gentle—the kind of touch that Maren rationed the way she rationed everything, dispensing tenderness in precise amounts that never revealed the depth of the supply.

"We'll be fine, Mum."

They sat together in the temporary kitchen with the old kettle on the counter and the broadsheet folded on the table and the sounds of strangers' footsteps in the corridor outside. Ash's father heated water on the channeling interface—slowly, carefully, the way he did everything—and made tea for four. They drank it. The tea was adequate. The mugs were borrowed. Nothing in this kitchen belonged to them except the jar of pickles and the old kettle and each other.

But the warmth in the room was real, and it came from more places than the heating lattice in the wall.

* * *

Ryn came the next morning.

Ash heard the knock and knew it was her before he opened the door—the particular rhythm of it, three quick taps followed by a pause, the knock of a person who'd been knocking on his door since they were both young enough to need permission to go outside.

She stood in the corridor in her work clothes—heavy trousers, a jacket with stains at the cuffs, her hair pulled back with a cord that was fraying at the knot. She looked the way she always looked: practical, direct, slightly windblown. As if the world were a thing she moved through rather than a thing that happened to her.

"Hi," she said.

"Hi."

She looked at him. The looking was careful but not calculating—not the assessment he'd grown used to over the past week, the evaluation that weighed his potential and measured his usefulness. She was looking at *him*. At the shadows under his eyes and the tension in his jaw and the way he stood in the doorway with one hand on the frame, not blocking her entry but not stepping aside either, caught in the strange hesitation of a person who wanted to be seen and was afraid of what would be visible.

"Can I come in?"

"Yeah. Sorry. Yes." He stepped back. She came in, and the small apartment was suddenly different—changed by her presence, by the smell of cold air and workshop oil she carried with her, by the particular way she occupied space that was neither tentative nor imposing. She moved through the kitchen the way she moved through every room: as if she'd already decided it was fine and didn't need to check.

She sat at the table. He sat across from her.

"I heard," she said.

"Everyone's heard."

"I heard last week. Lenn's sister works in the survey office—she told Lenn, Lenn told me." She paused. "I wanted to come sooner. I wasn't sure if you'd—" She stopped. Redirected. "I'm glad, Ash."

She said it simply. Two words. No performance, no qualifier, no elaborate expression of wonder or congratulation or the careful enthusiasm that everyone else seemed to bring to the subject of his transformation. Just: I'm glad.

The simplicity of it cracked something in his chest.

"Thank you."

"Have you been sleeping?"

"Some."

"Have you eaten?"

"My mother hasn't let me stop eating since I got back."

"Good." She glanced toward the counter, where the old kettle sat beside the borrowed mugs. "Have you talked to Haran?"

The question landed differently than the others. Sleeping and eating were immediate, physical, the concerns of a friend checking on a body. Haran was something else—a connection to the life before, the workshop and the mechanisms and the person Ash had been when his hands made things instead of breaking calibration stones.

"Not yet."

"You should."

"I know."

She didn't push. That was the thing about Ryn—she said what she thought and then left it there, the way you set a tool on a bench within reach. She didn't press it into your hand. She didn't explain why you needed it. She just made sure you knew where it was.

"So," she said. "A Wellspring."

"A Wellspring."

"What does it feel like?"

He thought about this. Not the practiced answer—not the one he'd given the examiners or Dorenne or the room full of Council members. The real one. The one he hadn't told anyone because no one had asked the question the way she'd asked it—not *what can you do* but *what does it feel like*.

"Like pressure," he said. "Behind my ribs. Like—" He searched for the metaphor and found only the literal. "Like being full. The way your stomach feels after a meal, except it's everywhere. In my hands, in my chest, in the back of my teeth. A warmth that doesn't stop."

"Does it hurt?"

"No. It's—" He stopped. "It's the opposite of what I had before. Before was empty. Cold water, cold stone, cold hands. This is..." He held up his palm. The skin was warm—faintly, constantly, the background radiation of the reservoir. "Full."

Ryn looked at his hand. She didn't reach for it. She looked at it the way she looked at mechanisms in the workshop—with attention, with interest, without reverence.

"You're being careful?"

"Dorenne's making sure of it."

"That's not what I asked."

He met her eyes. They were brown, steady, unimpressed—not with him, but with the entire apparatus of importance that had assembled around him in the past week. The Council briefings, the political calculations, the people who saw a lever. Ryn didn't see a lever. She saw her friend, who'd had something enormous happen to him, and she was checking if he was okay the way she'd check if he'd recovered from a fever.

It was exactly what he wanted. And somewhere beneath the wanting—small, irrational, buried deep enough that he almost didn't notice it—it wasn't enough.

Because she treated the Wellspring as something that had happened *to* him. Not as what he *was*. Not as the thing that had finally, after twenty-three years, made him extraordinary. She treated it the way she'd treat an injury or a windfall or a change in the weather—a circumstance, not a definition. And he appreciated this with his whole mind and resented it with something smaller and darker that lived underneath his mind, in the place where the old emptiness used to be.

He wanted her to be awed. He didn't want her to be awed. He wanted her to see him differently. He wanted her to be the one person who never did.

"I'm being careful," he said.

She nodded. "Good." She looked around the kitchen—the borrowed mugs, the temporary walls, the unfamiliar geometry of a space that wasn't theirs. "Your mum's leg?"

"Healing. The bone was clean. Dorenne sent an elder healer—someone good."

"And your dad?"

"Holding everything together. The way he does."

"And Maren?"

"Fielding questions. The way she does."

Ryn's mouth quirked—not quite a smile, but the precursor to one, the acknowledgment that she knew this family and its patterns and could hear the shorthand without needing the translation.

"And you?"

"I'm fine, Ryn."

"You keep saying that."

"Because I keep being asked."

She looked at him. The looking had changed—deeper now, more focused, the attention of someone who'd known him long enough to hear what he said and what he meant and the gap between them.

"You're not fine," she said. "But you're handling it. There's a difference."

Dorenne had said the same thing. The echo landed strangely—two women who saw through him in different ways, for different reasons, arriving at the same observation from opposite directions. Dorenne saw through the performance because she'd built performances of her own. Ryn saw through it because she'd watched him build this one, year by year, and she knew where every seam was.

"Come to the workshop," she said. "When things settle. Haran asks about you."

"I will."

"Don't say it if you don't mean it."

"I mean it."

She held his gaze for another moment. Then she stood—the movement easy, unhurried, the way she stood up from Haran's workbench at the end of an evening, brushing sawdust from her knees. She crossed to the door. Stopped.

"Ash."

"Yeah?"

"I'm glad." She said it again. The same two words, the same simplicity, but this time the emphasis was different—not *I'm glad this happened* but *I'm glad you're here*. The distinction was small enough to miss and large enough to hold everything.

She left. The door closed. The corridor sounds returned—footsteps, voices, the ambient noise of a building that housed strangers. Ash sat at the table with the old kettle on the counter and his hands in his lap and the warmth of the Wellspring pressing outward through his skin.

She was glad. She wasn't awed.

He sat with both of those things and tried to understand why they didn't feel the same, and couldn't.

* * *

That evening, alone in the temporary apartment while his parents slept and the city settled into its nightly cooling, Ash stood at the window and looked out at Solathis.

The view was different from the Third Terrace. Higher, broader—the city's terraces descending in the darkness like steps carved by a god with poor planning and unlimited power. The elder districts above still glowed faintly, their stone holding the day's channeling the way good stone held heat. Below, the middle terraces dimmed, and below those, the younger districts had already gone dark. The Splits, where Haran's workshop sat, was invisible from here—below the line where the maintained city ended and the city-by-hand began.

Somewhere down there, people were talking about him.

He could feel it the way he'd always felt the city's rhythms—not through channeling, but through the accumulated knowledge of a person who'd spent twenty-three years watching a world he couldn't participate in. The information was moving outward from the Hall of Infrastructure the way it always moved: through staff to families, families to neighbors, neighbors to district gossip, district gossip to the broadsheets that would print tomorrow's edition with more detail and less accuracy.

By morning, everyone would know. The dead weight who became a Wellspring. The Director of Infrastructure's aide who'd activated under a collapsing ceiling. The boy from the Fifth Terrace who could shatter calibration stones with a touch. The story would have a shape by then—the shape the tellers gave it, which was never the shape of the thing itself.

He pressed his palm against the windowpane. The glass was cold. The cold lasted for a second—maybe two—before the heat from his skin warmed it. A small circle of warmth spreading outward from his hand, fogging the glass with condensation, blurring the city beyond.

A week ago, the glass would have stayed cold. His hand would have pressed against it and taken the chill into his bones and that would have been the transaction—the world was cold and he was permeable to it. Now the exchange ran the other way. He was the warmth. The glass, the stone, the room, the building, the city—everything around him was the thing being warmed.

The reservoir pulsed behind his ribs. Full. Patient. Vast enough that a week of testing and demonstrations and one public display hadn't changed its depth by any amount he could perceive. Vasra's words were there—*fixed reservoir*, *working assumption*, *aquifers draw from rainfall*—but they were small, distant, buoys on an ocean too large to see the edges of.

Down in the city, a lever. Up here, in the quiet of a borrowed apartment, a person who was still learning to tell the difference between being wanted and being valued, and who wasn't sure, on this particular night, that the difference mattered.

He pulled his hand from the glass. The warm circle began to fade.

He went to bed. The reservoir hummed against his ribs in the darkness—steady, patient, waiting for whatever he would ask of it next. The city settled around him, the stone of the terraces releasing the day's heat in a slow, generational exhale. And somewhere in the space between sleeping and waking, in the territory where the performance dropped and the scaffolding went quiet and the person underneath was briefly, honestly visible—he felt it. The gravity.

Not the pull of the earth. The pull of the wanting. The city, the Council, the factions, the people clustering outside the building and whispering in corridors and drafting position statements and calculating leverage. All of it pulling toward the center of him, toward the thing he carried, toward the Wellspring that had cracked open inside a dead weight and turned him into the most wanted person in Solathis.

The pull felt like warmth. The pull felt like being held.

He didn't know, yet, that they weren't the same thing.
