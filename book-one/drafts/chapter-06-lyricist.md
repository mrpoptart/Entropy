# Chapter 6

The jacket didn't fit.

Ash turned in front of the mirror in Dorenne's anteroom, pulling at the collar where it pressed against the side of his neck. The fabric was fine—dark grey, close-woven, the kind of material that held its shape without channeling, which meant it was expensive. Dorenne's aide had brought it twenty minutes ago, folded inside paper, with a note that said only: *Wear this.*

"Stop touching the collar," Dorenne said from the doorway.

She was already dressed for it. Whatever *it* was—she hadn't used the word *event* yet, or *presentation*, or any of the other words that would have told him what he was walking into. She'd said *appearance*. "A brief appearance before some interested parties." She wore a coat he hadn't seen before, cut close at the waist, deep blue with copper clasps. Her hair was pulled back tighter than usual. She looked like she'd been built for a room he hadn't entered yet.

"Who are the interested parties?" he asked.

"People who need to understand what the Wellspring means for the city." She crossed the room and adjusted his collar herself—two fingers, a precise tug. The fabric settled. "Better. Stop fidgeting."

"I'm not fidgeting."

"You're fidgeting with admirable subtlety. It's still fidgeting." She stepped back and studied him the way she studied deployment maps—with the focused, organized attention of a mind that was already three moves ahead. "You look presentable."

"That's... not the word I was hoping for."

"Presentable is exactly the word I was hoping for. Remarkable is my job. You just need to stand there and not break anything." Something crossed her face—not quite a smile, but the terrain of one. "I mean that literally. Don't channel unless I ask you to, and when I ask you to, do exactly what we practiced. The heating lattice. Nothing more."

"The heating lattice."

"A controlled demonstration of scale. Not force. Not spectacle. Scale." She held up a finger. "The audience will want spectacle. They'll want the weight in the ceiling, the cracked stone, the theatrical displays. That's exactly what we're not giving them. We're giving them a man who can warm a room—and the room will be large enough that warming it is, in itself, extraordinary."

He understood. Or thought he understood. The lattice they'd practiced on was in the Hall's main assembly chamber—a cavernous space on the second floor, built for public audiences during the High Age, with heating channels woven through its stone walls in a lattice so vast that fully activating it required a team of six elder-generation channelers working in rotation. He'd done it alone in four seconds. The walls had glowed. The room had gone from cold stone to summer in a single breath.

"What do I say?"

"As little as possible." She said this without apology, the way she said everything—with the certainty of a person whose convictions had been earning interest for decades. "I'll introduce the Wellspring. I'll frame the context. If someone directs a question to you, answer simply. 'Yes.' 'Thank you.' If they ask about the reservoir's capacity, you say: 'We're still assessing.' If they ask about control—"

"I say I'm working with your team."

"You're developing your abilities under the guidance of the Director's office."

"Is that different from what I said?"

"Yours sounds like a person learning to use his hands. Mine sounds like a program. Programs attract funding." She paused. "And support. Which we need."

The *we* landed somewhere in the center of his chest. It felt warm. Not the reservoir's warmth—something else. Smaller, more fragile, harder to locate.

"Ash."

"Yes."

"You'll be fine." She said it with the same precision she applied to everything, but the precision had a layer underneath it—something that might have been genuine reassurance, if genuine reassurance were a language she spoke fluently. "You're not on trial. You're not defending yourself. You're standing beside me while I explain something wonderful to people who need to hear it."

*Something wonderful.* He turned the phrase over. Coming from Dorenne, it had the weight of a policy declaration.

"I should tell you something," she said.

He waited.

"The information has already spread. The scholars who tested you—Vasra's team, the examiners—they've been discreet within the bounds of their professional networks, which means the information reached every relevant person in the city within forty-eight hours." She straightened a sleeve he hadn't realized was crooked. "The Council has been receiving informal briefings from at least three different sources, none of them authorized. Two Council members contacted my office this morning. A third contacted Vasra directly."

"So this isn't an introduction."

"This is me deciding whether the story is told by the people who were in the room, or by the people who heard it from someone who heard it from someone who was in the room." Her eyes were steady on his. "I'd rather it be told correctly. Wouldn't you?"

He would. Of course he would. The logic was seamless—it was always seamless with Dorenne, each piece fitting into the next with the precision of mechanical engineering. She wasn't lying. She wasn't even shading the truth. The information was out. The story would be told. The only question was who held the pen.

And she was good at holding pens.

"Okay," he said.

"Good." She moved toward the door, then stopped. "One more thing. When the demonstration is over—when the room is warm and the audience is processing what they've seen—I need you to stand very still. Don't smile. Don't wave. Don't look at anyone in particular. Let them look at you."

"Why?"

"Because the people in that room are going to spend the next six hours deciding what you mean. Some of them will decide you're a gift. Some will decide you're a weapon. Some will decide you're a problem. How you behave in the first thirty seconds after the demonstration will influence which category gets the most signatures." She opened the door. "You're a person, Ash. But right now, you need to be a very still person."

The corridor outside was quiet. Late morning light through the Hall's tall windows, falling in long rectangles across the stone floor. The familiar rhythm of the building—footsteps two floors up, the hum of channeling interfaces in the walls, the distant murmur of staff. The same building where he'd carried briefs and deployment schedules. The same ramp he'd climbed forty flights every morning because he couldn't use the lift.

He followed Dorenne down the corridor. The jacket that didn't fit felt, with each step, more like something he was wearing for someone else's reasons.

* * *

The assembly chamber was already full.

Ash saw it from the side entrance—Dorenne's entrance, a narrow door behind the raised platform that the public never used and most staff didn't know existed. The chamber opened before him like a held breath: stone tiers descending in a gentle arc toward the central floor, the walls rising forty feet to a vaulted ceiling where channeling strips cast even, institutional light across everything. The heating lattice channels were visible in the walls—dark lines threading through pale stone, dormant, cold, the vast network that Dorenne had chosen as his stage.

The seats were full. Not packed—the chamber could hold three hundred, and there were perhaps a hundred and fifty people arranged across the tiers—but full in the way that mattered. Full of the kind of faces that made decisions. Council members in their formal coats, seated in the front row with the studied casualness of people who wanted you to know they'd been invited first. Behind them, senior administrators—faces Ash recognized from the corridors of the Hall, people who'd nodded at him when he carried briefs and who now didn't know he existed in any capacity except this one. Further back, scholars—he spotted two of the examiners from the basement room, the thin-faced man and the younger one, sitting together with the particular rigidity of people who had been told their presence was expected and were uncertain whether that was an honor or a warning.

No press. Or rather—no formal press. But Ash could see three people with bound notebooks and ink-stained fingers seated near the back, and a fourth sketching something on a loose page. The city's information networks didn't need formal credentials. They needed proximity and good handwriting.

Dorenne touched his arm. A light pressure—two fingers, the same gesture she'd used to fix his collar.

"Wait here. I'll signal when it's time."

She stepped through the side door and onto the platform, and the room shifted.

Not loudly. The murmur didn't stop—it changed register. Chairs creaked as people straightened. The sketcher in the back row looked up. The Council members in the front row produced the particular expression that meant they were paying attention while appearing not to.

Dorenne stood at the platform's center. She didn't wait for silence. She let it come to her—standing with her hands at her sides, her posture open, her gaze moving across the room with the unhurried certainty of a woman who had addressed this chamber a hundred times and would address it a hundred more. The silence settled around her like water finding its level.

"Thank you for coming on short notice," she said. Her voice carried without effort—the chamber's acoustics, designed in the High Age for speakers whose channeling could amplify their words, still worked beautifully for someone who simply knew how to use them. "I'll be brief. The situation is urgent but not, I want to be clear, a crisis. What I'm about to share with you is an opportunity. Possibly the most significant one this city has seen in a generation."

She paused. Let the word *generation* sit in the room. In a city defined by generational decline, the word did its own work.

"Six days ago, during a structural emergency in the Fifth Terrace, a member of my staff experienced a channeling activation. This is not, in itself, unusual. What is unusual—what is unprecedented, in fact—is the scale of the activation. The individual in question did not develop a standard trickle. They manifested a deep reservoir of a magnitude that exceeds every measurement on record."

The room was very still now. The murmur had died completely. In the front row, a Council member—a tall woman with cropped silver hair, elder generation, her posture the kind of straight that came from decades of expecting other people to stand straighter—leaned forward half an inch.

"The scholar Vasra, whom many of you know, has conducted a preliminary assessment. Her term for the phenomenon is a *Wellspring*—a deep-source reservoir, finite but vast. The exact capacity is still being measured. The preliminary readings exceed our instruments' range."

She said this the way she said everything—calmly, precisely, with just enough technical language to establish credibility and just enough plain language to ensure the Council members who valued directness over expertise didn't feel condescended to. She was brilliant at this. Ash, watching from the side door, could see the architecture of it—each sentence placed like a stone in a wall, each pause calculated to let the previous statement settle before adding the next load.

"The individual is a member of my staff. He has been working in my office for several weeks—a capable, intelligent young man who, until six days ago, had no channeling ability whatsoever."

A ripple. Not a sound—a shift. The scholars exchanged glances. The thin-faced examiner's hand tightened on his knee. The Council members' expressions performed varying degrees of controlled surprise, which meant they'd all heard some version of this already and were performing the surprise they hadn't felt when they'd actually found out.

"His name is Ashlyn Torren. He's twenty-three years old. And with your permission, I'd like to bring him out."

The signal. Ash felt it before he saw it—Dorenne's head turning a fraction toward the side door, her left hand opening at her side. A gesture meant only for him.

He stepped through the door.

The platform was three steps up from the chamber floor. Stone, worn smooth, the same stone that ran throughout the Hall. He climbed the steps and the room turned toward him—a hundred and fifty faces, each one calculating something different, each one seeing something different when they looked at a young man in a jacket that didn't quite fit, standing on a platform he'd never stood on, in a building where he'd been carrying files a week ago.

He stood beside Dorenne. Not behind her—beside her. She'd been specific about that. "Not in front, not behind. Beside. We're partners in this."

The word *partners* had sounded different in her office. Here, with the chamber's weight pressing in from every direction, it sounded like a frame around a painting—the thing that told you where to look.

"Ash," Dorenne said, turning to him with an expression he recognized. The one she used when she wanted him to feel seen. The one that had a purpose layered underneath the warmth, always, though the warmth was real too. "Could you demonstrate what we discussed?"

He nodded.

He turned to face the wall.

The heating lattice spread across the chamber's eastern face—a network of channeling channels carved into the stone during the High Age, when six elder-generation channelers had activated it as part of their morning routine. The channels were dark. Cold. They hadn't been fully active in years. The maintenance budget couldn't justify the expenditure for a room used a few times a month, and the partial activations the current staff managed—two or three channels at a time, enough to take the worst of the chill off—left most of the lattice dormant.

Ash placed his palm against the stone.

The reservoir shifted behind his ribs. The ocean—patient, vast, the pressure he'd carried for six days now like a second heartbeat. He found the lattice channels with his palm the way he found a crack in a wall—by feel, by the texture of the stone, by the way the mineral changed where the channels ran. He pushed.

Not hard. Not the way he'd pushed against the calibration stones in the basement, the brute force that had cracked and shattered and embedded weights in the ceiling. This was what they'd practiced—a sustained, even flow, directed into the lattice channels like water into a pipe. He couldn't do it precisely. His control was still crude, his efficiency terrible—he could feel the waste heat bleeding from his palm, warming the stone around the channels as much as inside them, the energy dissipating into the wall like a shout in a canyon. But the channels were wide, and the lattice was deep, and the reservoir behind his ribs didn't care about waste. There was enough. There was more than enough. There was an ocean behind a straw, and even the fraction that reached the target was more than six elder channelers could produce in a morning.

The lattice activated.

It started at his palm and spread outward—the dark channels brightening to a deep amber, the color moving through the wall like dawn breaking across a hillside. Line by line, channel by channel, the lattice woke. The amber deepened as the energy flowed through stone that hadn't been warm in years, filling channels that had been cold so long the mineral had contracted around them. The room began to change. Not gradually—Ash could feel it, the air shifting, the cold stone ceiling beginning to radiate, the hundred and fifty bodies in the chamber leaning slightly forward as the warmth reached them.

The lattice covered the eastern wall. Then turned the corner. Then spread across the northern face, the channels connecting and branching and carrying the energy through the room's skeleton like blood through veins. The stone glowed. The air thickened with warmth—not oppressive, not the violent heat of his early experiments, but a sustained, building warmth that moved through the chamber the way sunlight moved through a window. Slow. Steady. Enormous.

He held it.

The room was silent.

He held it for ten seconds. Twenty. The lattice was fully active now—every channel, every branch, every capillary line carved into the chamber's bones by builders who had assumed this level of power would always be available. The stone sang with it. A low harmonic, felt more than heard, the resonance of mineral carrying more energy than it had in a decade.

He pulled his hand back.

The lattice dimmed—slowly, the channels releasing their stored heat in a long, gradual exhale. But the room was warm. Deeply, thoroughly warm, the way it hadn't been since the elder teams had maintained it at full capacity. The cold stone that had pressed in from every direction was gone, replaced by an ambient warmth that settled into clothes and skin and the spaces between people.

Ash turned back to face the room.

The silence held for two more heartbeats. Then it broke—not into applause, not into murmur, but into something between them. A sound like a room full of people exhaling at once. The scholars traded looks that moved too fast to read. The thin-faced examiner had his pen out and was writing something on his sleeve—his notebook was on his lap, too far away, and he was writing on the fabric of his jacket with the frantic urgency of a man recording something he couldn't afford to forget.

The Council member with the silver hair—the one who'd leaned forward when Dorenne mentioned *unprecedented*—was looking at Ash with an expression that had moved past surprise into something colder and more organized. Assessment. Valuation. The face of a woman calculating what a heated chamber meant when multiplied across a city.

Ash stood very still. He didn't smile. He didn't wave. He didn't look at anyone in particular.

He let them look at him.

Dorenne stepped forward. "Thank you, Ash." She turned to the room. "What you just witnessed was a single individual activating a heating lattice designed for a six-person elder team. In approximately four seconds. The energy expenditure, based on our preliminary measurements, represents a negligible fraction of his total reservoir."

She let that land. *Negligible fraction.* The room absorbed it the way the lattice had absorbed the channeling—slowly, deeply, with the low resonance of something fundamental shifting.

"The implications for the city's infrastructure are significant. I don't need to tell anyone in this room that our maintenance capacity has been declining for years. The Wellspring represents a resource that could—" She chose her word with visible care. "—address the most critical failures in our system. Under appropriate guidance. With proper planning."

*Under appropriate guidance.* Ash heard the frame within the frame. Not just *the Wellspring is a resource.* Not just *the city can benefit.* But: *this resource is managed by my office.* She was positioning herself as the guardian, the gatekeeper, the person through whom the Wellspring's power would flow. And she was doing it in the same breath as the genuine pitch for the city's benefit, so seamlessly that the two were indistinguishable.

Because they were indistinguishable. To Dorenne, they were the same thing.

"I'll take questions," she said.

The questions came. Not from the Council members—they sat, processing, their questions saved for the formal briefings they would demand by end of day. The questions came from the administrators, the scholars, the people in the middle tiers who were close enough to the work to know what they were looking at.

"How is the reservoir replenished?"

Dorenne: "That's still being studied. The current assessment suggests a fixed but extremely large reservoir."

"What's the efficiency rate? The waste heat was noticeable."

Dorenne: "Ash is developing his control. He's had channeling ability for less than a week. Efficiency will improve with training and practice."

She said *Ash is developing* and *efficiency will improve* as though these were institutional certainties, outcomes already plotted on a chart. The questions continued. Dorenne fielded them—sometimes answering directly, sometimes redirecting to Vasra's ongoing research, always maintaining the frame: *this is managed, this is understood, this is in good hands.* Her hands.

One question reached Ash directly. A man in the middle tier—an administrator, by the look of his coat, someone who managed budgets and understood what the heating lattice demonstration meant in terms of cost-per-kilojoule.

"Mr. Torren. How does it feel?"

The room shifted its attention. Ash felt the weight of it—a hundred and fifty gazes, each one pressing against him from a different angle, each one wanting a different answer.

*How does it feel?*

He thought about the basement room. The calibration stones shattering under his touch. The too-small chair. The ocean behind his ribs. The three days of keeping his hands in his pockets because everything he touched broke or blazed. The morning his father had caught his arm at the door and said *be careful* and held on too long.

"It feels like a lot of responsibility," he said.

The room made a sound—a collective, barely audible hum of approval. The right answer. The humble answer. The answer that a hundred and fifty people could take home and use as evidence that the Wellspring was manageable, that the young man attached to it was serious and grounded and not a threat.

Dorenne's hand touched his arm again. The two-finger signal.

He stepped back.

She continued speaking. He stood beside her and watched the room watch him and felt, underneath the jacket that didn't fit, the reservoir pulsing against his ribs with the slow patience of something that had been waiting twenty-three years and could wait a little longer.

* * *

The aftermath had a rhythm Ash couldn't keep up with.

Dorenne's staff descended on the chamber the moment the event ended—three aides he'd seen in the corridors but never spoken to, moving with the coordinated precision of people who had done this before. They intercepted the Council members before they could reach the platform. They directed the scholars toward a side room where Vasra was waiting with prepared materials. They steered the administrators toward the anteroom with tea and talking points.

Ash was moved too—not roughly, not unkindly, but with the efficient certainty of a current. One of the aides touched his elbow. "This way, Mr. Torren." He was guided through the side door, down a corridor he didn't recognize, into a small room with a window overlooking the Second Terrace. The door closed behind him. The aide stayed outside.

He stood at the window. The Second Terrace spread below—the administrative quarter, the broad avenue that connected the Hall of Infrastructure to the Council chambers, the morning foot traffic of a city that didn't know yet. Or did know, partly, through the channels that Dorenne had described. The scholars' professional networks. The political whisper-chains. The information reaching outward from this building in concentric rings, each ring less accurate than the last, until the Wellspring became a rumor and the rumor became a story and the story became whatever the city needed it to be.

He pressed his palm against the window glass. Cool. The lattice channels in the walls here were dormant too—the Hall's heating ran on a rotating schedule, and this corridor wasn't due for another three hours. He could feel the channels through the stone. He could have activated them with a thought.

He didn't.

Through the glass, he watched two Council members emerge from the Hall's front entrance and stand on the steps. The silver-haired woman and a shorter man—younger, maybe middle generation, his coat less formal but his posture suggesting he wanted to be mistaken for someone more important. They spoke. Their expressions were the careful blankness of people discussing something consequential in a public space. The silver-haired woman gestured once—a sharp, contained motion, the kind of gesture that meant *I will handle this* or *this changes everything* or both.

A third person joined them. Then a fourth. They formed a loose cluster on the steps—bodies angled inward, voices pitched below the range of passersby. The political machine, digesting what it had been fed.

The door behind him opened. Dorenne.

She'd shed the formal coat. Underneath, she was the Dorenne he knew—sleeves at her elbows, a folio under one arm, the focused energy of a woman who had just accomplished something and was already calculating what came next.

"The Council has requested formal briefings," she said. "Four separate requests in the last twenty minutes. Lenara—the silver-haired member, you saw her—wants a private session. She chairs the Resource Allocation Committee."

"That's good?"

"That's essential. Lenara controls infrastructure funding. If she supports us, the other committees will follow." Dorenne set the folio on the room's small table. "Vasra's report will go to the Council by end of week. I've asked her to frame it as a preliminary assessment with recommendations for a supervised development program."

"Supervised by you."

"Supervised by my office. There's a difference." She paused. Her gaze moved from the folio to Ash—assessing, always assessing, but with something else in it now. A satisfaction that ran deeper than the political calculation. "You did well."

"I warmed a room."

"You warmed a room that six elder channelers couldn't fill, in front of a hundred and fifty people who control the city's resource allocation. That's not warming a room. That's changing a conversation." She opened the folio and scanned its first page—notes, names, the aides' shorthand from the event. "Three factions are already positioning. The Preservationists will frame the Wellspring as vindication—proof that the decline can be managed, that patience and stewardship are the correct response. The Accelerationists will use you as evidence that new power sources exist, that the old assumptions about scarcity are wrong. And the Authoritarians—"

"Will want to control it."

"Will want the Council to control it. Which, depending on how the committees break, means Lenara controls it, which means the question is whether Lenara trusts me to manage the resource or whether she wants direct oversight." Dorenne closed the folio. "That's my problem, not yours."

But it was his problem. It was his body, his reservoir, his power. The factions were calculating around a person who was standing in a room with a window overlooking the terrace, wearing a jacket that didn't fit, watching people he'd never met decide what he meant.

*Everyone sees a lever,* he thought. *Nobody sees—*

But that wasn't quite right either. Dorenne saw him. She'd seen him before the Wellspring, when he was no one. She'd given him a desk and a role and the first thing that had felt like belonging in twenty-three years. She saw him now—she'd said *you did well* and the warmth in it had been real, he was sure of it, as real as the calculation underneath.

The trouble was that the warmth and the calculation were the same thing. They operated on the same current. And he couldn't tell where one ended and the other began.

"You should go home," Dorenne said. "See your family. The next few days will be busy—the briefings, the development program, the Council review. Rest while you can."

He nodded.

"And Ash." She stopped him at the door. "The word will spread faster now. The people in that room have staff, colleagues, families. By tonight, your name will be in every district in the city. Be prepared for that."

He walked down the corridor. Past the aide who'd been waiting outside. Past the offices where the staff he'd worked beside for three weeks were already discussing what they'd heard, their voices low, their glances quick when he passed. Past the ramp entrance, where the transit lift's smooth disc waited in the wall.

He took the ramp down. The rhythm of the descent—his legs, the stone, the counting of flights—was familiar. The only thing that was.

* * *

The temporary housing was on the Third Terrace—a row of connected apartments that the city maintained for displaced families, three blocks from the Hall. Dorenne had arranged it the night of the activation, along with the healer, the supplies, the quiet efficiency that was her specific form of care.

Ash heard his mother's voice before he reached the door. Not words—tone. The high, tight register that meant she was arguing with someone, or trying not to argue, or holding herself back from an argument she'd already lost.

He opened the door.

His mother was sitting upright in the chair by the window—not the bed, the chair, which meant she'd moved herself there against the healer's instructions, because the bed made her feel like a patient and the chair made her feel like a person who was choosing to sit. Her leg was braced—the healer's work visible in the careful wrapping, the slight warmth of channeled bone-setting still radiating from the tibia. Her face was turned toward the window, but she wasn't looking out it. She was looking at the resonance pad on the table—a flat stone disc, paired with a twin somewhere in the city, the kind of communication device every household had. It was active. Someone had been speaking to her.

His father stood by the kitchen counter, his back to the door, his shoulders carrying the particular tension that meant he'd been absorbing something for the past hour and was running out of room.

"What happened?" Ash asked.

His mother's head turned. The expression on her face—he catalogued it automatically, the way he'd learned to read her over twenty-three years of being the thing she worried about most. Her mouth tight. Her eyes too bright. Her hands gripping the chair's arms with a force that had nothing to do with the broken leg and everything to do with whatever she'd heard.

"Mrs. Verath called," his father said, without turning around. "And the Kareth family. And two people from Maren's office." He paused. "And the woman from the third floor of the old building."

"What did they want?"

"To congratulate us." His father turned. His face was composed—the careful, patient composure of a man who had spent decades managing what other people felt about his son. "And to ask questions. And to—" He stopped. Chose a different word. "To express interest."

His mother's voice came out compressed. "They want to know if it's true. They want to know what he can do. They want—" She caught herself. Her jaw worked. Her hands tightened on the chair arms. "The Kareth boy asked if Ash could fix their heating. The heating in their apartment. As if my son is a—a service."

"He was asking for help," his father said quietly.

"He was asking for a piece of my son."

The room was small enough that the words had nowhere to go. They bounced off the walls and settled in the spaces between the three of them—the kitchen counter, the window, the chair where his mother sat with her braced leg and her bright eyes and the fierce, inarticulate terror of a woman whose purpose had been taken away and replaced with nothing.

For twenty-three years, she had protected him. From the world's cruelty, from the daily grind of a life without magic, from the whispered word—*dead weight*—that followed him through every corridor and classroom and job he'd ever tried. Her protection had been total, exhausting, and necessary. It had cost her sleep and friendships and the career she might have had if she hadn't been fighting for her son every hour of every day.

And now.

Now he was the most powerful person in the city. He didn't need her protection. He didn't need anyone's protection. The world that had been a threat to him had become a room full of people calculating his value, and she couldn't shield him from that because the thing she wanted to shield him from was not cruelty or indifference—it was want. The world wanted him now. And want was harder to fight than neglect, because it looked, from certain angles, exactly like love.

"I'm okay," Ash said.

"You're not okay. You're—" She gestured. The gesture encompassed the jacket that didn't fit, the building she'd never been inside, the political machinery she could sense turning around her son the way she'd always sensed the machinery that ground him down. "You're standing in front of rooms full of strangers and they're looking at you like you're something to use."

"Dorenne is managing it."

His mother's expression at Dorenne's name was a thing he chose not to examine. "Dorenne is managing *you*."

"She's protecting me."

"She's protecting the Wellspring. You just happen to be attached to it."

The silence that followed was the kind that contained too much to unpack. His father broke it—gently, the way his father broke every silence, by filling it with something practical.

"There's food," he said. "Maren brought soup before she left for work."

They ate. His mother moved from the chair to the small table—slowly, her braced leg extended, his father's arm steady at her elbow. She didn't ask Ash for help. She hadn't asked him for help since the activation. The absence of asking was louder than any argument.

The soup was good. Maren's doing—she cooked the way she did everything, with quiet competence and the unspoken understanding that someone needed to, and she would.

Ash's father asked about the event. Ash told him—the chamber, the lattice, the questions. He kept his voice neutral. He described the demonstration the way he might have described a deployment schedule—facts, sequence, outcome. He left out the part about the jacket. He left out the part about Dorenne saying *something wonderful.* He left out the expression on the silver-haired Council member's face, the one that looked like a woman calculating what a heated chamber meant when multiplied across a city.

His father listened. Nodded. Asked a question about the lattice design—a builder's question, specific and technical, the kind of question that reminded Ash that his father understood structures and systems in ways that had nothing to do with channeling and everything to do with forty years of working with his hands.

"The channels run deep," Ash said. "Deeper than I expected. The stone holds the heat for hours after—"

"Good design," his father said. "The old builders knew what they were doing." He paused. "You warm the building from the bones out. The bones remember."

Something in his father's voice. A weight that wasn't about lattice design.

Ash looked at him. His father was looking at his hands—his own hands, calloused and scarred, the hands that had shaped the door wedge that survived the apartment's collapse when the channeling-reinforced walls hadn't. He was holding his spoon with the careful grip of a man who knew the value of things that worked without magic.

"Your mother and I are proud of you," his father said. "You know that. We were proud before, and we're proud now, and that hasn't—" He stopped. Set the spoon down. Picked it up again. "That hasn't changed."

The emphasis on *changed* carried something that Ash heard but couldn't hold. The word wanted to open into a question—*do you know we were proud before?*—but his father wasn't a man who opened questions. He closed them. He sealed them with quiet, precise statements that said everything and asked nothing, because asking might reveal that the answer wasn't what he hoped.

"I know," Ash said.

His father nodded. Once. And went back to his soup.

* * *

Maren came in the late afternoon.

She looked the way Maren always looked—composed, put together, the particular quality of a person who had decided early that the world would see nothing it hadn't been invited to see. Her work coat was still on. She'd come straight from her shift—the municipal maintenance division, structural assessment, the kind of work that required precise channeling and didn't leave much room for a bad day.

"How was work?" their mother asked.

"Fine." Maren set a bag on the counter—more food, supplies, the practical offerings she brought every day. "Three inspections, two reclassifications. The Thornwall aqueduct has a new fracture at the fourth junction. They'll need a crew by end of week." She paused. "And everyone asked about Ash."

The way she said it—flat, factual, the emotion filed down to its thinnest edge—told Ash more than any elaboration would have. He could see it: colleagues stopping her in the corridor, the careful curiosity, the questions that were really requests for information disguised as small talk. *Your brother? The Wellspring? Really?*

"What did you tell them?" he asked.

"That you're my brother and you're doing well." She opened the bag and began unpacking—methodical, efficient, each item placed where it would be most useful. "And that the Director's office is managing the situation."

"Maren—"

"It's fine, Ash." She looked at him. Her expression was the composure she'd spent a lifetime perfecting—the smooth, capable surface that she showed the world while the work happened underneath. "People are curious. It's a big thing. They'll ask for a while and then they'll stop."

She went to their mother. Checked the brace—not with channeling, with her hands first, the physical assessment of someone who understood structures. Then she placed her palm against the wrapping and closed her eyes, and Ash felt the faint warmth of her channeling—thin, controlled, precise. A wisp of energy, carefully directed. She was checking the bone's alignment, the healer's work, the progress of the mend.

She did this every day. Morning and evening, without being asked. The discipline she applied to her work, applied to her mother's leg. A fraction of her trickle—a fraction she couldn't spare, not really, not when her shifts demanded most of what she had—spent quietly, privately, without anyone tallying the cost.

"Healing's on track," she said. "Another two weeks for the bone. The healer's good."

"I could—" Ash started.

"The healer's good," Maren repeated. The repetition closed the door.

He understood. Or he thought he understood. The healer was an elder-generation specialist Dorenne had sent—powerful, experienced, precise. Maren wasn't saying the healer was better than what Ash could do. She was saying the healer was already doing it, and the system was working, and what she needed from her brother was not another demonstration of what he could do.

But there was something else underneath. A tension in her jaw. A line between her brows that hadn't been there three weeks ago. The particular fatigue of a person whose trickle was stretched thinner than it should have been, whose discipline was compensating for a capacity that the world was quietly reducing, whose competence—the only currency she'd ever valued—was buying less each year.

She caught him looking. Her expression didn't change. That was its own kind of message.

"I brought honey cakes," she said. "From the stall on Marren Street."

Their mother brightened. The honey cakes were an old family ritual—a treat from the bakery in their old neighborhood, the one luxury their mother had insisted on even in the leanest months. Maren bought them every week. She hadn't stopped when the family moved. She'd just walked farther to get them.

The four of them ate honey cakes at the small table in the temporary apartment—Ash and Maren and their father and their mother with her braced leg extended and her bright, fierce eyes watching her son as if the world might reach through the window and take him. The cakes were warm. The room was cool—the apartment's heating on its rotating schedule, a few hours between activations, the standard allocation for displaced housing. Ash could have warmed it. He could have activated every lattice in the building.

He ate his honey cake and didn't.

* * *

The knock came after dark.

Ash was alone—his parents in the back room, his father's voice low and steady through the wall, the murmur of a man talking his wife through another night of worry. Maren had gone back to her own apartment an hour ago, with the quiet efficiency of someone who understood that staying too long meant admitting she didn't want to leave.

He opened the door.

Ryn.

She stood in the corridor with her work coat still on and her hair pulled back in the hasty way that meant she'd come from somewhere else and hadn't stopped to fix it. Her cheeks were flushed—not from channeling, from walking. She'd come up from the lower terraces, from the Fifth or Sixth, from the district where she worked and lived and maintained the heating systems that other people depended on. It was a long walk to the Third.

"Hey," she said.

"Hey."

She looked at him. The same Ryn look—direct, unimpressed, the gaze of someone who had watched him carry groceries with his teeth because his arms were full and who had once told him, at sixteen, that his cheerful act was "obvious to everyone who matters and invisible to everyone who doesn't." She looked at him now the way she'd always looked at him—seeing the person, not the event.

"Can I come in?"

He stepped aside. She walked past him into the apartment's small front room—scanned it with the quick, practical assessment of someone who evaluated rooms for a living. Cold. Adequate. Temporary. She didn't comment.

"I heard," she said.

"Everyone heard."

"I didn't hear from everyone. I heard from Tessaly." She paused. "Who heard from one of the aides. Who heard from the examiner. Who was apparently writing on his own sleeve." She almost smiled. "The information chain was... vivid."

"I should have told you myself."

"Yes. You should have." No anger in it. Just the fact, placed between them like a stone on a table. "But you've been—" She waved a hand. The gesture took in the apartment, the Hall, the city, the everything that had happened in six days. "Busy."

She sat on the edge of the kitchen table—not a chair, the table. She'd always done that. In his parents' old apartment, in Haran's workshop, on the wall outside the bakery on Marren Street. She sat on surfaces that weren't meant for sitting and made them look like they'd been waiting for her.

"I'm glad," she said.

Two words. She said them simply, the way she said everything—without performance, without the layered meaning that he was learning to hear in every other sentence directed at him. *I'm glad.* Not *I'm amazed.* Not *this changes everything.* Not *what does this mean for the city.* Just: I'm glad this happened to you.

Something loosened in his chest. A tightness he hadn't known was there—wound up over six days of testing and coaching and standing still while a room full of people calculated his worth—released a fraction of a turn.

"Are you sleeping?" she asked.

"Mostly."

"Eating?"

"Maren keeps bringing food."

"Have you talked to Haran?"

The question landed with more weight than its three words should have carried. He hadn't. Six days since the activation—three in the temporary housing with his hands in his pockets, one in the basement with Vasra, one in Dorenne's anteroom practicing the lattice, one standing on a platform in a jacket that didn't fit—and he hadn't gone to the workshop. Hadn't sent word. Hadn't thought about it until Ryn said the name and the absence opened up like a gap in a wall he'd been walking past without looking.

"Not yet," he said.

"You should."

"I know."

She watched him. The same Ryn assessment—not Dorenne's calculation, not the scholars' measurement, not the Council members' valuation. Something simpler. Someone who'd known him since he was a boy checking whether the boy was still in there.

"How does it feel?" she asked.

The same question the administrator had asked in the chamber. But different—different voice, different room, different intention. The administrator had wanted an answer he could carry. Ryn wanted the truth.

"It feels..." He searched. The words that came to mind—*vast*, *overwhelming*, *like an ocean*—were the words he'd been using in his own head for six days, and they were accurate and completely insufficient. They described the reservoir. They didn't describe the rest of it.

"It feels like everyone wants something," he said.

She nodded. As if this were obvious. As if this were the answer she'd expected from a man who had spent twenty-three years wanting to be wanted and was discovering that being wanted was its own kind of weight.

"The demonstration today," he said. "Dorenne set it up perfectly. The lattice, the room, the audience—it was exactly right. She positioned me and the Wellspring as a resource for the city, and she wasn't wrong, it is a resource, and the room needed to see it."

"But?"

"But I was standing there in a jacket someone else picked out, saying the words someone else wrote, and everyone in the room was looking at me and seeing something that wasn't me." He stopped. "That sounds ungrateful."

"That sounds honest."

"I'm not ungrateful. Dorenne has been—she's been incredible. She arranged the housing, the healer, the testing, the—everything. She's protecting me."

"From what?"

"From the factions. The Council. The people who'd use the Wellspring without—"

"She's one of those people, Ash."

The words arrived clean and landed hard. Not cruel—Ryn was never cruel, or rarely. Direct. The particular directness of a person who had no stake in the outcome except the person sitting in front of her.

"She's not—" he started.

"I'm not saying she's bad. I'm saying she's one of the people who's calculating what you're worth. She's just better at it than the rest of them." Ryn pulled one knee up onto the table. "She put you in a room and lit you up for a hundred and fifty people and stood beside you looking like the person who found the treasure. That's not protection. That's positioning."

"It can be both."

"It can. Is it?"

He didn't answer. The question sat between them, another stone on a table that was accumulating stones faster than he could sort them.

Ryn changed direction. She did this—moved past resistance without fighting it, the conversational equivalent of water finding its way around an obstruction.

"The lattice thing. Did it cost you much?"

"Nothing. Basically nothing."

"That's what I heard." She was quiet for a moment. "Vasra's word—*Wellspring*. Does it feel right?"

"I don't know what it feels like to have a word for yourself."

"It feels weird," she said. "I can tell you that for free." She slid off the table. Standing, she was shorter than him by half a head—the same height difference as always, unchanged by the power that had changed everything else. "Go see Haran. Tomorrow, if you can. He won't ask, but he'd want to see you."

"I will."

"And eat something that isn't Maren's soup." She moved toward the door. Stopped. Turned back.

"Ash."

"Yeah."

"I'm glad." She said it again—the same two words, the same simple delivery. But this time her eyes held his for a beat longer than necessary, and in that beat there was something he couldn't quite name. Not pity. Not awe. Not the careful distance of a person processing someone else's transformation.

Something older. Something that predated the Wellspring by twenty-three years and didn't need it.

She left.

He stood in the apartment's front room and listened to her footsteps down the corridor—brisk, practical, the stride of a person heading home after a long day. The sound faded. The apartment was quiet. Through the wall, his father's voice had stopped. His mother was sleeping, or pretending to sleep, or lying in the dark with her bright eyes open and her hands gripping the blanket the way she'd gripped the chair arms, holding on to something the world was pulling away.

Ash sat on the edge of the table where Ryn had sat. The surface was still warm from her. The apartment was cool. The reservoir behind his ribs pulsed with its steady, patient rhythm—vast, unmoved, carrying more power than the room full of Council members could comprehend and more weight than the jacket on his shoulders.

He thought about the silver-haired Council member. The expression on her face. The sharp, contained gesture on the steps.

He thought about Dorenne. *You're not on trial. You're standing beside me while I explain something wonderful.*

He thought about his mother. *They're looking at you like you're something to use.*

He thought about Ryn. *I'm glad.*

The same event, seen from four directions. The same person—him—reflected in four different lenses, each one showing something true and each one missing something essential. The Council saw power. Dorenne saw a resource. His mother saw danger. Ryn saw Ash.

And Ash—sitting on a table in a temporary apartment, wearing a jacket that someone else had chosen, carrying a reservoir that someone else had named—wasn't sure, for the first time in six days, which lens was the right one.

Not because they were wrong. Because they all felt true. The power was real. The resource was real. The danger was real. And he was real—he was still Ash, still the same person who'd climbed forty flights of ramp every morning, who'd pressed his thumb into the crack in the stairwell wall, who'd learned to smile because smiling was the rent he paid for existing.

But the wanting had started. The city wanted him now—wanted the Wellspring, wanted the heated rooms and the stabilized foundations and the answer to the decline that had been grinding everyone down for decades. And the wanting felt, in his chest, in his bones, in the space behind the reservoir's pulse—the wanting felt like love.

Not love. He knew that. He was smart enough to know the difference between being loved and being useful.

But it felt the same.

It felt exactly the same.

The reservoir pulsed. Steady. Patient. Vast. And for the first time, the vastness didn't feel like freedom. It felt like a door that had been opened, and behind it, a room full of people waiting to tell him what it was for.
