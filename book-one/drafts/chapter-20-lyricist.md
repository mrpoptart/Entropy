# Chapter 20

The stairs down from the command post were unlit past the third landing.

Ash took them by feel, one hand on the stone railing, the other pressed flat against the wall where the surface was rough and cold and hadn't been recharged in weeks. His feet found each step the way they'd found steps in the dark his whole life — measuring, testing, trusting the body's memory of descent. Twenty-two steps to the landing. Turn. Twenty-two more. The numbers lived in his legs the way the other numbers lived on the papers he'd left on the desk upstairs, folded once and set beneath the dead candle.

He didn't light the stairwell. He could have. A thought, a pulse, the walls glowing warm — the kind of casual expenditure he'd been making for weeks without counting, without noticing, the way you didn't notice breathing until someone told you the air was running out.

He kept his hands at his sides and walked down in the dark.

The command post's main corridor was quiet at this hour. A single lamp burned at the entrance, maintained by the overnight rotation — some middle-generation channeler spending a sliver of their daily pool to keep the hall navigable. Ash passed through the light and out the heavy door and the night air hit him like water, cold and immediate, carrying the mineral smell of Solathis after dark: stone cooling, the faint sulfur of heating lattices in the nearby buildings, woodsmoke from somewhere lower on the terraces where someone had given up on channeling and lit a fire instead.

He stood on the steps outside and breathed.

The city spread below him in its familiar gradient — bright at the top, dim in the middle, dark at the edges. The First Terrace glowed. The Third was lit unevenly, windows warm and then not warm, the pattern of a neighborhood where some people had enough and some people were deciding what to spend it on. Below the Third, the light thinned. Below the Fifth, it mostly stopped.

He went down.

* * *

The route to Haran's workshop was one his body knew better than his mind did. Three years of walking it — no, longer than that. Five years, six, since he'd first found the place as a teenager following the sound of a lathe through an open door. The route had been carved into him by repetition the way water carved stone: not by force but by persistence, the daily passage wearing a channel that his feet followed without instruction.

Down from the command post through the Fourth Terrace. Past the bakery that stayed open late, its windows fogged with the heat of ovens that ran on coal and wood because the baker's pool couldn't maintain a heating lattice through a full night's work. Past the junction where the old lift shaft stood dark and silent — it had run on channeled force once, a platform that rose and fell on columns of sustained pressure, carrying people between the terraces. The mechanism still existed. The power to run it didn't. Now it was a vertical tunnel with a ladder bolted to one wall, and people climbed.

Ash climbed. Down, in this case — three levels, the ladder's rungs cold iron under his palms, his boots finding the spacing by memory. The shaft smelled like damp stone and rust and the particular staleness of enclosed air that no one had bothered to move in months. He descended through it and came out on the Fifth Terrace, where the buildings leaned and the streets narrowed and the light from the upper terraces was a glow on the underside of the stone above, reflected and diffuse, like moonlight through cloud.

He kept walking. His breath made shapes in the cold air. The reservoir sat in his chest — warm, present, enormous, halved — and he didn't reach for it. He let the cold sit on his skin. He let his legs do the work of carrying him downhill. He let his body be a body, nothing more, the way it had been a body for twenty-odd years before everything changed.

The streets of the lower terraces were quieter than the upper ones but not empty. A woman sat on a step outside a building, wrapped in a coat, her hands cupped around something that steamed — tea, probably, heated by whatever she could spare. She looked up as he passed and didn't recognize him. In the dark, without the context of Dorenne's deployment schedule and the crew chiefs and the official attention, he was just a man walking downhill. He was grateful for it.

A cat watched him from a window ledge. Two men stood talking at an intersection, their voices low, one of them gesturing toward a crack in the building wall behind them — a fracture in the old channeling, the kind that let cold air through, the kind that would get worse before anyone had the pool to fix it. Ash looked at the crack as he passed. His hands twitched.

He kept walking.

The workshop was on a side street off the lower Fifth Terrace, wedged between a cobbler's shop and a storage building whose upper floor had been converted into someone's home. The street was narrow enough that the buildings on both sides nearly touched overhead, and the paving stones were uneven — channeled once, decades ago, by someone whose work had since cracked and shifted and settled into the particular unevenness of infrastructure that no one maintained. Ash's feet knew every uneven stone. The one that tipped was three paces from the workshop door, and he stepped over it without thinking, the way he always stepped over it, the way he would step over it in his sleep.

Light showed under the workshop door. A thin line of warm yellow, the color of oil lamps and late hours and a man who was always there.

* * *

He stood outside for a moment.

His hand was on the door latch and his hand wasn't moving. The metal was cold against his palm. Behind the door, he could hear the workshop's nighttime sounds — the tick of cooling metal, the low hum of the building settling, and beneath it, quiet and steady, the sound of someone working. A file, maybe. Or a hand plane. Something that made a rhythmic, patient sound, the sound of a tool being used by someone who wasn't in a hurry.

The papers were upstairs on the desk. The numbers were in his head. The candle had burned to nothing and the room had gone dark and he'd lain on the cot and closed his eyes and the dark behind his eyelids had been the same dark as the room and he hadn't slept. He'd lain there for an hour, or two, and the numbers had moved behind his eyes like fish beneath the surface of a pond — visible, unreachable, circling.

Eleven to twenty-one weeks.

He'd gotten up. He'd put on his boots. He hadn't decided where to go. His feet had decided.

Not home. Not to the apartment in the upper district that Dorenne's office had arranged, the one with heated walls and running water and a window that looked out over the city he was being paid to maintain. Not to his parents' home on the Third Terrace, where his mother would read his face before he'd closed the door and his father would grip his shoulder and hold on too long and neither of them would know what to say because neither of them knew what was happening to him.

Not to Dorenne. Not tonight. Not with the numbers still raw and unprocessed, still sitting in his chest like something swallowed that hadn't gone down. She would want to manage it. She would pull up charts and recalculate and find the optimal path through his remaining capacity, and she would be right to do it, and he couldn't bear it.

Not to Ryn. Not to Maren.

Haran.

The latch clicked under his hand. The door opened. The workshop's warmth rolled out over him like a held breath released, and the smell hit him at the threshold — oil and sawdust and the sharp mineral bite of cut metal, the same smell, the exact same smell, unchanged across every visit he'd ever made to this room since he was seventeen years old and had nothing and came here because here was the only place where nothing was enough.

Haran was at the central bench.

He had his back half-turned, sleeves rolled above the elbow, a hand plane moving across a length of wood in long, even strokes. The shavings curled away in pale spirals and fell to the floor where other shavings already lay. An oil lamp sat on the shelf above the bench, its light steady — Ash looked at it and couldn't tell whether the steadiness was the lamp's or Haran's, whether the wick burned on its own or burned because something kept it exactly where it was, a micro-pulse so small it was indistinguishable from physics.

The workshop had changed.

He saw it in pieces, the way you saw changes in a familiar room — not all at once but in the accumulation of wrong details, the things that were where they shouldn't be, the things that weren't where they should. The pump. The pump that had been half-built on the central bench the last time he was here, weeks ago, the housing and fittings and that outlet flange he'd fused with a casual thread of the Wellspring — it wasn't on the bench. It wasn't in the workshop at all.

In its place, across the back wall where the component racks used to stand, was a schematic. Hand-drawn on heavy paper, pinned at four corners, the lines precise and confident in the way that Haran's lines were always precise: a pump system. Not a pump — a system. The drawing showed interconnected components, branching pipes, a network of mechanical linkages and pressure chambers and gravity feeds that connected a water source on the upper terrace to a distribution manifold on the lower one. Arrows indicated flow direction. Annotations in Haran's careful handwriting noted pressures, capacities, maintenance intervals. At the bottom, a small notation: *Installed — Velden building and adjacent block. Operational.*

Ash stood in the doorway and looked at the schematic and the warmth of the workshop pressed against him and the cold of the street pressed against his back and he stood between them.

The pump was finished. Installed. Working.

He'd been gone for weeks. He'd been at the command post, at the deployment sites, at the briefings and the reconstruction zones, channeling millions of kJ into the city's bones while the pump he'd started helping with sat on this bench and waited. And then it hadn't waited. Haran had finished it. The other tinkerers had finished it. They'd designed the system, built the components, installed the network, and water flowed in the Velden building and the adjacent block — not because a Wellspring had forced it through the pipes but because physics worked and hands worked and someone had spent the weeks that Ash had spent elsewhere.

It didn't need a Wellspring. It never had.

The workshop hadn't changed and had changed. His workbench — the one on the north wall, the one that had been his since Haran cleared a space for him years ago — was clear. Not empty. Clear. The tools were arranged in the order he'd left them: calipers, then the small files, then the scribing tool he'd made himself from a broken awl and a bit of hardened steel. The vise was clean. The surface was wiped. Someone — Haran — had kept the bench ready. Not as a museum piece. Not as a rebuke. As a statement so quiet it barely registered as a statement at all.

*You'll come back. This will still be here.*

"Close the door," Haran said. He hadn't turned around.

Ash closed the door.

The cold from the street cut off. The warmth settled. The workshop held him the way it had always held him — low ceiling, close walls, the accumulated heat of a space where someone had spent decades making things comfortable through a hundred tiny applications that cost almost nothing and added up to everything.

Haran set the hand plane down. He turned.

His eyes found Ash the way they always found whatever arrived in his workspace — measuring, sorting, noting. The brief, assessing look that took in everything and commented on nothing. He held it for a moment. His expression didn't change, exactly, but something behind it shifted — a recalibration, a reading of the data that Ash's face and posture and hands and breathing were giving him, the accumulated intelligence of a man who had spent decades paying attention to the people around him.

He didn't ask what was wrong.

He crossed to the small stove in the corner — not the big one for metalwork, the small one for the kettle, the one that Ash had sat beside on a hundred evenings drinking tea that stayed warm longer than it should have. The kettle was there. Haran picked it up, tested its weight — enough water — and set it back on the plate. His hand rested on the kettle's side for a moment.

A warmth bloomed. Not visible, not dramatic — the faintest brightening of the air around the kettle, the ghost of heat passing through metal, so casual and so small that if Ash hadn't been watching for it he would have missed it entirely. A micro-pulse. A thumbnail of energy, precisely placed, warming the water and nothing else. The kettle didn't glow. The stove plate didn't heat. The air around Haran's hand didn't shimmer. Just the water, inside the kettle, accepting exactly what it needed and no more.

The kettle began to sing.

Haran took two cups from the shelf above the stove. He poured. The tea was the same tea it always was — dark, slightly bitter, the kind that came in bulk from the market stalls on the Third Terrace, nothing special, the tea of a man who valued function over refinement. He carried both cups to the central bench and set one down on Ash's side.

He sat on his stool.

Ash crossed the workshop. His legs felt strange — not weak, not tired, but aware of themselves in a way they usually weren't, as if the act of walking to this bench and sitting on this stool were heavier than the distance warranted. He sat. The stool creaked. The tea steamed.

Haran picked up his cup and held it between both hands and said nothing.

The silence stretched. It was a particular kind of silence — not empty, not waiting, not the silence of a man who expected Ash to speak. It was the silence of a man who had made tea and sat down and was drinking it, and if someone else wanted to talk, the space was here for that, and if they didn't, the tea was still good.

Ash looked at the schematic on the wall. The pump system. The arrows and annotations and the small notation at the bottom. *Installed. Operational.*

"You finished it," he said.

"We finished it." Haran's voice was unhurried. "Petra did the manifold work. Davel machined the pressure chambers. A kid from the Velden building — fourteen, no pool to speak of, good with his hands — he did the pipe runs." A pause. "Took about three weeks."

Three weeks. Ash had spent those three weeks channeling 3,800,000 kJ into the city's reconstruction — Dorenne's number, circled at the bottom of the attachment, the precise accounting of a woman who counted everything. In those same three weeks, Haran and Petra and Davel and a fourteen-year-old kid had built a water system from copper and brass and physics and put it in the wall and turned it on and it worked.

"The building has water," Ash said. Not a question.

"The building has water. The block next to it has water." Haran sipped his tea. "The maintenance cycle runs on basic hand-cranking. Anyone can do it. A kid with a wrench and twenty minutes, once a week." He set the cup down. "There's a small channeling contribution — optional. If someone in the building wants to add a little heat to the supply line, the system accepts it. Distributed. A few kJ from anyone willing. The heating element I put in takes micro-pulses. Even an adolescent can contribute."

*It doesn't need a Wellspring.*

The words arrived in his mind and sat there, heavy and obvious and complete.

"How much," Ash said. "The channeling component. If everyone contributes."

"Maybe forty kJ a day. Total. Split across however many residents want to participate." Haran's fingers turned his cup slowly. "It's not nothing. But it's not much."

Forty kJ. An adolescent's breakfast budget. A rounding error in Dorenne's deployment charts. The amount Ash had been spending in the time it took to walk between sites.

He looked at the schematic again. The arrows. The flow. The system designed so that it worked without magic, and worked better with a little, and never depended on a lot. A system built for the world as it was becoming, not the world as it had been.

He looked at Haran.

Haran was watching him. Not with the expression of a man making a point — not teaching, not proving, not saying *look what we did while you were gone*. Just watching. The way he watched everything. With attention, with patience, with the unhurried assessment of a man who had been paying attention for a very long time and knew that the things worth noticing arrived on their own schedule.

"Something happened," Haran said. Not a question.

Ash looked at his tea. The surface trembled — his hands, not the cup. The fine tremor that had started at the desk upstairs and hadn't stopped, the one he'd been ignoring because ignoring was what he did, had always done, the performance of okayness that had carried him through a lifetime of not being okay.

He pressed his palms flat against the bench. The wood was smooth under his hands, worn by years of work, sanded by use into a surface that held warmth the way good wood did — not from channeling but from the accumulated heat of a room where people had spent decades making things.

"I did the math," he said.

Haran waited.

The words came slowly. Not because he didn't know them — they'd been circling in his head for hours, organized and precise, the careful accounting of a mind that couldn't stop calculating. They came slowly because saying them out loud was different from writing them on paper. Paper held numbers. A voice held everything around the numbers, the weight and the fear and the thing in his chest that wasn't the reservoir, that was smaller and sharper and harder to name.

"The Wellspring started at roughly fifty million kJ." His voice was steady. He hadn't known it would be steady. "I've spent approximately twenty-eight million. The earthquake was fifteen million. Training and early deployments were about nine. Post-earthquake reconstruction, another four."

He stopped. Breathed.

"I've used more than half."

The workshop was quiet. The kettle had stopped singing. The oil lamp burned, steady, on the shelf above the bench. Haran's face was still.

"At my current deployment rate — Dorenne's schedule, three to six sites a day — I spend roughly 1,200,000 kJ a week. At that rate, the remaining reservoir lasts eighteen weeks. If the schedule expands — and it will expand — eleven."

He said the number and heard it in the air and the air didn't change. The workshop didn't shift. The warmth didn't falter. Eleven weeks hung between them, a small thing, a box drawn on a piece of paper, a rectangle of pencil marks containing the rest of his usefulness to the city.

"With maximum efficiency improvement — years of practice I don't have — twenty-one weeks. That's the ceiling." He looked at his hands on the bench. "Eleven to twenty-one weeks. Then it's gone."

Gone. The word fell into the silence like a stone into a well, and the silence accepted it without a sound.

Haran didn't move. His hands stayed on the cup, still and steady, the hands of a man who had heard something and was taking the time to hear it fully. His face showed nothing — not shock, not pity, not the calculating look that Ash had seen on Dorenne's face when new information arrived and was immediately sorted into the framework of deployment and allocation and use. None of that. Just attention. The deep, unhurried attention of a man who understood that some things needed space before they needed a response.

The silence lasted a long time.

Ash held it. He'd expected it to be unbearable — the quiet, the waiting, the gap after the numbers where something was supposed to happen. A response, a plan, a reaction. Something to move toward, some next step that would transform the fact into something manageable. But the silence wasn't unbearable. It was the silence of Haran's workshop, where silence had always been a tool, where the gap between actions was as deliberate as the actions themselves. The silence of a man who didn't fill space because he respected what space was for.

The oil lamp flickered. Steadied. The workshop breathed.

Finally, Haran set his cup down. The small sound — ceramic on wood, a quiet thing — landed in the silence with precision.

"So," he said. His voice was the same voice it had always been. Unhurried. Level. The voice of a man who built things and understood that every material had properties and every property was a fact and facts were what you worked with. "How much is left, and what do you want to build with it?"

The words arrived and sat in the air.

Not *how do you slow the loss*. Not *how do you get more*. Not *what went wrong*. Not *I'm sorry*. Not *we'll fix this*. Not the framework of preservation, not the framework of grief, not the framework of Dorenne's deployment charts and diminishing projections and the optimal allocation of a dwindling resource.

Build.

What do you want to *build*.

Ash's hands were still flat on the bench. The tremor had stopped. He looked at them — his hands, on the wood, the same hands that had been in this workshop for years, the same hands that had held tools and turned mechanisms and lapped valve seats and done the patient, physical, unremarkable work of making things function. The same hands that had, for eight weeks, poured an ocean into the bones of a city that swallowed it and asked for more. The same hands that would, in eleven weeks or twenty-one, hold nothing at all.

The question sat with him. It wasn't a solution. It wasn't comfort. It was a reframing so fundamental that the ground shifted under his understanding the way the ground had shifted during the earthquake — not violently, not dramatically, but completely. A tilt in the axis of the world.

The reservoir was not an identity. It was a resource. And resources were for building.

He'd been thinking about the Wellspring as himself — as the thing that made him Ash, the thing that had rescued him from a lifetime of nothing, the thing that meant he mattered. He'd been treating the countdown as a countdown to his own dissolution, as if when the reservoir emptied he would empty too, as if the kJ were the substance of his self and their absence would be his absence. The math on the paper upstairs wasn't a calculation. It was a death sentence.

But Haran hadn't said *how long do you have*. He'd said *what do you want to build*.

The difference was everything.

*How long do you have* was a question about loss. About the diminishing arc, the downward slope, the box on the paper getting smaller. It assumed the Wellspring was the point and its ending was the tragedy and everything between now and then was a managed decline.

*What do you want to build* was a question about use. About material and design and purpose. It assumed the Wellspring was a stockpile — wood and metal and energy — and the only question that mattered was what you made with it before it was spent.

A builder didn't mourn his materials. He used them.

"I don't know," Ash said.

"That's fine." Haran picked up his cup again. Sipped. Set it down. "You don't have to know tonight."

The relief arrived in his body before it reached his mind. Not happiness — the numbers hadn't changed, the projection hadn't moved, the box on the paper was still the same small rectangle containing the same finite range. But the suffocating pressure that had been sitting on his chest since the candle went out — the crushing weight of a countdown, the airless feeling of falling toward a number that couldn't be changed — that pressure eased. Not gone. Eased. The way pressure eased when a valve opened and the system found a path that wasn't through you.

He breathed. Really breathed, for what felt like the first time in hours. His ribs expanded. His shoulders dropped. The muscles along the back of his neck released something they'd been holding, and the release felt like setting down a weight he hadn't known he was carrying.

"I should have come here first," he said.

Haran's mouth moved — not quite a smile. The quality a smile might be made from. "You came here," he said. "The sequence isn't important."

* * *

They sat for a while.

The tea cooled and Haran made more, the same casual pulse — invisible, surgical, a thread of energy so precisely placed that the kettle warmed and nothing else did, the water accepting heat the way water accepted heat when you understood exactly what water needed. Ash watched him do it and thought about the micro-pulse technique from weeks ago, the thumbnail of warmth on the brass test piece, Haran's demonstration that had looked effortless because it was the product of decades of practice so thorough that effort had been refined out of it.

The second cup was the same tea. Ash held it and the warmth moved through the ceramic into his palms and the workshop held its sounds — the building settling, the distant noise of the city at night, the faint creak of the wooden shelves that lined the south wall where the component racks had been rearranged since his last visit.

"The kid," Ash said. "The fourteen-year-old. The pipe runs."

"Terrik." Haran nodded. "Lives in the Velden building. Lost his father in the earthquake — a structural crew, one of the buildings in the lower Fourth. The mother works double shifts. He showed up one morning and asked what needed doing."

"No pool?"

"Barely. Maybe three hundred kJ. He can warm his hands. Not much else." Haran turned his cup. "Best apprentice plumber I've had in years. Understands joints. Understands flow. His hands know things his pool will never tell him."

The words landed. Ash heard them and heard what was underneath them and what was beside them and what was behind them. A boy with barely any magic, living in a building without water, whose father had died in the earthquake, who had walked into a workshop and asked what needed doing. A boy who could warm his hands. Who understood joints. Whose hands knew things.

*A boy like me.*

The thought arrived without preamble and sat in his chest and he let it sit.

"He reminds me," Ash started, and stopped.

Haran looked at him. The look was level and warm and unhurried and contained, somewhere in its depth, the expression of a man who had been waiting for this sentence for a very long time.

"Yes," Haran said. He didn't need the end of the sentence. "He does."

The silence that followed wasn't empty. It was full — full of the workshop and the tea and the schematic on the wall and the pump that ran on physics and the boy who reminded them both of the person Ash had been before the ocean cracked open inside him. Full of the question that Haran had asked and the answer that Ash didn't have yet and the space between them, which was the space where building happened.

Ash looked at his workbench. The calipers. The files. The scribing tool he'd made from a broken awl. The vise, clean and ready. The surface, wiped.

"My tools are still here," he said.

"Your tools are always here."

"The geared mechanism. The one I never finished."

Haran's eyes shifted toward the bench. "Under the surface. Drawer on the left. Where you left it."

Ash didn't get up. He sat on his stool and held his tea and looked at the bench where his things waited, and the looking was enough for now. The mechanism was there. The tools were there. The bench was there. The workshop was there and Haran was there and the schematic on the wall showed a system that worked and the question in the air had a shape he could work with.

Not tonight. But.

"The pump system," he said. "The distributed channeling input — the micro-pulse heating. You designed it for the technique you showed me."

"I designed it for anyone," Haran said. "The technique helps. But a basic pulse works too. Even a child flooding the input — the system's tolerant. It takes what it gets and buffers the excess as heat loss. Not efficient, but functional."

"And if someone wanted to learn the technique—"

"They'd get better. Over time. With practice." Haran's fingers tapped the bench once. "The way everyone gets better at everything. Slowly, and then less slowly, and then they don't think about it anymore."

Ash thought about his own attempts. The brass test piece. The bloom of heat that spread to the edges when he wanted it at the center. The river that resisted being narrowed by hands. He'd tried it and failed and tried it again and failed less and Davel had said *much better* and it still hadn't been useful.

"I couldn't do it," Ash said. "The micro-pulse. When you showed me. I was too—" He searched for the word. "Big."

"You were flooding." Haran's voice held no judgment. "You had an ocean. The ocean pushed. That's what oceans do."

"Can I learn? Even with—" He gestured at his chest. The reservoir. The half that remained.

"Can you learn to whisper in a library because you learned to shout in a stadium?" The question wasn't rhetorical. Haran considered it, genuinely, the way he considered mechanism and material and the properties of things. "Yes. It's harder. The muscle memory is wrong. You'll have to unlearn the flooding before you can learn the precision. But your hands—" He looked at Ash's hands, on the bench, wrapped around the cup. "Your hands already know. They knew before the Wellspring. The hands haven't forgotten."

The words settled into Ash like something finding its place in a mechanism — a component sliding into a housing, the click of fit, the small sound of things aligning. His hands knew. They'd always known. The valve seat and the lapping compound and the eight minutes of patient work that made a surface true. The scribing tool fashioned from scrap. The gears he'd cut by hand, by feel, by the accumulated knowledge of a body that had spent its whole life working without magic because magic wasn't available.

The hands hadn't forgotten. The hands were still here.

"The efficiency changes the math," Ash said. Something was moving in his mind — not the circling fish from earlier, not the numbers swimming beneath the surface, but something more structured. The shape of a calculation that pointed forward instead of back. "If I learn the technique. If I can get my channeling to — master level, you said. Ninety percent."

"Over time," Haran said carefully. "Not in weeks."

"No. I know. But—" He was reaching for it, the thought just ahead of his fingers. "The deployment work. Dorenne's schedule. Three to six sites a day, structural reinforcement, the big applications. That's what's draining the reservoir. A million kJ a week. More."

"Yes."

"But if the work wasn't — if it wasn't all reinforcement. If some of it was building. Systems like the pump. Infrastructure that runs on physics and distributed contribution instead of—" He looked at the schematic. "Instead of me."

Haran was very still.

"The pump cost zero kJ from the Wellspring," Ash said. "Zero. And it replaced a channeling dependency that was costing two mid-generation channelers their morning pool every day. That's — what, five thousand kJ a day of maintenance that's just... gone. Replaced by copper and brass and a kid with a wrench."

"And three weeks of labor from four tinkerers," Haran added.

"And three weeks of labor." Ash's mind was moving now, the analytical engine that Dorenne had hired him for, that Haran had trained without either of them knowing it, the mind that saw systems and followed them and couldn't stop following until the whole weave was visible. "But the labor is renewable. The labor doesn't deplete. You can build another pump next month and another after that. The tinkerers don't run out of hands."

The thought opened up ahead of him like a road.

"If I spent the reservoir on catalytic work — not doing the maintenance, but building the systems that replace the maintenance — how far does it go? Not eighteen weeks of deployment. Something else. Something that lasts after I—"

He stopped. The sentence had a cliff at the end of it and he hadn't been ready for the edge.

"After the reservoir is gone," Haran finished. Steady. Unafraid of the words.

"Yes."

The silence that followed was different from the earlier ones. This one had a quality of weight — of something being assessed, not emotionally but structurally, the way Haran assessed a joint or a tolerance or the properties of a material he was about to work with. He was thinking. Ash could see him thinking — the slight narrowing of the eyes, the fingers that had stopped moving, the absolute stillness of a man whose body went quiet when his mind engaged fully.

"I have drawings," Haran said. "Not just the pump. Heating systems. Mechanical lifts. Water treatment. I've been making them for years."

Years. The word hung.

"You—"

"The decline is real," Haran said. Simply. Without drama. The way he said everything. "I've known it was real since I was younger than you. My generation — we could see the numbers. The pools shrinking. The work getting harder. I started drawing alternatives thirty years ago because someone needed to, and nobody with a strong pool was going to bother."

Thirty years. Thirty years of drawings in a workshop on the lower Fifth Terrace, made by a man with an elder's pool and a master's technique who had looked at the numbers and understood what they meant and started building the world that would be needed when the numbers reached zero. Not because anyone asked him to. Not because anyone was paying attention. Because the work needed doing and he had always been the kind of person who did the work.

"How many systems?" Ash asked.

"Enough for a district. Maybe two." Haran rose from his stool — slowly, the careful movement of a man whose knees knew the hour. He crossed to the far wall where a cabinet stood, the heavy wooden one that Ash had always assumed held supplies. Haran opened it.

Scrolls. Dozens of them, rolled and stacked, filling the cabinet's shelves in neat rows. Drawings, schematics, plans — the accumulated output of decades of quiet work, the engineering of a future that Haran had been building on paper while the world debated whether the future was coming at all.

Ash looked at the cabinet. At the scrolls. At the thirty years of work that had been sitting in this workshop, ten steps from his workbench, all the hours he'd been here, and he'd never opened the cabinet, never asked what was in it, never thought to look.

His throat tightened.

"You never showed me," he said. His voice came out rougher than he intended.

Haran closed the cabinet carefully. "You weren't ready."

"And now?"

The old man looked at him. The lamplight caught his face — the lines around his eyes, the grey at his temples, the patient and weathered expression of a man who had been waiting, without urgency, for a very long time. Not waiting for Ash. Waiting for the moment when showing would mean something. When the drawings would be more than paper. When someone would look at them and see not a hobby or an eccentricity or the quaint fixation of a tinkerer in the lower terraces, but a plan.

"Now you're asking the right question," Haran said.

* * *

It was past midnight when Ash walked back to the central bench and sat down. The tea had gone cold for the second time. Haran made more — the same pulse, the same invisible warmth, the kettle singing the same small song — and they sat with fresh cups and the silence had changed. It wasn't the silence of grief now, or the silence of space being held. It was the silence of two people sitting with an idea, letting it settle, feeling its weight and its shape the way you felt the weight of a tool before you started using it.

The question. *What do you want to build?*

He didn't have the answer. Not the specific, architectural, detailed answer that would come from Haran's drawings and Ash's calculations and the slow work of turning an idea into a plan. That would take days. Weeks. The kind of patient, iterative work that happened in workshops like this one, between people who understood that building was slow.

But the question had a shape. And a shape was the beginning of a structure.

"I need to see the drawings," Ash said.

"Tomorrow. Come in the morning. Bring your mind." Haran paused. "Leave the ocean at the door."

Ash almost smiled. Not quite — his face wasn't ready for it yet, the muscles of his expression still holding the shape they'd been holding since the dark room upstairs, the shape of a man carrying a number he couldn't set down. But the not-quite-smile was there, in the architecture of his face, like a foundation waiting for a wall.

"The ocean doesn't have a door," he said.

"Everything has a door." Haran drained his tea. "Finding it is the technique."

The workshop was quiet. The lamp burned. The schematic hung on the wall, showing a system that worked because physics worked and hands worked and someone had spent the time. The cabinet on the far wall held thirty years of drawings — the plans for a world that didn't need magic, or needed only a little, distributed and shared and built to last beyond the people who built it.

Ash looked at his workbench. His tools. The drawer on the left where the geared mechanism waited, unfinished, the project he'd abandoned when the Wellspring made projects like that seem small. A mechanism that ran without magic. A mechanism that would still run when the magic was gone.

The question sat in him. Not answered. Not answerable, yet. But present. Taking up space. Occupying the place where the countdown had been — not replacing it, because the countdown was still there, eleven to twenty-one weeks, the box on the paper — but sitting beside it. The countdown and the question, side by side. How long do you have. What do you want to build.

Both true. Both real. One of them was a cage. The other was a door.

He finished his tea. Set the cup down. The ceramic touched the bench with a sound that was small and clean and final, the way things sounded in Haran's workshop, where nothing was wasted and everything was placed.

"Thank you," he said.

Haran looked at him. The look held everything it always held — warmth and distance and patience and the deep, quiet recognition of a man who had been correctly understood. The expression that a smile might be made from, if it ever decided to finish arriving.

"Go sleep," Haran said. "You look terrible."

"I haven't slept."

"I know." He picked up the cups — both of them, both hands — and carried them to the small basin by the stove. He washed them with his hands. No channeling. Soap and water and the practiced motion of a man who washed cups every night because cups needed washing and his hands could wash them. "The cot in the back room is still there. If you don't want to walk back up."

Ash looked at the door to the back room — the small space behind the workshop where Haran kept a cot and a blanket for the nights when the work ran late and the walk home wasn't worth the effort. Ash had slept there before, years ago. Nights when the workshop was the only place that felt like it fit, when his parents' home was too full of the careful kindness that reminded him of what he wasn't, when the only thing that helped was the smell of oil and metal and the knowledge that his tools were an arm's reach away.

"Yeah," he said. "Okay."

He stood. The stool creaked. His body was tired — genuinely tired, the kind of tired that lived in the muscles and the joints and the heavy slowness of a body that had been carrying something for too long. Not the reservoir's weight. The other weight. The numbers, the projections, the dark room and the dead candle and the calculation of his own finitude.

He crossed to the back room. The cot was there — narrow, clean, the blanket folded at its foot. The same blanket. He sat on the edge and the frame gave a familiar groan and the room smelled like stored wood and linseed oil and the particular close warmth of a small space in a building that Haran kept comfortable by a hundred invisible applications, the accumulated micro-pulses of a man who understood that warmth was a daily practice, not a grand gesture.

Through the doorway, he could see the workshop. The bench. The lamp. Haran's back, turned toward him now, the old man tidying his workspace the way he tidied it every night — tools returned, shavings swept, the bench wiped clean. The same ritual, unchanged, whether the day had held routine or revelation. You cleaned up. You put things away. You left the space ready for tomorrow.

Ash lay down. The cot held him. The blanket was rough wool, warm from the room's warmth, and he pulled it over himself and lay in the dim light from the workshop and listened to Haran putting the day away.

The reservoir was warm in his chest. Present. Finite. Halved.

But the question was there too — warm in a different way, occupying a different space, a shape that wasn't the shape of loss. *What do you want to build.* Not tonight. Not yet. But the question existed, and the existence of the right question was the beginning of an answer.

His eyes closed. The workshop sounds settled around him — the last of Haran's tidying, then the lamp dimming, then the quiet, then the deeper quiet of a building at rest. Through it all, the warmth stayed. The room held its temperature. The walls held their heat. A hundred invisible pulses, accumulated across a day, releasing slowly into the dark.

He slept. And for the first time since the candle went out, the dark behind his eyelids didn't feel like a countdown.

It felt like a workbench, waiting to be used.
