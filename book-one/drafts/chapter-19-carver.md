# Chapter 19

The door closed behind him and the room was quiet.

Ash stood in the quarters Dorenne had given him—a narrow room off the command post's second corridor, stone walls, a cot, a desk with a single lamp that someone on her staff kept filled with oil. He'd been sleeping here most nights since the earthquake. The walk home took an hour he didn't have. That was the reason he gave. The reason underneath it was harder to name and easier to feel: the room had no mother watching from the window, no father gripping his shoulder, no sister who wasn't there.

He crossed to the desk. Sat down. The chair was wooden, straight-backed, the kind of chair that expected you to work.

He'd brought the attachment from Dorenne's chart—the single sheet of paper, weekly totals, the sum at the bottom that he'd read twice that morning and carried in his head all day like a stone in his shoe. He unfolded it and set it flat on the desk.

The numbers looked different in lamplight. Smaller. More precise. Dorenne's staff had neat handwriting—columns aligned, sums double-checked, the careful work of people who understood that numbers mattered even when they were guesses.

He stared at the sheet. Then he pulled a blank page from the desk drawer—there were always blank pages in this room, Dorenne's office supplied everything—and a pencil, and he began.

* * *

The first week.

He closed his eyes and reached for the memory. The reservoir had been three days old. He hadn't known what it was yet—hadn't known what *he* was yet. Dorenne's examiners had poked and prodded and measured from the outside, building their models, and he'd stood in their testing room and done what they asked.

*Channel into this stone. Now this one. Now hold it. Now release.*

He remembered the heat. Not the channeling itself—that had felt like breathing, like something his body had always known how to do and was only now being permitted to attempt—but the waste. The room had been warm when they started. By the end of the first session, the examiners were pulling at their collars. By the end of the second, someone had opened a window. The stone he'd been channeling into glowed faintly at its edges, residual heat bleeding off in waves.

He'd been running at—what? He thought about it. The examiners' notes had mentioned his efficiency. He hadn't paid attention at the time. It hadn't mattered. The reservoir was an ocean. Who measured the efficiency of an ocean?

But he could reconstruct it now. The output they'd measured against the effects they'd observed. The waste heat. The way ten minutes of his channeling had raised the room temperature by degrees. He'd been converting magical energy to physical work at maybe one part in five. Maybe one in six.

He wrote it down. *Week 1: efficiency ~15-20%.* Generous, probably. The examiners had been kind in their estimates.

What had he done that first week? The testing sessions—four of them, two hours each. Then the demonstrations. Dorenne had brought him to the Council chamber and he'd channeled for the assembly, a display of raw force that had cracked the demonstration stone and drawn gasps from people who'd been channeling their entire lives. Then smaller things—the euphoric, joyful waste of a man who'd never been able to do anything and could suddenly do everything. He'd heated his quarters until the walls sweated. He'd channeled force into a stuck door and shattered the frame. He'd pushed energy into the ground beneath his feet just to feel it spread, just to feel the reservoir *move*, that gorgeous rush of something flowing through him after twenty-three years of stillness.

He hadn't counted any of it. Why would he? The reservoir was bottomless. He'd checked—reached down into it with the clumsy new sense he was developing—and found nothing but depth. No floor. No walls. Just power, in every direction, more than he could comprehend.

He'd been wrong. But he hadn't known that yet.

He wrote the number. The first week's total—rough, built from memory and Dorenne's estimates. The testing sessions alone had cost more than he'd realized, because at fifteen percent efficiency, every unit of work required six units of expenditure. The waste heat that warmed those rooms had come from somewhere. It had come from him.

The number sat on the page. He looked at it.

*That much. In one week. Doing almost nothing.*

He moved on.

* * *

Weeks two and three. The training period.

Dorenne had paired him with her best channeling instructors—established-generation professionals who'd spent their careers refining technique. They'd taught him the basics: how to direct force through contact rather than flooding it. How to match output to task. How to channel into stone without overloading the crystalline structure. How to feel for stress points and fracture lines before applying pressure.

He'd learned fast. His mind had always been the real weapon—the analytical intelligence that had spent twenty years optimizing around constraints, making not-enough into just-barely-enough. Applied to channeling, it was devastating. Within days, his efficiency had climbed. Not to anything a trained professional would respect, but from catastrophic to merely wasteful.

He'd also been practicing on his own. Late nights in the testing room, pushing the reservoir through exercise stones, trying to find the subtlety his instructors demonstrated. He'd crack a stone, replace it, crack the replacement. The waste heat from those sessions had been considerable. Nobody had been tracking it. Nobody had thought to track it. The reservoir was unprecedented. Tracking it was like tracking the tide.

He wrote the numbers. Training sessions—formal, documented, Dorenne's staff timing each one. Practice sessions—undocumented, uncounted, the secret hours of a man intoxicated with his own capability. The joyful experiments—heating stones for fun, pushing force through walls to see how far it would spread, testing the limits of what he could do because for the first time in his life, the question *what can I do?* had an answer that wasn't *nothing*.

His efficiency during this period: maybe thirty percent. Maybe thirty-five on his best days. Improving, but still bleeding more than half his expenditure as waste.

He added the column. The number grew.

* * *

Weeks four and five. The first deployments.

He slowed down. This part mattered.

Dorenne's charts covered this period precisely—her staff had been timing him, measuring outputs, building their external model of his capacity. The eastern district heating grid. The southern quarter water systems. The residential tower reinforcement. The transit shaft. Each deployment documented: date, duration, estimated output, crew chief's notes.

He pulled the numbers from Dorenne's sheet and did the conversion. Output divided by efficiency gave total expenditure. His efficiency by this point had reached maybe forty percent—basic competence, the level most ordinary channelers operated at for daily tasks. But he wasn't doing daily tasks. He was reinforcing infrastructure that had been failing for years, driving force into stone and metal and water systems at a scale that made his instructors uncomfortable.

The heating grid alone. He remembered the eastern district—the cold homes, the woman who'd cried, the child who'd laughed. He'd flooded the lattices with raw power, channeling for hours, the grid blazing to life under his hands. Beautiful. Heroic. The district had been warm for the first time in months.

At forty percent efficiency, that single deployment had cost him—

He did the arithmetic. Wrote the number. Read it.

*That much. For one grid.*

The water systems. The tower. The transit shaft. Each one a genuine crisis, each one a community served, each one a withdrawal from an account he'd never bothered to check. He added them. The column grew.

And the unplanned expenditures. The building on Threadneedle Lane—the one that had drawn Dorenne's gentle correction about the eastern aqueduct. The small repairs, the personal interventions, the moments when he'd passed a failing structure and fixed it because he could. Because it was easy. Because the reservoir was infinite and the need was real and the feeling of being useful was a drug he couldn't stop taking.

Those weren't on Dorenne's charts. He had to estimate them from memory, and memory was imprecise, but the impression was clear: he'd been leaking power constantly, in acts too small to track and too frequent to ignore. A reinforced doorframe here. A sealed crack there. The small, thoughtless generosities of a man who believed his account had no limit.

He added them to the column. Rough. Approximate. Honest.

The total for weeks four and five was larger than he'd expected.

* * *

He set the pencil down. His hand was steady. The numbers were just numbers—marks on paper, arithmetic performed in lamplight by a man who was good at arithmetic. Nothing had changed. The reservoir still sat in his chest, warm and vast. The city still waited outside these walls, needing what he could give.

He picked the pencil up.

Week six. The start of the earthquake response.

He remembered the first tremor. The floor of Dorenne's office bucking under his feet. The sound—not thunder, deeper than thunder, the sound of stone grinding against stone at a scale that made human structures feel like toys. The dust. The screaming.

He'd been deployed within the hour. Dorenne's coordination had been flawless—she'd had contingency plans, response teams, communication channels already in place. She'd pointed him at the worst damage and he'd gone.

The residential building. Three floors pancaked. Eleven people alive in the gap between the second and third floor. He'd planted his hands against the fractured stone and held. Not reinforced—*held*. Every beam, every wall, every cracked joint, sustained by channeled force while the rescue team cut through from the side.

Hours. He'd held that building for hours.

At his efficiency by that point—maybe forty-five percent, improving under the pressure of necessity—the cost of holding a collapsing three-story residential building stable against its own weight and the ongoing aftershocks was—

He calculated. Structural weight. Gravitational force. Lateral load from the aftershocks, transmitted through the foundation, each one requiring a counter-force calibrated to the frequency and amplitude of the tremor. Hours of continuous, maximum-output channeling. No breaks. No rest. The reservoir flowing through him like a river through a narrowing canyon.

The number was enormous.

He wrote it down. Stared at it. Wrote it again, checking his arithmetic.

Eleven lives. The number didn't change.

He kept going.

The dam. The young woman—his age, her pool failing, the water behind her winning. He'd arrived and added his force to hers, sealing the rupture, holding back a volume of water that would have flooded an entire district. The channeling had been crude—brute force, no finesse, power dumped into stone and water with the subtlety of a sledgehammer. But it had worked. The dam held. The district stayed dry. The young woman had looked at him with something that wasn't quite gratitude and wasn't quite grief.

The cost. He calculated the force required to hold a compromised dam against the hydrostatic pressure of a full aqueduct reservoir. The aftershock loading. The duration. He'd been there for—how long? Three hours? Four?

He wrote the number.

The retaining walls. Three of them, in three different districts, each one threatening to release a hillside onto the buildings below. He'd reinforced them in sequence, moving from site to site, channeling at maximum output, the reservoir flowing and flowing and flowing.

Bridge reinforcements. Six bridges along evacuation routes, each one carrying the weight of hundreds of fleeing residents. He'd channeled stability into their foundations while people crossed above him, their footsteps vibrating through the stone he was holding together.

Rubble clearance. Streets blocked by collapsed buildings, rescue teams unable to reach survivors. He'd moved stone—tons of it—channeling kinetic force through contact, shoving debris aside, cracking obstacles, creating paths through wreckage with raw power.

Three days. He'd worked for three days with almost no sleep. The reservoir had carried no physical cost—he didn't tire from channeling the way a normal person tired from their pool—but the expenditure had been continuous, massive, the full force of the Wellspring applied to a disaster that demanded everything he had.

He added the earthquake column.

His hand paused over the total.

The number sat on the page. He read it once. Twice. His jaw tightened.

*That can't be right.*

He went back through the arithmetic. The residential building. The dam. The retaining walls. The bridges. The rubble clearance. The dozens of smaller interventions—cracked water mains sealed, heating systems restarted, structural assessments performed through channeled contact. Three days of continuous output at maximum capacity, at an efficiency that was good for a novice and catastrophic for the budget he hadn't known he was spending.

The number didn't change.

The earthquake—his finest hour, the thing that made him a hero, the days that proved he was meant for something—had consumed more of the reservoir than everything else combined.

He set the pencil down. Picked it up again.

* * *

Weeks seven and eight. Post-earthquake reconstruction.

Dorenne's deployment schedule. Three districts per week in the early phase, four in the second. The retaining wall he'd reinforced yesterday. The tinkerer workshop. The lintel on Colter Street. The small repairs he'd made in the district, the ones Dorenne had tallied on her folded paper.

His efficiency was better now. Fifty percent, maybe fifty-five. Competent range. The improvement meant each unit of work cost less—but the work itself had intensified. Dorenne's reconstruction plan called for deep structural reinforcement, the kind of foundation-level channeling that cost the most and lasted the longest. The efficiency gains were real. They didn't keep pace with the increasing demands.

He wrote the numbers. Added the column. The total climbed.

Then he drew a line under the final week and added everything.

The sum of eight weeks. Every test, every training session, every deployment, every joyful experiment, every unplanned repair, every act of heroism and every act of waste. The reservoir's total output since the day it activated, expressed in a number that sat on the page and refused to be anything other than what it was.

He looked at it.

Then he turned to the other calculation. The one he'd been building toward since he sat down. The one the first calculation existed to serve.

*How much is left?*

* * *

The examiners had estimated his reservoir at activation. Their methods were crude—they couldn't see inside him, couldn't measure the reservoir directly, could only extrapolate from the scale of his outputs and the rate of his apparent depletion. But they were careful people, and their estimate had been large enough to seem meaningless. A number so vast it registered as infinity.

Fifty gigajoules. That was their best guess. Fifty billion kilojoules—more than an elder's daily pool multiplied by five thousand. More than the annual output of a hundred channelers working full days. A reservoir that should have lasted decades of continuous use, centuries of careful stewardship.

He wrote it at the top of a fresh page. The starting balance. The account he'd inherited from whatever geological or magical process had packed that much energy into a single human body.

Below it, he wrote the sum from his calculation. The total expenditure. Eight weeks.

He subtracted.

The remainder sat on the page. He read it. The lamplight flickered—the oil was burning low, the wick needed trimming—and the number shifted in the unsteady light, but it didn't change.

Roughly half.

Half of everything he would ever have. Gone. In eight weeks.

He sat with it. The chair was hard. The room was cold—he hadn't heated it tonight, hadn't bothered, and now the absence of warmth felt different than it had before. Not an oversight. A cost.

Half.

He thought about what that meant. Not emotionally—he couldn't afford emotion yet, not while the arithmetic still had work to do. Practically. Mechanically. The way his mind had always worked, the analytical intelligence that had spent twenty years making not-enough into barely-enough. The same mind, applied to a new constraint.

He'd started with fifty gigajoules. He'd spent roughly twenty-five. He had roughly twenty-five remaining.

At the deployment rate Dorenne had planned—the reconstruction schedule stretching across her charts, weeks thirteen through twenty, twenty-one through twenty-eight, the columns dense with hash marks and rising estimated costs—how long would twenty-five gigajoules last?

He pulled her sheet toward him and worked forward. The planned deployments were getting more intensive as the reconstruction moved from surface repairs to deep infrastructure. Retaining walls. Terrace anchoring. Foundation-level reinforcement. The costs per deployment were rising even as his efficiency improved.

He built the projection. Week by week, column by column, applying his improving efficiency against Dorenne's escalating demands. He was generous with his own improvement—assumed he'd reach sixty percent efficiency within a month, seventy within three. Skilled range. Years of dedicated practice compressed into weeks by the scale of his daily work. The most optimistic trajectory a novice could follow.

Even with the generous assumptions, the curve bent toward zero.

He calculated the intersection. The week when projected cumulative expenditure would equal the remaining reservoir. The week when the account would empty.

The number was not large. It was not distant. It was not "someday" or "eventually" or "in the fullness of time." It was a date he could find on a calendar. A season he could name. A morning he would wake up and reach for the reservoir and find nothing there.

He wrote it down. Stared at it. The pencil was still in his hand. His fingers had tightened around it until the wood creaked.

He set it down carefully, precisely, the way you set down something you might break.

* * *

The early waste.

This was the part that hurt. Not the earthquake—the earthquake was heroism, was necessity, was the thing he would do again without hesitation because eleven people were alive and a dam was holding and a city was standing. The earthquake cost what it cost because the earthquake demanded what it demanded, and he would pay that price a hundred times.

But the first weeks. The joyful, careless, ecstatic waste of a man who'd been empty his whole life and was suddenly full. The heated rooms. The shattered door frame. The cracked exercise stones. The practice sessions—hours of them, pouring power through test materials just to feel it flow, the narcotic rush of capability after twenty-three years of nothing. The demonstrations. The flourishes.

He looked at the numbers. Weeks one through three. The period of maximum waste, minimum efficiency, pure exhilaration.

At his current efficiency—fifty, fifty-five percent—the same training, the same demonstrations, the same learning curve would have cost roughly a third of what it actually cost. The difference between what he'd spent and what he would have spent if he'd known, if he'd been careful, if he'd treated the reservoir as the finite resource it was instead of the infinite ocean he'd believed it to be—

The difference was a number. He calculated it. Wrote it down.

It was enough to power the eastern district heating grid for months. Enough to reinforce a dozen retaining walls. Enough to hold a building during an earthquake.

Gone. Bled off as waste heat in testing rooms and private quarters and the streets of a city where he'd pushed force through the ground just to feel the ground push back.

He'd been rich and stupid. The oldest story in the world.

His throat tightened. He swallowed against it and kept his eyes on the page.

* * *

He sat for a long time without writing. The lamp burned lower. The shadows in the room deepened, the corners retreating into dark, and the circle of light around the desk contracted until it held only the pages and his hands and the pencil lying where he'd set it down.

The reservoir sat in his chest. He could feel it—feel its edges now with a clarity that the arithmetic had sharpened into something undeniable. It was there. It was warm. It was vast by any measure that wasn't his own.

And it was half empty.

Every deployment he'd completed—every retaining wall, every water system, every bridge and transit shaft and heating grid—had been real. The work was good. The communities he'd helped were warmer, safer, more stable because of what he'd given. The eleven people pulled from the rubble were alive. The dam held. The bridges stood.

He didn't regret any of it. The arithmetic didn't ask him to regret it. The arithmetic only asked him to see it—to see the cost alongside the value, the withdrawal alongside the deposit, the shape of what remained beside the record of what was gone.

He'd do the earthquake again. Every hour of it. Every kilowatt dumped into failing stone, every bridge held, every wall sustained, every act of brute-force heroism that had burned through power he would never recover. He'd do it because eleven people were alive and because the alternative was letting them die, and no amount of arithmetic could make that equation balance differently.

But the reservoir had felt bottomless because he'd never bothered to check. He'd reached down into it in those first intoxicating days and felt nothing but depth, and he'd taken that feeling for truth. The depth had been real. The bottomlessness had been an illusion—the illusion of a man standing at the surface of a deep lake, looking down into dark water, mistaking his inability to see the bottom for the bottom's absence.

The bottom was there. It had always been there. And he was closer to it now than he was to the surface.

*Half.*

The thought sat in his chest beside the reservoir, beside the edges, beside the warmth that was still vast and still present and still, despite everything, enough to make him feel like more than he'd been. He held both things—the warmth and the number—and neither one cancelled the other.

* * *

He picked up the pencil.

One more calculation. His mind wouldn't let it go.

The world's pools. The generational decline that everyone knew about and nobody wanted to quantify. Elders at ten thousand kilojoules daily. Established generation at five thousand. Middle at twenty-five hundred. Young adults—his generation, Maren's generation—at a thousand.

The decline was roughly halving each generation. Not precisely—the curve was irregular, influenced by factors nobody fully understood—but the trend was clear. Each generation born with less than the one before. Each generation doing more with less, or doing less, or doing without.

He wrote the numbers in a column. The generational pools. The decline rate. The projection forward.

If the trend continued—and nothing in the historical record suggested it wouldn't—the generation after his would have roughly five hundred kilojoules daily. The one after that, maybe two hundred and fifty. Within three or four generations, the daily pool would be functionally negligible. Not zero—maybe never quite zero—but so small that the infrastructure built for a world of ten-thousand-kilojoule channelers would be unmaintainable. The transit shafts would stop. The heating grids would fail. The grand architecture of Solathis—the bridges and towers and terraces raised by the ancients, maintained by elders, already crumbling under the care of the middle generation—would decay beyond repair.

The world was doing what his reservoir was doing. Draining. The difference was scale. The difference was time.

His reservoir would empty in months. The world's would empty in generations. But the curve was the same. The mathematics were the same. The end was the same.

He wrote the projection. The year when the generational pool would drop below the maintenance threshold for Solathis's critical infrastructure. The year when the city's grand buildings would begin to fail not from neglect but from simple incapacity—no one alive with enough power to maintain them, no matter how skilled, no matter how dedicated.

The number was not distant. Not in the way people wanted it to be distant. Two generations, maybe three. A grandchild's lifetime. A future that someone alive today would live to see.

He stared at the page. The lamplight trembled. The two projections sat side by side—his personal countdown, the world's generational one. Same shape. Same trajectory. Different timescales.

And then—

Maren.

* * *

The thought arrived without invitation. He hadn't been thinking about her. He'd been thinking about numbers, about projections, about the cold mechanics of decline measured in kilojoules and generations. But the numbers pointed at her the way an arrow points at its target, and his mind followed.

Maren's pool. Young adult generation. Roughly a thousand kilojoules daily, and she was on the weak end of that range—he'd heard her say it once, years ago, in the way she said difficult things: flatly, without emphasis, as if reporting the weather. *My pool's on the low side. It's fine. I manage.*

She managed. She always managed. She managed with the discipline and precision that defined her—the efficiency that came from a lifetime of practice, of making every kilojoule count, of knowing exactly how much she had and exactly how much each task cost and exactly how far she could stretch a budget that had never been generous.

His mother's voice, from Chapter 18, in the kitchen with the tin kettle: *She's been picking up extra shifts. Maintenance work, the middle districts. They're shorthanded since the earthquake.*

Maintenance shifts. Channeling work—structural reinforcement, system repair, the daily upkeep that kept the city's infrastructure functional. Work that used her pool. Work that cost her.

How much? He did the math without meaning to, the way his mind always did the math, the analytical engine running whether he wanted it to or not.

A maintenance shift in the middle districts. Structural assessment, minor repairs, system checks. Four to six hours of intermittent channeling. At Maren's efficiency—she was skilled, genuinely skilled, years of practice compressed into technique that wasted almost nothing—the energy cost would be four to five hundred kilojoules. Half her daily pool. More than half on the bad days, when the damage was worse or the work lasted longer or the systems she was maintaining were older and hungrier.

She was spending half her daily budget on a single shift. Then going home—to their parents' house, to the kitchen where their mother kept the tin kettle—and using what remained to heat water, warm a room, handle the small channeling tasks that structured daily life. The tasks that elders did without thinking and that young adults budgeted for with the precision of accountants.

And the next day, she'd wake up. Her pool would recharge—a thousand kilojoules, maybe a little less, the same amount she'd woken up with yesterday and the day before and every day of her life. She'd go to work. She'd spend half of it. She'd come home. She'd budget the rest.

The same amount. Every day. No growth, no increase, no sudden windfall. The pool she was born with was the pool she would die with. The only variable was skill—how efficiently she used what she had. And she was already skilled. She was already efficient. She was already squeezing every fraction of utility from every kilojoule, because she'd never had the luxury of waste.

She'd never heated a room just to feel the warmth spread. She'd never shattered a door frame for the thrill of it. She'd never poured power through exercise stones in the middle of the night, drunk on capability, burning through energy she'd never measured because measuring it would have meant admitting it was finite.

She'd always known it was finite. She'd known since childhood—since the first time she'd run dry before dinner, since the first time she'd had to choose between warming her hands and heating the kettle, since the first time an elder had done in seconds what took her an hour. She'd grown up inside the math. The math was her life.

He stared at the page. The numbers he'd written—his reservoir countdown, the world's generational curve—sat on the paper in lamplight, and between them, a third calculation took shape. Not one he wrote down. One he held.

Maren's pool: a thousand kilojoules daily, declining over years at a rate too slow to see month-to-month but measurable across a decade. Her efficiency: skilled, maybe seventy-five percent, wrung from every day of practice since she was old enough to channel. Her career expenditure: four to five hundred kilojoules per shift, five or six shifts per week, fifty weeks per year.

The trajectory was the same as his. Not in speed—hers stretched across years where his compressed into months—but in shape. The same curve. The same direction. The same destination.

She was doing in years what he was doing in weeks.

He'd been sitting in this room calculating his own decline as though it were unique. As though he were the only person in the world facing the arithmetic of a finite resource spent against infinite need. As though the shock of discovering that power had limits was a revelation reserved for the man with the biggest reservoir.

It wasn't. It was Maren's life. It had always been Maren's life.

She'd never stood at the surface of an ocean and mistaken its depth for endlessness. She'd always been standing in a pond—shallow enough to see the bottom, small enough to feel the edges, contained enough that every cup she drew was visible as a loss. And she'd never once complained about it. She'd never raged against the injustice of it, never mourned the pool she should have been born with, never treated her finitude as a tragedy.

She'd managed. She'd been disciplined. She'd built a career and a skill set and a life inside the constraints of a budget that most people in her parents' generation would have found crippling. She'd done it without the luxury of waste, without the euphoria of abundance, without a single day in her life when the answer to *how much can I do?* was *as much as I want.*

And he'd walked past her. For eight weeks—for his entire life before that, but especially for these eight weeks—he'd walked past her. He'd lived in the same house, eaten at the same table, drunk tea from the same chipped cup, and never once asked what her day cost her. Never once calculated the price of her discipline. Never once looked at the math of her life and recognized it as a version of the math he was only now discovering in his own.

His chest hurt. Not the reservoir—the reservoir sat in its diminished warmth, oblivious to what his mind was doing with the numbers. The hurt was somewhere else. Somewhere older. The place where the person he'd been before the Wellspring still lived, the person who'd spent twenty-three years watching other people do what he couldn't—that person recognized what he was seeing. That person had always known what finitude felt like. He'd just forgotten, in the flood of the Wellspring, that finitude was the human condition, not the exception.

* * *

He thought about the maintenance crews. The ones Maren was covering for. Shorthanded since the earthquake—because the earthquake had broken things faster than the crews could fix them, because the crews were made up of young adults with a thousand kilojoules a day and middle-generation workers with twenty-five hundred, and the damage demanded elder-scale outputs that no one on those crews could provide.

No one except him. He'd been providing. Three districts per week, four in the second phase. Dorenne's deployment schedule, his reservoir filling the gap between what the city needed and what its channelers could give.

But the gap wasn't new. The gap had existed before the earthquake—before the Wellspring, before any of it. The gap was the decline itself, measured in weakening pools and aging infrastructure and the slow accumulation of deferred maintenance that defined the lower districts. The earthquake had widened the gap. He'd been filling it with power that was, it turned out, just as finite as everyone else's.

The crews would still be shorthanded when his reservoir was empty. The infrastructure would still be failing. The young adults on the maintenance shifts would still be spending half their daily pools on work that elders once did as a side task. The math didn't change because he'd shown up with fifty gigajoules and burned through half of it in two months. The math was the math. The decline was the decline. He'd bought time. That was all.

He looked at the page. The numbers. His personal countdown. The world's curve. Maren's invisible, unexamined, lifelong arithmetic.

The same math. The same finitude. The same floor, seen from different heights.

He'd been treating his decline as a crisis. A shock. An emergency that demanded panic, that warranted sitting alone in a room at night doing arithmetic by lamplight, calculating the end of something he'd possessed for eight weeks as though it were the loss of a limb.

Maren had been living in the crisis her whole life. Every young channeler had. Every maintenance worker spending half their pool on a single shift. Every healer rationing their energy across patients, choosing who to treat fully and who to patch and who to send home with instructions to rest because there wasn't enough to go around. Every parent in the lower districts choosing between heating the house and healing a child's scraped knee, performing the daily triage of insufficiency that was invisible to anyone who'd never had to do it.

He'd done it. For twenty-three years, he'd done it—the triage of having nothing, the daily choreography of a person who couldn't channel in a world built for channelers. He'd taken stairs while others rode lifts. He'd struck flint while others channeled heat. He'd stood at interfaces and felt nothing and smiled and found another way.

And then the Wellspring had opened, and he'd forgotten all of it. Forgotten what finitude felt like. Forgotten that most people's lives were built on the math he was only now rediscovering. Forgotten that the woman sitting across from him at dinner, the woman who let her own news go unheard, the woman who sealed cracks in cups with casual precision and picked up extra shifts to cover for shorthanded crews—that woman had been doing his math for twenty years, quietly, without anyone calculating it for her.

*She never had the luxury of thinking the well was infinite.*

He pressed his palms together. The reservoir hummed between them—warm, present, halved. He held the feeling and let it be what it was. Not an ocean. Not a bottomless pool. A resource. Large, finite, diminishing. Real.

The lamp guttered. The oil was nearly spent.

He looked at the pages on the desk. The columns of numbers, the projections, the arithmetic of a man doing what he should have done on the first day—what Haran had asked him to do, in different words, months ago. *How much did that cost you?*

He'd shrugged the question off. Haran had let him. That was Haran's way—ask the question, let the silence work, trust that the answer would come when it was ready. The answer had come. It had come in numbers, in lamplight, in the quiet of a room where no one could see his face.

He didn't move the pages. He didn't fold them or hide them or file them away. He left them on the desk, the numbers facing up, the arithmetic visible to anyone who opened the door. They were facts. They would still be facts in the morning.

He stood. Crossed to the cot. Lay down without undressing. The ceiling above him was channeled stone—reinforced, stable, the work of someone who'd spent their pool building something that would last. The stone was still standing. The person was gone. He'd thought this before, in this room, in this cot. The thought had a different weight now.

The reservoir sat in his chest. Half of what it had been. All of what remained.

He closed his eyes. Behind them, in the dark, the numbers waited. His. Maren's. The world's. The same numbers, at different scales, describing the same curve, approaching the same floor.

Sleep came slowly. When it came, it carried him nowhere.
