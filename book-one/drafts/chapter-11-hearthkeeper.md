# Chapter 11

The kitchen smelled like cardamom and rendered fat, and his mother was at the stove.

Ash stood in the doorway of the new apartment and watched her work. She had her back to him, one hand stirring something in the deep pot, the other resting on the counter beside the dented copper kettle she'd carried from the old place. The cane was propped against the wall within reach, but she wasn't using it — she'd planted herself at the stove with the rooted stability of a woman who had decided that this, at least, was still her territory.

"You're early," she said without turning around.

"The deployment finished ahead of schedule. Dorenne didn't have anything else for—"

"Sit down. You look thin."

He wasn't thin. The Wellspring carried no bodily cost — he could channel for hours without losing weight or strength, the energy flowing from the reservoir without touching his own metabolism. But his mother had spent twenty-three years reading his body for signs of damage, and the habit had outlived the condition that created it.

He sat. The kitchen table was the same one from the old apartment — scarred wood, one leg shimmed with a folded piece of leather his father had cut years ago. It didn't match the new kitchen. Nothing his mother had brought matched the new kitchen. The dented kettle on the smooth counter. The old spice tin beside the integrated channeling interface. The cracked ceramic bowl — the one she'd salvaged from the rubble — sitting on the windowsill where it caught the morning light, holding nothing, serving no function except the one she'd assigned it: proof that she could keep things whole by refusing to throw them away.

She set a plate in front of him. Rice, stewed lamb, a salad of pickled greens that she dressed with oil and the sour dried berries she bought from the woman at the lower market — the lower market, two districts down, even though the same berries were available three streets away. She still walked to the lower market. She still climbed the extra stairs. She still bought from the woman who'd known her when they lived in the lower-middle band, before the Wellspring, before the new apartment, before everything changed.

"Eat," she said.

He ate. The lamb was good — slow-cooked, the meat falling apart, flavored with the cardamom and the particular blend of spices she kept in the old tin. She cooked the way she'd always cooked: channeling the stove's interface with one hand and stirring with the other, her pool flowing into the heating element with the unconscious efficiency of a woman who'd been warming things her entire life. She didn't need the fire grate anymore. The new stove didn't have one. But she cooked as though the heat required tending — adjusting, checking, pressing her palm flat against the interface every few minutes as if the warmth might fade if she stopped paying attention.

It wouldn't. The new building's lattice held the heat all day, maintained by a crew whose pools were strong enough to keep the system running without interruption. His mother's channeling was a redundancy. But she did it anyway, the same way she still checked the locks before bed even though the building had channeled security, the same way she still laid kindling in the iron box she kept beside the new stove even though there was no fire grate to burn it in.

"How was the southern quarter?" she asked. She was standing at the counter now, wiping down the surface that was already clean. Her movements had a quality he noticed without examining — a precision that bordered on restlessness, the hands of a woman who needed to be doing something and had run out of things to do.

"Good. The water pressure's holding. Torek says the relay stations are maintaining charge better than—"

"You've been there three times this week."

"The system needs recharging. It dissipates—"

"I know it dissipates. You told me. You told me Tuesday." She folded the cloth and set it beside the kettle, her fingers lingering on the copper in a way that might have been absent-minded, might have been deliberate. "I'm asking why it has to be you. Every time. Before dawn."

"Because I'm the only one who can charge it fast enough to—"

"That woman works you too hard."

The words arrived with the flat certainty of a verdict already reached. His mother turned to face him, and her expression was the one he'd grown up with — the one that meant she had decided something was wrong and was preparing to stand between him and whatever it was. For twenty-three years, the thing she'd stood between him and had been the world itself: the city that had no place for a magicless boy, the systems that excluded him, the small cruelties of a society built on channeling that he couldn't do. She had been magnificent at it. Fierce and tireless and unflinching, the kind of mother who walked into a school administrator's office and didn't leave until the policy changed, who argued with healers and bureaucrats and neighbors and anyone else who tried to tell her that her son's situation was just the way things were.

Now the thing she wanted to stand between him and was Dorenne. And the expression on her face — the set jaw, the bright eyes, the particular angle of her shoulders when she was bracing for a fight — was the same. Exactly the same. As though nothing had changed.

"She's not working me too hard, Mum. I'm fine. The reservoir—"

"I don't care about the reservoir. I care about you. You don't eat breakfast. You leave before dawn. You come home smelling like basement stone and you're asleep before the second cup of tea." She picked up the cloth again, wrung it, set it down. "You need rest. You need meals. You need someone to—"

"I'm the most powerful channeler in the city."

He said it gently. Not to boast — to explain. But the words landed in the kitchen like something dropped, and his mother's hands went still on the counter.

"I know what you are," she said. Her voice was quiet in a way that was worse than volume. "I knew what you were before."

A silence moved between them. The stove ticked — the interface cooling from his mother's last pulse, the metal contracting with a small, precise sound that was different from the old stove, different from the creak and settle of iron over embers. The new stove didn't creak. The new kitchen didn't settle. Everything in the new apartment worked exactly as designed, maintained by systems that didn't need her, in a building that didn't need her, for a son who didn't need her.

"The Director came by yesterday," she said. She'd turned back to the counter, her hands finding the cloth again. "While you were at the pumping station. She brought papers."

"What papers?"

"A schedule. Deployment something. She wanted you to review it before tomorrow's briefing."

"She could have sent a message through the resonance shelf—"

"She came here. To this apartment. To drop off papers for my son." His mother's voice was level, controlled, the tone of a woman choosing her words with the care of someone defusing something. "I told her you'd look at them when you got home. She said it was time-sensitive. I said my son would look at them when he got home. She smiled at me."

Something about the way she said *smiled*. Not describing an expression — indicting one.

"Mum—"

"That woman smiles the way a door closes, Ashlyn. Politely. Completely."

He set down his fork. The lamb was half-eaten. His mother was standing at the counter with the cloth in her hands and her back very straight and the cane within reach but unused, as though admitting she needed it would weaken the position she was building.

"She's not what you think she is," he said.

"I think she's someone who sees what you can do and wants to make sure you keep doing it for her."

"For the city."

"For her." His mother turned. Her eyes were bright — not with tears, with the particular clarity that came when she was frightened and had decided to be angry about it instead. "I've been watching women like her my entire life. Women who know exactly what they're doing and call it something generous. She gave you a job. She gave us this apartment. She gives and gives and the giving is so careful that by the time you notice the cost, you've already agreed to pay it."

The kitchen was warm. The lattice in the walls hummed. Outside, the evening light was fading from the clean facades of the buildings across the street, and the transit lift at the corner was running, carrying people home from work, the sound of the platform's ascent a low, rhythmic pulse that vibrated through the stone.

"She gave me a chance when no one else would," Ash said. "Before the Wellspring. Before any of this. She saw me when—"

"I saw you. I saw you first. I saw you every day for twenty-three years."

Her voice cracked on the last word. Not dramatically — a small fracture, the sound of something under pressure that had held and held and was not breaking now but was showing where it would break if the pressure continued. She turned away. The cloth was in her hands and she wrung it — not wiping anything, just wringing, the tendons in her forearms standing out, the motion repetitive and purposeless and very controlled.

He stood up. He went to her. He put his hand on her shoulder and she didn't turn but she leaned — a fraction, a slight shifting of weight that pressed her shoulder against his palm, the way the old building's walls had leaned against each other in the lower-middle band, the mutual support of structures that couldn't stand alone.

"I know you did," he said.

She pressed her hand over his. Her palm was warm from the channeling, warm from the stove, warm from twenty-five years of pressing her hands against cold things and making them livable. She held his hand against her shoulder and she breathed, and the breath was careful, and the kitchen was quiet except for the tick of the stove and the hum of the lattice and the distant pulse of the transit lift carrying people who had nothing to do with them up through the levels of a city that was working, tonight, because of her son.

"Just eat your dinner," she said.

He went back to the table. He ate.

She stood at the counter with her back to him and wiped down the surface that was already clean.

* * *

Dorenne came to the apartment on a Thirdday afternoon.

Ash wasn't expecting her. He was in the main room, reviewing deployment reports — the latest round of lattice recharge data from the eastern district, the structural assessment of a retaining wall in the northern quarter that Harenn's team had flagged for follow-up. The work had become routine in the way that eating had become routine: necessary, regular, the rhythm of a life that had found its shape.

The knock was two quick raps, precise, the knock of a person who knew exactly how much sound was required to get attention and used exactly that amount. He opened the door.

Dorenne stood in the corridor in her half-cloak, the Infrastructure seal on the collar, a leather folio under her arm. Behind her, the hallway was empty — she'd come alone, no staff, which meant this was personal. Or strategic enough to look personal. With Dorenne, the distinction was architectural — you could see the structure from the outside but not tell which rooms were which.

"I brought the survey results from the northern quarter," she said. "And a question about Thursday's schedule. May I come in?"

"Of course."

She stepped inside. She moved through the apartment the way she moved through every space — with the economical awareness of a person who processed rooms for information before she processed them for comfort. Her eyes noted the deployment reports on the table, the cup of tea beside them, the dented kettle on the kitchen counter. She noted everything and filed it, and the filing was so natural it didn't register as observation. It registered as presence.

"Tea?" he asked.

"If your mother isn't—" She glanced toward the kitchen, the corridor that led to the bedrooms. A pause so brief it barely existed.

"She's at the market. My father's working."

Dorenne's expression didn't change, which was itself information. She sat at the table and opened the folio, and they were reviewing the northern quarter survey when his mother came through the front door.

The sound was the familiar sequence: the latch, the rustle of the heavy bag, the tap of the cane, the particular rhythm of a woman who arrived at every threshold as though expecting to be needed. The bag held vegetables and the berries from the lower market, and his mother was already talking as she came through — "Ashlyn, the woman at the stall says the next shipment of saffron won't—"

She stopped.

The kitchen table was small. The apartment was large but the kitchen was arranged around the table the way the old kitchen had been, because his mother had configured the space to match the shape she knew, and the shape she knew put the table at the center. Dorenne was sitting at the table with the folio open and a cup of tea and the deployment reports spread between them, and Ash was sitting across from her, and the scene was — he could see it through his mother's eyes for just a moment, the involuntary act of translation that happened when two versions of your life occupied the same room — the scene was a meeting. Professional. The kind of thing that happened at the Hall, in offices, in rooms where people who ran things sat and decided what would happen next.

It was happening at her kitchen table.

"Nessa." Dorenne stood. The motion was smooth — not abrupt, not deferential, the practiced rise of a woman who understood the social mechanics of another woman's home. "I apologize for the intrusion. I had survey results that needed Ash's review, and I thought it would save him a trip to the Hall."

His mother set the bag on the counter. "You're always welcome, Director."

The words were correct. The tone was correct. Everything about his mother's response was correct in the way that a precisely executed piece of channeling was correct — efficient, controlled, without a drop of energy wasted on warmth.

"I was just telling Ash about the northern quarter assessment. The retaining wall situation is—"

"I'm sure it's urgent." His mother began unpacking the bag. Vegetables on the counter, one by one, her hands moving with the deliberate pace of a woman who was not going to be hurried by the presence of someone important. "Everything is urgent."

"Mum," Ash said.

"I'm not interfering. I'm unpacking groceries." She set the berries beside the kettle. "Please. Continue your meeting."

Dorenne sat back down. She returned to the survey with the composure of a person who had navigated more fraught political situations than a mother's kitchen, and her composure was — Ash could see this, could feel the mechanics of it — both genuine and calculated. She was giving his mother space. She was also not leaving.

"The retaining wall," Dorenne said, turning a page in the folio. "Harenn's team has assessed the eastern face. The channeled reinforcement has degraded to roughly thirty percent of its original strength. They can maintain the current state with their crew, but to restore it fully, they'll need—"

"How long will that take?" his mother said.

The question came from the counter. Not directed at Dorenne, exactly — directed at the space between them, the way a stone dropped into a conversation falls without a target.

Dorenne looked up. "The restoration?"

"How long will my son need to stand in a basement pressing his hands against stone. For that wall."

"The estimate is six to eight hours of sustained channeling—"

"Six to eight hours." His mother turned from the counter. The cane was at her side but she wasn't leaning on it. She was standing the way she stood when she meant something. "He was at the pumping station for ten hours on Tuesday. He was at the eastern district for seven hours on Firstday. When does he rest?"

"The Wellspring doesn't carry physical fatigue the way normal channeling—"

"I'm not asking about the Wellspring. I'm asking about my son."

The kitchen was very quiet. The stove ticked. The lattice hummed. Outside, the evening light was settling over the clean buildings and the functioning transit lift and the broad street where everything worked.

Dorenne set down the folio. She folded her hands over it — the same posture she used in Council meetings, the composed, attention-giving posture of a woman who was about to be very careful about what she said.

"Nessa. I understand your concern. I share it. Ash's wellbeing is—"

"My son is not a deployment, Director."

"No one is suggesting—"

"Every time you come to this apartment, you bring paper. Schedules. Reports. You come to his home and you bring work." His mother's voice was even and clear and carried the particular steadiness of a woman who had been preparing for this conversation without knowing she was preparing for it. "I have watched my son work for you since before any of this happened. I watched him climb forty flights of stairs to carry your papers. I watched him come home exhausted from running your errands with nothing but a salary and the hope that it meant something. And now—"

"Mum." Ash stood. His chair scraped against the stone floor, the sound sharp in the warm kitchen. "Stop."

His mother looked at him. Her face was the face from his childhood — the fierce protector, the woman who had walked into administrative offices and argued with healers and stood between her son and every system that told him he was less. The face that meant: I am doing this because I love you, and I will not stop, and you cannot make me stop.

But something in it was different now. Something uncertain lived behind the fierceness — a question she couldn't ask, a ground that had shifted under her and left her standing in the same posture on unfamiliar terrain. She was doing what she had always done. And for the first time, it wasn't working.

"Director Kharren is here because I invited her to review the deployment schedule," Ash said. The words were measured and calm and cost him something he could feel in his jaw — the tension of holding two people he loved at the wrong distance, one too close and one too far and both of them looking at him to choose. "The work I do is important. The city needs—"

"I know what the city needs." His mother's voice had gone quiet. The dangerous quiet, the one that preceded either tears or the kind of silence that lasted for days. "I'm asking what you need."

Dorenne was watching. Not the way she watched in Council meetings — not the strategic observation, the political calculation. She was watching the way a person watches something they recognize but have decided not to participate in. Her hands were folded. Her expression was still.

"The northern quarter assessment is time-sensitive," Ash said. He heard himself say it. He heard the choice being made in the words — not the content but the fact of them, the act of answering Dorenne's agenda instead of his mother's question. "We should finish reviewing it."

His mother stood at the counter. The cane was beside her. The bag of vegetables was half-unpacked, the berries sitting beside the kettle, the sour dried berries from the lower market where she still walked because the woman there had known her before.

She picked up the cane. She walked to the corridor that led to the bedrooms, and the tap of the cane on the stone floor was steady and precise and unhurried. She did not look back. She did not slam the door. She closed it behind her with the careful, complete click of a latch catching in a frame that hung true.

Dorenne waited three breaths. Then she opened the folio to the northern quarter assessment, and they continued.

The deployment review took another forty minutes. They discussed the retaining wall, the structural crew's recommendations, the schedule for the following week. Dorenne was thorough and professional and at no point did she mention what had happened, the way a surgeon doesn't mention the weather during an operation — not because it isn't there, but because acknowledging it would mean pausing, and pausing wasn't something the work allowed.

At the door, as she was leaving, she stopped. "Your mother loves you very much."

"I know."

"She doesn't understand what you're capable of. That's not her fault. She's calibrated for a version of you that doesn't exist anymore." Dorenne's voice was kind. The kindness was real — Ash could hear it, could feel the genuine warmth beneath the words. "The work you're doing matters. The people you're helping — the five hundred families in the eastern district, the southern quarter, the transit system. Those are real. Your mother's worry is real too, but it belongs to a different problem. The problem of who you used to be."

She touched his arm — a brief, deliberate gesture, the kind of contact that Dorenne used sparingly and always with purpose. "You've outgrown something. That's not something to apologize for."

She left. The corridor was quiet. The door closed behind her with the soft click of a woman who knew how to leave rooms.

Ash stood in the doorway. In the kitchen, the vegetables were half-unpacked on the counter. The berries sat beside the dented kettle. The deployment reports were spread on the table where his mother ate breakfast every morning.

He gathered the reports and stacked them neatly. He unpacked the rest of the vegetables and put them where his mother kept them — the greens in the cool box, the root vegetables in the lower cabinet, the berries in the small jar she used for them, the one with the chip on the rim that she'd brought from the old apartment.

He put the kettle on. He made two cups of tea.

He knocked on the bedroom door. "Mum. Tea's ready."

A pause. The sound of movement — the creak of the bed frame, the tap of the cane. The door opened. His mother stood there with her face composed and her eyes dry and the cane held in a grip that was tighter than the cane required.

"I unpacked the groceries," he said.

She looked at him. She looked at the cup of tea in his hand. She took it, and her fingers brushed his, and the touch was warm, and neither of them said anything else about it.

They drank their tea at the kitchen table. The deployment reports were gone — put away, out of sight. His mother held her cup with both hands and looked at the window and the light was different now, the evening deepening into the blues and grays that meant the lattice would take over from the sun, the building's crew maintaining the warmth through the night without anyone asking them to.

"The berries were smaller this week," she said. "The woman says the harvest was poor. Something about the soil in the eastern valleys — the channeling that used to condition the farmland isn't reaching as far."

"I could look into it. If the agricultural lattice is similar to the heating systems, maybe—"

"Ash." She set down her cup. "Not everything is a deployment."

He closed his mouth. She drank her tea. The kitchen was quiet, and the warmth in the walls had nothing to do with either of them.

* * *

His father came home at dusk, sawdust in his hair and the smell of cut wood on his clothes.

Ash was at the table with the deployment schedule — he'd taken the reports back out after his mother had gone to the bedroom to rest, the cane leaning against the nightstand, the door closed with the deliberate softness she used when she wanted everyone to know she was being quiet. His father came through the door in the unhurried way he always entered — coat on the hook, boots beside the mat, hands washed at the basin. The routine was identical to the old apartment, performed in the new space with the same gestures, as though the actions themselves were the home and the walls were just what happened to be around them.

"Evening." His father settled into his chair. Not the same chair — the old chairs hadn't survived the building collapse. But his father had found secondhand chairs at a salvage market and repaired them himself, hand tools and wood glue, and the chair he sat in now had the slightly uneven quality of something made functional by effort rather than channeling. He sat in it the way he sat in everything — heavily, with the exhale of a man who'd been carrying things all day.

"The Orellen job?" Ash asked.

"Different one. Building in the Kelsmere district — not the towers, the row houses behind them. Channeled framing's degrading, and the owner wants physical bracing before the walls start showing cracks." His father rubbed the back of his neck. His hands were broad, thick-knuckled, the hands of a man who'd built his life on the kind of work that didn't require a pool. "Good work. Honest work."

He said *honest work* the way he always said it — with a satisfaction that was both genuine and preemptive, as though by naming the work's virtue he was defending it against a criticism no one had made.

"How's the retaining wall project going?" his father asked.

"Harenn's team assessed the eastern face. The reinforcement's at thirty percent — I'll need to do six to eight hours of sustained channeling to restore it, and then Harenn's crew can maintain from there."

His father nodded. The nod was the same one Ash had been watching his whole life — steady, considered, the nod of a man who listened to information and processed it before responding. But something about it was different now. A quality Ash couldn't name, something in the way his father's eyes moved from the deployment schedule to Ash's face and back, the way his hands wrapped around the cup of tea Ash had poured for him and held it without drinking.

"You remember the pump?" his father said.

"Which pump?"

"The water pump. The one you designed for Haran's workshop. You were — what, nineteen? Twenty? You spent three months on the impeller design because the casting kept failing, and you finally figured out that the problem wasn't the design, it was the casting temperature. You built a hand-cranked bellows to get the forge hot enough without channeling, and the bellows worked, and the impeller worked, and Haran installed one in the Vellan widow's kitchen."

"I remember."

"Three homes, eventually. Three families with running water from a hand-pumped system your son designed." His father took a sip of tea. His eyes were on the cup, on his hands, on the wood grain of the table. "I was proud of you for that."

"Dad—"

"And the timer. The escapement mechanism — the one for the cistern system. You filed those gear teeth by hand. I watched you do it. Two weeks of filing, and when it worked, you smiled in a way that—" He stopped. He drank his tea. The sentence ended where it ended, the way his father's sentences often did — cut off not by interruption but by the arrival of something he'd decided not to say.

Ash looked at his father across the table. The deployment schedule sat between them, the pages covered in Dorenne's precise handwriting — district names, dates, projected channeling costs. His father's hands rested beside the schedule, the sawdust still visible in the creases of his knuckles, the fingernails edged with wood dust. The hands of a man who had spent his entire life doing things the hard way in a world that had an easier way, and who had never once complained about it, and who had taught his son to do the same.

"I remember the pump," Ash said. "I remember the escapement."

"Good. That's good." His father nodded again — the same nod, careful and considered and carrying something underneath that Ash could feel without identifying, the way you feel a current in water without seeing the river's bed. "Those were good things you built. Before."

*Before.*

The word sat in the kitchen like a stone. His father didn't explain it. He didn't say *before the Wellspring* or *before you became powerful* or *before everything changed*. He just said *before*, and the word held all of those things without naming any of them, and Ash heard it and understood that something was being offered and didn't know what it was.

"I know," he said.

His father looked at him. The look held for a beat longer than usual — the steady, assessing gaze of a man who was checking whether he'd been heard. Not whether the words had been received, but whether the thing underneath the words had arrived. Whether his son understood that the pride in his voice wasn't new. That it had been there before the Wellspring, before the deployments, before the evening news about the eastern district's heating and the southern quarter's water and the spectacular, unprecedented things his boy could do now. That the pride had been there when the boy was filing gear teeth by hand in a workshop in the lower districts, and the pride had been exactly the same size then as it was now.

Ash didn't understand. He heard the words and he felt the warmth in them and he thought his father was reminiscing, the way parents did — reaching back into the past to find a version of their child that felt familiar. It was sweet. It was kind. He smiled and squeezed his father's arm and said, "Those pump systems are still running, you know. Haran says the Vellan widow's granddaughter uses hers every day."

"Is that right." His father's voice was soft.

"Three homes. Can you imagine what we could do now? If I channeled the forge for the castings, we could produce fifty impellers in a day. A hundred. The whole district could have hand-pump systems—"

"That'd be something," his father said. He finished his tea. He set the cup down with the careful precision of a man placing a tool back in its case, and the conversation moved on — to the Kelsmere job, to the condition of the building, to the mundane engineering of physical bracing in a wall whose channeling had degraded — and the thing his father had been trying to say settled to the bottom of the evening like sediment in a glass.

He'd been trying to say: *I loved you before this.*

Ash heard: *I'm glad you can do more now.*

The gap between the two was the size of a life.

* * *

Maren came for dinner on Fifthday.

She arrived the way she always arrived — quick, efficient, her bag slung across one shoulder with the controlled carelessness that meant she'd come straight from work. She kissed their mother's cheek, squeezed their father's arm, dropped the bag by the door. The choreography was identical to the Maren who'd walked into the old apartment, the Maren from before. Her hair was pulled back. Her hands were clean. She looked the same.

Ash watched her from the kitchen table where he was reviewing — he was always reviewing something now, the deployment schedule or lattice data or Dorenne's projections — and he noticed, without thinking about it, that she was rubbing the heel of her right hand against her thigh. A small motion, barely visible, the kind of gesture you made when a muscle was tired or a joint was stiff. She stopped when she saw him looking.

"The heating lattice in the upstairs hall is running hot," she said to their mother. Not as a greeting — as information, delivered on arrival, the way a maintenance technician delivers a status report. "The regulation channeling is off-balance. I'll fix it after dinner."

"You don't have to—" their mother started.

"It takes five minutes." Maren was already moving to the kitchen, her hands finding cups and plates with the efficiency of someone who'd been setting this table since she was tall enough to reach it. "If it runs hot all night it'll stress the wall channels. I'd rather catch it now."

She set the table. Four places, the same configuration, the same scarred wood surface with the shimmed leg. She moved around the kitchen with the quiet competence that had always defined her — channeling absent-mindedly as she went, warming a dish, sealing a hairline crack in a cup with a brush of her thumb, adjusting the stove's interface to balance the heat distribution. Every gesture was precise. Every expenditure was measured.

Ash had seen her channel a thousand times. It had always looked effortless — the smooth, controlled flow of a woman who got more out of her pool than anyone her age through sheer discipline and practice. She was the talented one. The competent sibling. The daughter who'd scored highest in her cohort and whose supervisor called her work the most precise she'd seen from anyone under thirty.

Tonight, something was different.

He noticed it first when she reached for the water pitcher. She channeled a small warming pulse into the ceramic — not heating the water, just taking the chill off, the kind of minor adjustment she did reflexively. The pulse came out uneven. Not dramatically — not the wild, sloppy discharge of an untrained channeler. A flicker. A brief unevenness in the flow, the channeling equivalent of a singer's voice catching on a note they'd hit perfectly a thousand times before.

She paused. Her hand stayed on the pitcher. Then she did it again — the same pulse, the same warming — and this time it was smooth. Clean. The Maren he knew. But the first attempt had taken two tries, and she was a person who never needed two tries, and the fact of it — the small, invisible failure and the quick, disciplined correction — passed through the kitchen without anyone noticing.

Except Ash didn't notice either. He registered the pause the way he registered the weather — as a fact that existed at the periphery, noted and immediately filed, the information sliding past without catching on anything important enough to hold it.

During dinner, his mother asked about his deployments and he told her. The retaining wall in the northern quarter. The transit shaft maintenance schedule. The possibility of extending the lattice recharge program to the western districts — Dorenne was drafting a proposal for the Council, and the numbers were promising. He talked about it with the easy confidence of a person whose days had shape and purpose and whose work had visible results, and his parents listened, and the conversation filled the kitchen with the warm, expansive energy of a family whose son was doing well.

Maren ate her dinner. She listened. She asked a question about the lattice recharge — a technical question, specific, the kind of question that came from someone who understood the architecture of heating systems and the mechanics of channeling into degraded stone. Her voice was the same. Her attention was the same. Everything about her was the same.

But twice during dinner, Ash saw her flex her right hand under the table. A small motion — the fingers curling and straightening, the tendons in the back of her hand moving under the skin. The gesture of a hand that had worked hard today. Harder than usual. He saw it and didn't think about it, the way you see a tree bending in wind and don't think about the root system underneath.

After dinner, Maren excused herself to fix the heating lattice in the upstairs hall. Their mother protested — "You've been working all day, let it wait" — and Maren said, "Five minutes," and went.

Ash cleared the table. He could hear her in the hallway — the faint hum of channeling, the sound of her palm pressing flat against the wall, the controlled output of a woman adjusting a heating system's regulation balance. A small job. The kind of thing she did reflexively, the maintenance work that kept the household running, the contribution she'd always made to the family's functioning without being asked and without being thanked.

He heard the channeling stop. Then start again. Then stop.

Two attempts.

He was drying a plate when she came back into the kitchen. Her right hand was at her side, the fingers slightly curled, the posture of a hand that had just been working. Her face was composed. Her expression was the expression of a woman who had done a small task competently and was not going to discuss it further.

"Fixed?" their mother asked.

"Fixed."

"Thank you, sweetheart."

Maren picked up a cloth and started drying the dishes beside him. They worked in the comfortable silence of siblings who had been doing chores together since childhood — the rhythm automatic, the division of labor unspoken, the proximity easy in the way that family proximity is easy, the bodies knowing where to be without negotiation.

"How's work?" he asked.

"Fine. Busy. The structural survey program is expanding — they've added two more districts to our assessment rotation." She dried a cup, set it in the cabinet. "More buildings showing degradation than the projections predicted. The lattice decline is accelerating in the older construction."

"I could help with that. If the lattice architecture is similar to what I've been working on in the eastern district—"

"We've got it." She said it quickly — not sharply, not defensively. Quickly. The speed of a person whose answer was ready before the question was finished. "The survey program is functioning. We're a good team."

He looked at her. She was drying the next dish, her movements precise, her face calm. The evening light from the kitchen window caught the side of her face and showed him what he always saw when he looked at his sister: the composure, the competence, the clean lines of a person whose architecture was complete.

He didn't see what was underneath. He didn't see the way her grip on the towel shifted when she wrung it — the right hand compensating for something, the strength redistributed. He didn't see the two-attempt channeling, the pulse that came out uneven before it came out clean. He didn't see his sister working harder than she'd ever worked to produce results that used to come easily, the pool that had always been at the weak end of her generation feeling somehow weaker now, thinner, the ocean of his Wellspring casting a shadow over the careful, disciplined stream of her daily capacity.

He didn't see it because he wasn't looking. He was thinking about the retaining wall in the northern quarter. He was thinking about the deployment schedule and the Council proposal and the districts he hadn't reached yet. He was thinking about the five hundred families in the eastern district and the water system in the southern quarter and the transit shaft that carried people up through the city because of what he'd done, what he could do, what he was.

Maren dried the last dish. She put it away. She folded the towel and hung it on the hook by the stove — the same hook, the same gesture, the same small act of order that she'd performed in every kitchen they'd ever shared.

"I should go," she said. "Early shift tomorrow."

"Walk you home?"

"It's three streets, Ash."

"I know, but—"

"Three streets." She smiled. The smile was real and warm and exactly right, the smile of a sister who loved her brother and was glad for him and was going home to her own small apartment where she would sleep and wake and go to work and channel at the level her pool allowed and come home and do it again. "I'll see you next Fifthday."

She kissed their mother's cheek. She squeezed their father's arm. She picked up her bag and slung it over her shoulder with the controlled carelessness that meant she had somewhere to be, and the door closed behind her with the soft click of a latch catching in a frame that hung true.

Ash went back to the deployment reports.

In the hallway, the heating lattice ran steady and even, the regulation balanced, the temperature perfect. Maren had fixed it. Nobody had thanked her. The warmth in the walls had nothing to do with the Wellspring and everything to do with a woman who had always done this — quietly, competently, without complaint — and would continue to do it, every week, in a household that had stopped noticing the effort because the effort had always been invisible.

The lattice hummed. The walls were warm. Ash read his reports, and the evening settled into the comfortable quiet of a family that was, from where he sat, doing fine.

* * *

The next morning, Ash woke early and went to the retaining wall.

He left before dawn — the apartment quiet, his parents still sleeping, the kitchen dark except for the faint glow of the lattice channels in the walls. He moved quietly out of habit, placing his feet the way he'd always placed them, avoiding the sounds. There were no sounds to avoid in the new apartment — no board that groaned outside his parents' door, no settling in the floor — but the choreography was in his body and his body didn't know the difference.

The streets were empty at this hour. He walked fast, the Wellspring warm behind his ribs, the morning air cold on his face. The transit lift at the corner was running — it was always running now, the rotation schedule functioning, the channelers maintaining the shaft with the supplemental charge he'd given it — and he rode it down three levels to the northern quarter, the platform descending smoothly through the shaft, the counterweights clicking above him, the sound of a city that worked.

Harenn was waiting at the retaining wall with two members of her structural crew. She was white-haired and sharp-eyed and she did not believe in pleasantries.

"Feed, not push," she said by way of greeting. "You cracked two stones last time."

"Good morning, Harenn."

"It'll be a good morning if you don't crack my wall."

He pressed his hands against the stone. The wall was massive — twenty feet of channeled masonry holding back the hillside on the northern quarter's upper edge, the kind of deep infrastructure that had been built in the high-magic era and maintained by diminishing crews ever since. The channeled reinforcement that held it together was at thirty percent, Harenn had said. He could feel it — the thinness, the places where the structural bonds had degraded, the stone holding shape through inertia rather than strength.

He fed the wall. Not pushed — he'd learned the difference, mostly, the distinction between forcing energy into stone and offering it, letting the material's own structure guide the flow. The Wellspring moved through his palms and into the masonry and the stone accepted it — slowly, reluctantly at first, then with a deepening pull as the old channels remembered what it felt like to be full.

The waste heat shimmered off his arms. Less than the first time — the raw practice of weeks of deployment channeling had trained his body toward a crude efficiency that was still wildly wasteful by any professional standard but measurably better than where he'd started. Harenn watched from three paces back, her arms crossed, her expression the expression of a woman who had recalibrated her expectations from "slow" to "adequate" and was reserving judgment on further upgrades.

He worked for six hours. The wall drank the energy and strengthened, the reinforcement rising from thirty percent to something Harenn assessed, with grudging approval, as roughly seventy. The structural bonds tightened. The stone settled. The wall would hold.

"Better," Harenn said. "You only cracked one stone today."

"I think that one was already cracked."

"It wasn't."

He wiped his hands on his trousers. The stone dust was gritty between his fingers, the mineral residue of old construction, and the warmth of the channeling lingered in his palms — not the waste heat of inefficiency but the genuine warmth of contact, of hours spent pressing his hands against a wall that needed him.

He walked home through the northern quarter. The morning had turned into afternoon while he worked, the light shifting from the thin gray of early dawn to the warmer gold of midday, and the streets were busy now — people moving through the district with the ordinary rhythms of their lives, errands and children and the traffic of a neighborhood that functioned. A woman was warming the wall of her building as she walked past — the casual civic gesture, palm against stone, the small maintenance that kept the district standing. Ash watched her do it and felt a surge of something warm in his chest, something proprietary and pleased: the transit lift she'd ridden to get here worked because of him, the retaining wall that held up the street she was walking on was stronger now because of him, the heating grid in the next district over was running because of him.

The city worked. Not all of it — not yet. But more of it than before, and more every day, and the parts that worked were his.

He walked through the streets of a city he was holding together and he felt, with the deep structural certainty of a man who had found his purpose, that everything was going to be fine. His mother would adjust. His father was proud. His sister was doing well — she always did well, she was Maren, competence was what she was.

Everything was fine.

In the eastern district, the lattice hummed. In the southern quarter, the water ran. In the northern quarter, the retaining wall held its new strength, the structural bonds tightening in the stone, the wall doing what it was built to do.

And in Maren's apartment, three streets from the family's new home, the heating lattice on her bedroom wall flickered — a brief, uneven pulse, the regulation drifting off-balance, the kind of minor failure that would have been invisible to anyone who didn't know what steady looked like. She sat on the edge of her bed in the morning light with her palm against the wall and her eyes closed and the channeling flowing through her hand, and the first attempt failed, and the second attempt held, and the effort of the second attempt was greater than it should have been, and her hand trembled when she took it away.

She flexed her fingers. She curled them into a fist and opened them again. The trembling stopped.

She stood up. She dressed for work. She left her apartment and walked to the survey office in the morning cold, and her stride was steady and her face was composed and her right hand was curled loosely at her side, the fingers relaxed, the trembling invisible, the cost of her competence carried in a place where no one could see it.

The city was warm. The walls held. The Wellspring hummed behind Ash's ribs like an ocean, and Maren's pool ran thin like a stream, and neither of them knew what the other was spending.
