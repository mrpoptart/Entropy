# Chapter 19

The candle on the desk was the only light in the room.

Ash sat on the edge of the cot with Dorenne's cumulative expenditure report unfolded across his knees, the paper creased where he'd been carrying it in his pocket since that morning. The numbers ran in a single column down the left side of the page—weekly totals, each one annotated in Dorenne's small precise handwriting, each one a sum of sums: sites visited, durations recorded, estimated energy costs extrapolated from observable output. Rough numbers. Honest numbers.

He'd been staring at them for twenty minutes.

The quarters Dorenne had given him were small—a cot, a desk, a washbasin, a window that looked out onto the reconstruction command post's inner courtyard. Stone walls, channeled decades ago by someone with a pool large enough to fuse the mortar permanently. The kind of room you gave to someone who was useful but not important enough for actual furniture. He'd never minded. He'd slept in worse.

Tonight the room felt like the right size for what he was about to do.

He reached for the desk and pulled the candle closer. Took the stub of graphite pencil Dorenne's aide used for field notes. Turned the expenditure report over to its blank side.

And began.

* * *

The first number was the hardest, because it was the one built entirely from memory.

Week one. The activation and its aftermath—three days of medical observation, scattered tests, the examiners prodding at something they couldn't measure from outside. Then the learning. Those early days in the training yard behind the command post, where Dorenne's senior channelers had tried to teach him what every child learned by age five: how to push energy through his hands, how to direct it, how to stop.

He'd been terrible at it.

He remembered the heat. Not the controlled warmth of clean channeling but the sloppy, radiating excess of someone who didn't know what he was doing—energy flooding outward in every direction, accomplishing a fraction of what it should have, the rest bleeding off as ambient heat that fogged windows and made the training yard feel like a forge in summer. He remembered the senior channeler—Voss, a middle-generation woman with steady hands and no patience for waste—stepping back from him after his third attempt at reinforcing a practice stone, her face flushed, sweat on her temples. "You're heating the air more than the stone," she'd said. "If I could bottle what you're losing, I could warm the entire district."

He hadn't cared. He'd been doing *magic*. After twenty years of nothing, of watching other people's hands glow while his stayed dark, he was channeling. The waste was irrelevant. The waste was joyful—proof that there was something to waste, that the reservoir inside him was so vast it could afford to spill.

He wrote the number.

Week one, estimated: the output had been small. Practice stones. A cracked wall in the training yard. A water pipe Voss let him try to seal as a test of precision. Each task trivial in scale. But the efficiency—he'd been operating at maybe one part in five. For every unit of work that reached the target, four units bled off as heat, as unfocused pressure, as the raw noise of a reservoir being opened by someone who'd never learned to control the valve.

He did the multiplication in his head. Small outputs, enormous inputs. The reservoir hadn't noticed. The reservoir didn't notice anything in those days—it sat in his chest like an ocean, formless and depthless, and he'd dipped a cup into it and the waterline hadn't moved.

But the cup had been full. And he'd poured most of it on the ground.

He wrote the waste fraction beside the week-one total. Drew a line under it. Moved on.

* * *

Weeks two and three were easier to reconstruct because Dorenne's records covered them.

The first real deployments. District heating grids in the lower terraces—neighborhoods where the communal warming lattices had been failing for years, maintained by residents whose pools couldn't keep up with the demand. He'd walked into those districts and poured force into the lattice nodes, and the buildings had warmed, and people had taken off their second layers of clothing for the first time in months, and children had played on heated floors instead of huddling near fire grates.

He remembered the woman in the Thornwall district who had cried. Not sobbing—just tears, running quietly down her face while she held her daughter's hand and felt the floor warm under their feet. He'd looked at Dorenne, standing beside him with her clipboard, and she'd nodded once. *This is what it's for.*

The heating work was straightforward but energy-dense. Each lattice node needed to be saturated—not just activated but charged deep enough to hold for weeks without additional channeling. He'd flooded them. That was the word Voss used: flooding. Pushing more energy into the target than it needed because his control wasn't fine enough to pulse. Like watering a garden with a fire hose because you hadn't learned to use the spigot.

His efficiency had been better by then. One part in three, maybe. For every unit of heat that stayed in the lattice, two units escaped into the surrounding stone, into the air, into his own body as a flush of warmth that made his skin pink and his heartbeat quicken. Better than week one. Still catastrophic by any standard a trained channeler would recognize.

He checked his math against Dorenne's estimates on the front of the page. Her staff had calculated the output—the measurable heat retained by the lattices, the observable effects. They couldn't see the input. They didn't know how much he'd spent to achieve what they'd measured. But he could feel the ratio, now, in retrospect. The way you could feel, looking back, how much food you'd wasted at a feast you'd thought would never end.

He wrote the numbers. Week two. Week three. Input, output, waste. Three columns, each one a story about a man learning to use something he'd never been taught to hold.

The waste column was the tallest.

* * *

He paused. Set the pencil down. Pressed his palms together and reached for the reservoir.

It was there. Warm, present, vast—still vast, still more than anyone else alive had ever held. But the geometry he'd been sensing for days was sharper tonight. The edges weren't close. They weren't crowding him. But they were *definite* in a way they hadn't been even this morning, as if the act of looking at numbers on paper had given his body permission to confirm what his mind was calculating.

The reservoir had a shape. The shape had dimensions. The dimensions were not infinite.

He picked up the pencil.

* * *

Week four. The water systems.

Dorenne had directed him to the city's secondary aqueduct network—the older system, built in the high-magic era by channelers whose pools dwarfed anything the current generation could produce. The joints were failing. The channeled stone that lined the conduits was thinning, losing the reinforcement that kept water flowing uphill against gravity, and the repair crews—young adults with pools a tenth the size of the builders'—couldn't push enough energy into the old work to restore it. They were patching. Buying time. Slowing a collapse they couldn't prevent.

He'd walked the aqueduct from the river intake to the upper-terrace distribution junction. Three miles of conduit, most of it buried, all of it requiring hands-on contact to channel. He'd knelt at each joint, pressed his palms to the stone, and pushed the reservoir through. The old channeling accepted his force the way dry earth accepted rain—greedily, pulling more than he intended, the ancient work so degraded that it absorbed everything he offered and asked for more.

His efficiency was improving. He could feel it in the way the energy moved—less scattered, more directed, the flooding giving way to something that approached a controlled pour. One part in two, roughly. Half of what he spent reached the target. The other half still bled off as waste heat, as uncontrolled pressure, as the excess of a man who was learning by doing because no one had ever taught a Wellspring before.

The aqueduct work had taken two full days. The conduits were sealed. The water flowed. Forty thousand people had clean water that wouldn't fail for a generation.

He wrote the number. It was large. Larger than weeks two and three combined—the aqueduct was deep infrastructure, the kind of work that demanded sustained, high-output channeling for hours at a stretch. The cost reflected that.

But the waste. The waste was still half of everything he'd spent. Half of two days of continuous channeling, lost to heat and imprecision and the fundamental inefficiency of an untrained hand doing elder-scale work.

*You could have done it in one day*, he thought. *If you'd known what you were doing. If anyone had known how to teach you.*

But nobody had known. Nobody had ever taught a Wellspring. Nobody had ever needed to.

He wrote the waste number and moved on.

* * *

Weeks five and six blurred together in his memory and on Dorenne's chart—a dense cluster of deployments, three districts per week, the reconstruction machine accelerating as Dorenne and the Council realized what they had in him. Structural reinforcement. Transit shaft repairs. Building foundations. Retaining walls. The deep, unglamorous work of keeping a city from falling apart, each site a withdrawal from an account he hadn't been checking.

His efficiency continued to improve. By week five, he was approaching two parts in three—for every three units he spent, two reached the target. Still wasteful by Haran's standards, by Voss's standards, by the standards of anyone who'd spent a lifetime learning to channel with precision. But better. Measurably, meaningfully better. His control was tightening. The flooding was becoming a pour. The pour was becoming, on good days, something close to a directed stream.

He cross-referenced his estimates with Dorenne's output records. The match was close enough to trust—her staff had been careful, methodical, building their model from the outside while he'd been paying no attention from the inside. Their numbers and his feeling aligned. The reservoir's withdrawals were real. Countable. Named.

He added weeks five and six to the running total at the bottom of the page.

The number climbed.

* * *

He stopped again. Stood. Walked to the window and looked out at the courtyard. The command post was quiet at this hour—staff gone home, the operations room dark, Dorenne's four panels standing in the shadows like sentinels. The city beyond the post was a scatter of lit windows and dark ones, the gradient of power visible in the light. The districts he'd repaired glowed warmer. The districts he hadn't reached yet were dimmer, cooler, waiting.

He pressed his forehead against the glass. It was cold—nobody had channeled warmth into this window in days, and the stone frame radiated the chill of a building that had been maintained just enough and no more.

The running total sat in his head. Six weeks of deployments, training, learning. Six weeks of wild inefficiency tapering toward something merely wasteful. The sum was a number he could hold, a number with weight, a number that meant something specific when placed beside the reservoir he could feel in his chest.

It was significant. But it wasn't the number that frightened him.

The number that frightened him was the next one.

He went back to the cot. Sat down. Picked up the pencil.

* * *

The earthquake.

He didn't have to reconstruct this from memory. He remembered every hour. Every building. Every street.

Four days. The first tremor hit at midmorning on a clear day—the ground lurching sideways like a living thing, the sound of it deeper than anything he'd ever heard, a bass note that bypassed the ears and resonated in the bones. He'd been at the command post when it started. He'd run.

Not away. Toward.

The first building was in the lower-east district. A residential block, four stories, home to sixty families. The foundation channeling—laid down two generations ago by established-tier workers whose pools were twice what the current maintenance crews could manage—had been thinning for years. Nobody had the energy to restore it properly. The building stood on competence and hope and the diminishing magic of people who gave what they had and prayed it would be enough.

The earthquake found every weakness. The foundation channeling sheared at the southeast corner. The building began to fold.

He'd gotten there in time. Hands on the stone, reservoir wide open, pushing force into the failing structure with everything he had. Not flooding this time—not exactly. Something rawer. Something that had no name because no one had ever done it before: a Wellspring poured into a collapsing building at maximum output, holding it up by sheer will and volume while rescue teams pulled families through the windows.

He'd held it for two hours. Two hours with his palms flat against buckling stone, the reservoir streaming through him like a river through a broken dam, the building groaning and settling and holding because he would not let it fall. The rescue teams worked above him. Children were passed from window to window. An old woman was carried down a ladder by two neighbors, her arms around their necks, her face gray with terror.

When the last family was out, he'd let go. The building sagged but held—his channeling had fused the cracks deeply enough to stabilize the structure even after he withdrew. Not permanently. Maybe a year. Maybe five. Long enough for the engineers to decide what came next.

He'd moved to the next building. And the next.

Four days. He slept in fragments—twenty minutes against a wall, an hour on someone's floor, once a full three hours in the back of a supply wagon while the aftershocks rumbled and the city groaned around him. Every time he woke, he channeled. The reservoir poured and he poured with it, holding walls, sealing ruptures, bracing foundations, pushing force into stone and earth and the bones of a city that was trying to come apart.

The dam. He remembered the dam—the emergency cistern in the upper terraces, a retaining wall that held back a reservoir of drinking water for the western districts. The channeled reinforcement had cracked during the main shock. Water was seeping through in sheets, each one carving the crack wider, the wall bowing outward under the pressure. If it failed, the flood would take the district below. Three thousand people lived in that district. Their homes were already damaged. They couldn't take a flood.

He'd pressed his entire body against the dam wall. Not his hands—his chest, his arms, his forehead against the stone. He'd pushed the reservoir through every point of contact, flooding the channeled reinforcement, filling the cracks, layering his own force over the ancient work so thickly that the wall didn't just hold—it solidified. The water stopped. The seeping ceased. The wall stood as if it had been built yesterday by someone with an ocean to spend.

It had taken him six hours. Six hours pressed against cold stone, the water thundering behind it, the reservoir streaming out of him at a rate he'd never approached before and hadn't approached since.

Now, sitting on his cot in the candlelight, he wrote the earthquake numbers.

He didn't rush. He took each day—day one, day two, day three, day four—and reconstructed the deployments. The residential block. The commercial district where three workshops and a school had buckled. The transit hub where the lift shafts had cracked and the upper platforms were sagging. The retaining walls along the eastern slope. The dam. The bridge in the lower terraces that connected the two halves of a neighborhood and whose failure would have cut off four hundred people from the emergency supply routes.

Each one a calculation: hours of sustained channeling, estimated output rate, efficiency fraction. His efficiency during the earthquake had been—he paused, pencil hovering. During the earthquake, he hadn't been thinking about efficiency. He'd been thinking about the building that was falling and the people inside it. He'd been flooding again—maximum output, minimum precision, raw force applied with the finesse of a hammer because there was no time for anything else. The waste heat had been staggering. He remembered the stone warming under his hands until it steamed. He remembered his own skin flushing red, his shirt soaked through, the air around him shimmering the way air shimmered above a forge.

One part in three. Maybe worse. Maybe one in four, during the worst of it—the dam, the collapsing residential block, the moments where he'd simply opened the reservoir and let it pour because the alternative was letting someone die.

He wrote the efficiency fraction. Wrote the hours. Multiplied.

The number hit him in the chest.

He checked it. Checked it again. The pencil moved across the paper, retracing the multiplication, testing each estimate against his memory of the work—how long had he held the residential block, really? Two hours? More? And the dam—six hours was his memory, but memory was unreliable under stress. Could have been five. Could have been seven.

He ran the calculation with five hours. Then with seven. The range mattered, but not as much as he wanted it to. Whether the dam had cost him five hours at one-in-three efficiency or seven hours at one-in-four, the number was enormous. The earthquake was enormous. Four days of sustained maximum-output channeling at combat efficiency—the phrase came to him from somewhere, maybe Voss, maybe one of the examiners—combat efficiency, meaning the worst possible ratio of input to output, because combat didn't wait for precision.

He added the earthquake total to the running sum.

The running sum was no longer a number he could hold comfortably. It was a number with mass, with gravity, a number that pulled at the reservoir in his chest the way a weight pulled at a rope. He could feel the correspondence—the number on the paper and the shape inside him, the edges he'd been sensing for days, the floor that was closer now than it had been this morning.

*That can't be right.*

He checked it a third time.

It was right.

* * *

The candle had burned down by a third. The room was darker at the edges, the candlelight pulling inward, the shadows on the stone walls reaching toward him like something curious. He didn't notice. He was inside the numbers now, deep in the architecture of his own arithmetic, the world outside the page reduced to the scratch of graphite on paper and the warm pulse of the reservoir confirming what the math already knew.

He wrote the post-earthquake weeks. Weeks seven and eight—the reconstruction deployments. Dorenne's schedule: three sites per day, structural reinforcement, transit repairs, the systematic work of putting a city back together. His efficiency had jumped after the earthquake—the days of maximum-output channeling had taught his body something his training sessions hadn't. He was channeling at roughly two parts in three now, consistently. Better control. Less waste. The flooding was gone. He was pouring, and the pour was clean.

But the volume was high. Three sites per day, each one a major structural element—retaining walls, building foundations, water system junctions. Each one demanding sustained channeling at a level that would have drained an elder's entire daily pool. Each one a withdrawal.

He added weeks seven and eight.

Then he drew a line under the total.

And looked at it.

* * *

The number sat on the paper in his own handwriting, graphite on the back of Dorenne's expenditure report, and it was not abstract. It was not symbolic. It was not a metaphor for loss or a philosophical meditation on finitude. It was a number. Kilojoules, the unit the examiners had used, the unit that mapped magical energy to physical energy because that was what magic was—conversion, not creation. A number of kilojoules, representing the total estimated expenditure of the Wellspring reservoir across eight weeks of activation.

He knew what the reservoir had been at the start. The examiners had estimated it during the first assessment—a range, not a precise figure, based on the observable outputs and the scaling models they'd cobbled together from theory and guesswork. But their range had been narrow enough to be useful. Fifty million kilojoules, give or take. The number had meant nothing to him at the time. Fifty million was the kind of number that sounded like infinity when you'd spent your whole life with zero.

He subtracted.

The remainder was approximately half.

Half.

He'd burned through half of everything he would ever have. In eight weeks.

The pencil was still in his hand. He set it down on the cot beside him and pressed his palms together. The reservoir pulsed—warm, present, enormous by any standard except the one that mattered. He could feel its shape now with terrible clarity, as if the math had given him a lens his body had been groping for. The edges weren't close. The floor wasn't close. But they were *there*, and the distance between where he stood and where they waited was not infinite. It was a number. He had just written it down.

*Half.*

He picked up the pencil again. Because the number told him where he was, but not where he was going. And his mind—the analytical mind, the one that had always been his real weapon, the one that had spent twenty years optimizing around constraints and counting coins and measuring portions—his mind wouldn't stop. It never stopped. That was the thing about intelligence: it didn't care whether you wanted the answer.

He wrote the deployment schedule. Dorenne's plan—the one on the fourth panel in the operations room, the columns stretching forward across months. Three sites per week in the early phase. Four in the later phase. The work expanding as the reconstruction moved from surface repairs to deep infrastructure—retaining walls, terrace anchoring, the ancient foundation channeling that had been thinning for a generation and couldn't wait any longer.

He estimated the cost per deployment based on the work he'd already done. Structural reinforcement at his current efficiency—two parts in three, maybe approaching three parts in four as his control continued to improve. He was getting better. He would keep getting better. The efficiency gains were real, and they mattered. Each percentage point of improvement stretched the reservoir further, made each deployment cheaper, bought time.

He wrote the projection.

At Dorenne's current deployment rate, accounting for continued efficiency improvements, accounting for the planned escalation from three sites to four to the eventual deep infrastructure work that would cost more per site than anything he'd done so far—

The reservoir emptied.

Not someday. Not in the abstract, comfortable distance of "eventually." The reservoir emptied in a number of months he could count on his fingers. A number so specific it had a season. A number that fell on a particular week of a particular month, a point on Dorenne's chart where the blue deployment sites would run out of the force to fill them.

He stared at the number.

He recalculated with more optimistic efficiency gains. The number moved. Not enough. He recalculated with fewer deployments—Dorenne's minimum schedule, the stripped-down version she'd presented to the Council as the baseline for structural safety. The number moved further. Still not enough. Still a date. Still a season. Still *then*.

He recalculated assuming he achieved master-level efficiency—the ninety percent that Haran operated at, the theoretical ceiling of human channeling skill, the precision that took decades of practice to approach. Even at ninety percent, even burning almost nothing, even channeling with the clean invisible economy of a man who'd spent his life learning to whisper—the reservoir emptied. Later. Not never. Later.

There was no version of the math where the reservoir lasted forever.

He put the pencil down. His hands were shaking. Not much—a fine tremor in the fingers, the kind you could hide if anyone were watching. Nobody was watching. He was alone in a small stone room with a candle and a piece of paper and a number that meant *this is temporary*.

The reservoir sat in his chest. Warm. Present. Halved.

* * *

He didn't move for a long time.

The candle burned. The shadows shifted. Outside the window, the city slept—or tried to, in the patchwork way that Solathis slept these days, the damaged districts restless, the repaired ones settling into the stability he'd given them, the stability that had cost what it had cost.

He thought about the earthquake.

The residential block. Sixty families. The old woman carried down the ladder. The children passed from window to window. The dam—three thousand people downstream, their homes already damaged, their lives held in place by a wall of channeled stone and the force he'd poured into it for six hours.

He didn't regret the spending. The thought formed clearly, tested, and held. He would do it again. If the earthquake came tomorrow, if the dam cracked tonight, if the buildings began to fold—he would run toward them and he would pour the reservoir into the stone and he would hold. He would hold until his hands blistered and the air steamed and the floor of the reservoir rose to meet him. He would not let them fall.

But.

The spending had felt like nothing at the time. The reservoir had felt like an ocean, and oceans didn't have floors. That was what made them oceans. He'd poured and poured and the waterline hadn't seemed to move, and so he'd assumed the waterline couldn't move, and the assumption had felt like truth because the alternative—that every building he held, every life he saved, every heroic act cost him a measurable piece of his finite self—the alternative was a kind of math he hadn't been ready to do.

Now he'd done it.

The earthquake—four days, combat efficiency, maximum output—accounted for the largest single block of expenditure on the page. Larger than the early training. Larger than weeks of deployments combined. The earthquake was a crater in the arithmetic, a place where the numbers dropped sharply and never recovered, the moment where half became a number instead of a feeling.

His greatest triumph. His most expensive act. The same thing.

Every life he'd saved had cost him time he would never get back. Not metaphorical time. Actual, measurable, calculable time—weeks or months shaved from the reservoir's remaining lifespan, each one a building held, a wall sealed, a dam braced, a person who got to go home because he'd spent what he'd spent.

*Worth it.*

He sat with the word. It was true. It was also not the point. The point was that he hadn't known. He hadn't checked. He'd spent from an account he'd never bothered to audit, and the receipt had arrived, and the balance was half of what he'd started with, and the spending had been heroic and necessary and it was still spending. You could do the right thing with the last coin in your pocket and it was still the last coin.

He looked at the paper. The numbers blurred for a moment—his eyes adjusting, the candlelight wavering—and then sharpened. The total. The projection. The date.

He folded the paper in half. Then in half again. Then he unfolded it, because folding it didn't make the numbers go away.

* * *

The early waste.

His mind came back to it the way a tongue returned to a broken tooth.

Weeks one through four. The learning period. One part in five, one part in three, slowly improving toward one part in two. Every deployment a celebration of capability, every act of channeling a joyful proof that he was not what he'd been, every waste-heat flush a detail too small to care about when the reservoir was an ocean and the ocean had no floor.

He recalculated the early weeks with master-level efficiency applied retroactively. If he'd channeled at ninety percent from day one—if he'd had Haran's decades of practice on the first morning—the waste column collapsed. The same outputs, the same heated districts and sealed aqueducts and reinforced walls, at a fraction of the input. The reservoir's total expenditure dropped by—

He wrote the number.

He stared at it.

The early waste—the joyful, careless, celebratory waste of a man who'd never had magic before and couldn't stop reveling in the having—accounted for nearly a quarter of everything he'd spent. Not a quarter of the waste. A quarter of the *total*. Days and weeks of reservoir life, burned to heat rooms he'd already left, to warm stone that didn't need warming, to push energy into targets that had been saturated three passes ago.

*If I'd known*, he thought. *If anyone had known. If there'd been a teacher, a guide, someone who could have said: stop flooding, start pulsing, every drop you waste is a drop you don't get back—*

But there hadn't been. Nobody had ever trained a Wellspring. Nobody had ever needed to, because nobody had ever been one. He'd learned the way the first person to hold fire learned—by burning himself and everyone around him and calling it progress.

The early waste was gone. It wasn't coming back. It sat on the page in his handwriting, a number he couldn't unknow, a fraction of his life spent in celebration of the thing that was now running out.

He closed his eyes. The reservoir pulsed in his chest—warm, halved, finite.

*You thought you were rich*, he thought. *You spent like you were rich. And now you've counted the coins and you know exactly how poor you're going to be.*

* * *

The mind wouldn't stop.

It never stopped. That was the curse of intelligence—it chased implications the way water chased cracks, finding every fissure, every gap, every connection between one number and the next. He'd calculated his own decline. He'd stared at the date when the reservoir would empty. He'd sat with the earthquake and the waste and the cost of heroism and the cost of joy.

But his mind kept running.

Because the reservoir wasn't the only thing declining.

He thought about Dorenne's charts—not the blue deployment panel, but the others. The first panel, the one that had been on her wall before the earthquake, before the Wellspring, before any of it. The infrastructure assessment. Every building in the city coded by condition: green for stable, amber for declining, red for critical. The gradient had shifted every time he'd looked at it—more amber each month, more red each quarter, the green retreating toward the upper districts where the elders lived and maintained their own buildings with pools that were still sufficient.

The gradient wasn't about him. It was about the world.

The pools were declining. Every generation born with less than the one before. The elders who maintained the grand buildings would die, and their children couldn't match their output, and their grandchildren couldn't match their children's. The arithmetic was the same arithmetic he'd just done on his own reservoir—total supply, rate of expenditure, projection forward, date of exhaustion. The same math. Different scale.

He wasn't doing anything unique. He was doing what the world was doing—spending from a finite account, watching the balance drop, heading toward the same destination. The difference was speed. He'd reach empty in months. The world would reach it in generations. But the floor was the same floor. The zero was the same zero.

He picked up the pencil. Not to calculate—he didn't need to. The world's numbers were in Dorenne's reports, in the Council's debates, in the broadsheets he'd been ignoring for weeks. Pool decline rates by generation. Infrastructure maintenance shortfalls. The gap between what the city's systems needed and what the available workforce could provide, widening every year like a crack in a retaining wall that nobody had the energy to seal.

He wrote the numbers anyway. Not to discover them—to see them beside his own. Two columns on the page. His reservoir's decline. The world's decline. The slopes were different. The destination was the same.

The world's pools were emptying the way his Wellspring was emptying. Slower. Quieter. But emptying.

And then—

Maren.

* * *

The thought arrived the way the worst thoughts always did—not as a logical conclusion but as a physical sensation. A cold weight in his stomach. A tightening across his shoulders. His sister's name surfacing in his mind like something breaking through ice.

Maren.

He set the pencil down.

His mother's voice from the kitchen: *She's been picking up extra shifts. Maintenance work, the middle districts. They're shorthanded since the earthquake.* And under the words, the thing his mother hadn't said and didn't need to: Maren was spending. Every shift, every repair, every crack sealed and lattice recharged—each one a withdrawal from a pool that was smaller than her mother's had been at the same age, and smaller than her grandmother's, and would be smaller still next year.

Maren's pool. A thousand kilojoules on a good day, maybe. The young-adult budget—the generation that ran out by late afternoon, that rationed every expenditure, that wore extra layers in winter because heating a room cost a third of what they had. A thousand kilojoules, renewable, replenished by sleep, back again every morning. But the morning total was lower than last year's morning total. And next year's would be lower still.

She was skilled. More skilled than anyone in her generation had a right to be—disciplined, precise, efficient. She got more out of her thousand kilojoules than most people got out of twice that. She'd been training since childhood, refining her technique with the obsessive rigor of someone who couldn't afford to waste a single unit. Because she couldn't. Because no one in her generation could. Because the pool was a daily budget and the budget was shrinking and the only way to keep up was to get better at spending less.

She'd been doing this her entire life.

The math reformed in his mind. Not his math now. Hers. A thousand kilojoules, declining by—what? Two percent per year? Three? The decline rates were debated, measured imprecisely, argued over in Council sessions he'd attended as Dorenne's aide and hadn't paid attention to. But the direction was clear. Down. Every year, a little less. Every year, the same tasks requiring a larger fraction of a smaller total. Every year, the margins getting thinner.

Maren's trajectory. The same slope as his, stretched across decades instead of months. The same finitude. The same floor.

She was doing in years what he was doing in weeks.

He pressed his palms together. The reservoir pulsed—his reservoir, the Wellspring, the unprecedented and enormous and *halved* reservoir that was still more power than any living person had ever held. More power than Maren would channel in her entire life, probably. More power than she would ever touch, compressed into a space behind his sternum that was shrinking toward a date he could name.

She'd never had the luxury of thinking the well was infinite.

The thought landed like something falling from a height. He sat with it. Let it settle into his body—the weight in his stomach, the tightness in his shoulders, the ache behind his eyes that wasn't quite tears and wasn't quite anything else. Maren, who'd grown up beside him, who'd carried her magic with the quiet discipline of someone who understood from childhood that every expenditure was a choice. Who'd watched him receive the Wellspring and smiled and said the right things and gone back to her thousand kilojoules, her daily budget, her declining pool. Who'd picked up extra maintenance shifts because the city was shorthanded and someone needed to do the work and she was skilled enough to make her small pool stretch further than it should.

She'd been living with this math her whole life. Not the specific numbers—not the kilojoules, the decline rates, the projections. The *feeling*. The knowledge that the well had a bottom and you were drawing from it every day and the draw was getting harder. The awareness that the things you could do this year, you might not be able to do next year. The discipline of conservation as a way of life, not a sudden crisis.

He'd been sitting in this room calculating his own decline as though it were unique. As though the sensation of the floor rising—the terrible new geometry of the reservoir, the edges he could feel, the number that meant *this will end*—as though these were things only a Wellspring could experience.

They weren't.

They were Maren's life. They were every young channeler's life. They were every maintenance worker who went home dry at the end of a shift and lay down to sleep knowing that tomorrow's pool would be a little smaller than today's.

They were the world's life.

He looked at the paper. Two columns. His decline and the world's decline. The slopes were different. The destination was the same. And Maren was on the world's slope—had always been on it—climbing the same gradient toward the same zero, just slowly enough that it looked like normal. Just gradually enough that nobody called it a crisis. Just quietly enough that her brother, sitting in a room full of numbers, had to *calculate* his way to what she'd known in her body since she was old enough to feel her pool run low on a cold afternoon and choose between warming her hands and heating her tea.

*She never told me.*

But why would she? What would she have said? *Brother, the well is finite, the magic is temporary, every day I make a hundred small decisions about what to spend and what to save and none of it will matter in the end because the pool is going down and it's never coming back up?*

She would never have said that. She would have smiled and been competent and picked up extra shifts and come home tired and gone to sleep and woken up with a little less than yesterday and done it again. Because that was what you did. Because that was what everyone did. Because the alternative was sitting in a small room with a candle and a piece of paper and a number that meant *this will end*, and nobody had the luxury of doing that except a man with a Wellspring who'd only just discovered that his ocean had a floor.

He folded the paper. This time he didn't unfold it.

* * *

The candle was guttering. The flame had reached the base of the tallow, the wick leaning sideways, the light unsteady. Shadows pulsed on the walls—expanding, contracting, the room breathing with the rhythm of a flame about to die.

He didn't relight it. He didn't channel warmth into the tallow to extend the burn. He sat on the cot in the gathering dark and held the folded paper in his hands and felt the reservoir in his chest—warm, present, finite, halved—and thought about his sister walking home from a maintenance shift in the middle districts, her pool spent, her hands cold, her body carrying the ordinary exhaustion of a woman who gave what she had and would give again tomorrow and the day after and the day after that until the giving ran out.

The candle died. The room went dark.

The reservoir glowed inside him—he could feel it, a warmth without light, a presence without edge, except it had edges now. It had a floor. It had a date. It had a number on a piece of paper in his shaking hands.

He lay down on the cot. The ceiling was invisible above him—stone, channeled, built to last generations by someone who'd spent their pool to make something that would outlive them. He'd thought about that ceiling before. He'd thought about the person who built it and the person who was gone.

Now he thought about Maren.

She was out there somewhere in the dark city, walking home or still working, her small precise pool stretched across a shift that asked more than it should. She was doing the math in her body the way she'd always done it—not with pencil and paper, not with numbers and projections, but with the tired ache of muscles that had channeled all day and the quiet knowledge that tomorrow there would be a little less to spend.

She'd been doing this since before he had magic. She'd be doing it after his was gone.

The thought settled over him like a weight, and under the weight was something that wasn't grief and wasn't anger and wasn't fear. Something quieter. Something that had the shape of understanding, and the understanding was this: he was not special. His finitude was not unique. His countdown was not the only one running. The math he'd done tonight—the terrible, precise, inescapable math—was the math of every channeler in the world, compressed and accelerated and rendered in numbers large enough to see. But the math was the same. The floor was the same. The zero was the same.

He lay in the dark and held the folded paper against his chest, over the reservoir, over the warmth that was half of what it had been, and he breathed.

The sleep was a long time coming. But when it came, it didn't carry him very far, and the numbers followed him down.
