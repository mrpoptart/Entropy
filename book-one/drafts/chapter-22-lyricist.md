# Chapter 22

The streets were empty in a way that only happened after midnight—not abandoned but resting, the city's breath drawn in and held, the stone cooling under a sky that had gone from black to the particular dark blue that meant dawn was still hours away.

Ash walked.

He'd left the overlook sometime after the sun went down. He wasn't sure when. The hours between afternoon and now had passed in a blur of movement—terraces descending, districts shifting around him, his feet finding their own route while his mind turned the same few things over and over like stones in a tumbler. Dorenne's pencil. Ryn's hand on his arm. The deployment chart. The numbers. The question Haran had asked in the workshop that felt like it belonged to a different lifetime and had only been two days ago.

*What do you want to build?*

He didn't have an answer. He was walking anyway.

The upper districts were behind him now. He'd come down through the administrative terraces in the last of the evening light, past the Council annex where the night staff moved behind lit windows, past the broad plaza where the fountain still ran on channeled pressure that someone—a crew of three, probably, splitting the cost from pools that couldn't spare it—had refreshed before going home. The water caught the lamplight and threw it back in fragments. He didn't stop.

He took the long way. Not because he'd decided to—his body had decided, the way bodies do when the mind is full and the legs are the only thing still operating on instinct. Down through the transitional streets where the upper terraces gave way to the middle districts, where the architecture shifted from the heavy permanence of elder-built stone to something lighter, more patched, the walls showing their history in layers of repair like geological strata. Each layer a generation. Each generation a little weaker than the last.

He walked through a district he'd reinforced three weeks ago.

The wall on his left—a retaining wall, thirty meters of dressed stone holding back the weight of the terrace above—glowed faintly with his work. Not visibly, not to anyone who wasn't looking. But he could feel it. The resonance of his own channeling in the stone, the signature of force he'd poured into the structure during a deployment that had taken him forty minutes and cost him roughly two hundred thousand kJ. He remembered the number because Dorenne had logged it. He remembered the wall because a woman had come out of the building above while he was working and handed him a cup of tea without a word.

The wall was solid. It would hold for years—decades, maybe, if nothing catastrophic happened. He'd done that. He'd pressed his hands to the cracked stone and poured a piece of himself into it, and now families slept behind it without knowing the wall had nearly failed, without knowing the name of the person who'd held it together, without knowing that the force in the stone was finite and borrowed and ticking down toward zero.

He kept walking. Past the wall, past the building, past the dark windows where people slept behind the warmth he'd given them. His boots on the stone. The sound of his own breathing. The reservoir sitting in his chest like a second heartbeat—steady, warm, diminished, its edges as clear as the walls of the room it had become.

Fifty percent. Give or take.

The number had stopped feeling like a wound somewhere in the last few hours. It hadn't become comfortable—it would never be comfortable—but it had settled into something he could carry without flinching every time it shifted. The way you adjusted to a pack on a long walk. The weight was there. You stopped noticing the straps.

He turned a corner and the district opened up ahead of him—a residential stretch that ran along the middle terrace, wider streets, buildings set back from the road, the kind of neighborhood where families had lived for three generations and would live for three more if the infrastructure held. If someone maintained it. If the pools didn't thin past the point where maintenance was possible and the cracks started winning.

The heating lattices in the paving stones were dim. Not dead—not yet—but fading, the amber glow that someone refreshed each morning already burning down by midnight. He felt the cold through his boots. A familiar sensation. He'd walked on cold stone his entire life, before the Wellspring, when cold stone was the default texture of his existence. He'd stopped noticing it during the months of power—months when the cold was a problem he could solve with a thought, when his body radiated enough ambient heat to warm the air around him, when the stone warmed under his feet because he couldn't help it, because the Wellspring bled energy like an overfull cup.

Now he noticed. The cold came up through the soles of his boots and into the bones of his feet and it felt like recognition. Like the city reminding him what it was actually made of, underneath all the channeling and the maintenance and the careful daily effort of a million people pouring themselves into stone.

Stone was cold. That was the truth under everything.

* * *

He passed Dorenne's office at a quarter past midnight.

He didn't plan it. The route had carried him along the Second Terrace's lower edge, below the administrative buildings, and her office window was visible from the street—third floor, eastern corner, the window he'd looked up at a thousand times from the plaza below when he was her aide and the lit window meant she was still working and he should bring tea.

The light was on.

He stopped. Not a decision—his feet simply stopped moving, the way you stop at the edge of a drop before your mind registers the height. He stood on the street below and looked up at the warm rectangle of light in the dark face of the building, and he could see, faintly, the shape of someone moving behind the glass. A figure at a desk. The particular silhouette of a woman who sat with her back straight and her elbows on the surface and her head bowed over something she was reading.

She was working. Of course she was working. It was past midnight and she was at her desk because the city didn't stop needing management at sundown, because the deployment schedule needed revision now that its central variable had walked out, because the aqueducts wouldn't wait and the western district sites wouldn't wait and the forty thousand people who drank from the Thornwall main every morning wouldn't wait for a young man to finish deciding whether he was a person or a resource.

She was doing what she'd always done. Managing. Calculating. Finding the optimal allocation for a system that was running down, that had been running down her entire career, that would keep running down after Ash was spent and forgotten and the Wellspring was a footnote in a ledger somewhere. She'd been doing this work before him. She'd do it after.

His chest ached. Not the sharp ache of the break—that had dulled through the hours of walking, worn smooth by repetition the way water wore stone. Something softer. Something that lived closer to gratitude than grief, though it had roots in both.

She'd given him a chance. That was real. The ramp she'd kept open. The posting she'd ignored the requirements for. The morning she'd looked at a man who couldn't activate a transit lift and seen something worth hiring. All of it real, all of it tangled with the calculation, and standing here in the dark looking up at her lit window he could hold both truths without needing to choose between them.

The care was real. The calculation was real. They lived in the same woman, drew from the same well, and the thing he'd learned today—the thing that had crystallized on the walk, slowly, the way ice formed on standing water—was that he didn't have to resolve them. He didn't have to decide whether Dorenne was the person who sat with him in the transit hub or the person who picked up the pencil. She was both. She would always be both. And the fact that he couldn't live inside her framework anymore didn't erase the years he'd spent inside it, didn't unmake the person she'd helped him become.

He looked at the light for a long time.

Then he put his hands in his pockets and walked on. The window shrank behind him. The light stayed.

* * *

The middle districts at night had a texture the upper terraces didn't—rougher, more honest, the kind of wear that showed up when no one was refreshing the surfaces every morning. Ash moved through it the way he'd moved through it his entire life: reading the cracks.

It was automatic. A skill from his years as Dorenne's aide, honed further during the months of deployment—the habit of looking at infrastructure the way a doctor looked at a patient, seeing the symptoms before they became emergencies. The hairline fracture in the retaining wall at the corner of Vessel Street. The discoloration where a water main was seeping behind the stone facade. The transit shaft three blocks down that he knew, from memory, ran at half capacity because the maintenance crew's combined pools couldn't sustain full operation.

He'd fixed a dozen sites in this district. He could point to each one—there, the bridge joint he'd reinforced on a Tuesday afternoon. There, the cistern wall he'd sealed in the rain. There, the heating lattice junction he'd rebuilt from the substrate up, a job that had taken him twenty minutes and would have taken a crew of four most of a day.

Every warm building a piece of himself he'd never get back.

The thought arrived without bitterness. That surprised him. He'd expected bitterness—had braced for it during the walk, the way you braced for the return of pain after the initial numbness faded. But the bitterness didn't come. What came instead was something he didn't have a word for. Not pride, exactly—pride implied he'd made a choice, and the spending hadn't felt like choosing. It had felt like breathing. You breathed because your lungs demanded it, and he'd channeled because the city demanded it and his body had been full of something that wanted out.

But standing in the dark on a street where the stone was warm because of him, looking at buildings where people slept in comfort because of him, feeling the hum of his own work in the infrastructure around him—

He'd do it again.

The knowledge settled into place with the quiet finality of a door latching. He'd do all of it again. Every site. Every expenditure. Every piece of himself he'd poured into stone and water and the invisible machinery that kept a city alive. Not because Dorenne's math justified it, not because the deployment chart said so, but because the woman who'd handed him tea had done it without knowing his name, and the families behind the wall were sleeping, and the water was flowing, and that was what the power was *for*.

It was just that now he knew the cost. Now he could see the walls of the room. And knowing the cost changed the calculation—not the value of what he'd done, but the question of what came next.

He walked on. The district thinned. The buildings grew older, more worn, the maintenance gaps visible even in the dark—cracks that had been patched and recracked and patched again, the perpetual cycle of a city trying to hold itself together with less and less. A heating lattice in a wall was fully dead, the stone dark and cold as the night air. Someone had hung a blanket over a window where the sealing had failed. A hand-chalked sign on a post read: *TRANSIT SHAFT CLOSED — USE STAIRS*.

Use stairs. He almost smiled.

The stairs had been his whole life, once. Forty flights to the market district, counted and recounted, his legs learning the rhythm of a pace that looked easy and cost him everything. The stairwells of Solathis had been his private country—the places where no one went because everyone had magic, the empty landings where he could let the smile drop and sit with the weight of what he was.

What he'd been. What he might be again.

The thought didn't terrify him the way it had this morning. This morning, in Dorenne's office, the word *dying* had fallen out of his mouth and surprised him with its accuracy. Not accurate in fact—the Wellspring's depletion wouldn't kill him—but accurate in shape. The end of the power would be a death of the person he'd become. The Wellspring Ash. The extraordinary one. The one who mattered.

But the person before the Wellspring—the one who climbed forty flights and fixed the cabinet hinge and made the porridge and smiled—

That person had been real too. That person had walked into Dorenne's office on a Tuesday morning and been hired not because he could channel but because he could carry things and take notes and make a joke about the transit lift. That person had built mechanical devices in Haran's workshop and understood gears and springs and the physics of leverage. That person had been worth something. Not enough—not by the world's measure, not by the measure that counted magic as the baseline for human value—but something.

He was still that person. Under the Wellspring, under the reservoir, under the months of power and deployment and the intoxicating, devastating experience of being extraordinary—he was still the man who climbed the stairs.

* * *

He found the broadsheet on a wall near the junction of the middle and lower districts.

Someone had pasted it to the stone with channeled adhesive—a sheet of heavy paper, hand-printed, the ink dark and the words crowded together with the urgency of people who had more to say than space to say it. He stopped because the lamplight caught the paper's edge and the whiteness of it stood out against the dark stone like a signal.

He read it standing in the street with his hands in his pockets and his breath making clouds in the cold air.

*TO THE COUNCIL AND THE ELDER GENERATION—*

*We, the young channelers of Solathis, demand an accounting. Our pools buy less than our parents' did. Our parents' pools bought less than theirs. The decline is measured. The decline is real. And while the elder districts maintain their comfort on pools five and ten times the size of ours, we are told to work harder, to be more efficient, to accept that the systems built for a different era are simply how the world works.*

*They are not how the world works. They are how the world is failing.*

*We demand redistribution of infrastructure priority. We demand transparency in allocation. We demand that someone—anyone—answer for the fact that our generation will inherit a city built to require power we do not have.*

The text went on. Signatures at the bottom—dozens of them, names he didn't recognize, the handwriting varied and urgent. Someone had added a note in the margin: *If this were a building, they'd call it condemned. But it's just us, so they call it normal.*

Ash stood in front of the broadsheet for a long time.

He read it again. And again. And each time the words rearranged themselves slightly, the way a pattern shifted when you changed the angle of your attention, and what he saw—what he couldn't stop seeing, once he'd seen it—was his own story.

Not the Wellspring. Before the Wellspring.

Twenty-three years of climbing stairs because the transit lift required a pulse he couldn't give. Twenty-three years of cold water because the heating lattice required a hand that worked. Twenty-three years of living in a world built for people who could do a thing he couldn't, being told that his struggles were personal—his condition, his limitation, his problem to solve with workarounds and good cheer and the kind of relentless self-management that looked, from the outside, like resilience and was, from the inside, exhaustion.

These young channelers—these people with pools that bought less every year, that stretched thinner every generation, that would one day buy nothing at all—they were living his life. The whole world was living his life. Slowly, generation by generation, the magic was draining, and everyone would eventually stand where he'd stood: in front of a system that required something they couldn't give, being told to try harder.

The world's reservoir was finite. His reservoir was finite. He was living at high speed what the world was going through in slow motion, and the broadsheet's anger—the desperate, clear-eyed fury of people who could see the decline and couldn't make anyone listen—was an anger he recognized in his own chest. Not as a feeling he'd ever named. As a shape he'd always carried.

He touched the paper. Just his fingers, lightly, on the edge. The ink was dry. The adhesive was still warm—whoever had posted it had done so recently, maybe an hour ago, channeling the paste to the wall in the dark because that was how you did things in a world where everything, even protest, ran on magic.

He dropped his hand. Stepped back.

The broadsheet stayed on the wall. The words stayed in his head. He turned downhill and kept walking, and the anger—not his anger, not theirs, but the shared shape of it, the geometry of a world running down—walked with him like a second shadow.

* * *

He heard the crew before he saw them.

The sound of work at night—tools on stone, the low murmur of coordination, the particular hiss of channeling applied to a fracture. It came from a side street two blocks into the lower-middle district, where the residential buildings crowded close and the water mains ran shallow under paving stones that had been relaid three times in living memory.

Ash turned the corner and stopped.

Four people, working under portable lamps that threw sharp shadows across the street. A cracked water main—the fracture visible as a dark line running diagonally across the paving, water seeping through in a slow, steady pulse that caught the lamplight and turned it into something that looked almost like blood. The crew had dug out the section, exposing the pipe where it ran through a channel of reinforced stone, and they were working in rotation—two bracing the surrounding structure, one mixing a compound by hand, and one channeling.

The one channeling was Maren.

She was on her knees beside the exposed pipe, both hands pressed flat against the stone, her eyes closed, her face lit by the lamps and drawn with the particular exhaustion of someone who'd been working for hours and had hours left to go. Her sleeves were pushed up past her elbows. Her forearms were taut. She channeled the way she did everything—precisely, without waste, each pulse of energy measured and directed with the discipline of a person who'd spent her entire life making every drop count.

Because she'd always had to.

Ash stood in the shadow of a doorway and watched his sister work.

Her pool flickered. He saw it—not with his eyes but with the sense the Wellspring had given him, the ability to feel channeling in his proximity the way you felt heat from a fire. Maren's pool was a small, steady flame, and it wavered. Dimmed. Steadied again. She adjusted—a tiny shift in her hand position, a redistribution of effort that would have been invisible to anyone who wasn't watching with exactly his attention—and the channeling resumed, thinner now, more careful, the precise calibration of someone spending the last of what they had.

The fracture sealed. Slowly—not the instantaneous fusion he could have managed, but a gradual closing, millimeter by millimeter, the crack knitting itself shut under the pressure of a pool that was barely adequate and a technique that was extraordinary. The stone changed color where her channeling touched it, darkening from gray to a deeper gray that meant the bond was setting, the structure was holding, the pipe was whole again.

She pulled her hands back. Flexed her fingers. The wince was small—the kind of wince that meant she'd used more than she should have, that she'd pushed into the margin where spending became an ache that would follow her home and into sleep and out the other side into tomorrow.

One of the crew—the compound mixer, a heavyset man with the tired eyes of someone on his third consecutive shift—said something Ash couldn't hear. Maren nodded. She wiped her face with the back of her hand, leaving a streak of dust across her cheek. She stood. Picked up her tools—a bag slung across one shoulder, the weight of it pulling at her frame in a way that said she'd been carrying it all night.

She moved to the next section. The crew moved with her. Another crack, another fracture, another piece of the city's circulatory system that needed a human being to kneel on cold stone and pour themselves into it to keep the water flowing.

This was what finitude looked like.

Not the dramatic, vertiginous finitude of the Wellspring—not the ocean discovering it had walls, not the math scene with its devastating columns of numbers and its inescapable conclusion. This was the other kind. The quiet kind. The kind Maren had been living with every day of her life, the kind every young channeler in Solathis was living with, the kind that didn't announce itself with a calculation but with the daily, grinding reality of not having enough and doing the work anyway.

She'd been here the whole time.

While he was channeling at Dorenne's direction, pouring his ocean into sites across the city, accomplishing in minutes what crews like this needed nights to finish—Maren had been here. On her knees. In the dark. Sealing cracks with a pool that bought less every year and a discipline that bought her more than raw power ever could. She'd been managing her finitude the way she managed everything—quietly, precisely, without asking for help and without complaining about the cost—because she'd never had the luxury of ignoring it.

The math from Chapter 19 hit him again. Not the numbers—not the columns, not the projections, not the declining curve of the Wellspring plotted against deployment rates. The other math. The comparison he'd made without meaning to, sitting at the desk with the candle burning down, when he'd looked at the numbers and thought of his sister's pool and realized: she's been here the whole time. Living with the countdown. Getting up every morning with less than yesterday and going to work anyway. He'd been doing in months what she'd been doing in years, what the whole world was doing in generations—spending something finite, watching it thin, and deciding each day what was worth the cost.

Maren finished the second repair. She stood again. Slower this time—the fatigue visible in the set of her shoulders, the way she shifted her weight, the half-second pause before she reached for her tool bag. She said something to the crew. One of them laughed—a short, tired sound that carried in the quiet street. She didn't laugh. She adjusted the bag on her shoulder and turned to survey the next section of pipe.

Her face, in profile, was very like his.

He stood in the doorway shadow with his hands at his sides and his throat tight and watched his sister do the work. The lamplight caught her cheekbone, her jaw, the dust on her face, the set of her mouth that said she was tired and wasn't done and wouldn't stop until the water was flowing. She looked—

She looked like their father. The same broad shoulders, the same patient exhaustion, the same quiet competence of a person who understood that the work was the work and it didn't care whether you had enough left to finish it. You finished it because it needed finishing. That was all.

His mouth opened.

*Maren.*

The word was right there. Her name. Two syllables. He could step out of the shadow and into the lamplight and she'd turn and see him, and—

And what.

What would he say? I watched you work. I saw your pool flicker. I know the math now—your math, the one you've been doing every day without a dramatic revelation, without a calculation scene, without the floor dropping out from under you because the floor was never there for you in the first place. I know what it costs you. I've always known. I just never—

He didn't call out.

He stood in the dark and watched her move to the next crack. Her hands found the stone. Her pool steadied—thinner now, dimmer, but steady. The channeling resumed. The crack began to close.

The crew worked around her. The lamps threw their shadows on the buildings. The water, somewhere behind the sealed pipe, began to flow again.

Ash turned away. His boots were quiet on the stone—the old instinct, the lifetime habit of moving through the world without taking up space. He walked out of the side street and back to the main road and his throat stayed tight for a long time.

* * *

The lower districts smelled different at night. Less stone, more earth—the damp, mineral scent of the gorge rising through the narrower streets, mixing with the residual warmth of buildings that had been heated during the day and were slowly giving it back. Cooking smells, faded. Wood smoke from the few households that still burned fuel. The particular ozone absence that marked the poorest streets—no channeling in the air at all, just the raw, unmediated world.

He knew these streets. He'd grown up three blocks from here, on the fifth floor of a building where the transit lift worked when someone's pool could spare it and the stairs were the reliable option. He'd mapped the creaking boards outside his parents' door. He'd learned to make porridge on an iron stove and fix cabinet hinges with a screwdriver and wash in cold water because the heating lattice required a hand he didn't have.

The streets led him without his choosing. Past the building where old Kessler had sold hand-made candles from a ground-floor window. Past the intersection where the gutter channeling had failed so long ago that moss grew in the drainage channel. Past the stairwell—the stairwell—where a small alcove held a stone bench that nobody used because nobody took the stairs.

He stopped.

The alcove was there. The bench was there. The cold stone where he'd sat a hundred times in the space between destinations, letting the smile drop, letting the scaffolding release, giving himself a minute before putting it all back up and walking into whatever came next.

He didn't sit. He looked at the bench and felt the ghost of every version of himself that had sat there—the teenager, the young man, the twenty-three-year-old on his way to the Council annex with saffron in his pocket and a stubbornness that wouldn't quit. The person who'd climbed forty flights to look at a posting he couldn't qualify for and been hired anyway by a woman who appreciated things that functioned under constraints.

That had been the beginning. Dorenne and the ramp and the job and the first morning he'd walked into the infrastructure office feeling like someone who belonged somewhere. The beginning of everything that had led, by a path he couldn't have predicted, to the Wellspring, to the power, to the months of extraordinary capacity, to the earthquake, to the deployment chart, to the math, to the break, to this—a man walking through a sleeping city at one in the morning with a finite reservoir in his chest and a question in his head that he couldn't answer yet.

*What do you want to build?*

The bench sat empty in the alcove. The stairwell stretched up above and down below, forty flights in each direction, and the echo of his footsteps would carry the way it always had—the sound of one person moving through a space designed for no one.

He moved on.

* * *

Haran's workshop was dark.

Ash stood outside the door with the key already in his hand—the key Haran had given him years ago, pressed into his palm with the casual weight of a man who didn't make speeches about things that mattered. *In case you need to work late. Or early. Or just need somewhere to be.* The key was iron, heavy, worn smooth by use. It fit the lock with the familiarity of a thing done ten thousand times.

He turned it. The lock clicked. The door swung open on oiled hinges—Haran maintained them himself, a drop of oil every week, the kind of small, precise maintenance that defined the man and the place—and the workshop breathed out at him.

Sawdust. Oil. The sharp tang of cut metal. The fainter smell of the coal stove in the corner, long since cooled but leaving its trace in the air the way a voice left its trace in a room after the speaking stopped. And under it all, the particular scent of wood shavings and mechanical lubricant and the indefinable something that accumulated in a space where people built things with their hands.

Ash stepped inside and closed the door behind him.

The darkness was total for a moment. Then his eyes adjusted and the workshop assembled itself out of shadows—the workbenches along the walls, the tool racks, the shelves of salvaged parts and half-finished projects, the lathe in the corner that Haran had restored from a junkyard find and maintained with a craftsman's obsessive care. The windows were small and high, letting in the faintest suggestion of moonlight that caught the metal edges of tools and turned them into small, pale signals in the dark.

He didn't channel a light. He didn't need to. His feet knew this floor. His hands knew these surfaces. He'd spent half his life in this room, and even in the dark his body mapped the space—three steps to the first bench, seven to his station, the gap between the lathe and the shelving unit that you had to turn sideways to pass through.

His workbench.

He reached it by feel. His hands found the edge—scarred wood, nicked and gouged by years of work, the surface carrying the record of every project he'd ever built and every mistake he'd ever made on it. A deep groove near the left edge where a chisel had slipped. A burn mark from the time he'd overheated a soldering iron. The smooth patch in the center where his forearms rested, worn to a polish by repetition.

He pulled out the stool and sat down.

The stool creaked. It had always creaked—a protest from the right rear leg that Haran had offered to fix and Ash had declined because the creak was a sound that meant he was here, in this place, doing this work, and some sounds you kept because they were the sounds of your life.

His workbench. His tools. In the dark, his hands found them: the small precision pliers in their leather sleeve. The set of screwdrivers, sized from needle-fine to heavy-gauge, standing in the wooden block he'd carved to hold them. The magnifying lens on its articulated arm. The jar of machine oil. The tray of springs and gears and tiny brass fittings that he sorted by size and kept within reach because you never knew which one you'd need until you needed it.

The projects were there too, arranged along the back of the bench in the order he'd left them—how long ago? Weeks. Months. Before the earthquake, before the deployment schedule consumed him, before the Wellspring became the only thing he was. He reached out in the dark and his fingers found the first one.

The self-winding lamp. A brass cylinder housing a spring mechanism connected to a small dynamo—the spring unwound at a controlled rate, the dynamo converted the motion to heat, the heat ignited a wick. No channeling. No magic. Just physics—the stored energy of a wound spring released through gears and leverage into light. He'd built three prototypes. The first one burned too fast. The second one burned unevenly. The third one—the one under his fingers now—burned clean and steady for four hours on a single winding.

Four hours of light from a wound spring. In a world where light was channeled, where every lamp required a person to pour their pool into it, where the simple act of illuminating a room cost something that was running out—four hours, free, renewable, powered by a hand on a crank and the patient ingenuity of a person who'd spent his life solving problems without magic.

His fingers moved to the next project. The spring-loaded pump. Not Haran's pump—the big one, the community project that ran on distributed micro-channeling and had twelve households turning a crank every morning. This was smaller. A tabletop model, designed for a single household—a hand-pumped mechanism with a spring accumulator that stored mechanical energy and released it in controlled bursts. Enough to move water from a ground-level cistern up two floors. Not fast. Not elegant. But functional, and independent, and the kind of solution that would still work when the magic was gone and the cisterns still needed filling.

The third project was the one he'd never finished. A geared mechanism—he wasn't sure what it was yet, hadn't been sure when he'd started it, had been working from intuition and the shape of a problem he could feel but not articulate. Gears of different sizes meshed on a common shaft, the ratios calibrated by hand, the tolerances tight enough that the mechanism moved with a smooth, continuous motion when he turned the input gear. It was precise. It was beautiful, in the way that well-made mechanical things were beautiful—the beauty of function, of parts that fit, of a system that worked because every piece was in the right place doing the right thing.

He didn't know what it was for. He'd figure it out. That was how the workshop worked—you built first and understood later, you let your hands find the shape of the solution before your mind caught up, and sometimes the thing you made turned out to be the answer to a question you hadn't known you were asking.

He sat at his workbench in the dark and held the geared mechanism in his hands. The brass was cold. His fingers found the input gear and turned it, slowly, and the mechanism responded—the gears meshing, the shaft turning, the smooth continuous motion of a system converting one kind of movement into another. Click. Click. Click. The sound of precision. The sound of things working.

He didn't channel.

His hands were on the mechanism and the reservoir was in his chest and he could feel the boundary between them—the edge where his body met the power, the place where intent became energy, the threshold he'd crossed a thousand times since activation. He could feel how easy it would be. A pulse into the brass. A warmth in the gears. The small, instinctive expenditure that the Wellspring offered constantly, the way a full cup offered to spill.

He didn't.

He sat with his hands on the cold brass and his feet on the worn floor and the dark workshop around him and he didn't channel. Not as a statement. Not as a decision. Not as the dramatic refusal of power that would make a good ending to a story about a man who learned to let go. He didn't channel because his hands were already doing something. They were holding a mechanism they'd built. They were turning a gear they'd cut. They were remembering the work—the patient, physical, human work of shaping metal with tools and fitting parts together and making something that functioned because the maker understood the principles and cared about the tolerances and had spent enough hours at this bench to know, in his hands, what right felt like.

His hands remembered.

The gears turned under his fingers. Click. Click. Click. A sound like a heartbeat. A sound like the ticking of a clock that measured something other than time—something slower, something that accumulated over years of practice and didn't deplete, didn't count down, didn't have a number attached to it that shrank with every use.

The work was always real.

Before the Wellspring. Before the activation, the euphoria, the months of extraordinary power, the earthquake, the deployments, the math, the break, the walk through the city—before all of it, there was this. A bench. A set of tools. A pair of hands that knew how to make things. The fundamental, unglamorous, irreplaceable skill of building something that worked.

*What do you want to build?*

He still didn't know. The question sat in the dark with him, patient as the tools, patient as the workshop itself. It didn't demand an answer tonight. It was a workbench question—the kind you came back to, the kind you turned over in your hands the way you turned a gear, feeling for the shape of it, testing the tolerances, letting the answer emerge from the work itself.

He'd come back to it. Tomorrow. And the day after. And the day after that.

He set the mechanism down. His hands found the pliers—not reaching for them, exactly, but settling where they wanted to be, the leather sleeve familiar under his fingers, the weight of the tool right in the way that only a tool you've used a thousand times is right. He picked them up. He picked up a brass fitting from the tray. He held both in the dark and felt the alignment of hand and tool and material that was the beginning of making.

Outside, the city slept. The heating lattices dimmed in the paving stones. The water flowed through a pipe Maren had sealed on her knees. The broadsheet hung on a wall in the middle district, its ink drying, its anger intact. The Director's light burned in her third-floor window. The transit lifts sat still in their shafts, waiting for morning, for the first hands of the day to press against the interface stones and feed them the pulse that made them rise. And somewhere, in a building Ash had reinforced, behind a wall that held his work in its bones, a family slept in warmth they didn't know was borrowed.

The workshop was quiet. The stool creaked when he shifted. The pliers were steady in his hand.

He didn't have the answer yet. He was in the right place to start.

Outside the high windows, the sky held the first faint suggestion of something—not dawn, not yet, but the particular quality of darkness that came just before it. The hour when the night was deepest and the turning had already begun, invisible, inevitable, the slow pivot toward a light that was coming whether anyone was ready for it or not.

Ash bent over his workbench. The pliers closed on the brass fitting. His hands began to work.
