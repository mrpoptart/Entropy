# Chapter 7

The new apartment smelled like fresh plaster and someone else's channeling.

Ash stood in the doorway and breathed it in — the clean mineral scent of walls that had been smoothed and sealed by a professional, the faint warmth of heating lattices that hummed at full strength, the absence of dust. Behind him, two men from Dorenne's office carried the last of the crates up the corridor, their footsteps steady on stone that didn't crack or settle or groan under the weight. The floor was level. Every surface was level. The door hung true in its frame, the hinges silent, the latch catching with a crisp, satisfied click when he pushed it closed.

Three rooms, plus a kitchen and a washroom. More space than his parents had ever had. The windows faced east, which meant morning light — not the grey, occluded light of the lower-middle band, filtered through buildings that leaned against each other like exhausted men, but clean light that came in at an angle and touched the far wall and made the pale stone glow.

He opened the window. Below, the street was broad and well-paved, the walkway edged with low walls that channelers had carved into smooth, rounded shapes — decorative, intentional, the kind of detail that existed only in districts where people had pool to spare on beauty. Across the street, a row of buildings matched this one: clean facades, intact rooflines, windows that sealed properly. No blankets hung over broken lattices. No cracks webbing up the eastern faces. A transit lift stood at the corner, and even from here he could see the schedule posted beside it — current, legible, the times printed in the crisp institutional hand of a maintenance office that actually maintained things.

He turned back to the apartment. The kitchen was twice the size of the old one. The stove had a channeling interface built into its surface — not mounted on the side like an afterthought, but integrated, a smooth disc of pale stone set flush with the iron top. No fire grate. No flint. The apartment didn't need them. Everything here ran on channeling, the way everything in the upper districts ran on channeling, because the people who lived here could afford to power the world around them without thinking about it.

His mother's old stove — the one with the iron fire grate, the one he'd lit every morning with three strikes of the flint — was buried under rubble in a building that would probably be condemned by the end of the month. The new stove was better. Everything here was better.

He pressed his palm flat against the kitchen wall. The heating lattice hummed under his skin, warm and steady, maintained by the building's channeling crew, powered by a rotation of residents whose pools were strong enough to keep the system running through the night. The stone was warm all the way through. Not the fragile, fading warmth of his mother's nightly channeling — the kind that started leaking away by midnight and left the walls cold by three in the morning — but a deep, structural warmth, the kind that came from lattices installed by professionals and maintained by people who had energy to spare.

His family would be warm tonight. All night. Without his mother spending herself to make it so.

He stood in the kitchen of the new apartment with his palm on the warm stone and his chest full of something that pressed outward against his ribs — not the reservoir, though the reservoir was there too, vast and patient. Something else. Something that felt like the first full breath after years of holding.

* * *

His mother arrived an hour later, leaning on his father's arm.

Her leg was healing — the elder-generation healer Dorenne had sent came every morning, and the bone was knitting with the accelerated precision of someone who understood anatomy the way a mason understood stone. She could walk now, carefully, with a cane his father had shaped from a piece of ash wood he'd found in the temporary housing's storage closet. The cane was simple and well-made, the handle worn smooth already from the grip of her palm.

Ash met them at the door. His father guided her through with the practiced attentiveness of a man whose body had learned to anticipate hers — a hand at her elbow, a half-step ahead to check the threshold, the slight shift in his weight that gave her something to lean against without making it look like support. Twenty-five years of marriage compressed into the choreography of moving through a doorway.

"Careful," his father said. "There's a step."

"I can see the step, Torren."

"Just mentioning it."

"You've mentioned it four times since the carriage."

His father's mouth did the thing it did — the almost-smile, the one that lived in the neighborhood of amusement without committing to full residence. He caught Ash's eye over her head and raised his eyebrows fractionally. Ash pressed his lips together and looked at the wall.

His mother stopped in the center of the main room. She leaned on the cane and looked around — at the windows, the walls, the pale stone floor, the intact ceiling with its smoothly integrated lighting strips. Her gaze moved slowly. She touched nothing.

"It's big," she said.

"Three rooms. Kitchen, washroom. The bedroom in the back has the best light — I thought you and Dad could—"

"It's very big, Ash."

She moved to the kitchen. The cane tapped against the stone — a new sound, rhythmic, the percussion of a woman learning to navigate a space that wasn't hers yet. She stopped at the stove. Her hand found the channeling interface, her thumb tracing its edge the way she might trace a scar — with knowledge, with recognition, without warmth.

"No fire grate," she said.

"It doesn't need one. The interface is integrated — you just—"

"I know how a stove works, Ashlyn."

His full name. The one she used when tenderness and correction occupied the same breath. He closed his mouth.

She stood at the stove for a long moment. Then she turned to the window and looked out at the street — the broad, well-paved street with its carved walkway edges and its functioning transit lift and its row of clean-faced buildings — and her expression was something Ash couldn't quite parse. Not displeasure. Not gratitude. Something between recognition and distance, the face of a woman standing in a room that was everything she should have wanted and not quite anything she knew.

"I'll make tea," she said.

"The kettle—"

"I brought one."

She had. It was in the crate his father was carrying — the dented copper kettle she'd used for fifteen years, the one with the dark spot on the bottom where the channeling interface on the old stove had burned hot one winter, the handle she'd wrapped in cord when the original grip cracked. She lifted it out of the crate herself, both hands, the cane propped against the counter. She filled it at the tap — the water ran warm here without anyone touching anything, a baseline the building's lattice maintained — and set it on the new stove and pressed her palm to the interface.

The water heated. The kettle made a sound. Not the same sound — the new stove's interface was smoother, more evenly distributed, and the kettle sat differently on the flat iron surface than it had on the old stove's uneven grate. The whistle was the same, though. The same thin, rising note that had been the first sound of every morning in Ash's memory, the signal that the kitchen was open, that the day had officially started, that his mother was at her post.

She made tea. Three cups. She carried two of them to the table — slowly, the cane hooked over her forearm, the cups held with the careful steadiness of a woman who refused to spill even when her body was arguing with her about walking. His father took his cup and sat. Ash took his and stayed standing, watching her move, watching the cost of each step register in the tightness around her eyes and the set of her jaw and the way her free hand found the counter, the wall, the chair back — anything solid — and pressed against it, drawing support without asking for it.

She sat down. She wrapped both hands around her cup. The warmth from the tea and the warmth from her own channeling — habitual, automatic, the low hum of a woman who had been warming things with her hands for her entire life — met in the ceramic and settled.

"It's a good apartment," his father said. He said it to the room, or to both of them, or to the space between. His voice had the careful weight of a man choosing each word like a tool, picking the right one for the job.

"It is," Ash said.

His mother drank her tea.

* * *

They went back to the old building on a Fifthday morning to pack what was salvageable.

Ash hadn't been inside since the day of the activation. He'd seen it from the street — the blown-out wall on the fifth floor, the structural bracing Sorren's team had installed to keep the rest of the building from following. The residents had been relocated, some to temporary housing, some to family in other districts. The Merrat family was with relatives near the western pool. Old Verath had gone to her daughter's, the cat tucked inside her coat.

The building was quiet. Not the quiet of a place at rest — the quiet of a place abandoned, the silence of stone that no longer carried the vibration of lives being lived inside it. The stairwell was intact but damaged, cracks running along the walls in patterns Ash recognized from his years of measuring them with his thumb. Wider now. The plaster was gone in places, exposing the old stone beneath — the high-era work that Sorren's team had noted, the channeling woven into the load paths by someone whose power dwarfed anything the current generation could produce.

His mother took the stairs slowly, his father on one side, the cane on the other. Ash climbed ahead, checking each landing the way Maren had checked them on the day of the activation — the day they'd carried his mother down these same stairs with her leg broken and his hands still warm and the power humming in his blood like something barely contained.

Fifth floor. The apartment door was propped open with a piece of rubble. Inside, the damage was worse than he'd prepared himself for. The bedroom ceiling was still collapsed — the beam that had pinned his mother lay where it had landed after he'd thrown it, half-embedded in the far wall, surrounded by shattered stone. The exterior wall was gone, replaced by a sheet of heavy canvas the structural team had hung as a temporary barrier. The canvas billowed in the wind, letting in light and cold and the sound of the street below.

The kitchen was partly intact. The stove was damaged but standing — the iron box tilted where the floor beneath it had cracked, the channeling interface dark, the fire grate buried under a layer of stone dust. The counter had held. The cabinets above it had not — the doors hung open or were missing entirely, the contents scattered.

His mother moved through the apartment like someone walking through a memory. Her cane tapped on the broken floor. She didn't speak. She didn't cry. She moved from room to room with the deliberate attention of a woman cataloguing losses, assessing what remained, making decisions with the part of her mind that ran on practical necessity while the rest of her did something Ash couldn't see.

She started with the kitchen. She opened the one intact cabinet and began taking things out — cups, plates, a wooden spoon with a burn mark on the handle, a small ceramic bowl with a crack running from the rim to the base. She held each object, turned it, and either set it in the crate his father had brought or set it on the counter.

The cracked bowl went in the crate.

Ash watched her. The bowl was broken — not just cracked but structurally compromised, the kind of damage that would split it the next time hot liquid touched it. He could see the fracture line running through the glaze, branching at the base where the ceramic had weakened. It would never hold soup again. It would never hold anything.

She set it in the crate, carefully, between a folded cloth and a stack of plates.

She moved to the bedroom. What was left of it. She navigated around the collapsed beam and the rubble with the cane and her husband's arm and the fierce, contained determination of a woman who would walk through the ruins of her own home on a broken leg and not be carried. She found a curtain that had been half-buried under stone — singed along one edge, the fabric discolored by dust and heat, but intact enough to fold. She folded it and put it in the crate.

"Leya," his father said. Quietly. The name was a question and an offer and a patience so deep it was nearly geological.

"I'm fine." She found another thing — a small wooden frame, empty, the picture it had held lost or destroyed. The wood was scorched on one side. She turned it over in her hands, running her thumb along the grain, and put it in the crate.

In the kitchen, behind the broken cabinet door, she found the old kettle. The dented copper one — the one she'd already brought to the new apartment, the one she'd used to make tea their first morning in the new place. But this wasn't that kettle. This was a different one. Older. Smaller. A tin kettle with a flat bottom and a handle that had been repaired twice — once with wire, once with a strip of leather — and a dent on the side where someone had dropped it years ago.

Ash hadn't seen this kettle in years. His mother had replaced it with the copper one — when? When he was twelve? Thirteen? He'd forgotten it existed.

She held it. She didn't turn it or examine it or test its structural integrity. She just held it, both hands, the way she held her tea. Then she put it in the crate, beside the cracked bowl, beside the singed curtain, beside the scorched picture frame.

"Mum, we have—" He stopped. He didn't know how to finish the sentence. *We have new things now. We have a new apartment with a new stove and warm walls and windows that seal and a transit lift that runs on time. We have everything we need. You don't have to carry the broken things.*

He didn't say any of it. Something in the set of her shoulders — the particular rigidity that meant she was holding herself together with the same disciplined force that Maren used, the same controlled stillness that meant the effort was immense and invisible — told him that the sentence had no ending she wanted to hear.

His father picked up the crate. He carried it the way he carried everything — steadily, without complaint, with the professional attention of a man who understood what labor looked like and did it regardless of whether the weight made sense.

They left the apartment. The stairwell was cold. The heating lattice on the third-floor landing was still dead — not just dead now, but irrelevant, because no one would ever channel into it again. The building was done. Whatever it had been — home, shelter, the place where Ash had pressed his thumb into the plaster every morning and felt the cracks growing — it was done.

Outside, the afternoon was grey and sharp. His mother stood on the walkway with her cane and her crate of broken things and looked up at the building. At the fifth floor. At the canvas billowing where the wall used to be.

"Let's go," she said.

* * *

He brought the gifts on a Secondday evening.

The copper kettle came first. He'd found it at a metalworker's shop in the middle districts — not the market district where his mother bought saffron, but a smaller street where the craftspeople worked in open-front workshops and the air smelled like hot metal and oil. The kettle was well-made, heavy, with a broad base and a spout that poured clean and a handle of turned wood that fit the grip of a hand naturally, without wrapping, without repair. It cost more than anything he'd ever bought. The money came from Dorenne — his salary, adjusted upward since the activation, since the testing, since the word *Wellspring* had entered the institutional vocabulary and rearranged his position in the Hall's hierarchy.

He carried it home — *home*, the word still awkward in his mouth, still pointed at a place he'd lived in for twelve days — and set it on the table after dinner.

"What's this?" his mother said.

"Open it."

She unwrapped the cloth. The copper caught the light from the channeling strips — warm, bright, the particular glow of metal that hadn't been dented or dropped or repaired with wire. She turned it in her hands. Her thumbs moved across the surface, finding the smoothness, the weight, the balance of a thing made well.

"It's beautiful," she said. She said it the way she said things that were true — simply, without elaboration, with a weight that lived in the word itself rather than in the performance of saying it.

"For the new stove. The old one — the copper one — it doesn't sit right on the interface. This one's made for integrated stovetops."

"The old one sits fine."

"Mum."

"It sits fine, Ash." But she was holding the new one, turning it, her hands learning its shape the way they'd learned the shape of every tool she'd ever used. "It's beautiful. Thank you."

That evening, after he'd gone to bed, he heard her in the kitchen. The tap running. The click of a cabinet door. He didn't get up.

In the morning, the new copper kettle sat on the stove. The old copper kettle — the dented one, the one with the cord-wrapped handle and the dark spot from the old stove's interface — was in the cabinet above the counter, on the highest shelf, pushed against the back wall. Not thrown away. Not given away. Put somewhere. Kept.

He noticed it when he reached for a cup. He looked at it for a moment — the dented copper, the wrapped handle, the dark spot — and then he took the cup and closed the cabinet and went to make tea with the new kettle, which sat on the stove the way it was supposed to and poured clean and heated evenly and was, in every measurable way, better.

He brought blankets next. Thick, well-made, the kind that came from the textile district's better workshops — properly channeled fiber, the warmth woven into the material at the molecular level by a skilled craftsperson, the kind of blanket that held heat all night without needing to be recharged. His parents had used layered bedding their whole marriage — three thin blankets stacked, because three thin blankets were cheaper than one good one, and the warmth was close enough if you didn't think about it.

His mother spread the new blanket on the bed. She ran her hand across it — the surface smooth, the warmth immediate, the channeled fiber humming faintly against her palm. She folded the old blankets and put them in the chest at the foot of the bed.

She didn't throw them away.

Tools for his father. A new set of woodworking chisels — forged steel, properly tempered, handles of oiled hardwood that seated in the palm without shifting. His father unwrapped them one at a time, holding each one up to the light, testing the edge with his thumb the way he tested everything — physically, directly, with the hands-on assessment of a man who trusted his body's knowledge more than anyone else's opinion.

"These are good," his father said. He said it with the quiet emphasis of a craftsman recognizing quality. Then he looked at Ash across the table, and his eyes held something that wasn't quite a question — or was a question, but one he'd learned not to ask, the way he'd learned not to ask a lot of things over twenty-three years of watching his son navigate a world that wasn't built for him.

His hand found Ash's shoulder. The grip was firm — the same grip from the night Ash had told them about the structural team, the same grip from a hundred other moments where his father's body said what his mouth couldn't. But the grip held a beat longer than usual. Not squeezing. Just staying. As if his hand needed to verify that the shoulder beneath it was the same shoulder it had always known.

"Thank you," his father said. He released the grip and picked up the first chisel again and turned it in the light, and the moment was over, and the question — *Did you know I loved you before this? Did you know I was proud of you when you couldn't do any of this?* — went unasked, the way it had always gone unasked, the way it would keep going unasked because asking it would mean naming something they'd both spent years learning to carry in silence.

* * *

The new neighborhood was quiet at night.

Not the quiet of exhaustion — the old building had been quiet at night too, but it was the quiet of people who'd spent their pools and their energy and their patience and had nothing left for noise. This was a different quiet. The quiet of sufficiency. Of walls thick enough to hold sound, of heating lattices that hummed at a frequency below hearing, of lives conducted at a volume that assumed privacy and expected it.

In the old building, Ash had known the rhythms of every family on his floor. The Verath cat padding through the hallway at dawn. The Merrat children arguing over breakfast. The couple two doors down whose laughter came through the walls on good nights and whose silence came through louder on bad ones. He'd known which doors stuck, which stairs creaked, which neighbors would nod and which would stop to talk and which would press a cup of tea into his hands on cold mornings without being asked and without making it charity.

Here, the neighbors smiled. They nodded. They held doors. A woman on the second floor introduced herself the first week — her name was Tellen, she worked in the records office, she hoped they'd settle in well. A man on the fourth floor brought a small cake on their third day, wrapped in cloth, the kind of gesture that was polite and calibrated and meant exactly what it appeared to mean: welcome, this is a good building, we are courteous here.

Nobody stopped to talk. Nobody pressed tea into his hands. Nobody borrowed sugar or asked after his mother's leg or watched him climb the stairs with the complicated expression that contained both pity and a kind of communal ownership — *this is our neighborhood's boy, this is the Torren family's son, this is ours*.

The people in this building didn't know the Torrens. They knew the Wellspring's family. The distinction was invisible to Ash and visible to everyone else — a membrane through which courtesy passed easily and familiarity did not. People were kind. People kept a distance that looked like respect and felt like glass.

His mother noticed. He could see it in the way she stood at the window some evenings, looking down at the well-paved street, her tea in her hands, her face carrying the particular stillness of a woman who was adjusting to a world that had changed shape around her without asking permission. She didn't complain. She wasn't the kind of woman who complained about having a nicer apartment and a healing leg and a son who brought her copper kettles. She drank her tea and looked out the window and said nothing, and her silence was loud in a way Ash registered without understanding, the way you register a change in air pressure without knowing it means a storm.

His father adapted. His father always adapted — it was the quality Ash had inherited most directly, the ability to settle into new circumstances the way water settled into new containers, taking the shape of whatever held it. His father found the nearest hardware supplier and the nearest bar and the nearest place where a man could sit with other men who worked with their hands, and within a week he had a routine. He carried his new chisels to jobs. He came home with sawdust on his sleeves. The apartment received him the way the old apartment had received him — as a place to set down the weight of the day, to eat, to sit with his wife, to be quiet in the particular way that twenty-five years of marriage made quiet meaningful.

But even his father stood in the corridor one evening, just standing, his hand on the wall beside the apartment door, his palm flat against the stone the way Ash's mother laid her palm against the heating lattice — touching, measuring, learning a surface that wasn't yet familiar. He stood there for maybe ten seconds. Then he went inside.

* * *

The heating lattice in the bedroom was the first thing Ash broke.

Two weeks into the new apartment. A cold morning — the kind where the air had an edge even in a well-heated building, the kind where the stone held a memory of the night's temperature in its deeper layers. He woke and the bedroom was comfortable, the lattice humming faintly in the walls, the warmth steady and structural and maintained. But his feet were cold. His feet were always cold in the morning — a lifetime of cold-water washing and unheated rooms had made his extremities skeptical of warmth, as if his body expected the heat to be temporary and refused to invest in believing otherwise.

He pressed his palm to the lattice.

*Just a little.*

The reservoir responded. The connection between his hand and the stone opened — not gradually, not with the careful modulation of someone who'd spent years learning to control their output. It opened the way it always opened: all at once, a flood through a channel that had no valve, no dimmer, no mechanism for small.

The lattice turned hot. Not warm — hot. The pale stone interface went from its steady, maintained hum to a high, bright whine in less than a second, the mineral structure absorbing the energy he poured into it and converting it to heat with the desperate efficiency of a system receiving ten times its design load. The wall around the interface grew warm. Then very warm. Then uncomfortable.

Ash pulled his hand back. The lattice didn't stop. The energy he'd dumped into it was still there — stored in the stone, radiating outward, the mineral structure glowing faintly in the morning light with a heat that made the air above it shimmer. The bedroom temperature was climbing. He could feel it on his face, on his arms, the room going from comfortable to stifling in the time it took him to cross to the window and push it open.

The cold air hit the warm room and condensation bloomed on every surface — the walls, the window frame, the mirror above the washbasin. Water droplets formed and ran in tracks down the pale stone. The lattice was still radiating, still hot, the energy working through the mineral on its own timeline, indifferent to his intentions.

A crack appeared. Small — a hairline, running from the edge of the interface stone to the corner of the wall. The sound it made was thin and sharp, like a fingernail on glass.

"Ash?"

Maren's voice, from the next room. She was staying with them while the family settled — sleeping on a pallet in the front room, arriving each evening with food and her quiet, disciplined presence and leaving each morning before anyone else was up. She appeared in the bedroom doorway in her sleeping clothes, her hair loose, her face carrying the focused attention of someone who'd been woken by a sound she recognized.

She looked at the lattice. At the shimmer in the air. At the condensation on the walls. At the hairline crack.

She crossed the room and placed both palms flat on the wall, flanking the overloaded interface. Her eyes closed. The stillness settled into her — the channeling stillness, the focused compression of effort that Ash had seen in Sorren's team, in the examiners, in every skilled channeler he'd watched work. But Maren's version was quieter than any of theirs. Smaller. The stillness of a woman who had learned to do precise work with limited resources, who got more out of her pool than most through sheer discipline and years of practice.

The lattice cooled. Slowly — the energy didn't disappear, couldn't disappear, but Maren drew it outward through the stone, redistributing it along the wall's full surface, spreading the concentrated heat into the building's thermal mass where it could dissipate over hours instead of minutes. The shimmer faded. The whine dropped to a hum, then to silence. The crack remained — she couldn't undo that, couldn't reverse the fracture in the mineral structure, but she sealed it, her channeling moving along the fracture line with the delicate precision of a needle following a thread.

She opened her eyes. Pulled her hands from the wall and flexed her fingers — the wince, the one that meant she'd used a meaningful portion of what she had for the day.

"The interface needs to be recalibrated," she said. "The mineral took too much charge. It'll run hot for a day or two until the excess bleeds off."

"I'm sorry. I was trying to—"

"I know." She looked at him. Her expression was composed, unreadable, the face she wore the way he wore his smile — so habitual it was nearly invisible. "Just don't touch it for a while."

She went back to the front room. He heard her folding her blankets, beginning her morning routine, the quiet sounds of a woman who had been woken an hour early by her brother's inability to do gently what she did as naturally as breathing.

He looked at the crack in the wall. Hairline. Sealed now, invisible unless you knew where to look. But he knew where to look. He'd spent twenty-three years mapping cracks.

* * *

It happened again three days later. The lighting strip in the kitchen.

He'd been reaching for a cup — early morning, the apartment dark, the lighting strips dormant in their recesses along the ceiling. In the old apartment, lighting had been his mother's domain, a morning ritual: she'd walk from room to room pressing her palm to each interface, and the strips would glow with the warm, steady light of her channeling, and the apartment would wake up around her like something being tended. Here, the lighting ran on a shared building system — a channeler on the maintenance rotation activated the common circuits each morning, and individual apartments drew from the shared feed through their own interfaces.

Ash touched the kitchen interface out of habit. His hand found the smooth stone beside the doorframe — the same position as the old apartment, the same reflexive reach — and the reservoir responded before he could think about scale.

The lighting strips flared. Not the warm, measured glow of the building's shared system — a white, flat blaze that filled the kitchen like a struck match in a dark room. He flinched. The light burned afterimages into his vision — purple ghosts of the kitchen counter, the stove, the kettle on its surface. The strips buzzed, a high, angry sound, and the one nearest the interface cracked along its mounting seam with a pop that sent a chip of mineral skittering across the floor.

He pulled his hand away. The light dropped — not to the normal warm glow but to an uneven flicker, some strips bright and some dim and one dark entirely, the cracked one, dead in its recess like a closed eye.

He stood in the flickering kitchen and looked at his hand. The palm was warm. Not burned — warm, the same persistent background radiation that lived in his skin now, the reservoir's presence expressed as a temperature he was learning to ignore.

A maintenance worker came that afternoon. A young woman, late twenties, with the efficient manner of someone who serviced half a dozen buildings in the district. She replaced the cracked strip, recalibrated the interface, tested the circuit. She didn't ask what had happened. She looked at the damage, looked at Ash, and something moved behind her expression — recognition, maybe, or the careful blankness of a person who had heard about the Wellspring and was now standing in his kitchen looking at the evidence.

"Try not to touch the interface directly," she said. "Use the secondary panel." She pointed to a small stone disc set into the wall at waist height — a lower-power access point, designed for fine adjustment, the kind of control Ash didn't have. "It's got a limiter built in."

"Thank you."

She left. The lighting worked. The crack in the ceiling where the strip had broken was invisible after she sealed it — the same invisible repair, the same evidence erased, the same small damage absorbed by someone else's competence.

Maren was in the front room when he came out of the kitchen. She was sitting cross-legged on her pallet, reading, her hair pulled back, her posture straight. She didn't look up.

"I heard," she said.

"It was just the lighting."

"I know." She turned a page. "The secondary panel has a limiter. Use that."

"The maintenance worker said the same thing."

"Good." She read. Her composure was perfect — the surface still, the discipline intact, the carefully constructed ease of a woman who had spent her life not adding to the family's burden of struggle. She turned another page, and her hand was steady, and her face was calm, and she said nothing else about the lighting strip or the crack or the maintenance worker or the pattern that was building — one small break at a time, one quiet repair at a time — in the walls of the apartment her brother's power was supposed to be protecting.

* * *

The third time was the washroom.

He'd been trying to heat the basin — a deliberate attempt, this time, not an accident. He wanted to prove he could do something small. Something domestic. Something his mother did every morning without thinking, the basic act of warming water, the foundational kindness of magic that every child in Solathis learned by age five.

He touched the basin's heating interface. He tried — genuinely, consciously tried — to find a thread instead of a river. He thought about the calibration stones in Vasra's basement, the practice stones meant for children, the way each one had shattered under the lightest touch he could manage. He thought about Vasra's careful words: *orders of magnitude*. He thought about the younger examiner's face, the blankness of demolished assumptions.

He pushed. As gently as he could. The gentlest push he had — the minimum, the floor, the absolute least he could give.

The water went from cold to boiling in four seconds. Steam erupted from the basin, filling the washroom, fogging the mirror, turning the air into a wet, dense wall of heat. The stone surface of the basin cracked — not the dramatic explosion of the calibration stones, but a slow, definitive split that ran from the heating interface to the drain, the ceramic parting along a stress line that his channeling had created and his channeling couldn't undo.

Water ran through the crack onto the floor. Hot water, steaming, spreading across the stone in a thin sheet that reached the door before he could move.

He stepped back. His feet were wet. The washroom was a cloud of steam and the sound of water running where it shouldn't and the basin was ruined and the heating interface was dark and the mirror was opaque with condensation and the whole small room was evidence of his inability to do the simplest thing in the world.

He cleaned it up. Towels on the floor, the water mopped, the basin emptied by hand — by hand, the way he'd done everything for twenty-three years, because his hands knew how to clean up a mess even if they didn't know how to stop making one. He opened the window to clear the steam. The cold air came in and the condensation ran down the walls in the same tracks it had run in the bedroom three days ago, the same water on the same pale stone, the same evidence.

Maren fixed the basin the next morning. She was there when he came out of the bedroom — already in the washroom, already kneeling on the floor, her palms on the cracked ceramic, her eyes closed. The crack sealed under her hands with the same delicate precision she'd used on the heating lattice — slow, controlled, the channeling moving along the fracture line like thread closing a seam.

She didn't look up when he appeared in the doorway.

"It's the glaze," she said. "The crack went through the substrate. I've sealed it, but the interface took damage. You'll need the maintenance worker again."

"Maren—"

"It's fine." She stood. She flexed her fingers. The wince. "Just don't use the basin interface until it's recalibrated."

She walked past him to the kitchen. He heard her fill the kettle — the new copper one, the one he'd bought — and set it on the stove. The sound of water heating, of morning beginning, of the apartment absorbing another small repair into the texture of its daily life.

He stood in the washroom doorway and looked at the basin. The crack was invisible now. Maren's work was always invisible — precise, efficient, leaving no trace except the faint residual warmth of her channeling in the ceramic and the cost of it in her pool, the meaningful portion of her daily budget spent repairing what he'd broken before breakfast.

The maintenance worker came again that afternoon. The same young woman. She recalibrated the basin interface, tested the flow, checked the surrounding lattice for stress. She made a note on a small card she carried — the same card, Ash realized, that she'd made a note on after the lighting strip. A record. A log of repairs. A pattern, documented in the crisp hand of a professional who serviced half a dozen buildings and had never needed to visit the same apartment three times in two weeks.

"The limiter on the secondary panel," she said. She pointed. The same advice. The same careful, neutral tone.

"I know," Ash said. "I'll use it."

She looked at him. Her expression held something that had migrated from careful blankness to something more human — not pity, not judgment, but the particular weariness of a person who fixed things for a living and was watching someone break them faster than she could schedule the repairs.

"I'll come back on Fifthday," she said. "Check the whole apartment. Preventive."

She left. The basin worked. The lighting worked. The heating lattice worked. Everything in the apartment worked, because Maren repaired what Ash broke and the maintenance worker repaired what Maren couldn't, and the small damages were absorbed and the cracks were sealed and the evidence was erased and the pattern continued — invisible, cumulative, documented on a card in a maintenance worker's pocket.

* * *

On a Fourthday evening, two weeks after the move, Ash came home with one more gift.

The apartment was warm and lit and his parents were at the table. His mother had cooked — something with cumin and onions, the smell filling the kitchen the way it had filled the old kitchen, the one familiar thing that had survived the move completely intact. The scent of his mother's cooking was the same in any kitchen, on any stove, in any apartment. It was hers. It traveled with her.

He set the package on the table. "For you, Mum."

She wiped her hands on a cloth and opened it. Inside was a bowl. Ceramic, pale blue, well-made — the glaze smooth and even, the rim perfectly round, the base weighted for stability. It was the same size as the cracked bowl she'd packed from the old apartment. The one that would split if hot liquid touched it. The one that was useless and broken and sitting in the crate in the corner of the bedroom where she'd put the things she'd salvaged.

"To replace the old one," he said. "I saw it in the market district. Same size."

His mother held the bowl. She turned it in her hands, her thumbs moving across the glaze, finding the smoothness. The bowl was beautiful. It was whole. It was everything the old one wasn't.

"Thank you, Ashlyn." She said his full name with the tenderness version — the one that meant love without correction, the one she used when she was holding something she wanted him to know she valued. She set the bowl on the counter, beside the new copper kettle.

She did not throw away the cracked one.

He didn't notice.

Later, after dinner, after his father had gone to the bedroom and his mother had finished washing the dishes — by hand, with water she heated through the stove interface and soap she'd brought from the old apartment and the particular rhythm of a woman who washed dishes the same way regardless of which kitchen she stood in — she opened the cabinet. The high one. The one above the counter.

The old copper kettle was there. The dented one. The one with the cord-wrapped handle. She moved it to the side, making room, and placed the cracked bowl beside it. The bowl and the kettle sat together on the highest shelf, pushed against the back wall — the cracked and the dented, the broken and the kept, the things that had no practical use and occupied space that could have held the new things her son kept bringing home.

She closed the cabinet. She turned off the kitchen light — through the secondary panel, the limiter, the careful way. She went to bed.

Ash was in his room. He lay in the dark and felt the reservoir humming behind his ribs, vast and patient, and listened to the building's quiet — the good quiet, the quiet of sufficiency, of walls that held and lattices that worked and neighbors who smiled and held doors and didn't know his name.

The apartment was warm. His family was safe. His mother had a new kettle and a new bowl and a bedroom with thick blankets and a stove that didn't need flint. His father had good chisels. Everything was better. Everything was measurably, demonstrably, undeniably better than it had been three weeks ago, when they'd lived in a building that groaned on cold mornings and the heating died at three a.m. and the cracks grew a handspan per week and the only warmth came from a woman spending herself one pool at a time.

He was providing. He was, for the first time in his life, the person who provided instead of the person who was provided for. Every gift was proof. Every new thing that replaced an old thing was evidence that the power was good, that the Wellspring was good, that the change in his life was translating into change in theirs. This was what the power was for. Not the testing, not the press event, not the instruments and the measurements and the word *Wellspring* stamped on documents he'd never read. This. The apartment. The kettle. The bowl. The blankets. The look on his father's face when he held the chisels up to the light.

He closed his eyes. The humming didn't stop. It was the sound of everything he could do now, everything he could give, everything he could be. It was the sound of not being a burden.

In the kitchen, in the cabinet, on the highest shelf, the old kettle sat beside the cracked bowl in the dark. Keeping each other company. Keeping the shape of a life that had been lived in a building that groaned and a kitchen that went cold and a world where warmth cost something and the cost was carried by a woman who never once complained about the carrying.

The apartment was quiet. The neighborhood was quiet. The distance between the old life and the new one grew by another day, measured in new things and polite smiles and cracks that were sealed before anyone could see them.

The old kettle stayed in the cabinet.

Nobody took it out. Nobody threw it away.

It just stayed — dented, repaired, useless, kept — in the dark, on the highest shelf, where his mother's hands had placed it, where her hands could find it again if she ever needed to hold something that remembered what warmth used to cost.
