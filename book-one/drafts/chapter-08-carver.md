# Chapter 8

The workshop smelled the same.

Sawdust and oil and the particular bite of cut metal—a combination Ash had absorbed so many times it had stopped registering as smell and become something closer to state of mind. He stood in the doorway for a moment and let it settle over him.

Inside: the long workbench along the east wall, covered in its permanent archaeology of projects—finished things stacked to the left, current things spread in the middle, salvage and raw material heaped toward the right without ceremony. Three lamps hung from the ceiling, two lit and one dark. The cold shelf in the corner was propped open with a folded cloth. A coil of copper pipe sat on the floor near the door, still tied with its market twine.

Haran was at the bench.

He had the frame of a water pump half-assembled in front of him—the cylinder and piston housing laid bare, the fittings and connectors arranged in rows with the deliberateness of a man who thought spatially and wanted to see the whole before touching any part. He was fitting a coupling, working it by hand, and he didn't look up when Ash came in.

"Close the door."

Ash closed it. The latch caught with the familiar resistance—stiff on cold mornings, easier once the workshop warmed—and he crossed to the workbench.

Two tinkerers were working further back. A younger woman—early twenties, her hair pulled up and pinned with what looked like a shim—was bent over a gear assembly at the secondary bench. A man with close-cropped gray at his temples was marking out measurements on a length of iron bar with a scribe and a practiced eye. Neither of them acknowledged Ash beyond a brief glance. He knew them by sight: Sellie and Vorn. They'd been coming to the workshop for years.

"The Ketterbach Building," Haran said.

Ash looked at the pump frame.

"District water system's been failing. Third time this year the main pressure dropped enough that the upper floors couldn't draw." He turned the coupling in his fingers, testing the thread. "They've been pushing it through with channeling. Four people on rotation, covering for a pump that needs replacing. Four people who have other work."

"How long has the pump been out?"

"Eight months." Haran set the coupling down and picked up a different one. "They kept thinking they'd get it repaired. Kept not getting it repaired. That's how it goes."

That was how it went. Ash had spent enough time in fraying districts to know the pattern—the repair that got deferred once, then twice, then became load-bearing in everyone's schedule until the schedule itself was the problem.

"What do you need?"

"Mostly patience." Haran held the coupling up against the lamp. "Yours or mine, I haven't decided."

He set the coupling in position against the cylinder housing and reached for his hand clamp. His movements were unhurried, the economy of motion that came from long practice—reaching for the right tool without looking because the tools had been in the same places for thirty years.

Ash watched. The pump frame was clean work—Haran's design, the lines functional and unadorned, nothing there that didn't need to be there. The cylinder housing was cast iron with a machined seat. The piston rod was steel, smooth, the tolerances tight. The whole assembly was built to be dismantled—every joint a fitting, every component accessible, every piece designed to be replaced by whoever came next without needing to know who had built it.

Ash lasted about thirty seconds.

There was a stuck fitting on the inlet side—a threaded brass collar that had seized, probably from the previous pump's corrosion. He could see it from where he stood. The threads were visible under the collar flange, and they'd bonded in place. Someone had tried to turn it with a pipe wrench—the flats were slightly chewed.

He reached in. Let the reservoir pulse once, pushing warmth and force into the metal at the point of adhesion.

The collar turned. More than turned—released completely, the threads giving all at once.

He set it down, satisfied. Then he looked at it.

The brass was slightly darker where he'd touched it. The thread surfaces—which had been seized but intact—had micro-fused. The collar would thread on. It would hold. But it wouldn't come off again without tools and effort and probably damage.

*That works fine*, he thought. *It'll hold for years.*

He put the collar on the bench.

The piston rod needed final straightening before installation—a slight bend near the top, the kind of thing that came from storage or handling, almost imperceptible to the eye but enough to create drag in the cylinder bore. Ash turned it in his hands, found the bend, and channeled into the metal.

The rod straightened. Also stiffened. The controlled flex that a piston rod needed—slight, distributed, a material property as much as a dimension—was gone. The steel was rigid now, crystalline, the grain structure altered by the energy he'd pushed through it. It would work. It would almost certainly work for years. But it would be more brittle than designed, and when it eventually failed, it would fail suddenly and completely instead of showing wear first.

He set it beside the collar.

Haran's hands hadn't stopped moving. He was fitting the cylinder head now, working the bolts down in sequence—two turns each, working across the pattern, drawing the joint even. He'd done this ten thousand times. His hands knew the pattern without consulting any part of his mind that used words.

Sellie glanced up from her gear assembly. Her eyes moved across the bench—the collar, the stiffened rod, the pump frame with its half-fitted components—and then back to her work. Something in her expression had adjusted, the way expression adjusts when you've registered information you're filing for later. She turned a gear with her thumb and went back to measuring.

There was a pipe that needed bending—a copper run that would carry water from the pump outlet to the main riser. The radius was marked on the bench: a gentle curve, nothing severe. Ash had bent hundreds of pipes. He picked it up, seated it against the floor peg Haran used as a forming guide, and channeled.

The pipe bent. Fast—faster than hand work, faster than a proper bending spring and steady pressure would have bent it. The copper took the shape exactly. It also work-hardened, the crystalline structure driven into rigidity by the speed and force of the bend. A pipe bent this way would hold its angle under pressure. It would not flex or adjust. If the building settled—if the riser shifted, if the fitting moved—the pipe would transmit that stress as a point load instead of distributing it. At some load, it would crack.

That load might not arrive for a decade. It might not arrive at all.

Ash set the pipe beside the other components.

Vorn had stopped marking his iron bar. He was looking at the bench with the same careful neutrality Sellie had—reading it the way you read a structure, finding the stress points, not making any particular face about what he found. He picked up his scribe and went back to work.

The pump needed a valve calibrated.

This was different from the fittings and the pipe. The valve seat was a precision component—a cone of machined brass, the sealing surface ground smooth to tolerances tighter than Ash could see with his eyes. It sat in a housing, and the task was to feel its position, test it against the seat, and adjust the stem setting until the seal was perfect. Too loose and the pump bled pressure. Too tight and the valve wouldn't open under normal working force.

He'd done this before. Dozens of times. His hands knew how—the sensitivity in his fingertips, the slight back-pressure when the cone seated correctly, the particular give that meant the fit was right. Physical work. The best kind.

He picked up the valve assembly.

He tried. Threaded the stem, felt for the seat. And the power came with him, rising under his palms before he could stop it, flooding the metal with a sensitivity that was also force—every attempt to feel the valve translated into a push, a nudge, a micro-correction that was itself too large by a factor of ten. He could feel everything, the entire geometry of the valve in exquisite detail, but his touch kept intervening. Too much. Each adjustment compressed the seat a fraction too hard, then the next correction overshot the other way.

He backed off. Tried again, more carefully.

The stem turned. He felt the seat engage. He applied the barest pressure to test the seal—

The valve housing shifted. The brass cone tipped off its axis, and the housing cracked.

Not badly—a hairline, running along the casting where the material was thin. The valve was still functional, probably. But the crack was there.

He set it down. His hands were steady. The reservoir behind his ribs hadn't shifted.

He looked at the hairline crack in the brass.

*The same hands*, he thought. *Same hands that did this a hundred times.*

He put the valve assembly on the bench and stepped back.

"Hm," Haran said. He'd been watching—or not watching, but the kind of not-watching that missed nothing. He set down his wrench and picked up the valve housing. He turned it, found the crack, and ran his thumbnail along it. He set it back down. He picked up the valve stem instead, threaded it in by hand, and paused.

"Your hands still work," he said.

Ash said nothing.

"The power doesn't replace them." Haran seated the cone, both hands on the assembly, and began to feel for the valve position. His movements were small. Smaller than Ash had thought was possible—adjustments measured in fractions of degrees, pressures that came from the tendons of his fingers rather than his wrists or forearms. "It's another tool. You don't use a hammer on glass."

He worked the stem. His eyes weren't on the valve—they were on the middle distance, looking at nothing, listening through his hands. His expression was the same expression he wore when solving any problem: interested, patient, expecting no particular answer.

The valve settled. Ash heard it—the small, definitive sound of a precision surface finding its mate. Haran tested the stem: resistance, then release, a clean and repeatable action.

He set the assembly down.

"The crack won't propagate under normal use. I'll pin it." He picked up his wrench again. "The collar and the rod will hold. The pipe is fine."

He left a pause that Ash didn't fill.

"But the rod will snap when it fails instead of bending. And the collar will cost someone an afternoon when they need to remove it." Haran held up a bolt and examined its thread. "Not your problem, probably. Theirs. Whoever fixes the pump five years from now."

He threaded the bolt in.

"Come here."

Ash moved to the bench. Haran was setting up a test assembly—a short section of pipe, a fitting, a spool of copper wire. Practice materials.

"Give me your hand."

Ash put his hand on the pipe. Haran's hand covered his, dry and warm, the skin rough and mapped with decades of callus.

"Feel the pipe."

Ash felt it. The metal—cool, slightly rough with scale on the outside, smooth machined bore inside. He could feel all of it, every surface, the grain structure if he pushed into it, the specific displacement of air around the threads.

"Now feel it without pushing into it."

He didn't know what that meant. He tried anyway. Pulled back from the information, stopped reading the grain. Just—the pipe. Cylindrical, cool, present.

"Better. Now." Haran's hand moved on top of his, guiding—not demonstrating, exactly, more like suggesting a direction. "A thread of warmth. Not heat. A thread."

He tried. The reservoir rose—immediately, abundantly, the way it always did when he let it—

"No." Haran's hand pressed slightly. "Less."

Less was not a concept that translated. Less was like asking a river to be a trickle. He clenched the output down mentally and pushed—

The pipe was warm under his hand. The wire on the spool smoked.

He let go.

Haran studied the wire. Reached over and unspooled a clean section. "Again."

Ash tried again. The same result—different only in that the wire caught this time instead of just smoking.

Haran unwound more wire without comment. He was not impatient. He'd been watching tinkerers fail at problems his whole life. He had the patience of a man who knew that failure was the majority of the process and had stopped resenting its cost.

"You know precision," Haran said. He wasn't looking at Ash. He was straightening the new wire section with his fingers—a slow, even tension applied along its length. "You've worked it your whole life. You fixed that calibration valve correctly fifty times before today."

He set the wire in place.

"Your hands haven't forgotten that. They can't." He tapped his own fingers on the bench—not impatiently. A gesture that meant: here, this is the thing. "The power doesn't know what your hands know. You need to teach it."

He took Ash's hand again. Placed it back on the pipe.

"The valve work. When you calibrated it by hand, before. What were you feeling for?"

"Back-pressure. The cone seating. There's a specific resistance when it's right—you can feel the whole seat load through the stem."

"What else?"

Ash thought. "Temperature change. The brass warms up slightly when the seat makes full contact—friction from the setting motion. You feel for the point where the warmth stops increasing."

Haran's hand didn't move. "And your hands felt that."

"Yes."

"So your hands already know what precision feels like. They know what they're looking for." He lifted his hand from Ash's. "The power is interference. You're trying to channel *and* feel at the same time, and the channeling is louder than the feeling. Turn the channeling down until the feeling comes back."

*That's not how it works*, Ash thought. *That's not—*

He tried it anyway.

He touched the pipe. Let the reservoir surface rise—just surface, the way oil floated on water. Not pushing. Not reading. Just present, available, like a hand held open.

His fingertips told him: metal, cool, slightly rough, the scale pattern from manufacture.

He pushed the barest thread of warmth.

The pipe warmed. Slightly. A degree, perhaps two. The wire didn't smoke.

He held it there.

His hands were still. The pipe was warm. The wire was intact. The amount he'd channeled was so small that he couldn't measure it against the reservoir—it registered as nothing, a rounding error in an ocean.

"Good," Haran said. He said it the way he said it when a problem yielded—quietly, as if the word were a fact and not a reward. "That's the direction."

He went back to the pump. The cylinder head was fitted now; he was working the inlet manifold into position, testing the geometry against his design. His hands moved with that familiar economy—the same motion repeated, adjusted, refined. He reached for a fitting and channeled it warm without looking up—just a flick of warmth to loosen the thread, so natural it was barely a gesture.

Ash watched his hands.

He'd been watching Haran work for years—every afternoon in this workshop, hundreds of hours of shared silence over problems and pipe and metal shavings. He'd seen Haran heat the workshop on cold days, light the third lamp with a wave, warm a cup of tea without reaching for the kettle. He'd always read those moments as utility—a man with a small trickle using it efficiently for small tasks.

He looked again.

Haran's hand on the fitting. The warmth that came from it—controlled, specific, limited exactly to the thread and not a millimeter beyond. No waste heat in the air, no ambient warmth. The energy had gone where he directed it and nowhere else.

He thought about the efficiency tables he'd half-memorized since the activation. What that looked like in numbers. A master channeler, maybe 90% efficiency, almost no waste—

He watched Haran pick up a lamp and light it with a gesture.

The flame appeared at the wick and nowhere else. No surrounding warmth, no spread. Just the wick, lit, from a distance of half a meter.

*That's not a small trickle*, Ash thought. *That can't be.*

He watched Haran work the manifold fitting. The old man's hands moved with the certainty of someone who didn't need to think about the motion—decades of the same work, the knowledge distributed through his body. But the channeling was happening alongside it, woven into it, not replacing it but augmenting at specific moments—warmth to loosen, a thread of force to hold alignment while both hands moved, a pulse of energy so small it barely registered.

The casualness of it. Not the power—the casualness. The way you held something heavy for a long time and stopped noticing the weight, except Haran wasn't holding something light. He was doing something difficult with the efficiency of someone who had done it so many times that the difficulty had become invisible.

Ash thought about the day Haran had told him the workshop heated itself on cold mornings. *I use what I've got*, he'd said. Nothing more. Ash had understood it as a small man making small use of small means. It had never occurred to him to ask what "what I've got" actually meant.

He looked at the lamp. The clean flame. The zero waste.

He thought: *How old is Haran? Late sixties. Maybe seventy. Elder generation. What does an elder's trickle look like?*

The numbers arrived without effort. He'd learned to do math quickly since the activation—the Wellspring's scale had made quantities vivid in a way they hadn't been before. An elder with a strong trickle. Decades of formal training. Efficiency at master level.

*He could have maintained a district.*

The thought settled with the quiet weight of something obvious arriving late.

Haran set the manifold in place and tightened the last bolt. He looked up. His eyes found Ash's face and he read it without effort—the expression of a man who'd just understood something he should have understood years ago. There was no reaction on Haran's face beyond a small softening around the eyes, the kind that came from being seen accurately after a long time of not being looked at closely enough.

He picked up his wrench and went back to work.

Ash stood at the bench and said nothing for a while. The pump was taking shape—the cylinder, the manifold, the inlet fittings, the whole mechanical logic of it assembling toward function. Haran's hands moved. Sellie's gear assembly clicked through its range of motion. Vorn's scribe marked iron.

"You chose this," Ash said.

Haran didn't look up. "Chose what."

"The workshop. This." Ash gestured at the space—the worn bench, the coil of copper pipe, the half-finished pump. "You could have—" He stopped. The sentence was too large for the room. He tried again. "You didn't have to be here."

"Neither did you," Haran said. He seated a bolt and gave it two turns. "Yet."

The correction was gentle. Ash accepted it.

He pulled a stool to the bench and sat. The pump frame was half-built. The day was half-gone. He looked at the components still waiting—the valves, the outlet manifold, the piston rod he'd stiffened and the collar he'd fused.

"What's left to do?" he asked.

Haran listed it. Valve assembly, outlet manifold, rod and cylinder—those could go in today. The mounting brackets would need the Ketterbach basement's measurements and a second visit. The seals would need a proper bed of compound and time to cure.

"Weeks," Ash said.

"At least."

Ash reached for the outlet manifold. He turned it over in his hands—held it the way his hands had learned to hold components, reading weight and balance, finding the machined faces. He found the mounting holes. Found the inlet ports. His hands knew what they were looking at.

He reached for the corresponding section of the pump frame. Held both pieces, feeling how they needed to fit, where they'd bear against each other.

He set the manifold in position.

He reached for a bolt.

He threaded it in by hand—no channeling, his fingers on the bolt head, the familiar resistance of a thread finding its mate. He worked it until it snugged against the surface, then picked up a wrench.

Haran was watching. Not intrusively—the sideways attention of a man who noted what was happening without making it a performance.

Ash tightened the bolt. Two turns across the pattern—the way he'd learned it, the way the joints set evenly. The third bolt wouldn't start by hand. He worked the thread with his finger, found the entrance, and tried again. It caught.

His palms were warm. The reservoir pressed against them—not urgently, just present, the way a full reservoir was always present now. He didn't open the gate.

The fourth bolt went in clean.

Sellie had come closer without quite appearing to—her bench was nearby and she was working on something at this end of it, but Ash noticed she'd been there for a few minutes. Watching, or not watching, the way tinkerers watched when they were interested and didn't want to say so.

He kept working. The manifold was solid—hand-tight, the faces bearing evenly. He'd need Haran to check the torque and the seal compound before the pump could be tested, but the geometry was right.

His father's voice in his memory: *You'll get the feel for it.*

Different kind of feel than his father had meant. But maybe not entirely different.

He set the wrench down. The outlet manifold was fitted. The pump was still half-built. It would take weeks—more visits, measurements, the proper installation at Ketterbach. When it was done, the four people on channeling rotation would have their afternoon hours back. They'd spend them on something else.

He thought about the collar. The fused threads, the stiffened rod, the hairline crack in the valve housing. Those components would function. They would probably outlast the pump itself in terms of calendar time. But whoever came to repair the pump in five years—whoever had to service it, had to adapt it if the building changed, had to fix it when it failed—would find the collar, and the rod, and the cracked housing, and would spend an afternoon Ash would never know about dealing with choices Ash had made in thirty seconds without thinking.

He hadn't thought about that afternoon, five years from now, belonging to someone.

He was still thinking about this when Haran set down his tools and straightened up from the bench with the ease of a man who had learned to stand without telegraphing the effort.

"Leave the rod," Haran said. "I have a replacement blank."

He went to the storage shelf—the deep shelving at the back of the workshop, stacked with raw materials and salvaged components in Haran's organization system, which appeared chaotic and was not. He came back with a steel rod, the right diameter, unworked. He put it on the bench.

"I'll turn it to fit tomorrow," he said.

The stiffened rod sat beside it. Ash picked it up and turned it in his hands. It was rigid—he could feel the difference now that he was looking for it, the absence of the small give that should have been there. He set it down.

"Sorry," he said.

Haran picked up the stiffened rod and weighed it in one hand. He set it with the salvage heap—not the waste bin. Salvage. Useful for something. "The collar might be extractable," he said. "I'll see."

He began tidying the workbench—the particular kind of tidying at the end of a session, not clearing but resetting, returning things to their positions so tomorrow's work could start from order. Ash helped. He knew this tidying. He'd done it hundreds of times.

Sellie and Vorn began their own closing sequence—tools back, surfaces wiped, the gear assembly set carefully on the storage shelf with a cloth over it. The workshop's end-of-day sounds: the soft knock of tools finding their hooks, the scrape of a stool, the lamp Vorn turned down.

Ash reached for his coat. The lamp beside the door still burned—the two that had been lit when he arrived were still lit, the one dark one still dark. He'd been in the workshop for four hours without noticing the time pass.

Haran walked him to the door. Not ceremonially—just the natural drift of two people ending a shared session. He paused at the door, his hand on the frame, and looked at the inlet side of the pump where the fused collar sat waiting.

"How much did that cost you?" he said. "The collar. The rod. The pipe."

Ash thought about it. He'd barely registered the expenditure at the time. The reservoir, checked against itself—

"Nothing I can feel," he said.

Haran nodded. Not surprised. He looked at the collar for a moment longer.

"Good. Remember what the answer is."

The lamp burned. The workshop was warm—Haran's ambient channeling, the heating that had been there all afternoon without Ash registering its source. The consistent temperature of a space whose warmth cost someone something, distributed in pulses so small they were invisible.

"You might want to check it again later," Haran said. "That answer."

Ash looked at him.

"A month from now. A year." Haran turned from the pump. His expression was the same as always—dry, interested, without instruction. "What the answer is then."

Outside, the district was doing its evening work—the sounds of a neighborhood that didn't use channeling for the things it could do otherwise. A cart on the road. Someone hammering two streets over, the particular rhythm of a person fitting something at the end of the day while the light held. The water system for this block was running on the four rotating channelers until the pump was built. Someone's evening shift, starting.

"You're learning what you can do," Haran said. He said it without inflection, the same way he'd say a pipe would take a forty-five degree bend or a valve seat needed cleaning. "Good."

He let a beat pass. The sounds of the neighborhood came through the door—the cart, the hammer, the routine life of a district managing with what it had.

"Don't forget what you don't need to do."

He went inside. The door didn't close immediately—it hung open for a moment, the workshop warmth coming through, the smell of sawdust and oil and cut metal following Ash out into the evening air. Then it closed. The latch caught with its familiar resistance.

Ash stood on the step.

The street was ordinary. Uneven paving, the stones heaved slightly where roots had been working at them. A lamp post at the corner, channeling-lit but dimmer than the ones on the Third Terrace, running on what the district's channelers could spare for civic lighting. A woman crossed the street carrying a bundle, moving quickly, already thinking about the next thing.

He stood there for a moment.

The fused collar. The stiffened rod. The hairline crack. The outlet manifold he'd bolted by hand in the last hour, his palms warm with suppressed channeling, the reservoir patient and vast and untouched. Both things together. Both things his.

The lamp at the corner threw its circle of light. Enough to see by. Not more than enough—not more than the district could afford to run.

He started walking.

The evening was cool. The Third Terrace was uphill and north, its transit lift already humming, its heating lattices already warming rooms against the night. His apartment would be warm when he got there. Maren had calibrated the lattice last week—a careful adjustment, a fraction of her daily trickle spent on precision—and it had held. It would still be holding.

He thought about the pump. Still half-built. Still weeks from done.

He thought about Haran's hands on the valve assembly—the small adjustments, the patience, the accumulated knowledge of ten thousand valves. He thought about the lamp lit cleanly from half a meter. The workshop warm without waste. The forty years of a strong trickle deployed in pulses too small to register as individual acts, adding up across decades into something that looked like restraint and was actually—

He tried to find the right word for it.

Not restraint. Restraint was withholding. What Haran did was not withholding. It was using completely. Every pulse of channeling exactly as large as the task required and not a fraction larger. Efficiency that had stopped being a discipline and become a way of inhabiting the world—so deep it was invisible, the way a great musician's technique was invisible inside the music.

*He could have maintained a district.*

He'd chosen a workshop in a neighborhood where the paving heaved and the district lamp was dimmer than it should be and four people spent their evenings on channeling rotation for a pump that had been broken for eight months.

The hammer had stopped. Two streets over, something was fitted, and the day's work was done.

Ash walked uphill toward the Third Terrace. The reservoir moved with him, steady and full, pressing gently against his ribs—the ocean inside him, patient, enormous, unchanged by a day of bad fittings and one good manifold and a valve he'd cracked and a lesson he was only beginning to understand.

*Remember what the answer is.*

He would check it. A month from now. A year. He didn't know yet what he'd find.

The transit lift hummed in its shaft as he reached the Third Terrace. The street widened. The lamp posts were brighter here, the paving unheaved, the buildings faced in clean stone. His apartment was six blocks north—the warm hallway, the lattice Maren had calibrated, the copper kettle on the stove.

He took the stairs instead of the lift.

Not for any reason he could have named. The stairs were there and his legs worked and the stairwell smelled faintly of stone and old plaster, something between the Third Terrace's clean surfaces and the stairwells he'd known his whole life. He climbed. His hands found the railing without thinking, the way hands found familiar things.

The reservoir was still full when he reached the landing.

He stood for a moment in the hallway. The lattice hummed—even, steady, holding the warmth Maren had set it to. The lighting strips burned at their calibrated glow. Everything was working, the way everything in the Third Terrace worked: smoothly, reliably, maintained by people whose daily trickles went into systems too large for any one of them to see.

He walked to the kitchen. The copper kettle was where he'd left it.

He filled it from the tap—carefully, both hands on the handle, watching his grip. The water ran cold. He carried it to the stove and set it on the interface disc, and he looked at his hand on the handle.

He touched the interface. Let the reservoir surface just barely rise—a thin membrane of channeling, the smallest consistent output he could manage. The disc warmed. The kettle warmed. No ozone smell. No crack in the stone.

He stood with his hand on the interface and let it heat.

Outside, the Third Terrace was quiet. Maintained. The evening settling into the particular silence of a place where things worked, where people paid other people to make them work, where the channeling hummed in the walls day and night without anyone in particular having provided it.

The kettle heated slowly. He stood there and let it.
