# Chapter 10

The map covered half of Dorenne's desk.

Ash had seen maps of Solathis before — the framed one in the Hall's main corridor, the simplified transit diagram on every platform — but never one like this. This one was annotated in Dorenne's hand, the ink dark and precise, notes running along the margins in the cramped shorthand she used when she was thinking faster than she could write. Districts were outlined in different colors. Red marks clustered in the east and south, each one a small circle with a date beside it. The dates went back months.

"Sit," Dorenne said. She was standing behind her desk with her hands flat on the map's edges, as though holding the city down.

He sat.

She didn't start with pleasantries. She never did — or rather, her pleasantries were efficiency: a cup of tea already waiting for him on the side table, brewed and still hot. She'd remembered that he took it without anything. He wrapped both hands around the cup and felt the heat soak through his palms, the ordinary warmth of something made for him, and waited.

"The eastern district's heating grid has been failing since autumn," she said. "You know this."

He did. He'd seen the reports when he worked as her aide — the weekly maintenance summaries that came in from the district crews, the numbers declining in a pattern he'd recognized before he understood what the pattern meant. Fewer shifts filled. More systems flagged for emergency repair. The language had been careful, bureaucratic, the tone of people who knew they were writing reports that wouldn't change anything.

"The situation has gotten worse." She moved her hand across the map, tracing the eastern district's boundary — a roughly triangular section pressed against the plateau's edge, the buildings there older and lower and built in an era when heating lattices were woven into every wall by channelers whose pools could sustain a neighborhood. "We lost the secondary lattice network in the Thornwall blocks six weeks ago. The primary network has been running at partial capacity since before winter. The maintenance crews assigned to the grid are pulling twelve-hour shifts and they're still losing ground."

She looked at him over the map. Her eyes were steady, the particular quality of attention she gave to things she'd been thinking about for days. "Three hundred families in the Thornwall blocks. Another two hundred in the adjacent sections. Five hundred households without reliable heating. In winter."

He held the cup. The tea was going from hot to warm, the ceramic cooling against his fingers. He could feel the Wellspring behind his ribs, the patient weight of it, the way it pressed at the borders of his body like a tide held in place by nothing but his decision not to move.

"The crews are good people," Dorenne said. "Experienced. But their pools —" She paused. Not searching for the word. Choosing not to say it the way she'd say it in a Council meeting, the way she'd say it with data and projections and the careful language of institutional decline. She was talking to him. "Their pools aren't what they were. The strongest channelers on the eastern crew are mid-generation. They spend half their daily capacity maintaining four blocks and they go home exhausted and they come back the next morning and do it again, and every week the lattice network loses a little more than they can put back."

She picked up a report from the stack beside the map. He recognized the formatting — district maintenance, weekly summary, the same reports he used to file. She didn't hand it to him. She set it back down with the kind of precision that meant she already knew everything in it.

"I could assign more crews. I don't have more crews. I could pull from other districts, but every district I pull from falls further behind." She straightened. "Or I could send you."

The room was quiet. Beyond the window, the Hall of Infrastructure's courtyard caught the morning light — pale stone, the fountain that hadn't run in months because the channeling to maintain it had been redirected to the water system, the bare branches of the trees that lined the walkway. A normal morning. The sounds of the building filtered through the walls: footsteps, a door closing somewhere, the low hum of the heating lattice in Dorenne's own office running at the temperature she preferred.

"Tell me about the people," he said.

Something shifted in her expression. Small — a settling, a fraction of tension releasing from the line between her brows. As though his question had been the right one and she was allowing herself, for a moment, to stop making the case.

"The Thornwall blocks are lower-income. Most residents are young-generation or middle-generation — families, elderly on fixed allocations, people who moved to the eastern district because it was what they could afford." She folded her hands over the map. "When the heating grid runs, the lattices in the walls keep the buildings at livable temperature. When it doesn't run, the walls go cold. The buildings weren't designed with insulation — they didn't need it. The lattices were the insulation. Without them, the stone pulls heat out of the rooms faster than anyone can replace it."

He thought about that. Stone walls bleeding warmth. Families sleeping in layers. Children whose breath clouded in rooms that were supposed to be warm.

"How cold?"

"Cold enough that the district clinic has been treating exposure. In a city."

She let that sit. She was good at letting things sit — the measured silence, the space she left for the information to arrive in his body before she continued. He'd watched her do it in Council meetings, the same technique used on different people, and he'd admired it then without understanding that it was being used on him too. Or maybe he understood and didn't mind. The information was real. The cold was real. The people were real.

"I want you to look at the heating lattice network," she said. "Assess the damage. If you can reinforce it — even temporarily, even crudely — that's five hundred households with heat tonight."

He set the cup down. The tea was warm now, not hot, the ceramic the temperature of his hands. "When?"

"Today. Now, if you're willing. I have a crew leader who can walk you through the lattice layout. Her name is Sera — she's been running the eastern maintenance team for eight years." Dorenne paused. "She's tired, Ash. They're all tired. Don't make promises I can't keep. But if you can do what I think you can do, those people will be warm tonight."

He looked at the map. The red circles in the east — each one a failure point, a place where the lattice network had thinned past function, where the wall went cold and the room behind it went cold and the family inside the room went cold. Five hundred households. Five hundred kitchen tables and five hundred beds and five hundred mornings where people woke with their fingers stiff and their breath visible and the stone walls bleeding the warmth from the room before they could get dressed.

"I'll go," he said.

* * *

The eastern district looked different from the ground.

He'd seen it from the transit platforms — the lower rooflines, the older stone, the way the buildings crowded together as though leaning on each other for support. From above, it had a kind of worn dignity. Close up, the dignity was thinner. The streets were narrower than in the central districts. The paving stones were uneven, some of them cracked, the channeled reinforcement that kept the road surface smooth having faded years ago into a rough, patched unevenness that caught at his boots. The buildings rose on either side — four stories, five, the stone facades stained with weather and age, the windows smaller than in newer construction because the builders had relied on channeled light and warmth rather than glass and orientation.

Sera met him at the district's main junction. She was mid-forties, middle-generation, with short-cropped hair and the weathered hands of someone who'd spent years pressing her palms against stone and pushing heat into it. She looked at him the way maintenance workers looked at anything new in their territory: with the careful attention of someone who'd learned that new things usually meant more work.

"Director Kharren said you'd be coming." She looked him up and down. Not his clothes, not his face — his hands. "You're the Wellspring."

"Ash."

"Sera." She turned and started walking. "The primary lattice runs through the load-bearing walls — south face, north face, the central spine. The secondary network is dead. Has been for six weeks. The primary is running at maybe forty percent. I'll show you the worst of it."

She walked fast. Not impatient — efficient. The walk of a person who'd been running short on everything for long enough that wasting time had become physically intolerable. He fell into step beside her and tried to match her pace, and the streets narrowed further as they moved into the Thornwall blocks — older buildings, five stories, the stone darker here, the windows glowing faintly with whatever light the residents could afford. It was mid-morning and the light was thin, the sun not yet high enough to reach these streets where the buildings stood close.

He could feel the cold. Not through the Wellspring — through his skin, the ordinary animal recognition of air that was too cold for a city that had heating. The temperature in the street was fine. But when Sera led him through a doorway and into the first building's ground-floor corridor, the cold came up through the floor like water. The stone beneath his boots was icy. The walls, when he brushed his fingers against them in passing, were colder than the air.

"Touch the wall," Sera said. She'd stopped at a section of corridor where the plaster had cracked, exposing the stone beneath. "Feel it."

He pressed his palm flat against the stone. Cold. Deep cold, the kind that went past the surface and into the material, the kind that meant the stone had been cold for weeks and had accumulated the chill the way stone accumulates anything — slowly, thoroughly, all the way through. The lattice channels were in there somewhere — the network of channeled pathways that threaded through the walls like veins, designed to carry warmth from a central source and distribute it evenly through the building. He could feel them. Not visually, not with any trained technique — just the Wellspring pressing against his palm and the wall pressing back, and somewhere in the stone, the faint, attenuated hum of pathways that had once been full and were now nearly empty.

"The lattice channels are intact," Sera said. She was watching him, her arms crossed, the posture of a person reserving judgment. "The infrastructure is there. The problem is throughput — we don't have enough channelers with enough capacity to keep the lattice charged. We push what we can, it dissipates in hours, and the stone goes cold again."

She walked him down the corridor. Doors on either side — apartments, one per floor, the building's residents behind them in rooms that he could now imagine clearly: the cold walls, the extra blankets, the small channeling sources — personal heaters, warming stones, whatever the residents could power from their own pools — fighting a losing battle against the mass of stone that surrounded them. The building was built to be heated by the lattice. Without the lattice, the building was a machine designed to take warmth and bury it.

"Here," Sera said. She stopped at a section of wall where a maintenance access panel had been removed, exposing a cross-section of the interior. He could see it: the lattice, the network of thin channels cut into the stone and sealed with channeled material, a web of pathways that ran through the wall like capillaries. Some of them glowed faintly — a residual warmth, the last of whatever the crew had pushed through that morning. Most were dark.

"The crew puts in about four hours on this building every day," Sera said. "Three channelers, rotating shifts. They charge the lattice, the lattice runs for maybe six hours, and then it cools. By midnight, the walls are cold again. By morning, the residents are sleeping in their coats."

Her voice was level. Not performing outrage, not asking for sympathy. Reporting. The tone of a person who had been reporting this for months and had stopped expecting the report to change anything.

"Can you show me where the main feed points are?" he asked.

She looked at him. The reservation in her expression shifted — not gone, but reconfigured. She nodded and led him to the building's basement.

* * *

The basement was cold enough that his breath clouded.

Sera showed him the feed points — four of them in this building, one at each corner of the foundation, where the lattice network converged into thicker channels that ran up through the walls. This was where the maintenance crews channeled: hands pressed to the stone, pushing heat into the feed points, the energy flowing upward and outward through the lattice like warmth spreading through a body from the heart.

He stood at the first feed point. The stone was rough under his palms — old stone, cut and placed generations ago by builders who had threaded it with channels designed for elder-level pools, channels wide enough to carry the sustained output of someone with ten thousand kilojoules to spend. The channels were intact. The infrastructure was there. It just needed what nobody had enough of anymore.

Except him.

He pressed his hands flat against the stone and opened the channel.

The Wellspring moved. Not the careful trickle he'd been practicing at the quarry — not the coin-sized precision he'd managed for ten seconds on a flat rock with Ryn calling corrections. This was the other thing, the big thing, the current that lived underneath his control like a river under ice. He let it go. Not all of it — some fraction, some portion that felt small from the inside the way a cup of water from an ocean felt small — and the power flowed down his arms and through his palms and into the stone.

The feed point lit up.

He could feel it — the channels accepting the energy, the lattice network waking as the warmth moved through it, pathway by pathway, the stone conducting the channeled heat upward and outward. The sensation was strange and intimate. The building was drinking from him. He could feel the lattice like a second set of veins, the network spreading through the walls the way blood spreads through tissue, and wherever the energy reached, the stone warmed. The cold in the basement began to recede — not slowly, not in the incremental way it receded when three middle-generation channelers spent four hours coaxing warmth into the system. Fast. The walls were warming. He could feel it through his palms, the stone going from ice-cold to cool to warm in a steady, accelerating progression that climbed upward through the building.

The heat bled off him. Waste — the inefficiency that Haran had tried to teach him about, the enormous percentage of energy that a novice channeler lost to ambient dispersal. He could feel it: heat shimmering off his arms, his face flushing, the air around him going thick and warm with the energy that hadn't made it into the lattice. Crude. Sloppy. He was pouring water through a sieve and enough was getting through to fill the cup anyway, because what he had was an ocean and what the building needed was a bath.

He moved to the second feed point. Pressed his hands flat. Pushed.

Sera was standing at the base of the stairs. She hadn't moved. She hadn't spoken. He could see her in his peripheral vision — her arms no longer crossed, her hands at her sides, her posture changed in a way that he registered without analyzing. She was watching the walls.

The walls were glowing.

Not brightly — not the dramatic luminescence of the quarry stones, not the detonation that had left Ryn shielding her eyes. A soft, deep warmth suffused through the stone, the lattice channels carrying the heat he was feeding them and distributing it evenly through the building's structure. The exposed cross-section that Sera had shown him earlier was alive now — every channel bright, the network visible through the stone like embers through ash, a web of warmth threading through the walls.

He fed the third point. The fourth. The building drank and drank and he gave it what it asked for, and the waste heat poured off him in waves, and his shirt was damp with sweat, and the basement had gone from cold enough to cloud his breath to warm enough that the warmth felt almost tropical against his flushed skin.

He stepped back from the last feed point. His hands were shaking — not from exhaustion, not from any depletion. From the sensation of holding the channel open that wide and then closing it, the abrupt cessation of flow, the Wellspring settling back behind his ribs like a wave pulling back from shore.

The basement was warm. The walls were warm. The building, from foundation to roof, was warm.

He looked at Sera.

She was pressing her palm against the wall. Her eyes were closed. The expression on her face was one he'd never seen on a maintenance worker before — or rather, one he'd never seen directed at a wall. She was feeling the lattice, checking the charge, doing the professional assessment that was her job and her instinct and the thing she'd spent eight years learning to do. Her hand moved across the stone the way a physician's hand moves across a patient, reading the signs.

She opened her eyes.

"How long will that hold?" she asked. Her voice was different. Controlled, the same level tone, but with something behind it — a vibration, a tension in the throat, the sound of a person managing a reaction.

"I don't know," he said. "How fast does it usually dissipate?"

"Crew charge holds about six hours."

He'd fed the lattice more energy in five minutes than a three-person crew could deliver in twelve hours. The math was crude but directional. "Longer than that."

She looked at him. Then she looked at the wall. Then she pressed both hands against the stone and stood there, feeling the warmth coming through, and her shoulders dropped — a small motion, the release of tension held so long it had become structural — and she said, very quietly: "The people upstairs are going to be warm tonight."

"Yeah," he said.

She took her hands off the wall. She straightened. The professional composure was back — reassembled, functional — but her eyes were bright, and when she spoke, her voice had the careful steadiness of someone holding a line. "There are eleven more buildings in the Thornwall blocks. The lattice layout is similar — four feed points per building, same network architecture. Can you do them all?"

He checked the reservoir. The internal assessment that was becoming habit — the quick inward glance, the way you'd check the level in a well. The Wellspring sat where it had been that morning. Vast. Full. The amount he'd poured into this building was a thimble from a lake.

"I can do them all," he said.

* * *

He did them all.

Eleven buildings. Forty-four feed points. He moved through the Thornwall blocks with Sera at his side, building by building, basement by basement, his hands on the stone and the Wellspring flowing through him and the lattice networks waking under his palms. Each building was a variation on the same architecture — the same era of construction, the same lattice design, the same feed points at the foundation corners — and by the third building he had found a rhythm. Press. Open. Feel the channels accept the energy. Feed until the lattice was full, the warmth saturating the stone, the building alive again. Move on.

The waste heat was enormous. By the fifth building, his clothes were soaked. The air around him shimmered. Sera had stopped standing close — the radiant heat coming off him was uncomfortable at arm's length. He was running hot, flooding the lattice with more energy than it could absorb cleanly, the excess bleeding off through his skin and his breath and the heated air that followed him through the basements like a wake.

He knew this was inefficient. He knew it the way he knew the sky was blue — factually, distantly, without it touching anything that mattered. Haran's voice in his head: *you don't use a hammer on glass.* But this wasn't glass. This was a heating grid designed for elder-level output, starved for years, and what it needed wasn't a surgeon. It needed a flood.

By the eighth building, Sera had stopped checking the lattice after each charge. She'd press her palm to the wall, feel the warmth, nod, and move on. The assessment had become perfunctory. The lattice was full every time. The buildings were warm every time. What had taken her crew months of grinding, exhausting, insufficient effort — Ash was doing in minutes.

By the tenth building, people had started to notice.

The warmth moved upward through the buildings faster than he moved through the district. He'd feed a building's lattice and move on, and by the time he reached the next basement, the building behind him had changed. The walls that had been cold all winter were warm. The floors that had leached heat from bare feet were comfortable. The air in the corridors shifted — not just the temperature, but the quality of it, the way a room feels different when it's warm, the way the air itself seems to soften.

He was in the eleventh building's basement when Sera said, "You should come upstairs."

He followed her up the stairs and out the building's front entrance and into the street.

The Thornwall blocks were different. He could see it — feel it, the shift that had happened while he'd been in the basements. The buildings on either side of the narrow street were radiating warmth through their walls. Not dramatically, not visibly, but the street itself was warmer. The cold that had pooled in these narrow lanes all winter, the chill that clung to the shadowed stone — it was thinning. The buildings were exhaling. Generations of accumulated cold being pushed outward by the lattice networks he'd charged, the stone releasing its stored chill as the warmth replaced it from inside.

A door opened.

An old woman — small, wrapped in layers, a heavy shawl over a heavy sweater over what looked like two more layers beneath — stepped into the corridor of the building across the street. She stood in the doorway with one hand on the frame and the other pressed flat against the wall, and she stayed there. Not moving. Not speaking. Just standing with her hand on the warm wall, the way you might stand with your hand on the flank of an animal you'd been worried about, feeling it breathe.

Another door. A man in work clothes, young, mid-twenties, stepping into the hall with a child on his hip. The child — maybe three, bundled in so many layers she was nearly spherical — was squirming, reaching for the wall. The man let her touch it. Her small hand pressed flat against the stone, and she went still, and then she said something that Ash couldn't hear, and the man laughed. The sound carried. It bounced off the warm walls and reached the street where Ash stood with Sera, and it was the sound of a person startled by something good.

More doors. Up and down the street, the buildings opening, the residents coming out — not in a rush, not a crowd, but the gradual emergence of people who had felt something change and needed to verify it with their own hands. An elderly couple standing in their doorway, the man's arm around the woman's shoulders. A teenager sitting on the stairs with her back against the wall and her eyes closed, her face tipped back as though sunbathing. Two children — five, six years old — chasing each other down the corridor, their jackets unzipped, their voices carrying the bright, careless volume of children who aren't cold.

Sera was watching him.

He didn't know what to do with his face. The feeling that was moving through him was large and physical and had nowhere to go — it sat in his chest like heat, like the waste energy that had been pouring off him all morning, except this warmth had nothing to do with channeling. His throat was tight. His eyes were wrong — too bright, too full, the kind of thing he'd have turned away from if anyone were looking.

The old woman across the street was still standing with her hand on the wall. She hadn't moved. She was crying — not dramatically, not with sound, just the quiet kind, the tears that come when a body that has been cold for a long time is suddenly warm and doesn't know what to do with the relief except let it fall.

He looked at the ground. He looked at his hands — red from the basement stone, raw at the palms, still radiating the faint shimmer of waste heat. These hands. These hands that had spent twenty-three years unable to warm a cup or light a lamp or activate a lattice that a five-year-old could power. These hands that had opened hinges and tightened bolts and struck flint in a world that had moved past flint a hundred years ago. These hands had just heated a neighborhood.

"Ash," Sera said.

He looked up.

"There's more. The secondary lattice — the one that's been down for six weeks. The feed architecture is different. Deeper. If you could—"

"Show me," he said.

* * *

The secondary lattice took longer. The channels were narrower, the architecture more complex — a finer network that handled the detail work of heating, the even distribution that kept corners from going cold and exterior walls from freezing. The main lattice was a furnace. The secondary lattice was the system that made sure the warmth reached everywhere.

He worked through the afternoon. Sera led him through the access points, explained the architecture, showed him where the network converged and where it branched. Her professional reserve had settled back into place — the brief, unguarded moment in the first basement was gone, replaced by the steady competence of a crew leader working a problem. But she stayed close. She checked his work. She told him when he was pushing too hard into a section that couldn't take it, and when he was missing a branch that needed attention, and when the waste heat bleeding off him was getting close to damaging the plaster.

He learned from her. Not the way he'd learned from Haran — not philosophy, not precision, not the subtle art of doing less. He learned the building. The lattice network's logic. The way a heating system was designed to flow, the hierarchy of channels from main feeds to branches to capillaries, the intended distribution pattern that the original builders had engineered and that decades of decline had degraded. Sera knew this system the way a doctor knows a body, and she walked him through it with the patient authority of someone who'd been its caretaker for years.

By late afternoon, the secondary lattice was running. The Thornwall blocks were heated — fully, deeply, the way they'd been designed to be heated when the buildings were new and the maintenance crews had elder-level pools and the city's infrastructure hummed with the comfortable excess of a high-magic era.

He stood in the street and felt the warmth coming through the walls. Not just from the buildings — from the ground. The paving stones were warming too, heated from below by the lattice networks that ran under the street, the buried infrastructure that nobody had been able to charge for years. The whole district was different. The air was different. The cold that had lived in these streets like a resident — permanent, structural, accepted — was lifting.

Sera walked beside him. They were heading back toward the main junction, the route retracing the morning's path through streets that looked different now. Not physically — the buildings were the same, the cracked paving stones, the stained facades, the narrow sky. But the quality of the place had changed. Doors were open. People were standing in their doorways and sitting on their steps and leaning against walls that were warm, and the sound of the street was different — voices carrying differently in warmer air, the particular acoustic shift that happens when stone stops being cold.

A woman stopped him. She was carrying a baby and a cloth bag of something from the market, and she stepped directly into his path with the purposefulness of someone who had decided to do this ten minutes ago and was going to do it before she lost her nerve.

"You're the one," she said. Not asking. "You fixed the heating."

"I charged the lattice. Your maintenance crew keeps it running."

"It hasn't been warm since autumn." She looked at him — not at the Wellspring, not at whatever impossible thing he represented. At him. A young man with raw hands and sweat-damp clothes standing in her street. "My daughter was born in that building. In November. The midwife wore gloves."

He didn't know what to say. The baby on her hip was watching him with the round, unblinking attention that babies give to unfamiliar things, and the woman was looking at him with an expression that was gratitude and something else — something harder to name, something that lived in the space between relief and anger, between thank you and where were you six months ago.

"The crew's been trying," he said. "They've been working—"

"I know they've been trying. I know Sera. She's been killing herself." The woman shifted the baby to her other hip. "I'm glad you came. That's all."

She walked past him. The baby turned its head to keep watching him over her shoulder, and then they were gone, around the corner, back into the life of a district that was warm for the first time in months.

He stood in the street.

*I did that.*

The thought was small and bright and it sat in his chest like an ember. Not the triumphant blaze he'd felt at the quarry — not the spectacular joy of hurling boulders and punching craters and watching Ryn's face as the impossible happened. This was quieter. Deeper. This was a baby born in a cold room, and the room was warm now because he had pressed his hands against the stone and given it what it needed.

*I did something that mattered.*

He walked. The street opened up as he neared the main junction, and the light was better here — the sun had cleared the rooflines and was falling directly onto the paving stones, and the combined warmth of the sun and the charged lattice made the junction feel almost mild. People moved through it with the ordinary rhythms of an afternoon — errands, children, the traffic of a neighborhood. But there was a quality to the movement that he hadn't seen before. Looser. A woman on a bench had her coat open. Children were playing without hats.

Sera was waiting at the junction. She had a small notebook in her hand — the crew leader's log, he realized, the daily record of lattice status and charge levels and deployment notes.

"I'm going to need to write this up," she said. "For the Director."

"Okay."

"I'm going to tell her that one person did in eight hours what my crew of fourteen couldn't do in three months."

He looked at her. The late afternoon light was behind her and her face was in shadow, and the expression she wore was one he was learning to read — the professional composure held firmly in place over something larger.

"I'm also going to tell her that we need you back," Sera said. "The charge won't hold forever. The lattice drains. It always drains. What you did today will last — days, maybe a week, longer than anything we've managed. But it will fade, and when it does, we'll be back to where we were."

She put the notebook in her pocket. "Can I tell her you'll come back?"

"Yes," he said. The word came out before the thought behind it had finished forming. "Yes. Tell her."

Sera nodded. She didn't thank him. She turned and walked back into the district, her hands in her jacket pockets, her stride the same efficient pace she'd maintained all day. A woman going back to work. A crew leader with a full lattice and a notebook full of numbers that, for the first time in months, were going in the right direction.

He watched her go. Then he turned toward the main road and started walking west, toward the Hall, toward Dorenne's office, and the afternoon light was on his face and the eastern district was warm behind him.

* * *

Dorenne was waiting.

Not at her desk — at the window. She turned when he came in, and something crossed her face that was almost quick enough to miss. Relief. Or satisfaction. Or the particular expression of a person whose calculation has come back correct.

"Sera sent a message," she said. "The lattice is fully charged. Both networks."

"Both networks."

She crossed to her desk and sat down. He stood in the doorway. He was still in the clothes he'd worn to the eastern district — damp with sweat, creased with the grime of twelve basements, his hands raw and red from pressing against stone. He could smell himself: the mineral scent of old buildings and the sharp, metallic warmth of waste heat that had been pouring off his skin for hours.

"Sit down," she said. "Tell me what you saw."

He sat. He told her. The lattice architecture, the feed points, the secondary network, the buildings warming from inside. He told her about Sera's crew, about the twelve-hour shifts, about the maintenance log with its declining numbers. He told her about the old woman standing in her doorway with her hand on the warm wall.

Dorenne listened. She listened the way she did everything — attentively, completely, the information being received and processed and filed and cross-referenced against whatever model she carried in her head. When he finished, she was quiet for a moment.

Then she opened a drawer and took out a second map. This one was larger — the whole of Solathis, not just the eastern district. The same annotation, the same red circles, but more of them. Many more. Clusters in the south. Scattered marks across the lower-middle districts. A constellation of small failures that, taken together, described a city losing its grip on the systems that kept it functioning.

"The eastern district isn't unique," she said. "It's the worst. It's not the only one." She set the map beside the first one, the two views of the city side by side — the close view and the wide view, the single neighborhood and the whole. "The southern quarter's water system has been intermittent for two months. The residential towers on the Kelsmere road need structural reinforcement — the foundation channeling is degrading. Transit shaft seven hasn't run since last summer."

She looked at him across the maps. "You charged a district's entire heating lattice in one day. One person. One day."

He looked at the maps. The red circles. The dates. The scope of something he hadn't understood when he was her aide — the scale of the infrastructure failure, the quiet, grinding crisis that happened when a city built for one era tried to survive in another.

"I want to build a deployment schedule," she said. "Not open-ended — targeted. The highest-priority systems first. We'll work with the district crews — Sera's people, their counterparts in the south and the middle ring. They know their systems. You have the capacity. Together, we can stabilize the critical infrastructure."

The way she said it — *we*. Not "I need you to" or "the city requires." *We.* The word of partnership, of shared purpose. The word she'd used when he was her aide and the shared purpose was filing reports and attending meetings, and the word she used now when the shared purpose was holding a city together.

"The water system first," she said. "The southern quarter. Tomorrow, if you're able. I'll have the crew leader meet you at the main pumping station at dawn."

Tomorrow. He'd heated a neighborhood today and she was already seeing tomorrow, the next red circle, the next system, the next crisis to be addressed. The machine was starting. He could feel it — the mechanism, the logic, the gears interlocking. Dorenne identified the need. Ash filled it. The city worked. The red circles became something else.

"Okay," he said.

"And Ash—" She paused. The professional composure was in place, the careful armor of a woman who had spent decades learning to manage everything, including the way she expressed things that mattered. But her voice was different. Softer. The register she used when she was talking to the person, not the resource. "What you did today. Those people. That matters."

He looked at her. She was sitting at her desk with her hands folded over the maps of a failing city, and the late light was coming through the window behind her, and her face was the face of a woman who had spent years fighting a war of attrition against entropy and had just been given the first weapon that might actually work. The gratitude in her expression was real. The calculation was also real. Both things lived in the same face, the same voice, the same folded hands.

"I know," he said.

* * *

The southern quarter's water system was different from the heating lattice. The infrastructure was larger — a network of channeled pumping stations that moved water uphill from the reservoir through a series of relay points, each one requiring sustained force to keep the flow moving against gravity. The maintenance crews here were stronger — a few older channelers who'd been doing this work for decades — but the system was designed for a capacity they couldn't provide. The water ran in shifts: six hours on, six hours off. Half the southern quarter had water in the morning. The other half had it in the afternoon. Nobody had it at night.

He went at dawn, as Dorenne had arranged. The crew leader — a man named Torek, elder-generation, with the broad shoulders and quiet manner of someone who'd spent forty years pushing water uphill — met him at the main pumping station. Torek didn't talk much. He showed Ash the system, explained the relay points, positioned him at the primary intake, and stepped back.

Ash channeled. The pump took the energy and moved the water. The relay points lit up one by one as the force he was pushing into the system cascaded through it — each station picking up the flow and boosting it to the next, the whole network activating in sequence like a line of lanterns being lit. Within an hour, the southern quarter had full water pressure for the first time in months. Every tap, every bath, every cistern — all of it running, all of it full, the system doing what it was built to do.

He spent the morning at the pumping station, sustaining the flow while Torek and his crew ran checks on the relay network. The waste heat was less this time — not because he'd gotten more efficient in any technical sense, but because the water system absorbed energy differently than stone. The force flowed through pipes and pressure points and the physics of moving liquid, and the system's design gave the energy somewhere to go. He was still sloppy. Still pouring more into the system than it needed. But less of it was bleeding off as useless heat, and the improvement — the raw practice of channeling for hours, the body learning through repetition what the mind couldn't teach — was real.

By noon, Torek walked him through a secondary section of the network — a branch that served the lower quarter's public baths. The baths had been closed for four months. Not enough water pressure, not enough heating capacity. Ash charged the branch line and heated the cisterns, and the work took him the better part of an hour, and when it was done Torek stood at the inlet valve and opened it and watched the water flow with the expression of a man watching a river he thought had dried up.

"We'll need you back in two days," Torek said. "Maybe three. The charge dissipates."

"I'll come back."

* * *

Day three: the residential towers on Kelsmere road. Structural work — reinforcing the channeled foundations that held the towers stable on their bedrock. This was different again. The lattice had been about heat, the pumping station about force, but structural reinforcement was about something subtler: pushing energy into the crystalline structure of stone and metal in a way that strengthened the molecular bonds, that tightened the architecture of the material itself. It was the work that had built Solathis — the deep channeling that turned ordinary stone into something that could support impossible structures on a plateau that should have been too narrow and too high for a city this size.

He was terrible at it. The structural crew — three elder-generation specialists who'd been doing foundation work for longer than he'd been alive — positioned him at the base of the north tower and told him to channel into the stone, and he did, and the stone cracked.

Not catastrophically. A surface fracture, the kind that happens when you push force into a material without understanding its grain. The crew leader — a woman named Harenn, white-haired, with the impatient economy of someone who did not suffer fools — looked at the crack and then looked at him, and her expression was the expression of a person deciding whether to be angry or instructive.

"You're pushing," she said. "Don't push. Feed. The stone knows what shape it wants to be. You're telling it to be something else."

He tried again. He cracked it again. The second crack was smaller.

Harenn showed him. She pressed her palm to the stone beside the crack he'd made and channeled — a slow, even flow, nothing dramatic, the energy moving into the stone's grain structure the way water moves into soil. Under her hand, the crack sealed. Not fused — healed. The stone's structure knitting back together along the lines of its own nature, the molecular bonds reforming because the energy had shown them how.

"Feed," she said again. "Not push."

His third attempt didn't crack the stone. It also didn't reinforce it — the energy went in and dissipated, absorbed without effect, like water poured onto sand. But nothing broke, and Harenn nodded once, the nod of someone who had recalibrated her expectations from "hopeless" to "slow."

By the end of the day, he could feed structural energy into the foundation without breaking it. He couldn't reinforce it — not yet, not with any useful effect. But the elder specialists worked beside him, guiding the force he provided, shaping it with their expertise while he supplied the raw capacity. One of them described it afterward: like having a river and a team of engineers. The river doesn't know how to build a dam. The engineers can't move the water. Together, something gets built.

Dorenne noted the structural work as partial success, pending follow-up. She added it to the schedule.

* * *

Day five: transit shaft seven.

Transit shafts were the vertical backbone of Solathis — channeled platforms that rose and fell through the city's levels, carrying people and goods between districts separated by hundreds of feet of elevation. They ran on sustained force: channelers at the base and apex of each shaft maintaining pressure columns that lifted and lowered the platforms. When the shafts worked, the city flowed. When they didn't, people walked. Transit shaft seven served the eastern district — the same neighborhood whose heating he'd fixed three days ago — and it had been dead since summer.

The shaft was impressive up close. A cylindrical chamber cut through solid rock, sixty feet in diameter, three hundred feet tall. The platforms — heavy, counterweighted, designed to carry thirty people and cargo — hung motionless on their rail guides, the channeling interfaces at the base dark and cold. The shaft echoed when he spoke. The air smelled of mineral dust and the faint, oily residue of the mechanical lubricants that kept the rail guides functional.

The crew here was small. Two young channelers who'd been assigned to monitor the shaft and report on its status — which had been the same status for nine months: inoperative. They looked at Ash the way the eastern district residents had looked at him — with hope that had the specific texture of hope that has been disappointed too many times.

He pressed his hands against the channeling interface at the shaft's base. The Wellspring flowed. The interface woke — the pressure column built in the shaft, a sustained upward force that pressed against the platform's underside. The platform groaned. Metal stressed against the rail guides. Then it moved.

It rose. Slowly at first, the ancient mechanisms protesting after months of dormancy, the counterweights swinging into motion, the rail guides singing with the friction of resumed travel. Then faster, the platform ascending smoothly, the pressure column steady under Ash's sustained output. The shaft filled with the sound of working machinery — the rhythmic clank of the counterweight system, the hiss of air displaced by the rising platform, the deep, resonant hum of a transit shaft doing what it was built to do.

The two young channelers watched the platform rise. One of them put a hand over her mouth.

He held the shaft running for four hours while the crew tested the systems, ran the platforms through their cycles, checked the mechanisms. The sustained channeling was different from the burst work he'd done on the heating lattice — less intense but more continuous, a steady flow rather than a flood. His efficiency was marginally better. Marginally. He was still losing enormous amounts to waste heat — the shaft chamber was noticeably warm by the end of the fourth hour — but the platform ran, and the eastern district had vertical transit again, and when the first group of actual commuters rode the platform down from the upper level to the eastern junction, one of the young channelers started crying and pretended she wasn't.

* * *

The days began to run together.

Dorenne's schedule filled and he followed it. Water systems, lattice recharges, a retaining wall in the northern quarter that was slowly leaning into the street. He went back to the eastern district twice to recharge the heating lattice. He went back to the southern quarter three times for the water system. Each return visit was shorter — the charge lasted longer each time, though he couldn't tell whether that was because the lattice was holding energy better or because he was delivering it more efficiently.

He was getting better. Not precise — not anything Haran would call controlled. But the raw practice was doing something. The eighth time he pressed his hands against a feed point, the motion was more natural than the first time. The fifteenth time he channeled sustained force into a pumping station, the waste heat was less, the flow steadier. His body was learning the rhythms of infrastructure channeling the way a musician's body learns an instrument — not through understanding but through repetition, the muscles and the channeling pathways finding their patterns through sheer volume of practice.

The waste was still enormous. He knew this in the abstract way he knew most things about the Wellspring — as a fact that existed at a distance, observable but not yet felt. He could feel the heat pouring off his skin. He could see the shimmer in the air around his arms. He could calculate, roughly, that he was losing sixty or seventy percent of everything he channeled to ambient dispersal. But sixty or seventy percent of an ocean was still a lake, and the remaining thirty or forty percent was more than entire maintenance crews could produce in weeks. The inefficiency didn't matter because the scale made it irrelevant. That was the math. That was the logic. And the logic felt like freedom.

He walked through the districts he'd helped. The eastern district in the evening, the walls warm, the lights working, the transit shaft carrying people up and down through the city's levels. The southern quarter at midday, water running from every tap, the public baths open for the first time in months — he passed the entrance and heard laughter coming from inside, the echo of voices in tiled chambers, and the sound sat in his chest like something he'd made. The residential towers on Kelsmere, the foundations holding, the elder specialists still doing the fine work but with his energy feeding their expertise.

He walked and the city worked. Not all of it — not the districts he hadn't reached yet, not the systems that Dorenne hadn't scheduled, not the thousand small failures that accumulated in a city where the magic was slowly, generationally running thin. But the parts he'd touched were different. The parts he'd touched were whole.

The feeling was larger than happiness. Larger than pride. It was the bone-deep, structural satisfaction of a life that had found its shape. Twenty-three years of being the person who couldn't help — the boy who watched other children light their first candle and couldn't light his own, the teenager who stood aside while his classmates maintained the school's heating grid, the young man who took a job as an aide because there was nothing else a magicless person could do in a city built on magic. Twenty-three years of carrying the weight of his own uselessness like a stone in his chest, and now the stone was gone. Not lightened, not eased. Gone. Replaced by something that felt like the opposite — a warmth, a solidity, a sense of himself as a person who existed in the world for a reason.

He was useful. He was necessary. Five hundred families in the eastern district slept warm because of him. The southern quarter had water because of him. Transit shaft seven carried people because of him. The facts were physical, tangible, as real as the stone he pressed his hands against. No one could take them away. No one could tell him he was a burden, a drain, a dead weight in a city that ran on channeling. He was the channeling. He was the thing the city needed.

Dorenne's schedule grew. New requests came in — from district crews, from Council members, from the offices of people he'd never met who had heard about the eastern district and wanted to know if the Wellspring could be deployed to their neighborhood. Dorenne filtered them. She prioritized. She built timelines and allocation plans and presented them to him with the same careful precision she brought to everything — here is the need, here is the schedule, here is how we make the most of what you can do.

He said yes. He said yes to everything, because every yes was a district that worked, a system that ran, a group of people whose lives got measurably better because he showed up and pressed his hands against the stone and gave it what it needed. The logic was irresistible. The logic was perfect. The need was real, and he could fill it, and filling it felt like the first true thing he'd ever done.

Dorenne's position strengthened. He could see it — in the Council meetings he occasionally attended, in the way other officials deferred to her, in the subtle shift of institutional gravity that happened when one person controls access to the most powerful resource the city has ever seen. She didn't flaunt it. She didn't need to. The results spoke: districts stabilized, systems restored, maintenance crews supported. She was the Director who had found the solution. She was the person who knew how to deploy the Wellspring. The ledger was running in her favor, and every entry was something real, something good, something that mattered.

He didn't mind. Or rather — he noticed, the way he noticed many things, with the analytical part of his mind that was always running, always observing, always filing details for later examination. He noticed that Dorenne's calendar filled as his calendar filled. He noticed that her reports to the Council emphasized the deployment program she'd designed. He noticed that when people praised what the Wellspring had done, they praised it through her — "Director Kharren's infrastructure initiative," "the deployment program," "Dorenne's project."

He noticed and he didn't mind, because the alternative to Dorenne's framework was no framework at all. Left to himself, he'd have charged the eastern district and then — what? Wandered the city looking for cold walls? Dorenne saw the city whole. She knew which systems were critical, which could wait, which needed him and which needed something else entirely. She was the architect. He was the river. Together, they built.

He believed this. He believed it the way the eastern district believed in warm walls and the southern quarter believed in running water — with the unquestioning certainty of a person whose experience confirmed it every single day.

The reservoir hummed behind his ribs. Patient. Vast. Unchanged.

He didn't count. He didn't need to count. There was always more.
