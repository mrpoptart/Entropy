# Chapter 9

The quarry road ended where the plateau broke open.

Ash stood at the edge and looked down. The gorge dropped two hundred feet to the river, the walls striped with layers of pale stone and darker stone and a band of something reddish that caught the morning light. Wind came up from the water, carrying the mineral smell of wet rock and the faint green of whatever grew along the banks far below. Behind him, the last buildings of the outer district sat low against the skyline — warehouses, a storage depot with its doors chained, a water cistern that hadn't been maintained in years. Ahead, nothing. Just the plateau's edge and the open sky and the quarry pit off to the left where the city had once pulled stone and then stopped pulling stone and left the wound to weather.

Ryn was sitting on a boulder near the quarry rim, eating an apple.

"You're late," she said.

"I walked."

"From where?"

"Home."

She looked at him — the particular look she had that started at his shoes and ended at whatever expression he was wearing, cataloguing everything between without seeming to. "You could have taken the lifts."

"I wanted to walk." He wanted the time, was what he meant. The morning had been too bright, the reservoir humming behind his ribs like a second pulse, and he'd needed the long route through the outer district to settle into his own skin. The power had been like this since the workshop — not demanding, not urgent, just present. A tide held in place by nothing but his decision not to let it move.

She took another bite of the apple. "So," she said. "Show me."

He'd asked her to come. Two days after the workshop with Haran, after a night of lying awake with his hands pressed flat against the mattress feeling the Wellspring push gently against his palms like something breathing, he'd walked to her building and knocked on her door and said: "I need someone to watch. Who won't make it a thing."

She'd said: "When."

That was what he loved about her. Not loved — the word he used in his own head when he wasn't careful, the word that lived in the space between what they were and what they'd never quite become. What he valued. What he relied on. Ryn didn't turn things into performances. She didn't ask him to explain what he meant. She heard the shape of the request — *I need to be reckless with someone who'll tell me the truth about it* — and she said when.

Now she was here, in her work trousers and a canvas jacket with grease on the cuff, sitting on a boulder at the edge of the world, eating an apple at eight in the morning, and waiting.

He walked to the center of the quarry floor. The ground was flat here, scraped clean by decades of excavation, scattered with rubble and loose stone. The quarry walls rose on three sides — rough-cut faces of pale limestone, twenty feet high in places, the marks of old tools still visible in the stone. It was a good space. Open. Empty. Nothing to break that anyone would miss.

He spread his hands.

The first thing he did was lift a stone. A small one — the size of his fist, sitting on the quarry floor three feet from his boots. He reached for it with the Wellspring, the way he'd been learning to reach: a push of force through the air between his palm and the stone's surface, channeled down his arm and out through his fingers. The stone rose. It hung in the air at eye level, wobbling slightly, and he held it there.

He looked at Ryn. She raised an eyebrow.

He let the stone drop and reached for a bigger one.

* * *

The boulder was the size of a cart. It sat at the base of the quarry wall where it had fallen from the rim — limestone, dense, its surfaces rough with age and weather. He could see the fracture line where it had separated from the wall, the clean face still pale against the darker, weathered exterior.

He put both hands out. He breathed. He pushed.

The boulder came up off the ground with a sound like the earth clearing its throat — a deep, grinding separation of stone from stone, soil tearing, the smaller rocks that had settled around its base scattering outward in a spray. It rose. Slowly at first, wobbling, the force uneven beneath it — he could feel the weight through the channeling, not as strain but as information, the stone telling him where it was dense and where it was lighter, where the grain ran and where it had fractured. It was heavier than anything he'd ever lifted. It didn't matter. The reservoir behind the effort was so vast that the expenditure barely registered — like dipping a cup into a lake.

The boulder hung ten feet above the quarry floor. Dust sifted from its underside. Small stones, shaken loose, pattered down like rain.

He pushed harder.

The boulder accelerated. Not gently — it went up like something thrown, twenty feet, thirty, the air displaced beneath it rushing outward in a gust that flattened the scrub grass along the quarry rim and sent a scatter of gravel skittering across the stone. Ryn's hair blew back. She put a hand up to shield her eyes.

At the top of its arc, the boulder hung for a moment — enormous, absurd, a piece of the earth suspended against the sky. Then Ash shifted his hands and threw it.

The boulder sailed out over the gorge. It flew in a long, slow arc — slower than it should have been, the channeled force still partly holding it, cushioning the fall — and then he let go, and it dropped. The impact came a long time later: a deep, concussive boom that rolled up the gorge walls and echoed off the quarry, followed by the smaller sounds of splash and scatter and settling.

The quarry was quiet.

Ash looked at his hands. They were shaking — not from effort, from something else. His blood was running fast and his chest was tight and the corners of his mouth were doing something he couldn't control.

He turned to Ryn.

She was standing now, the apple forgotten on the boulder behind her. Her mouth was slightly open. She closed it. Opened it again.

"Ash," she said.

"Yeah."

"You just threw a boulder off a cliff."

"Yeah."

"A big boulder."

"Yeah."

They looked at each other. The wind from the gorge blew between them, carrying the smell of disturbed rock and river water and something burned — the waste heat from the channeling, dispersing into the morning air.

Ryn started laughing first. It came out of her like a cough — sudden, involuntary, the sound of someone whose body had decided to process an experience before her mind caught up. Ash followed a second later, and then they were both laughing, hard, the kind of laughter that bent you over and made your eyes water, the kind that had nothing to do with humor and everything to do with the fact that neither of them had a framework for what had just happened.

"Again," she said, when she could breathe. She wiped her eyes with the heel of her hand. "Do it again."

* * *

He did it again.

He picked up another boulder — smaller this time, more manageable, if *manageable* was a word that still applied — and this time he didn't throw it. He held it above the quarry floor and moved it. Side to side, in slow arcs, the stone drifting through the air like something underwater. He brought it close to the quarry wall and set it, gently, against the stone face. Then he pulled it away and moved it to the other side. Then he dropped it.

The impact shook the ground. A low, resonant vibration that traveled through the quarry floor and up through the soles of his boots and into the bones of his legs. A crack appeared in the stone where the boulder had landed — a single clean line, radiating outward.

"Lighter," Ryn said from the rim. She'd moved back to her boulder, perching on it with her knees drawn up, watching him the way she watched things she was trying to understand. Not awe. Assessment. "Can you set it down without cracking the ground?"

He tried. Lifted the boulder again, brought it up, brought it back down. Slower. Gentler. The stone touched the quarry floor and settled — heavily, the weight pressing down, the ground beneath it compressing, but no crack this time. Just the deep, groaning sound of something very heavy meeting something solid.

"Better," she said.

He looked at her. "You sound like Haran."

"I'll take that." She ate another bite of the apple. He hadn't noticed her pick it back up. "What else can you do?"

What else could he do. He didn't know yet. That was the point of today — not demonstration, but discovery. The workshop with Haran had been about precision, about the small and the controlled, about fitting his power into spaces designed for human-scale channeling. This was the opposite. Here there was nothing to fit into. Just the open quarry and the sky and the question of what the Wellspring could actually do when he stopped trying to make it behave.

He turned away from her and faced the quarry wall. He put both hands up, palms flat toward the stone. He breathed. He pushed.

The force hit the wall like a hammer. Not a precise blow — a broad, rolling wave of channeled energy that struck the limestone face across its full width and kept going. The stone didn't crack. It moved. The entire surface of the quarry wall shifted — pressed inward, deformed, the rock absorbing the force and redistributing it through its mass in a ripple that traveled outward from the impact point like a stone dropped in water. Dust erupted from every seam and crack. A section of the rim above the wall buckled and a slab the size of a table separated and fell, crashing into the quarry floor with a boom that sent up a cloud of white dust.

The shockwave hit the grass at the quarry's edge and flattened it. Ryn flinched — not dramatically, just a tightening of her shoulders and a shift backward on the boulder, her hand coming up to brace against the stone behind her.

The dust settled slowly. The quarry wall had a new shape — the center pushed in, concave, a shallow bowl pressed into solid limestone by nothing but the force that had come out of his hands. The edges of the depression were smooth, the stone deformed rather than broken, as though it had been heated and pressed.

Ash lowered his hands. His arms were warm — not from effort, from the waste heat bleeding off through his skin. The air around him shimmered slightly, the way air shimmered above a hot road.

He looked at the depression in the wall. He looked at his hands.

_I did that._

The thought was simple and enormous and it sat in his chest like a bright, hot coal. He'd pushed a wall. Not a door, not a stuck fitting, not a valve seat. A wall. Solid stone, twenty feet of it, and the stone had listened.

He turned around. Ryn was watching him. The dust was still settling around them both, and her face was pale beneath the fine white powder that had coated everything, and she was very still.

"You alright?" he said.

"Fine." She dusted her shoulders off. Her voice was steady. "That was—" She stopped. Regrouped. "Can you do that without the shockwave? I'd like to keep my eardrums."

He laughed. The sound came out of him easy and bright, and it surprised him — how good this felt. Not just the power. The having someone here. The sharing of it with a person who didn't need him to be careful about their feelings, who wouldn't turn this into a report or a deployment plan or a family crisis. Ryn sat on her boulder and told him to do it without the shockwave, and the morning was still early, and the sky above the quarry was the clean, pale blue of autumn, and he was happy.

He was so happy.

* * *

They took a break. Ryn had brought food — bread and cheese and two apples and a flask of tea that had gone lukewarm in her bag. She spread it on a flat stone near the quarry's edge and they sat across from each other and ate.

Ash was covered in dust. Limestone powder in his hair, on his clothes, ground into the creases of his palms. He could feel the grit between his teeth when he bit into the bread. He didn't care. His blood was still singing, the reservoir humming behind his ribs — unchanged, undiminished, the morning's expenditures registering as nothing. Like breathing. Like blinking. He'd moved boulders and reshaped stone and the Wellspring hadn't noticed.

Ryn poured tea into the flask's cup and handed it to him. The cup was warm from the tea inside, and his hands closed around it automatically — the old gesture, the comfort of heat against cold fingers on a cool morning, except that his hands weren't cold. They hadn't been cold in weeks. The Wellspring ran warm through him constantly, a low furnace that kept his skin at a temperature slightly above what it should have been. He took the cup anyway. The warmth of the tea was different from the warmth of the power — smaller, simpler, given from someone's bag instead of drawn from an ocean inside him.

"So," Ryn said. She had her own cup — smaller, chipped at the rim, the kind of cup that lived in a work bag and got banged around. She held it in both hands the way she always did, elbows on her knees, face close to the steam. "The workshop. How did it go?"

He'd told her some of it already — the walk over, the mention of Haran, the basics. Not the details. Not the fused fittings and the rigid pipe and the valve seat he'd nearly ruined. Not the way Haran had looked at him when he'd seen the bayonet mount sealed shut.

"I helped with a pump," he said. "Mechanical, for the Velden building. The kind that runs without channeling."

"I know the building. The water's been out for weeks." She blew on her tea. "And?"

"I was too much. Too heavy. I sealed a joint that was supposed to come apart." He said it simply, the way you said things to Ryn — without dressing them up, without performing the lesson you'd learned. She didn't need the performance. She'd hear the thing underneath. "Haran showed me a technique. Micro-pulses. Tiny inputs. Surgical."

"Could you do it?"

"No." He turned the cup in his hands. "Better than before. Still not useful."

She nodded. Not sympathetically, not with the encouraging tone that other people used when he admitted failure — just a nod, the acknowledgment of a fact, the way she'd nod if he told her it was going to rain. "Can you show me?"

He looked at her.

"The technique," she said. "The small stuff. I want to see what you're working with."

He set down the cup. He looked around the quarry floor and found a stone — flat, smooth, roughly the size of his palm. He picked it up and set it on the flat rock between them.

"Haran heated a test piece," he said. "Just the center. A spot the size of a thumbnail. Precise." He held his finger above the stone. "I'm supposed to do that."

He pushed. A thread of the Wellspring — the smallest he could find, the thinnest channel between the ocean and his fingertip. The stone heated. Not just the center. The whole thing, the warmth spreading outward from his finger in a wave, the surface going from cool to warm in a breath. He could feel the heat radiating back against his hand — the waste, the bleed, the eighty percent of what he'd channeled that went everywhere instead of where he'd aimed it.

He pulled his hand back.

Ryn looked at the stone. She touched its surface with two fingers. "Warm."

"All of it."

"All of it," she confirmed. She picked the stone up and held it in her palm, feeling the weight and the heat. Then she set it back down on the flat rock and looked at him.

"Let me show you something," she said.

She held her hand above the stone. Her brow furrowed — the small crease between her eyes that meant she was concentrating, the expression he'd seen ten thousand times across a lifetime of watching her work. She pushed.

Nothing visible happened. No shimmer, no sound, no dramatic shift. The stone sat there. But after a moment — five seconds, maybe ten — she pulled her hand back and said: "Touch the center."

He touched it. Warm. Just there, just in the center — a circle of gentle heat the size of a coin, the edges of the stone still cool beneath his fingers. The contrast was precise. Not dramatic — she hadn't made the stone glow, hadn't made it hot enough to flinch from. Just warm, in exactly the place she'd intended.

"That's what I've got," she said. Her voice was matter-of-fact, the way she talked about her work — the tone of a person describing what their tools could do, without pride or apology. "My whole day's allotment could probably do that thirty, forty times. Depending on what else I need."

He looked at the warm spot on the stone. He looked at her.

"Match me," she said.

* * *

He tried.

The stone sat between them on the flat rock, Ryn's warm spot fading as the autumn air took the heat back. He held his finger above the center. He thought about Haran's test piece — the thumbnail, the precision, the quiet blooms that appeared and vanished like breathing. He thought about the valve seat and the way Haran's hands had known how much was enough.

He pushed. Less. Less than less. The smallest thread he could manage, drawn through the narrowest channel he could hold open.

The stone heated. Unevenly — the center got warmer first, which was progress, but the warmth bled outward within seconds, reaching the edges, spreading through the body of the stone the way warmth always spread when he channeled it. Not the surgical precision of Ryn's demonstration. Not the thumbnail Haran had produced. A full-body blush of heat where he'd wanted a single warm spot.

He pulled back.

"Too much," Ryn said.

"I know."

"Try again. Think smaller."

He tried again. Smaller. The metaphor that came to him was water through a pinhole — not a stream, not a thread, just the barest seep of the Wellspring finding its way through the tightest aperture he could hold. He placed his finger on the stone and pushed.

The heat came. Slower this time — he could feel it building under his fingertip, the warmth concentrating at the point of contact before it started to spread. For a moment — one second, maybe two — the warm spot held its shape. A circle under his finger, not much bigger than the one Ryn had made. Then the edges blurred and the heat radiated outward and the whole stone went warm again.

He made a sound — frustrated, a breath pushed through his teeth.

"You had it," Ryn said. "For a second."

"For a second isn't useful."

"For a second is better than yesterday." She leaned forward. "Again."

He tried again. The stone heated. Too much, too wide, the Wellspring leaning into the connection the moment he opened it, pouring through the channel he'd tried to keep narrow. Like asking a river to run through a straw — the pressure behind the flow was simply too great, the volume too vast, and the straw's walls gave way the moment the water touched them.

He pulled back. The stone was warm all the way through, radiating heat like a kettle.

Ryn picked it up and turned it over in her hands. "Hot," she said. "Not just warm. Hot."

"Sorry."

"Don't apologize. It's just data." She set the stone down and blew on her fingers. "You're getting closer to the shape. The amount is the problem."

"The amount is always the problem."

She looked at him — that look again, the one that started at his shoes and ended at whatever was behind his eyes. The crease between her brows was different now. Not concentration. Something else.

"Try one more time," she said. "And this time, don't think about small. Think about me."

He frowned. "What?"

"Match me. Don't try to be small — try to be me. Pretend you've got what I've got. A normal pool, a normal day. Pretend you're tired from work and you've got enough left for this one thing and you need to be careful with it because there's dinner to heat and hands to warm and you can't afford to waste it."

He looked at her. The morning light was coming in from the east and catching the side of her face, and there was dust in her hair and a smear of limestone powder across her jaw, and she was looking at him with the directness that was the most Ryn thing about her — the quality that had made her invaluable to him since they were children. She didn't flinch. She didn't soften. She looked at the thing in front of her and said what it was.

He placed his finger on the stone.

_Pretend you're tired. Pretend this is all you've got._

He pushed.

The warmth came slowly. It built under his fingertip — a spot, a concentrated point of heat, the stone accepting it in the way stone accepted heat, molecule by molecule, the grain structure warming from the contact point outward. He held the channel as tight as he could. Tighter. The Wellspring pressed against the restriction and he pressed back, and for a moment the flow held — a thin, controlled trickle, the heat staying where he'd aimed it, the warm spot sitting under his finger like a coin on the stone's surface.

Three seconds. Four. The edges held. The center was warm and the edges were cool and the spot was roughly the size of Ryn's — maybe twice the size, but close, closer than anything he'd managed before.

Five seconds. The channel widened. He felt it go — not a collapse, not a rupture, just a gradual opening, the pressure behind the flow finding the weakness in his control and easing through it. The warm spot bloomed. Grew. The heat reached the edges of the stone and the precision was gone.

He pulled his hand back.

Ryn touched the stone. She moved her fingers from center to edge, feeling the gradient. "Better," she said. "Much better."

"Still not—"

"Much better," she said again, and the tone was different this time — not assessment, not data collection. Warmth. The real kind, the kind that came from a person and not from channeling. She was looking at him and her mouth had the shape that meant she was pleased, the small curve at the corners that she only gave to things she actually meant.

He sat back on his heels. His hand was warm — waste heat bleeding off through his skin, the shimmer of inefficiency visible in the air above his fingers. But the five seconds had been real. Five seconds of something like precision, something like control. Five seconds of matching her.

"How much did that cost you?" she asked.

He checked — a quick internal assessment, the way you'd glance at a gauge. The reservoir sat where it had been all morning. Full. Vast. Unchanged by everything he'd done today — the boulders, the shockwaves, the wall, the precision attempts. All of it together had been a thimble drawn from a lake.

"Nothing," he said. "Essentially nothing."

Something moved across her face. Quick, not quite identifiable — there and gone, like a cloud shadow crossing open ground. She picked up her cup and drank the last of her tea.

"Let's try a different test," she said.

* * *

She found a stone — a loose piece of limestone, roughly the size of a fist, pale and dense. She set it on the flat rock between them and placed both hands around it.

"I'm going to put everything into this," she said. "Not a spot. Everything. My whole day."

"Ryn—"

"It's fine. I'll sleep it off." She gave him a look — the one that said don't argue, the one that had ended a thousand childhood debates before they started. "I want to see the difference. The real difference. Not the small stuff."

She closed her eyes. Her hands tightened around the stone. He watched her face — the crease between her brows deepening, the set of her jaw, the small tension that came into her shoulders as she channeled. It was effort. Visible effort. Her cheeks flushed and her breathing changed, coming shorter, and the muscles in her forearms stood out as though she were gripping something heavy.

The stone between her hands began to glow.

Not dramatically — a soft, amber warmth that started at the surfaces where her palms pressed and spread inward, the limestone accepting the energy and converting it to heat and a faint, warm light. It was beautiful. The glow built slowly, the stone becoming luminous from within, the color deepening from amber to a steady, even gold. Ryn's hands shook slightly. Sweat stood on her forehead.

After about ten seconds, she pulled her hands away and sat back. She was breathing hard. Her face was flushed and her eyes were bright with the effort and she looked, for a moment, the way she used to look after sprinting — the particular expression of a body that had given everything it had and was now taking stock.

The stone glowed on the flat rock between them. Warm, golden, beautiful. A whole day's channeling, made visible.

"There," she said. "That's me."

He looked at the stone. He looked at her. The dust on her face, the sweat at her temples, the way she sat — slightly slumped, the effort showing in her posture, the day's energy spent.

"You sure?" he said.

"Do it."

He picked up another stone — similar size, similar shape, another piece of loose limestone from the quarry floor. He held it in one hand. He didn't close his eyes. He didn't brace or concentrate or set his jaw.

He pushed.

The stone didn't glow. It detonated.

The heat came all at once — not a gradual build, not a warming, but a violent, instantaneous conversion of energy into light and force and sound. The limestone cracked and then shattered, fragments flying outward in a spray that pinged off the quarry walls and scattered across the flat rock. The air between his hands filled with a flash of white-gold light — brighter than Ryn's glow, brighter than daylight, bright enough that he flinched and Ryn threw an arm up and for a moment neither of them could see.

The sound followed the light — a sharp, concussive crack, like a stone splitting in frost, but louder. The echo bounced off the quarry walls and came back to them doubled.

When the light faded and the afterimages cleared, there was nothing in Ash's hand. Fragments of limestone littered the flat rock, some of them still faintly warm. A scorch mark sat on his palm — not a burn, just a discoloration, the skin darkened where the heat had passed through.

They stared at the fragments.

The wind blew through the quarry. Ryn's stone still glowed on the flat rock, warm and golden and whole.

Ash started to laugh. The sound came out of him ragged and too loud, the laugh of someone who didn't know what other sound to make. Ryn joined him a beat later — shorter, sharper, the laugh pushed out through pressed lips, the kind that came with wide eyes and a shake of the head.

"I think," Ryn said, "yours was bigger."

"I think mine was a lot bigger."

"I think mine is prettier."

He looked at the golden stone, still glowing warmly beside the scattered fragments of his. "Yeah," he said. "Yours is prettier."

The laughter faded. Not abruptly — it trailed off, the way a song trails off when the singer loses the thread, and what was left in its place was something quieter. They sat across from each other on the quarry floor, the flat rock between them, and the morning light lay across the stone like something given freely, and the silence was not uncomfortable but it was full.

Ryn picked up her stone. The glow was fading now, the heat dissipating, the gold dimming toward amber toward the ordinary color of limestone. She turned it in her hands.

"That's really it, isn't it," she said. Not a question. "That's the difference."

"Ryn—"

"Don't." She held up a hand — not sharply, not with anger. With the precision of someone who knew where a conversation was headed and chose to stop it before it arrived. "I don't need you to make it okay. It's just math."

She set the stone down. It was ordinary again — just a piece of limestone, warm to the touch, slightly glowing at its core but fading fast. She looked at it for a moment. Then she looked at him, and her face did something complicated — a series of small adjustments, the mouth shifting, the eyes shifting, the whole expression rearranging itself into something that was both a smile and not a smile, both warm and distant at the same time.

"Show me more," she said.

* * *

He showed her more.

The afternoon opened up and he filled it. He lifted stones and moved them — not hurling this time, not spectacular, but precise. Or trying to be precise. He'd pick up a piece of rubble the size of his head and float it across the quarry, trying to set it down in a specific spot. The first few times, his aim was off — the stone would drift left, or settle too fast, or clip the edge of the target and bounce. Ryn would call corrections from the rim. "Left. Too fast. You're overcorrecting. There."

He got better. Not good — better. By the fifth attempt, he could place a stone within a handspan of where he'd aimed. By the tenth, within a few inches. The precision was rough, the control intermittent, but the improvement was real, and each time he set a stone down cleanly he felt a small, bright pulse of satisfaction that had nothing to do with the Wellspring and everything to do with the doing.

He tried force — not against the wall this time, but against the quarry floor. He knelt and pressed both palms flat to the stone and pushed downward, and the ground cracked beneath him in a circle, the limestone splitting along its grain lines, a crater forming under his hands. He stood up and looked at it. The crater was three feet across and maybe a foot deep, the edges clean, the bottom rough with broken stone.

They both went quiet.

The crater sat in the quarry floor like a statement — an impression made in solid rock by nothing but the force that came through his hands. The edges were precise where the stone's grain had guided the fracture, and rough where it hadn't, and the whole thing looked like something that should have required tools and time and the coordinated effort of a dozen workers. He'd done it in three seconds, kneeling on the ground with his palms flat, and the reservoir hadn't noticed.

"Ash," Ryn said.

He looked up. She was standing at the quarry's edge, outlined against the sky, and her voice had a quality in it that he couldn't read — not fear, not awe, something between the two, something that lived in the space where those words overlapped.

"Watch this," he said. And he put his hands down again and pushed, and the crater deepened, and the sound of stone breaking filled the quarry, and the dust rose around him in a white cloud, and he was laughing — laughing into the dust and the noise and the morning, his voice cracking with it, his eyes bright behind the grit. "Watch this. Watch this."

He looked up at her through the settling dust. She was watching. Her arms were crossed and her weight was on one hip and she was watching him the way she'd always watched him — with the full weight of her attention, the complete, undivided focus of a person who had decided a long time ago that he was worth looking at.

He stood up. His knees were white with dust and his palms were raw from the stone and he was grinning — the grin that felt like it came from somewhere deeper than his face, from the place where the reservoir lived, from the bright hot center of whatever he was becoming. He spread his arms. "What do you think?"

She was quiet for a moment. The wind moved her hair across her face and she pushed it back with one hand, the gesture automatic, her eyes on him.

"I think you're going to need a bigger quarry," she said.

He laughed. She smiled. The smile was real — it reached her eyes, it sat in the lines of her face the way smiles sit in faces that have earned them, warm and true. But underneath it, like a stone beneath clear water, there was something else. Something that he might have seen if he'd been looking for it. Something that lived in the particular way she held her shoulders, in the set of her jaw, in the fact that she was smiling with her whole face and her hands were gripping her elbows hard enough that her knuckles had gone white.

He didn't see it. He was covered in dust and his blood was singing and the morning was the best morning of his life and he wanted to show her everything — every impossible thing his hands could do, every wall he could press, every stone he could move, every rule of the world that bent when he pushed against it. He wanted her to see.

"Come here," he said. "I want to try the precision thing again."

* * *

They sat cross-legged on the quarry floor, facing each other, a fresh stone between them. The afternoon light had shifted — lower now, the sun past its peak, the shadows of the quarry walls lengthening across the pale stone floor. The air was cooler. The dust had settled. The quarry was quiet except for the wind from the gorge and the distant sound of the river far below.

"Match me," Ryn said again.

She placed her hand over the stone. The crease appeared between her brows. She channeled — and a small bloom of heat appeared in the stone's center, precise, coin-sized, the warmth held exactly where she'd aimed it. She held it for a few seconds and then pulled back.

"Your turn," she said.

He placed his hand over the stone. He reached for the Wellspring — carefully, the way you'd reach for something fragile on a high shelf, stretching, controlled, the fingers delicate. He tried to match her. The same size, the same intensity, the same precision.

The heat came. It bloomed at the center — approximately the center, close enough — and it spread. Not as fast as before. Not the instant, full-body blush of his earlier attempts. The warm spot held its shape for a moment, and then another moment, and then the edges softened and the heat crept outward and the precision dissolved.

"Closer," Ryn said.

"Not close enough."

"Closer than this morning. Again."

Again. He tried. She demonstrated. He tried to match. She showed him what normal channeling looked like — the effort of it, the deliberateness, the fact that for her, every application was a choice made against a finite daily budget. She warmed the stone and he tried to warm the stone the same way, and every time he was too much — too hot, too wide, too vast. The bonfire trying to match the candle.

But he was learning something. Not precision — not yet, maybe not for a long time. Something subtler. He was learning what channeling looked like from the outside, at normal scale, with normal stakes. Ryn's face when she channeled — the effort, the concentration, the small cost of each application visible in the set of her mouth and the tension in her hands. This was what it looked like for everyone else. This was the daily arithmetic that every channeler in the world performed without thinking about it: *I have this much, this will cost that much, is it worth it?*

He had never had to ask that question. The reservoir rendered it meaningless. Everything he'd done today — the boulders, the wall, the craters, the precision attempts — had cost him functionally nothing. He could do it all again tomorrow and the day after and the day after that and the Wellspring would still be full, vast, inexhaustible, and every morning Ryn would wake up with her day's allotment and spend it carefully and go to sleep and do it again.

"One more," she said.

He held his hand over the stone. He thought about her face when she channeled — the crease, the effort, the cost. He tried to channel like her. Like a person with a budget, a person who couldn't afford to waste.

The warm spot held. Four seconds. Five. Six. The edges wobbled but didn't spread. The center was warm and the edges were cool and the spot was the size of — not a coin, not a thumbnail, but close. Close enough that when Ryn touched the stone and felt the gradient, she looked up at him with something that might have been surprise.

"There," she said. "That. Hold that."

Seven seconds. Eight. The channel wavered. The pressure behind the flow built and built, the Wellspring pressing against his control like water pressing against a finger held over the end of a hose. He held. Nine seconds. The warm spot trembled at its edges. Ten seconds.

The channel broke. The heat flooded outward and the stone went uniformly warm and the precision was gone.

He sat back. His hand was shaking. Not from the effort of channeling — from the effort of *not* channeling, of holding the gate closed against the weight of everything behind it.

"Ten seconds," Ryn said. She was quiet for a moment, her fingers still on the stone, feeling the warmth. Then she looked at him. "That's real progress."

"It's ten seconds."

"It's ten seconds more than yesterday."

He looked at her. The afternoon light was behind her now, catching the dust in her hair and turning it gold, and her face was in partial shadow, and the expression she wore was one he'd known his whole life — the look of Ryn deciding that something mattered and committing to it. The look she gave to broken things, to problems she intended to solve, to the work that filled her days.

She was giving it to him.

"Come on," she said. She picked up the stone and moved it to a fresh spot and set it down. "Again."

* * *

The sun was low when they stopped. The quarry had gone golden, the limestone walls catching the late light and holding it, the whole space suffused with a warm, amber glow that made everything look softer — the craters in the floor, the depression in the wall, the scattered fragments of the detonated stone. Beautiful damage. The record of a day spent pushing at the edges of what was possible.

They sat side by side on the quarry rim, feet dangling over the edge, the gorge falling away below them. The river was a dark thread at the bottom, catching the last of the sun. The wind had died. The air was still and cool and carried the smell of dust and stone and the green scent rising from the gorge.

Ash was filthy. Limestone dust in every fold of his clothes, in his hair, under his nails, between his teeth. His palms were raw from pressing against stone. His face ached from smiling.

"Today was good," he said.

Ryn was quiet beside him. She'd pulled her knees up and wrapped her arms around them, and she was looking out at the gorge with an expression that the failing light made difficult to read. The line of her jaw. The way she held herself — compact, contained, everything pulled in close.

"Ryn?"

"Today was good," she said. Her voice was even. She turned her head and looked at him, and the gold light caught her face, and she was smiling — the same smile from before, the one that was real and reached her eyes and had something underneath it that he almost saw but didn't.

"Thank you," he said. "For coming. For—" He gestured at the quarry behind them, the craters and the dented wall and the dust. "For watching."

"Always," she said. The word came out easy, automatic, the kind of word that lived in the foundation of a friendship so old it had its own grammar. Then something shifted in her face — a tightening, a small contraction, as though the word had meant more than she'd intended it to and she was adjusting for it.

She looked back at the gorge.

He looked at her. The profile he'd known since childhood — the nose, the jaw, the way her hair fell when she didn't push it back. The person who'd warmed his cup when his hands couldn't. Who'd lit the path when they walked home late. Who'd done a hundred small things without thinking about them, and who had never — not once, not ever — made him feel like less.

"Ryn."

"Hmm."

"I'll come back. We can do this again. I want to get the precision right. The matching thing — what you were showing me. I want to be able to do that."

She looked at him. The smile came back — slower this time, deliberate, the kind of smile that was built rather than given. "I'd like that."

"Same time next week?"

"I'll bring more food. You ate all the cheese."

"I threw a boulder off a cliff. I earned the cheese."

She laughed. The sound was quick and sharp and genuine, and it sat in the air between them for a moment before the wind from the gorge carried it away.

He stood up. He offered her his hand — the automatic gesture, the reflexive reach of a friend helping a friend stand. She took it. Her grip was strong, calloused, the hand of a person who worked with tools and knew what effort felt like. He pulled her up and she stood beside him and for a moment they were close — closer than the gesture required, the way they'd been close a thousand times without either of them acknowledging what the closeness meant.

She stepped back. Not far. Just enough.

"Walk me home?" she said.

"Yeah."

They walked. The quarry road stretched ahead of them, winding back toward the outer district, the warehouses and storage buildings dark against the fading sky. The first lights of the city were coming on in the distance — channeled lamps and heated windows, the warm glow of a city settling into evening. Solathis at dusk, seen from the plateau's edge, looked like something precious. Something worth keeping.

Ash walked beside her, and his hands were warm, and the reservoir hummed behind his ribs — patient, vast, unchanged. The best morning. The best day. The power was in him like a song, and Ryn was beside him, and the evening was coming on soft and cool, and he kept replaying the moments — the boulder arcing over the gorge, the shockwave flattening the grass, the five seconds of precision, the ten seconds, the look on her face when she'd said *there, hold that.*

"Hey," he said. "Watch this." He held out his hand, palm up, and concentrated — a small bloom of heat, just the center of his palm, a coin-sized warmth that he held for a moment, wobbling, imprecise, before it spread and his whole hand went warm.

She looked at his hand. She looked at his face — the brightness in his eyes, the grin, the dust in his hair, the pure, uncomplicated joy of a person who had found something wonderful and wanted to share it with the person who mattered most.

"Watch this," he said again, and tried to make the spot smaller, and failed, and laughed.

Ryn watched. She saw him. She was glad for him — the gladness was real, it lived in her chest, it was the thing she'd carried since childhood, the fierce, protective, bone-deep happiness of watching someone she cared about finally get something good. She was glad.

And she was watching him walk ahead of her now, his hand held up, the warm glow fading from his palm, his voice carrying back to her as he said "watch this, watch this" — and the distance between them was three feet, four feet, the ordinary distance of two people walking the same road home, and it was also the distance between a bonfire and a candle, between an ocean and a cup, between a boy she'd known her whole life and whatever he was becoming.

Both things were true. Both things were the same walk home.

She put her hands in her pockets and followed him, and the evening settled around them both — warm and cool, near and far, golden and fading — and neither of them said the thing that lived in the space between "watch this" and the watching.

The quarry road turned. The city lights grew brighter. They walked.
