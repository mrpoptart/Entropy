# Chapter 9

The quarry opened like a wound in the plateau's edge, and Ash stood at the rim with dust on his boots and the whole sky above him.

He'd been here before. Years ago, before the Wellspring, before Dorenne, before any of it—a kid scrambling down the switchback trail with scraped knees and no particular reason except that the quarry was there and it was enormous and nobody told you not to. The city didn't use it anymore. The stone had been cut out generations ago by channelers with pools deep enough to reshape geology, and what remained was a bowl of raw rock sixty meters across and thirty deep, its walls striated in bands of pale limestone and darker ironite, the floor cracked and weedy and littered with boulders that had calved off the walls over the decades. Beyond the quarry's far lip, the plateau dropped into the gorge—a sheer fall to the river, the kind of vertical that made your stomach lift even when you were standing well back.

Open ground. No buildings. No people. No infrastructure to accidentally fuse.

Ryn was sitting on a boulder at the quarry's edge with her legs dangling over the drop, eating an apple.

"You're late," she said.

"I walked."

"From where?"

"The Hall." He came to the rim and looked down. The quarry floor was bright in the mid-morning sun, the shadows short, the weeds between the cracks already brown from the season's turn. A good place. A place where you could break things without consequence.

She looked at him. She had the sharp, assessing look she'd had since they were children—the one that said she was deciding whether to say the thing she was thinking or let him figure it out himself. She bit the apple. She let him figure it out.

"I want to try something," he said.

"Obviously."

"Something big."

"Also obviously." She tossed the apple core over her shoulder into the quarry. It fell a long time before it hit. "That's why you asked me to come out here instead of, I don't know, the workshop where there are actual things to practice on."

"The workshop has things I can break."

"The workshop has things you already broke."

He laughed. She was right—she was always right about this, the practical inventory of his recent history. The fused fitting. The bent pipe. The sealed joints that were permanent when the design called for removable. Haran's quiet nod as Ash left with a cracked gear casing in his pocket and a lesson he was still turning over.

"Out here," he said, "I can't hurt anything."

Ryn looked at the quarry. Looked at the gorge beyond it. Looked at the boulders, the rock walls, the sixty meters of open air between them and anything that mattered.

"All right," she said. She stood up. Brushed the grit off her trousers. "Show me."

* * *

He started small. Relatively.

A boulder near the quarry floor—maybe half a ton, grey limestone, rounded by weather and time. He stood ten meters above it on the rim and reached down with the Wellspring. The reservoir was there, behind his ribs, vast and patient. He'd spent almost nothing since the activation. Everything he'd done—the fittings at the workshop, the small tests in his room at night, the heating lattice he'd accidentally overloaded—had been drops from something he couldn't find the bottom of.

He pushed.

The boulder moved. Not gracefully—it lurched, tipped, scraped across the quarry floor with a sound like a groan pulled from the earth's throat. Dust bloomed around it. He pushed harder, and the boulder slid three meters, leaving a pale scar on the rock behind it.

Ryn watched from the rim beside him. She didn't say anything.

He pushed again. This time he found the shape of it—not just force against mass, but force applied *under* the boulder, at its center of gravity, the way you'd pry something up if you understood where it wanted to tip. The boulder lifted. It rose a meter off the quarry floor, hung there, trembling, trailing dust like a comet tail.

"Ash."

"I know."

"That's half a ton."

"I know."

He held it. The effort was nothing. That was the strangest part—not the spectacle, not the impossibility, but the *absence of cost*. He'd watched channelers his whole life, watched them wince and flex their fingers and sit down afterward and say they were spent, say they needed to rest, say they'd used most of what they had. He'd watched Ryn warm a cup of tea and then pause, a half-second recalibration as her pool adjusted to the expenditure. He'd watched his sister seal a crack in a wall and shake out her hands like she'd been gripping something too tight.

This felt like breathing.

He let the boulder drop. It hit the quarry floor with a concussion that shook the rim under their feet, and dust rose in a column that caught the light and turned gold.

Ryn looked at the dust. Looked at him.

"Again," she said.

* * *

He threw the next one.

Not a toss. Not a push that ended in motion. A *throw*—all the force concentrated into a single instant, the boulder launching off the quarry floor like something fired from a catapult. It arced across the open space, climbing, turning slowly, and hit the far wall of the quarry forty meters away with a sound that arrived a half-second after the impact, a deep crack that echoed off the walls and came back changed, doubled, layered.

Rock dust rained down from the impact point. A section of the quarry wall fractured and slid, rubble cascading to the floor in a rushing hiss.

They stood in the silence afterward.

"How much did that cost you?" Ryn asked.

He checked. He reached inward, the way he'd been learning to do—feeling for the edge of the reservoir, trying to sense whether the level had changed. It was like standing on a cliff and trying to tell whether the ocean below was a centimeter lower than it had been a moment ago.

"Nothing."

She nodded. Not impressed, exactly. Ryn didn't perform awe. But her eyes were wider than usual, and she was standing very still in the way people stand still when they're recalibrating something they thought they understood.

"Do it again," she said.

He did it again. A bigger boulder this time—a full ton, maybe more, a rough wedge of dark ironite that had been part of the quarry wall before some ancient fracture dropped it. He lifted it, held it, felt the mass as a kind of pressure against the Wellspring's output—not resistance, exactly, but *presence*. The boulder knew it was heavy. The Wellspring didn't care.

He threw it straight up.

It rose. Twenty meters, thirty, forty—a dark shape climbing against the bright sky, slowing, hanging at the apex for a moment that stretched and stretched, a ton of rock motionless against the blue—

Then falling. Accelerating. The whistle of displaced air growing into something lower, something that vibrated in his sternum. He stepped back. Ryn stepped back. The boulder hit the quarry floor and the world detonated.

The impact threw dust and rubble outward in a ring. The ground cracked—not just where the boulder hit, but radiating out from the point of impact, a web of fractures spreading across the quarry floor like something alive. Fragments of rock pinged off the walls. A chunk the size of his fist sailed past his head and he didn't flinch because the Wellspring was already there, a reflexive pulse of force that deflected the fragment into a harmless arc. He hadn't decided to do that. The power had done it on its own, or his body had done it, or the boundary between the two was thinner than he'd thought.

The dust settled. Where the boulder had been, there was a crater. Two meters across, half a meter deep, the rock at the center compressed and fractured into a starburst pattern.

They looked at the crater.

The silence was different this time. Longer. Heavier. The kind of silence that comes after something that can't be taken back.

Then Ryn laughed.

It was not a polite laugh. Not a measured response. It was the laugh of someone who had just watched physics lose an argument—sharp, slightly unhinged, the sound of a worldview cracking open. Ash felt it catch in his own chest and then he was laughing too, both of them doubled over at the quarry's edge, laughing until his ribs hurt, until she had to sit down on the boulder she'd been sitting on before, until the laughter ran out of air and became wheezing and the wheezing became breathing and the breathing became quiet.

"You made a crater," she said.

"I made a crater."

"You made a *crater.*"

"I did."

She wiped her eyes. She looked at the quarry floor—the web of fractures, the debris field, the impact point. She looked at him. Her expression was one he hadn't seen before, or hadn't noticed before, or hadn't been the cause of before: a mix of something bright and something that was not bright, held together by the same face.

"What else can you do?" she asked.

* * *

He showed her.

Force first. He pressed his palms flat against the quarry rim and channeled downward, a broad pulse that radiated through the rock, and the air above the quarry floor shimmered—not heat haze but displacement, the atmosphere itself bending around a pressure wave that flattened the weeds and sent a ripple of dust skittering across the stone. From above, it would have looked like a breath passing over water. From where they stood, it sounded like a door slamming in a cathedral.

Then heat. He put his hand on a boulder and pushed warmth into it, the way he'd tried with the heating lattice, the way he'd tried at the workshop—but here there was no system to overload, no fittings to fuse, just raw stone and raw energy and the question of how hot a rock could get before something interesting happened. The surface under his palm went warm, then hot, then too hot to touch. He pulled his hand away. The boulder's surface was glowing—a dull red that brightened to orange as the heat spread, the stone's crystalline structure drinking the energy and converting it to light. He could feel the waste heat radiating off the stone in waves, the air above it warping and dancing.

"How hot is that?" Ryn asked, from a safe distance.

"I don't know. Hot."

"Useful clarification."

He pushed more. The glow deepened. The surface of the boulder began to change—not melting, exactly, but softening, the stone's structure losing coherence at the boundaries, becoming something between solid and liquid. The color shifted from orange toward white. The heat was fierce now, pressing against his face even from ten meters away.

"Ash."

"I see it."

"Then stop."

He stopped. The boulder sat in the morning air, white-hot at the center, orange at the edges, radiating heat in concentric waves that made the air above it look like something painted by a drunk. They watched it cool. It took a long time.

"You could forge with that," Ryn said. "You could forge anything."

"I could melt anything."

"That's not the same thing."

No. It wasn't. He thought about Haran's hands on the valve disc. The thumbnail of heat placed in the exact center of a brass test piece. The difference between *could* and *should*, which was also the difference between a hammer and a tool.

But that was for later. Right now the quarry was open and the sky was huge and the Wellspring was singing behind his ribs and he didn't want to think about precision. He wanted to throw another boulder.

So he did.

* * *

By midday the quarry floor looked like something had been angry at it.

Three craters, the largest one four meters across. A section of the far wall collapsed where he'd hit it twice. Rubble strewn across the floor in a debris field that would have taken a crew of channelers a full day to clear. The air was warm and tasted like dust and ozone—a sharp, clean smell that came from energy conversion at scale, the Wellspring's waste heat baking the moisture out of the stone and leaving something mineral and electric behind.

Ash sat on the rim with his feet dangling over the edge. His hands were dusty. His shirt was grey with rock powder. There was a scrape on his forearm where he'd been too slow to deflect a fragment—a small failure in a morning of impossible successes, and even that seemed funny. He was grinning so hard his face hurt.

Ryn sat beside him. She was eating the lunch she'd brought—bread and hard cheese and a strip of dried meat that she tore into pieces with the efficient, unhurried movements of someone who ate the same lunch every day because it worked and she saw no reason to complicate things. She offered him half the bread. He took it.

"Your turn," he said.

She looked at him sideways. "My turn to what?"

"Show me what you can do. Same thing. Pick a rock, channel into it."

She chewed the bread slowly. She looked at the quarry—the craters, the collapsed wall, the debris field, the white-hot boulder that had cooled to a sullen grey with a crack running through its center where the thermal stress had split it.

"You want me to pick a rock," she said, "and channel into it."

"Yeah."

"After that." She gestured at the quarry.

"It's not a competition."

"Obviously it's not a competition. You just reshaped the landscape."

"I just want to see. For comparison. So I have a reference point."

She looked at him for a long moment. Then she shrugged, stood up, and walked to a small stone near the rim—palm-sized, flat, the kind of stone you'd skip across water if there were water nearby. She picked it up. She held it in both hands and closed her eyes.

Ash watched. He'd watched Ryn channel a thousand times—the small, ordinary magic of a person with an ordinary pool. The way her hands went still. The slight tension in her jaw. The micro-adjustments of someone working within a budget she knew by feel, the way you know the weight of your wallet without counting the coins.

The stone began to glow. Not the searing white of Ash's boulder—a soft, warm light, like a coal in a banked fire. It pulsed once, twice, settling into a steady radiance. Heat came off it—gentle, the kind of warmth you'd hold against cold hands on a winter morning. The glow was beautiful. Contained. The light of someone who knew exactly how much they had and spent it with the precision of long practice.

Ryn opened her eyes. "That's about a third of my day," she said. She held the stone up. It glowed between her fingers like a small, captured sunrise. "I could keep it warm for an hour or so. Heat a cup of tea. Warm a cold room if it was small enough."

Ash took the stone. It was warm in his palm—a steady, even heat, nothing wasted, nothing bleeding off the edges. Clean channeling. Competent. The work of someone who'd been managing a budget her whole life and had gotten good at it because the alternative was going cold before dinner.

"Now you," she said.

He picked up a stone. Similar size, similar shape. He held it and reached for the Wellspring.

The stone lasted about a quarter of a second.

The glow came on like a flashbulb—white, instant, blinding. He felt the stone's structure collapse under the influx of energy, the crystalline lattice shattering as heat overwhelmed the molecular bonds faster than the material could conduct it. The stone didn't melt. It *detonated*—a sharp crack, a flash of white, and fragments spraying outward in a ring. He threw up a reflexive shield—another pulse of force, instinctive, unplanned—and the fragments pinged off the invisible barrier and clattered to the ground.

Silence.

Ash opened his hands. A scorched mark on each palm where the stone had been, already fading as the heat dispersed. A small circle of blackened rock at his feet where the fragments had fallen. The smell of ozone, stronger now.

Ryn stared at his hands. She stared at the scorch marks on the ground. She stared at the empty air where the stone had been.

Then she started laughing.

But this laughter was different from before. It had the same pitch, the same breathlessness, the same physical quality of surprise—but underneath it, running through it like a darker thread in a braid, there was something else. A frequency that hadn't been there when they were throwing boulders. A vibration that lived in the space between *this is amazing* and *this is the distance between us measured in a thing that used to be a rock*.

Ash heard it. He almost asked about it. He almost said *what?* in the voice he used when he knew something was wrong but didn't want to be the one to name it.

He didn't. He laughed with her instead, and the moment passed, and the frequency was still there, humming just below the register of things they were willing to say out loud.

* * *

"Match me," Ryn said.

They were standing on the quarry floor now, down among the craters and the rubble. The afternoon sun was past its peak and the quarry walls threw long shadows across the broken stone. Ryn had a piece of ironite in her hand—dark, dense, the size of a fist.

"What do you mean?"

"I'm going to channel into this. A little. Just warmth—like the stone before. And you're going to match me. Same amount. Same intensity. Nothing more."

He looked at the rock in her hand. He thought about Haran's micro-pulse technique—the thumbnail of heat, the breathing rhythm, the control that came from decades of practice compressed into a single precise gesture. He'd tried it at the workshop. He'd been terrible. His output had come out three or four times too large, flooding the test piece when the exercise called for a whisper.

But that had been in the workshop, surrounded by mechanisms and fittings and the specific pressure of trying to match Haran's mastery. Here, with Ryn, the exercise was different. Simpler. Not *match the master*. Just *match me*.

"All right," he said.

Ryn held the stone and channeled. The glow came on—soft, warm, steady. The same controlled radiance she'd demonstrated before. She held it out toward him, and the warmth of it touched his face like a breath.

"That's my baseline," she said. "That's what a normal person putting a normal amount of energy into a normal stone looks like. Match it."

He picked up a stone of his own. Same size. Same material. He held it and reached for the Wellspring, and this time he tried to reach *gently*—not the full-arm plunge of the morning's spectacle, but a careful, fingertip extension, feeling for the edge of the reservoir the way you'd feel for the edge of a table in a dark room.

The stone flared white and cracked in half.

He dropped the pieces. They were smoking.

Ryn said nothing.

He picked up another stone. Tried again. This time he went even more carefully—not just fingertip contact but *thought* of contact, the intention of touching the reservoir without actually touching it, the mental equivalent of reaching for a soap bubble.

The stone glowed. Too bright. Much too bright. But it didn't crack. He was pouring energy into it at maybe ten times the rate Ryn was managing, the waste heat rolling off the surface in visible waves, the air around his hands shimmering. His jaw was tight. His shoulders were tight. Everything in his body was clenched around the effort of *less*—and the Wellspring didn't understand *less*. The reservoir was a pressure, a constant weight behind his ribs, and controlling it was like trying to drink from a waterfall by cupping your hands and hoping.

"Closer," Ryn said. She was watching his stone. "Way too much, but closer."

He bore down. He thought about Haran's breathing—the rhythm of the micro-pulses, the steady in-and-out cadence of someone who had spent decades learning to channel in sips instead of gulps. He tried to breathe with it. In—a tiny pulse. Out—release. In—another pulse.

The stone's glow dimmed. Fluctuated. Flared. Dimmed again. It was like watching a candle in a wind—no stability, no consistency, the output lurching between too-much and still-too-much. But the lurches were getting smaller. The peaks were lower. The valleys were closer to Ryn's baseline.

"Better," she said. "Hold it there."

He held it. For three seconds, four, five—the stone glowing at approximately twice Ryn's brightness, the waste heat pushing outward in steady pulses that matched his breathing. Then his concentration slipped—a momentary lapse, an instant where the Wellspring surged against his grip—and the stone flared white and split along a hidden fault line with a sound like a knuckle cracking.

He dropped it. Shook his hands. The scorched marks were back—fainter this time, already fading.

"That was five seconds," Ryn said.

"I know."

"At twice my output."

"I know."

"So in five seconds, at twice my rate, while trying as hard as I've ever seen you try anything, you managed to sort of approximate what I do by default."

He looked at her. She wasn't mocking him. That was the thing about Ryn—she could deliver a precise accounting of your failures without cruelty, the way a doctor reads test results. Just facts. Just the shape of where you actually stood versus where you wanted to be.

"Yeah," he said. "That's about right."

"Try again."

He tried again. And again. And again.

The quarry floor accumulated a small graveyard of split stones and scorched circles—each one a failed attempt at control, each failure slightly less catastrophic than the last. He went through a dozen stones, then two dozen. Ryn stood beside him and channeled her steady, even warmth into her own stone, holding it like a lighthouse, a fixed point he could steer toward.

Slowly—painfully, incrementally, with the grinding frustration of a strong man trying to thread a needle—he got closer.

Not close enough. Not by any standard that mattered. By the time the shadows had lengthened and the quarry floor was half in shade, his best effort was still roughly three times Ryn's output—a controlled burn rather than a bonfire, but still nothing like the whisper Haran had demonstrated on the brass test piece. The technique was there, somewhere. He could feel the shape of it the way you could feel a word on the tip of your tongue—present, recognizable, maddeningly out of reach.

But closer. Measurably closer.

"That's enough," Ryn said. She put her stone down. It was still glowing—a fading warmth that dimmed slowly, like an ember going out. "You're going to burn through every loose rock in the quarry."

"There are a lot of loose rocks."

"Not as many as there were this morning."

He sat down on the quarry floor. The stone was warm under him—residual heat from the morning's experiments, the whole floor baked by hours of waste energy radiating off his failed attempts. His hands ached. Not from the channeling—the Wellspring didn't tire the way a normal pool did—but from the clenching, the sustained physical tension of trying to hold something enormous very still. His shoulders were knotted. His jaw was sore.

The precision was harder than the power. Enormously, laughably harder. Throwing boulders was easy—open the valve, let the reservoir pour, watch the world rearrange itself. This was the opposite. This was trying to not pour. Trying to hold the ocean back and let through a single drop.

*But I got closer.*

He flexed his hands. Looked at his palms—clean now, the scorch marks long gone. The same hands that had split a dozen stones today had also, for five seconds, held something almost gentle. Almost controlled.

Almost.

* * *

The light went gold. Late afternoon, the sun dropping toward the plateau's edge, the quarry walls catching the last direct light and throwing it back in tones of amber and rust. The shadows of the boulders stretched long across the broken floor. The air had cooled.

They climbed back to the rim and sat where they'd sat that morning—the same boulders, the same view, the quarry below them transformed. It looked like a different place. The craters, the collapsed wall, the debris field, the scattered remnants of stones he'd broken trying to be gentle. A record of the day written in broken rock.

Ryn pulled out a flask and drank. Offered it. He took it—water, cool, tasting of the tin lining.

"So," she said.

"So."

"You can throw boulders."

"I can throw boulders."

"You can heat rocks until they glow."

"Until they melt. Or explode."

"You can make craters."

"I can make craters."

"You can almost, on a good day, with enormous effort, sort of match what I do without thinking."

He laughed. The sound came easily, warmly—the laugh of someone who'd had the best day of his life and was sitting with the one person who could tell him the truth about it without ruining it. "That's fair."

She didn't laugh with him. She was looking at the quarry, her face in half-profile, the golden light catching the angles of her jaw and cheekbone. She was quiet for a moment that went on longer than a moment.

"What?" he said.

"Nothing." She drank from the flask again. "It's just strange."

"What is?"

"Watching you be bad at something." She turned to him. The half-smile on her face was complicated—real amusement, real affection, and something underneath that was neither. "You've always been bad at the big stuff. The channeling, the magic, all of it—you couldn't do any of it. And I was the one who could. I warmed the cups. I lit the path. I opened the doors when your hands were full. A thousand stupid little things you couldn't do and I didn't think about."

"I thought about them."

"I know you did. That's not the point." She looked at the quarry again. "The point is, now you can do all of that. You can do it a thousand times over. You can do it until the quarry looks like this." She gestured at the ruined landscape below them. "And the thing you're bad at—the thing that's genuinely hard for you—is the thing I do every day. The normal amount. The regular human dose."

She said it plainly, the way she said everything. No performance. No sentiment. Just the observation, laid out like a tool on a workbench.

Ash felt something shift in his chest. Not the Wellspring—something older, smaller, less spectacular. The feeling of hearing a truth that had been in the room for hours, waiting for someone to say it out loud.

"I'm working on it," he said.

"I know." She knocked her shoulder against his. A small contact—deliberate, familiar, the kind of gesture they'd been trading since childhood. The kind of gesture that cost nothing and meant everything. "You'll get it."

"You think so?"

"You always get things. You just take longer than you want to."

He looked at her. The golden light was doing something to her face—softening the sharpness, warming the angles, making her look like a painting of someone he'd known forever. She was looking at the quarry, not at him. She was looking at the craters and the rubble and the white-hot boulder that had finally cooled to grey and the hundred scattered stones he'd cracked and scorched and broken in his attempt to be gentle.

She was looking at the distance between what he could do and what she could do, and the distance was enormous, and the distance was the thing he couldn't see because he was inside it.

"Watch this," he said.

He picked up one more stone. Small—barely larger than his thumb. He held it in his palm and reached for the Wellspring, and this time he didn't try to match Ryn. He didn't try for precision or control or the micro-pulse technique or any of it. He just let a thread of energy flow—a thin, bright line from the reservoir to the stone—and the stone glowed.

It was too bright. Of course it was too bright. Five times what Ryn's baseline would have been, the waste heat rolling off in visible waves. But it was steady. For ten seconds, fifteen, twenty—the stone glowed in his palm at a constant rate, the output holding, the line from reservoir to stone neither surging nor fading. Not controlled. Not precise. But *consistent*.

He held it out to her. The stone in his palm, burning bright, too hot to touch with bare skin but not hot enough to crack.

"Look," he said. "Stable."

She looked at the stone. She looked at his face—the dust, the grin, the brightness in his eyes that had nothing to do with the light he was producing. She looked at a man holding a burning stone in his hand and offering it to her like it was a flower.

"Watch this," he said again, and he poured more in, and the stone flared from warm to white and the heat pushed against her face and the air between them bent and warped and the stone was a tiny sun in his palm, a star, and the light of it caught in her eyes and made them luminous, and he was laughing, saying "Watch this, watch this—"

She watched.

She saw the boy who used to walk beside her in the dark because he couldn't light the path. She saw the teenager who made jokes to cover the silences where magic should have been. She saw the young man who'd been so grateful for Dorenne's job that he'd almost cried in the hallway afterward, and she'd pretended not to notice because that was what you did for someone whose dignity was held together with practice and prayer.

She saw him now. Dust on his face, light in his hands, laughing at something impossible. The happiest she'd ever seen him. The most alive. The most himself—or the most something, anyway. The most the version of himself that had magic and could use it and didn't have to apologize for existing.

She was glad. The gladness was real and complete and it sat in her chest like something solid, something she could hold. She was glad for him the way you're glad when a drought breaks—with your whole body, with relief that goes deeper than thought.

And underneath the gladness, quieter, in the place where she kept the things she didn't say:

*I can't follow you there.*

Not jealousy. She'd examined it, turned it over, held it up to the light the way you hold a stone to check for cracks. It wasn't jealousy. She didn't want the Wellspring. She didn't want the power or the attention or the craters or the stones that detonated in her palm. She had her pool and her work and her life and she'd built them herself, brick by brick, and they were enough.

But they'd always been equals. Both limited. Both clever. Both making do with what they had and finding ways to laugh about the gaps. That was the ground they'd stood on—shared ground, level ground, the kind of ground where you could look someone in the eye without tilting your head.

The ground was gone.

Not destroyed. Just—tilted. Shifted. Rearranged by the same force that had rearranged the quarry floor. She stood on one side and he stood on the other and the distance between them was made of something she couldn't channel her way across.

She didn't name it. She wouldn't name it today. Today was his day—the day of craters and boulders and a stone held out like a gift, burning too bright and too hot and still somehow offered with the same gesture he'd used when they were kids and he'd found an interesting rock on the path and said *look at this*.

"Watch this," he said.

She watched. She was glad. The two truths lived in her chest at the same time, in the same space, each one making the other sharper.

"That's beautiful," she said.

He grinned. "I can go brighter."

"You always can."

The stone flared. The light between them was blinding, white, a small sun in a dusty hand, and in the glare his face was all teeth and joy and the particular reckless happiness of someone who has discovered they are extraordinary and hasn't yet learned what extraordinary costs.

She closed her eyes against the brightness. The light pressed through her eyelids—warm, red, encompassing. When she opened them again he'd let the stone dim, and it sat in his palm like a cooling ember, and the quarry was quiet, and the afternoon was ending, and the golden light was going out of the sky the way it always did, the way it always would, and nothing about the sunset had changed.

Everything else had.

* * *

They walked home in the late light.

The path from the quarry ran along the plateau's edge for half a kilometer before curving back toward the city, and the view was the kind of view that made you stop talking—the gorge dropping away to the left, the river a dark ribbon at the bottom, the far wall of the canyon catching the last sun in bands of red and amber. They'd walked this path a hundred times. A thousand. It was the path home from every childhood adventure, every teenage escape, every evening when the city felt too small and the sky felt like the only thing big enough to hold them.

Ash walked with his hands in his pockets, the cracked gear casing from the workshop still there, its familiar weight against his fingers. His blood was still humming. The reservoir was still full—or so close to full that the difference was meaningless. He'd thrown boulders. He'd melted rock. He'd made craters. He'd spent hours trying and failing and almost succeeding at the simplest channeling exercise a person could attempt. And the ocean behind his ribs hadn't dropped a centimeter.

He wanted to talk about it. He wanted to talk about the boulder that had hung in the air, about the pressure wave that had flattened the weeds, about the five seconds of almost-controlled warmth that had felt harder and better than any of the spectacle. He wanted to say *did you see that* and *I think I can get better* and *what if I practice every day* and a dozen other things that were all variations on the same bright, insistent need to be witnessed.

He said some of them. Not all. Ryn walked beside him and listened, and responded, and asked the right questions—*how did the control feel different from the force* and *what happens when you try to pull back instead of push forward*—and each question was good, was precise, was the kind of question that only someone who understood channeling at the practical, daily, body-level could ask.

She was good at this. She'd always been good at this—not the power itself, but the thinking about it, the understanding of what it meant to work within limits. She had a budget. She'd had a budget her whole life. She understood cost the way Ash was only beginning to.

"The precision thing," he said. "Haran's technique. It makes sense to me—I can feel what he's describing. The micro-pulses. The breathing. But when I try it, the reservoir just..." He made a gesture—both hands opening, fingers spreading, the universal sign for *too much*.

"It floods."

"Every time. I can get it down to maybe three times what it should be. Four on a bad attempt. But I can't get it to one. I can't get it to normal."

"You've been practicing for one day."

"Haran's been practicing for decades."

"Then you've got decades to catch up. Lucky you—you've got the time."

He looked at her. The sharpness in her voice was gentle—not a blade, but the flat of one, the side that says *I'm here and I'm real and I'm telling you something*. She was looking straight ahead, at the path, at the city rising in tiers ahead of them, the upper districts catching the last light, the lower ones already in shadow.

"I'll practice," he said. "I'll come out here every week. Will you come?"

"To watch you blow up rocks?"

"To give me a reference point. I need something to aim at."

She was quiet for a few steps. Her boots on the path—a steady rhythm, the same cadence she'd always had, unhurried, certain. The rhythm of someone who knew where she was going and how long it would take to get there.

"Sure," she said.

They walked.

The city grew around them—the first buildings of the outer districts, the walls showing their age, the heating lattices in the stone giving off their uneven warmth. The sounds of evening: someone channeling a door closed, the hum of a transit lift in its shaft, a child's laughter from an open window. The ordinary sounds of ordinary life in a city built by magic and maintained by people who had less of it every year.

Ash walked through it and felt the Wellspring pulse behind his ribs—vast, patient, full. He'd spent the day doing impossible things, and the cost had been nothing. The power was there. It would be there tomorrow. It would be there the next day and the day after that, an ocean that didn't recede, a sun that didn't set. He could practice the precision technique for years and never run out. He could throw a boulder every morning and melt a rock every afternoon and still have more than any channeler who'd ever lived.

He was so happy.

The happiness was in his shoulders—loose, easy, the absence of the tension he'd carried for years without noticing it. It was in his stride—longer, more open, the walk of someone who didn't need to calculate the cost of each step. It was in his hands, which kept finding the gear casing in his pocket and turning it over, a tactile reminder of the workshop and Haran's lesson and the gap between what he could do and what he could do *well*, and even that gap felt like a gift because it meant there was more to learn, more to try, more days like this one.

"Same time next week?" he said.

Ryn looked at the city. Looked at the lights coming on in the upper districts—the bright, effortless illumination of neighborhoods where elder channelers maintained the systems that kept the world running. Looked at the lower districts where the lights were dimmer, later, the product of smaller pools and longer hours.

"Same time," she said.

They reached the junction where their paths split—hers left, toward the district where she lived and worked and maintained the infrastructure that nobody noticed until it failed; his right, toward the new residence, the better neighborhood, the quarters that were closer to Dorenne's office and further from the street where they'd grown up.

He turned to her. The grin was still on his face—dimmer now, settled, but still there, still genuine, still the expression of someone who'd had the best day of his life and wanted the person standing in front of him to know it.

"Thank you," he said.

"For what?"

"For coming. For the reference point thing. For—" He gestured vaguely at the evening, at the city, at the day behind them. "For watching."

She nodded. Small nod, economical, the Ryn equivalent of a speech. She reached out and brushed the rock dust off his shoulder—a gesture so automatic, so practiced, so deeply embedded in the muscle memory of their friendship that neither of them registered it as anything until her hand was already dropping back to her side.

"Go home," she said. "Clean up. You look like you fought a quarry."

"I won."

"The quarry would disagree."

He laughed. She almost laughed. The almost was a hairline fracture—invisible, structural, the kind of crack you couldn't see until you knew where to look.

He turned right. She turned left.

He walked the rest of the way home with dust on his clothes and light behind his ribs, and his hands kept finding the gear casing, and his mind kept replaying the moments—the boulder hanging in the air, the crater, the stone detonating in his palm, the five seconds of almost-control that had felt like the hardest thing he'd ever done. He was already planning next week. He was already thinking about what he'd try, how he'd push the precision, how he'd narrow the gap between his flood and Ryn's steady warmth.

*Watch this*, he'd said. *Watch this, watch this.*

She had.

She had watched, and she had seen, and the seeing was the thing he needed more than the power—someone who'd known him before, who remembered the boy without magic, who could witness the man with it and hold both versions in her gaze without flinching.

He didn't think about what it cost her to hold them.

He didn't think about that at all.
