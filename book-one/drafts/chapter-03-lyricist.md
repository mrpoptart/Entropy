# Chapter 3

The last of the evening light caught the upper towers of the Hall of Infrastructure and turned them gold. Below that line, the city dimmed by degrees — gold to amber to the gray-brown of stone that nobody had polished in years. Ash walked downhill, hands loose at his sides, and felt the change beneath his feet before he saw it.

Good paving gave way to cracked paving. Cracked paving gave way to cobblestones with gaps where the channeled filler had worn through. The gaps widened. Weeds pushed up through them — stubborn green in the fading light, the only things in Solathis that grew without anyone's help.

He was walking fast. Not because the district made him nervous — he'd been walking this route since he was fourteen — but because there was energy in his legs that wouldn't sit still. Three days at Dorenne's table and the feeling hadn't dimmed. If anything it was getting sharper, more insistent, a hum in his chest that wanted to become words. He needed to tell someone who would understand.

Not his parents. They'd be proud, and the pride would come with his mother's hands on his face and his father's careful questions, and he loved them for it, but it wasn't what he needed right now. Not Ryn either — she'd listen, she'd be glad, but she'd ask the careful questions that made him think too much, and he didn't want to think. He wanted to talk the way you talk when you're full to the brim and the words come out fast and loose and a little foolish.

He wanted Haran.

The district dropped another level. The walkway narrowed between two buildings whose walls leaned toward each other like old men sharing a secret. A woman sat on her stoop channeling heat into a cracked cookstone, her hands cupped around it the way you'd hold a small bird, eyes half-closed with the effort. The stone barely glowed. She'd been at it a while.

Ash stepped around her. Nodded. She nodded back without opening her eyes.

Below and to the left, the rooftops staggered down toward the gorge in uneven steps. The buildings here were older than the Hall of Infrastructure — built in the early expansion, when the city was pushing outward and downward from the plateau's crown. Good bones, his father would say. Decent stone. But the channeled reinforcements that held them plumb and sealed were thinning, and you could see it in the way doorframes sagged and window casings pulled away from their walls. Small failures. The kind that nobody fixed because the people who lived here spent their trickles keeping the heating lattices alive through winter, and by the time spring came, there was nothing left for maintenance.

The air changed as he descended. Warmer, somehow, and thicker — the gorge channeling up its damp breath, mixing with woodsmoke from the handful of homes that had gone back to burning fuel. Woodsmoke was a poor-district smell. Elder neighborhoods didn't have chimneys.

Ash turned down a narrow lane, ducked under a clothesline heavy with damp fabric, and there it was. The same battered door, the same hand-painted sign that Haran had never bothered to replace: a gear and a lever, crossed like a coat of arms, with lettering so faded you had to already know what it said.

He pushed the door open and the smell hit him like a homecoming.

Sawdust. Oil. The sharp tang of cut metal. Underneath that, the warmth of bodies and work — the particular heat that came from a room where people had been building things all day. The workshop was long and low-ceilinged, every surface covered. Shelves of parts and materials climbed the walls in no particular order. Half-finished projects occupied two of the three workbenches. A contraption involving a series of interlocking wooden gears sat on the floor near the back wall, surrounded by shavings. Someone had been at it recently — the shavings were still curled, still pale.

Haran was at the far bench, bent over something Ash couldn't see. His back was to the door. He didn't look up.

"Hands," he said.

Ash looked down at his fingers. They were clean — he'd washed them before leaving the Hall. But Haran didn't mean that kind of clean. He meant: are your hands ready to work?

"Give me a minute."

He hung his jacket on the hook that had been his since he was fifteen — third from the left, below the shelf with the mismatched jars of rivets. Rolled his sleeves to the elbow. Found the rag on the ring by the door and wiped his palms, more habit than need. His hands were broad, calloused across the base of each finger, the skin rough from years of work that nobody in the upper districts did anymore. In here, they were the right kind of hands.

He crossed the room and leaned against the bench beside Haran. The old man was fitting a spring into a housing — a delicate job, the kind that required a steady grip and the patience to start over when the tension was wrong. His fingers were dark with oil. His hair, what was left of it, stood up in white tufts where he'd run those oily fingers through it. He wore the same canvas apron he always wore, stained with the history of a hundred projects.

"What's that?" Ash asked.

"Latch mechanism. For Pella's door."

"The one that sticks?"

"The one that broke." Haran held the spring with a pair of needle-nose pliers, eased it into position, and released. The spring seated itself with a clean click. He turned the housing over, tested it with his thumb, and set it aside. "What's got you walking like that?"

"Like what?"

"Like a man who just found money in his coat."

Ash grinned. He couldn't help it — the grin had been sitting behind his face since he left the Hall, waiting for someone to let it out. "I have to tell you about this week."

Haran straightened. He was tall when he remembered to stand up — taller than Ash, though the stoop of decades at a workbench hid it. His face was weathered, creased deep around the eyes and mouth, the kind of face that looked like it had been carved from the same wood he worked with. He studied Ash for a moment with an expression that gave nothing away.

"Tea first?"

"I don't need tea. I need you to listen."

"I can do both." Haran turned toward the small stove in the corner — a real stove, iron-bellied, fed with actual fuel. One of a handful in the district. He'd built it himself from salvaged parts seven years ago, and it was still the most reliable heating source on this level. But as he reached for the kettle, he paused, held his palm an inch above the water, and the water began to steam.

It happened so fast Ash almost missed it. The smallest channeling he'd ever seen — no visible effort, no hesitation, just a flicker of intent that passed through Haran's hand and into the kettle like a thought barely spoken. The water went from cold to nearly boiling in the time it took Ash to blink.

Haran set two cups on the bench. Dropped dried leaves into each. Poured.

That was Haran's channeling. Never showy, never wasteful. A man with a trickle strong enough to heat the whole building who used it to warm a kettle, and only because the stove would take ten minutes and Ash was bouncing on his heels. The efficiency was invisible unless you knew what to look for. Ash had watched him for years and still couldn't tell most of the time whether Haran was channeling or just being very good with his hands.

"Talk, then," Haran said, handing him a cup.

Ash talked.

He started with the scheduling conflict — the two maintenance crews and the offline junction — and how he'd seen the pattern before anyone else in the room. He talked about Tessaly's face when he'd pointed it out, the way her eyebrows had gone up and then immediately back down, like she was angry at herself for being surprised. He talked about Dorenne's table — the actual table, the heavy stone one in the center of the room — and how she'd moved him from the corner to the table's edge on his second day.

"She said — not in these words, but basically — she said I earned it. That the scheduling thing proved I could see the system, not just the parts."

Haran sipped his tea.

"And today, there was this councilor — Aldren, big man, strong trickle, the kind who takes up a whole room when he walks in — and he was pushing Dorenne on the northern water main. The budget doesn't cover it, everyone knows the budget doesn't cover it, but he keeps pushing because his district is the one that's dry. And Dorenne was about to—" Ash paused, trying to find the right word. "She gets this tension in her shoulders. Just before she's about to say something she'll have to stand behind for the rest of the week. I could see it building."

"What did you do?"

"I brought tea."

Haran's mouth twitched. Just the corner.

"I brought tea and I mentioned — just mentioned, in passing, while I was setting the cups down — that the northern water main crew had a gap in their schedule next month because of the Thornwall reassessment. Nobody had to give up anything. It was already there. Aldren heard 'next month' and his shoulders dropped two inches."

"And Dorenne?"

"Dorenne looked at me for about three seconds. Didn't say anything. Just—" Ash made a small, precise nodding motion. Chin dipping once, slowly. "That nod. She has this nod. Like she's filing you somewhere in her head."

"Filing you."

"In a good way. I think." Ash took a swallow of tea. It was too hot but he didn't care. "Haran, the scope of what she manages — I had no idea. I've lived in this city my whole life and I didn't know how much of it runs on her office. The transit schedules, the maintenance rotations, the structural assessments, the budget fights — all of it funnels through that room. And she keeps it all in her head. She knows which districts are three months behind on reinforcement and which ones are hiding surplus trickle in their maintenance crews to avoid contributing to the common pool. She knows everything."

"She sounds sharp."

"She's brilliant. And she doesn't waste time. Everyone in that office earns their seat or they're gone. Tessaly's been there nine years and she's still the first one in every morning." He set his cup down. The grin was back, wider than before. "For the first time, I feel like I'm actually contributing something. Like I'm doing work that matters to people who need it."

Haran looked at him.

Then Haran looked at the far wall.

On the far wall, mounted on a bracket Ash had built, was the prototype of the mechanical water pump Ash had designed last month. Not this one — this was the original, the proof of concept. Three working copies were currently installed in homes in this district, pulling water from cisterns to upper floors without a drop of channeling. Nira Dellos on the fourth level had cried when Ash fitted hers. Her trickle was so thin she'd been carrying buckets up three flights of stairs every morning for two years. She'd held Ash's hands and said something in the old trade-tongue that he didn't understand, but her face said enough.

Haran looked at the pump. He didn't say anything.

He picked up a drawknife from the bench, tested the edge with his thumb, and held it out to Ash.

"Make yourself useful, then."

Ash took the knife. "What are we working on?"

"Hinge pins. The wooden ones crack in the cold. I want to try a composite — hardwood core, metal sleeve. Should flex without snapping."

"For who?"

"For whoever needs hinges." Haran pulled a length of dark hardwood from beneath the bench. "Which, in this district, is about half the doors."

Ash settled into the work and the last of the buzzing in his chest found somewhere to go. His hands knew this — the angle of the blade, the pressure of the draw, the grain of the wood telling him which way to cut by the sound it made under the edge. The first shaving curled up long and clean. He adjusted his grip, deepened the stroke, and the second came up thinner. Better.

This was the part of his mind that Dorenne's office didn't reach. The scheduling, the politics, the systems — that was one kind of thinking, fast and verbal, like arranging tiles in a pattern. This was different. Slower. Deeper. The drawknife and the wood had a conversation that happened through his hands, and all he had to do was listen to it. The core narrowed. He checked the taper by rolling it on the flat of the bench. Smooth. Even. Good.

"Tighter on the shoulder," Haran said without looking up from his own work.

Ash adjusted. The next cut was cleaner.

They worked in silence for a while. Haran's silence was never empty — it was full of small sounds. The rasp of his file on metal. The creak of his stool. The occasional low hum, tuneless and unconscious, that he made when a piece was coming together right. Outside, the district's evening noises filtered in: voices from windows, the clatter of a handcart on cobblestones, a child shouting something that ended in laughter.

The door opened and a young woman slipped in, pulling off a pair of heavy work gloves. Lenne. Twenties. Thin-shouldered, dark circles under her eyes that never quite went away. She worked maintenance on the mid-level aqueduct — a job that demanded more from her trickle than it could comfortably give. She'd started coming to the workshop six months ago, drawn by the same logic that had drawn Ash years before: the desire to be somewhere that her limits didn't define her.

"Evening," she said.

"Lenne," Haran said.

She crossed to the third bench — hers, by habit if not by right — and pulled a project from beneath it. A small box of hinged wooden slats. Ash had seen her working on it for weeks without knowing what it was.

"Ventilation control," she said, catching his look. "For the lower tunnels. The channeled airflow system failed last month and nobody has the trickle to restart it. I figured—" She shrugged. "Mechanical louvers. Open and close with a lever. Won't move as much air, but it'll move enough."

"Will the foreman accept it?" Ash asked.

"The foreman can't breathe in the lower tunnels right now. He'll accept whatever works."

She opened the box of slats and began fitting them into the frame she'd been building. Her hands were careful, precise — the hands of someone who spent her days channeling and her evenings learning a different kind of skill. She was getting better. The joints were cleaner than they'd been a month ago.

Ash turned back to his hinge pins. The repetition was good — shape, check, set aside, start the next. His mind stayed in his hands. The wood told him what it needed.

The door opened again. This time it was Oben — sixties, retired, a man who'd spent thirty years managing channeling crews for the transit authority and now came to the workshop because he liked problems he could hold in his hands. He carried a canvas bag of parts that clinked as he walked.

"Got the bearings," he said, setting the bag on the floor near Haran's bench.

"From Kessel?"

"From Kessel's nephew. Kessel's sick. His nephew doesn't know what half this stuff is, so I got it cheap." Oben opened the bag and began laying parts on the bench in careful rows — metal cylinders, flat washers, balls of hardened glass that Ash didn't recognize. "These, though. I don't know what these are."

Haran picked up one of the glass balls, rolled it between his fingers, and held it to the lamplight. "Channeling foci. Old ones. See the cloudiness? They've been saturated and burned through so many times the crystal structure's degraded." He set it back on the bench. "Useless for channeling. Might work as bearing surfaces if we polish them down."

"That's what I thought," Oben said. "But I wanted your eye on it."

He settled into a stool and began sorting his acquisitions. The workshop absorbed him the way it absorbed everyone — gave him a task, a space, a rhythm to fall into. Within minutes, he and Haran were discussing bearing tolerances in the quiet, technical shorthand of men who'd been solving problems together for years.

Ash finished his third hinge pin and set it beside the others. Good work. Consistent. The kind of small, invisible work that kept a district's doors on their frames. He picked up the next blank and started shaping.

The door banged open.

"I figured it out!"

Tal. Sixteen, all elbows and enthusiasm, a boy who thought mechanisms were the most important thing happening in the city and would tell you so at length. He had a trickle — thin, adolescent, barely enough to warm his own hands — and he didn't care. He carried a sheaf of papers covered in drawings, held together with a clip he'd made himself from bent wire.

"The gear ratio," he said, already halfway across the room. "The one that was slipping? It's not the teeth. It's the axle diameter. If the driven gear's axle is even a hair too wide, the teeth don't mesh at the right depth and the load transfers to the tips instead of the root. I drew it." He slapped the papers on Haran's bench. "Look."

Haran looked. He studied the drawing for a long moment, turning the page once, then back.

"You drew this?"

"This morning. Before school."

"The geometry's right."

Tal's face split into a grin so wide it looked like it might hurt. "I know. I checked it four times."

"But your solution won't hold."

The grin faltered. "What? Why?"

Haran tapped the drawing with one oil-dark finger. "You've narrowed the axle to fix the mesh depth. Good instinct. But now the axle can't handle the torsional load. It'll twist under stress. Break, if the load spikes."

"So... I'm wrong?"

"You're half right. The diagnosis is perfect. The fix needs work." Haran slid the drawing back across the bench. "What else could you change to get the mesh depth right without weakening the axle?"

Tal stared at the drawing. His brow furrowed. He pulled a charcoal stick from his pocket and began sketching in the margin, muttering to himself.

Haran watched him for a moment, then turned back to his own work. Ash caught the expression on his face — the barest ghost of a smile, there and gone, the warmth of a man watching someone learn the thing that mattered.

The workshop hummed. Five people in a cluttered room, each bent over a different problem, connected by the sound of work and the comfortable understanding that nobody here needed to explain themselves. Lenne's louvers took shape. Oben sorted bearings. Tal muttered and sketched. Haran filed. Ash drew the knife along wood and felt the world narrow to the blade and the grain and the clean curl of the shaving.

This was the place.

It had always been the place. Since he was fourteen and walked through that door the first time, drawn by the sound of hammering and the smell of sawdust, not knowing what he was looking for. He'd found Haran sitting at this same bench, building a lock mechanism, and Haran had looked up and said, "You any good with your hands?" and that was it. No questions about his trickle. No careful politeness. Just: can you do the work? He could. He always could.

* * *

An hour passed. Maybe more. The light outside had gone fully dark and the workshop's own lamps — oil-fueled, wick-trimmed, because Haran's stove wasn't the only non-magical system in this room — threw warm uneven light across the benches.

Ash had moved from hinge pins to a project of his own. It sat in front of him now: a small mechanical timer, no bigger than his fist, designed to regulate the flow of water through a pipe by opening and closing a valve at set intervals. The concept was simple. The execution was not.

The problem was the escapement — the mechanism that controlled the timer's pace. Too fast and the valve opened and closed in a useless flutter. Too slow and the water pressure built past what the pipe joints could hold. He needed a rate that matched the natural flow of the district's gravity-fed cistern system, and that rate was different for every installation depending on the height differential and the pipe diameter and a dozen other variables he'd been mapping for weeks.

He'd been working on it for a month. Longer than that, really — the idea had been in his head since last winter, when Haran had pointed out that the district's water distribution was either feast or famine. People opened their taps and drained the cistern dry because there was no regulation. By the time the cistern refilled, the lower floors had gone without for hours.

"You can't fix a system by asking people to use less," Haran had said. "People are people. Fix the system."

So Ash was fixing the system. The timer would sit in the main distribution line and portion the flow — enough for everyone, all the time, without anyone needing to think about it. Without anyone needing to channel. A mechanism that managed a resource because the mechanism didn't get tired, didn't run low, didn't decide to spend its trickle on something more urgent.

He picked up the escapement wheel and turned it between his fingers. Brass, hand-cut, the teeth filed to a precision that would have been trivial for a channeler shaping metal with their mind and was painstaking by hand. Each tooth had taken him twenty minutes. There were thirty-two teeth. He didn't let himself calculate the hours.

The wheel dropped into the housing. He fitted the pallets — the small projections that caught the teeth and released them one at a time, tick by tick, measuring out seconds. The first pallet seated cleanly. The second caught.

He backed it out. Checked the angle. Filed the contact surface down by a hair. Tried again.

Click.

The mechanism engaged. He wound the mainspring — a flat coil of steel he'd spent two days tempering over Haran's stove — and released the lock.

Tick. Tick. Tick.

Even. Steady. Each tick exactly the same distance from the last.

His breath caught. He held himself still, not wanting to jar the bench, not wanting to disturb the tiny perfect rhythm of something he'd made with his hands and his mind and nothing else.

Tick. Tick. Tick.

"Haran."

The old man looked up. Looked at the timer. Listened.

He didn't say anything for a long time. He just listened to the ticking — counting, probably, checking the rate against whatever internal clock he kept in his head. Then he nodded. Once. The real nod, the one that meant something.

"That's clean work."

Ash's chest expanded. Not pride, exactly — or not just pride. Something deeper, something that lived in the muscles of his hands and the ache in his back and the knowledge that this thing existed because he'd made it exist. Nobody channeled it into being. Nobody poured their trickle into shaping the metal or cutting the teeth. He'd done it with a file and a flame and ten years of learning how things fit together.

"Still need to calibrate it," he said. "Different for each installation."

"Obviously."

"But the mechanism works."

"The mechanism works."

Tal had drifted over, his gear-ratio drawings forgotten. He stared at the timer with the naked hunger of a boy who wanted to understand everything.

"How did you get the teeth that even? Did you use a jig?"

"A filing guide. I can show you."

"Can you show me now?"

Ash glanced at Haran. The old man had already turned back to his own work, the ghost-smile briefly visible again.

"Bring a blank and a file," Ash said. "I'll walk you through it."

He spent the next half hour teaching Tal to cut gear teeth by hand — the angle of the file, the importance of counting strokes, the trick of using your fingertip to feel for burrs too small to see. Tal was impatient but sharp. His first attempt was rough. His second was better. His third was surprisingly good.

"You've got steady hands," Ash said.

"My sister says I fidget too much."

"Fidgeting and steadiness aren't the same thing. You fidget because your hands want to be doing something. When they're doing something that matters, they're steady."

Tal looked at him like he'd said something enormous. Ash hadn't meant it that way. He'd just been describing what he saw.

Lenne had stopped working too. She was leaning on her bench, arms folded, watching the lesson with a half-smile. Oben was holding one of his polished glass bearings up to the light, but his eyes were on Ash and Tal.

The workshop was full. Not crowded — five people in a room built for more — but full in the way that mattered. Full of work and warmth and the easy presence of people who'd chosen to be here. Not because they had to. Not because they were getting paid. Because this room was the one place in the city where what you couldn't do didn't matter as much as what you could.

Ash felt it — the rightness of the room, the way it fit around him like a garment he'd worn so long it had shaped itself to his body. His hands knew every tool. His feet knew every loose board. The smells were so familiar they'd stopped being smells and become the texture of a place that was home in ways his parents' apartment wasn't.

But even as he felt it, part of his mind was already elsewhere. Back in the Hall of Infrastructure, at the stone table with Dorenne's maps spread out before him. The scheduling grids. The maintenance rotations. The scale of it — the understanding that those maps represented tens of thousands of people, their water, their heat, their safety.

*That's where the real work is.*

The thought was small and quiet and he didn't examine it.

* * *

Tal left first, papers rolled under his arm, already talking about what he'd try next. Oben left shortly after, his bag of bearings repacked and organized. Lenne stayed another twenty minutes, finishing a joint on her louver assembly, then stood and stretched and said good night with the tired satisfaction of someone who'd spent their energy well.

The workshop emptied. The sounds reduced to two: Ash's file on metal, Haran's hands on wood. The old easy quiet.

"She's good," Ash said. "Lenne. Getting better fast."

"She is."

"The ventilation louvers — will they actually work?"

"At half the capacity of the channeled system. Maybe less." Haran set down the piece he was working on. "But half is enough to breathe."

Ash nodded. He was fitting the valve assembly to his timer now, the delicate part — connecting the mechanism's rhythm to the physical opening and closing of a brass gate. The linkage had to translate rotary motion into linear motion without jamming. He'd designed three versions. The first two hadn't worked. This was the third.

He worked by feel, fitting pin to slot, testing the action with his thumb. The gate opened. Closed. Opened. Each time smooth, each time clean, driven by the tick of the escapement that he could feel through the housing more than he could hear.

"You said something earlier," Haran said.

"I said a lot of things earlier."

"About contributing. How it was the first time."

Ash looked up. Haran's face was steady, unreadable. The kind of face that could hold a question for twenty minutes without giving any sign it was there.

"I just meant — at the Hall. The scale of it. Dorenne's managing the whole city's infrastructure, and I'm actually helping with that. Scheduling, logistics, pattern recognition. Things I'm good at."

"You are good at those things."

"It's different when it matters. When it's not just—" He gestured at the workshop, then caught himself. "I didn't mean—"

"I know what you meant."

Haran reached beneath his bench and pulled out a worn leather notebook. He opened it to a page near the back and slid it across the bench. Ash recognized the handwriting — Haran's cramped, precise script.

It was a list. Addresses. Dates. Notes.

*Dellos, N. — 4th level, Oren Lane. Water pump installed. Operational.*
*Massat, K. — 2nd level, Thornwall walk. Water pump installed. Operational.*
*Vosten, R. — 3rd level, lower market court. Water pump installed. Operational.*

Three addresses. Three pumps. Three homes where people no longer carried buckets up flights of stairs every morning.

"I keep a ledger," Haran said. "For the installations. Maintenance schedules."

He slid the notebook back. Closed it. Set it under the bench where it had been.

Then he picked up his file and went back to work.

Ash stared at the space where the notebook had been. Something turned over in his chest — a half-formed thought, a flicker of recognition that didn't quite catch. He thought Haran was showing him the ledger to suggest the workshop needed better record-keeping. A practical observation. Good idea, actually. He should probably start a similar system for his timer installations.

"I should set up something like that for the flow regulators," he said.

Haran's file paused on the metal. One beat. Two.

"Couldn't hurt," he said.

He went back to filing.

Ash returned to the valve assembly. The third linkage design was holding. The gate opened and closed in the timer's rhythm — smooth, reliable, mechanical. No channeling required. No trickle spent. A device that would keep working through the night, through the winter, through the decade, without asking anything of anyone.

*Build things that work without you.*

He'd heard Haran say it a hundred times. A thousand. It was the workshop's creed, spoken and unspoken, built into every project that came off these benches. Devices that didn't need a channeler standing over them. Systems that ran on their own weight, their own spring tension, their own stored energy. The opposite of everything the city depended on.

Ash understood the philosophy. He believed in it. He built according to it, every time he sat at this bench.

But now there was a version of that principle operating at a scale he'd never imagined. Dorenne's office. The scheduling grids. The maintenance rotations that kept water flowing to forty thousand people. That was systems thinking too — not mechanical, but structural. Human systems. Political systems. The architecture of a city keeping itself alive.

And he was part of that now.

He fitted the last pin. Tested the valve one more time. It opened and closed with the timer's tick, steady as a heartbeat.

"This is ready for a field test," he said. "I was thinking Maret's building — the cistern that drains every morning."

"Good choice. Short pipe run. Low pressure."

"I'll install it this weekend."

"You need help?"

"I can manage."

Haran nodded. He set down his file, folded his hands on the bench, and looked at Ash the way he sometimes did — straight on, unguarded, with an expression that contained more than Ash could read. Warmth, certainly. Something that might have been amusement. And underneath both, a patience so deep it looked like stillness.

"You should go," Haran said. "Long walk back up."

Ash looked at the time — or tried to. The workshop didn't have a clock. It had never needed one. Work started when someone arrived and ended when they were done.

"It's late," he agreed, though he wasn't sure it was. The workshop made time strange. An hour felt like ten minutes. The outside world went quiet and far away.

He cleaned his workspace. Tools back in their places — file in the rack, drawknife on its hook, scraps into the bin for sorting later. His timer went into the felt-lined box he'd made for it, nestled beside the spare springs and the bag of brass fittings. He'd carry it carefully. The escapement was fragile, and the walk uphill was long.

He pulled his jacket from the hook. The leather was soft and worn, shaped to his shoulders. It smelled faintly of oil — everything he owned smelled faintly of oil, a permanent souvenir from this room.

"Haran."

"Hm?"

"Thanks."

Haran raised an eyebrow. "For what?"

"I don't know. The tea."

"The tea cost me nothing."

"Then thanks for nothing."

The corner of Haran's mouth twitched. "Any time."

Ash pushed through the door and out into the dark.

The air hit him — cool, damp, smelling of the gorge and the faint mineral bite of the river far below. The lane was quiet. Most of the windows on this level were dark or dimly lit, the evening channeling spent, the day's trickle used up. A few oil lamps burned in brackets along the walkway — not channeled light but actual flame, the kind that consumed fuel and gave off smoke and kept burning whether anyone stood beside it or not.

He walked uphill.

The climb was long and his legs knew every step of it. Forty flights, more or less, from Haran's level to his parents' district. He'd been making this walk since he was fourteen. Sometimes tired, sometimes angry, sometimes with the blank-faced endurance of a person who'd spent his energy elsewhere and had nothing left but the discipline of one foot after the other.

Tonight was different. Tonight his legs were light. The felt-lined box under his arm held a working mechanism, a timer that would ration a resource with a precision no channeler could match because no channeler could stand beside a pipe all day and all night and never lose focus, never get tired, never decide their trickle was needed somewhere else.

And in his head, alongside the timer's rhythm, another pattern ran — the grid of Dorenne's scheduling system, the maintenance rotations, the way a city's needs could be organized and met if someone could see the shape of the whole.

Two kinds of building. Two kinds of work. One in his hands and one in his head, and for the first time both of them were real.

He climbed past a dark transit lift — stone platform in a vertical shaft, inert without a channeler to power it. He'd been climbing past this lift his entire life. Tonight he barely noticed it.

The paving improved as he ascended. Cracks sealed, cobblestones even, the channeled filler intact. The buildings stood straighter. Window light grew brighter — not lamps but the soft, steady glow of channeled illumination, powered by residents whose trickles could afford the luxury. The woodsmoke smell faded. The air lost its damp edge.

The city's stratification unfolded beneath him as he climbed away from it. Haran's district, with its leaning walls and improvised solutions. The mid-levels, where things worked but only just. The upper reaches, where the decline was something you discussed at dinner rather than something you lived.

He didn't think about this. He never thought about this. The gradient was so familiar it had become invisible — just the shape of the walk home, just the way the city was.

At the top of the final flight, he paused. Not winded — the climb that had once left him gasping at fourteen was routine now, his body built for it the way a miner's body was built for tunnels. He paused because the view caught him the way it sometimes did at night: the city spread below in scattered light, channeled and flame, the gorge invisible in the dark, the plateau's edge marked only by the absence of stars where the stone dropped away.

Beautiful. Impossible. A city that shouldn't be here, clinging to rock because centuries of powerful people had made the rock obey.

Ash looked at it and thought about Dorenne's maps. The maintenance rotations. The scheduling grids. The weight of a system so large that a single scheduling conflict could leave a district without water for a week.

He thought about the timer in the box under his arm.

Both mattered. He was sure of that. But one of them mattered at a scale he was only beginning to understand, and the pull of it — the sheer scope of Dorenne's world, the feeling of standing at the center of something enormous and being useful — was a kind of gravity he hadn't known existed.

He turned and walked toward home. Behind him and below, the workshop's windows went dark as Haran finished for the night. Ash didn't see it. He was already thinking about tomorrow's scheduling review, the northern water main, the problem he could solve before Tessaly even saw it coming.

His hands still smelled like oil and sawdust.

He didn't notice.
