## Criterion-by-Criterion Analysis

### 1. Voice & Style

**Carver (9/10).** The tightest voice match to the established chapters 4-5. The Carver's prose has the declarative clarity the style guide demands, with narrative distance modulating precisely between medium-third for the institutional scenes and deep close for the family scenes. The "what to avoid" checklist is honored almost perfectly: emotions rendered physically, world shown through use, exposition woven into action. The controlled lyrical peak in the closing — "Outside, the city glowed. Inside, the ocean pressed" — is earned by pages of grounded restraint. One minor issue: the Carver occasionally over-relies on the X-that-was-Y construction ("the architecture of a smile," "the grammar of management"). These are individually strong but the pattern repeats enough to become a tic.

**Hearthkeeper (8/10).** Warmest tone of the three drafts, which suits the family scenes but occasionally crosses into territory the style guide flags as over-explanation. The Hearthkeeper has the best instinct for domestic texture — the father pressing the jacket with the mother channeling heat from the bed, the jar of pickles carried from the rubble, the old kettle on the counter. These details do real work. The weakness is a tendency to narrate characters' interiority beyond what Ash could observe. The "she couldn't protect him from this" passage followed by a full paragraph explaining why is narrator-as-essayist rather than Ash-as-observer. The broadsheet detail is a strong Show move that the other drafts lack.

**Lyricist (8/10).** The most propulsive read. Strong rhythmic control in the demonstration scene and the best dialogue rhythm of the three. The Lyricist handles Dorenne's voice most distinctly — "Presentable is exactly the word I was hoping for. Remarkable is my job" is sharper and more characterful than either other draft's version. Weakness: the Lyricist over-narrates the final section, with the four-lens passage veering into the territory of explaining the theme rather than dramatizing it. The style guide says "trust the reader." The Lyricist doesn't, at the end.

### 2. Emotional Impact

**Carver (9/10).** The strongest emotional architecture. Each family scene lands with increasing force: the father's shoulder-grip and the unspoken question ("Did you know I was proud of you before this?"); Maren's "Twenty-three years, and there were none"; the mother's hand on his face and "Everything. They'll want everything." These scenes earn their weight because the Carver trusts physical rendering. The mother's hand "rough — a working hand, a mother's hand" is devastatingly specific. The closing — "Nobody had seen his hands" — is the strongest single emotional line across all three drafts. It crystallizes the entire chapter's theme in five words.

**Hearthkeeper (8/10).** Strong emotional work with the family, particularly the mother. The Hearthkeeper gives the mother the most space and the most voice. "They put you on a stage, Ashlyn. They put you on a stage and they watched" — the full name landing with tenderness and fury — is the best mother line across all drafts. The father's observation — "I'm worried you'll let them, because it'll feel like being useful" — is the sharpest father beat. The Hearthkeeper's weakness is the Ryn scene, which comes the next morning rather than the evening of the event, reducing its emotional immediacy.

**Lyricist (7/10).** The demonstration scene is the most immersive — the lattice activation spreading through the chamber walls is physically convincing and rendered with genuine awe. But the family scenes are weaker than the other drafts. The father and Maren scenes feel compressed. The mother scene, while effective, lacks the Carver's devastating specificity and the Hearthkeeper's fierce voice. The Lyricist's strongest emotional moment is actually in the Dorenne material — "You're not on trial. You're standing beside me while I explain something wonderful" carries genuine warmth-as-manipulation that the reader can feel working on Ash. The closing four-lens passage is intellectually precise but emotionally detached.

### 3. Pacing & Structure

**Carver (7/10).** Earns most of its length but sags in two places: the Q&A section after the demonstration runs long with institutional back-and-forth that dilutes the momentum after the stone-lighting climax; and the father's kitchen scene, while individually strong, repeats emotional ground the corridor scene with the mother already covered. The structure — prep, event, aftermath, family, Ryn, reflection — is clean and logical. The chapter opens strong with the suit/mirror beat and closes with momentum.

**Hearthkeeper (6/10).** The longest draft and the one that most struggles with pacing. The demonstration/event section works well, and the corridor-aftermath material — with overheard fragments from behind the curtain — is effective. But the family section is where the pacing collapses. The chapter visits the family apartment, introduces the father, introduces Maren, then brings Ryn the next morning. Each scene is individually well-drawn but the cumulative effect is repetitive: four successive conversations in the same kitchen, each covering adjacent emotional territory. Separating the Ryn scene to the following morning breaks the chapter's temporal unity.

**Lyricist (7/10).** Well-paced through the demonstration and Dorenne aftermath. The lattice demonstration is the most cinematically rendered — the slow spread of amber light through the channels is the best single scene across all drafts. The Q&A is tighter than the Carver's. The family section is more compressed but the mother, father, and Maren scenes feel slightly rushed. The closing reflection runs long — approximately 30 lines of thematic summary that the style guide would flag as telling.

### 4. Character

**Carver (9/10).** The strongest character work across the board. Each person feels like themselves per the character profiles.

*Dorenne:* The coaching scene captures her perfectly. "They will want to see a weapon. A spectacle. A solution to every problem they've been too afraid to name. You are not those things" is the best single Dorenne speech across all drafts — genuine care and strategic framing operating simultaneously.

*The mother:* "You're twenty-three years old and a room full of politicians just watched you glow." Fierce, precise, the mother's character profile made vocal.

*Maren:* The collar adjustment — quick, precise, the "small repair she made without thinking" — is the most Maren gesture in any draft. Her "Twenty-three years, and there were none" is devastating.

*Ryn:* The best Ryn across all drafts. "Don't thank me for coming. Thank me for not asking you to glow" is the most naturalistic dialogue line in any version and perfectly captures her profile.

*The father:* "Your mother is your mother" — three words that carry an entire family's history.

**Hearthkeeper (9/10).** Nearly matches the Carver on character, with particular strengths in the family scenes. The father's "I'm worried you'll let them, because it'll feel like being useful" is the most psychologically astute father line across all drafts. Maren's "I'm used to being the Torren who channels. Now I'm the Torren who's related to the Torren who channels" is excellent — self-aware, wry, with the pain filed down to its thinnest edge. The Ryn scene is slightly weaker — the exploration of Ash's contradictory feelings about her lack of awe is accurate to the outline but over-narrated.

**Lyricist (8/10).** Dorenne is the star here. The Lyricist's Dorenne is the most fully realized — her coaching dialogue is the sharpest, and the "something wonderful" line captures her genuine warmth-as-strategy better than either other draft. Ryn's direct challenge about Dorenne ("She's one of those people, Ash") pushes the timeline — the outline suggests this level of directness should arrive closer to Chapter 14. The family scenes are the weakest across drafts.

### 5. Worldbuilding Integration

**Carver (9/10).** World shown entirely through use. The demonstration — channeling into a scaling stone, waste heat raising the room's temperature, channeling strips dimming in response — is rendered as physical experience rather than explained mechanism. The tea scene with the father — Ash choosing not to cool the tea because "the heat was his father's pool" — is the best single worldbuilding-as-character beat across all drafts.

**Hearthkeeper (8/10).** The broadsheet detail is a worldbuilding contribution the other drafts lack — it shows how information moves through Solathis and gives the reader a physical artifact. The "dead weight" term appearing in neighbor gossip is a strong callback. Weakness: some political-faction material reads more like outline scaffolding than lived world.

**Lyricist (9/10).** The most immersive demonstration scene. The lattice activation — amber spreading through cold channels, the room warming from the bones out — is the most physically convincing rendering of magic-in-use across all drafts. The detail about channels "cold so long the mineral had contracted around them" is specific and believable. The Lyricist also gives the best sense of the Hall as a physical space.

### 6. Continuity

**Carver (9/10).** The strongest continuity with chapters 4-5. The suit/collar motif, ramp and forty-flights callbacks, mother's broken leg, temporary housing, naming consistency (Vasra, Dorenne, Maren, Ryn, Haran, Tessaly), the scar from Haran's workshop, the Councilor's gaze at Ash's hands — all track.

**Hearthkeeper (8/10).** Good continuity. The mother's cooking details connect to Chapter 4's kitchen scene. The old kettle motif is maintained. Naming is consistent. One concern: placing Ryn's visit the next morning reduces the day's compression.

**Lyricist (7/10).** Good continuity with the Hall and Dorenne material. However, introduces elements that don't connect to prior chapters, and Ryn's direct challenge about Dorenne presumes awareness she hasn't been shown developing.

### 7. Seeds & Setup

**Carver (9/10).** All four required threads progressed precisely:

*Thread 6:* "Control the question" and "Dorenne didn't describe the world. She curated it" plant the management-as-care dynamic.

*Thread 9:* "Everything. They'll want everything" is the mother's defining line and plants the fear driving her arc.

*Thread 11:* "I'm glad" delivered with required simplicity. Resentment present but faint — correctly calibrated for this early stage.

*Thread 12:* "It felt the same as being wanted. He couldn't tell the difference" directly hits the chapter's required emotional beat. "Nobody had seen his hands" is the identity fusion in miniature.

**Hearthkeeper (8/10).** All four threads addressed. Thread 6 handled through Dorenne's "those two things are not in conflict. Yet." Thread 9 is the Hearthkeeper's strongest. Thread 11 slightly weakened by next-morning placement. Thread 12 progressed through the gravity metaphor in the closing.

**Lyricist (8/10).** All four threads addressed. Thread 6 is the Lyricist's strength. Thread 9 is weaker — the mother gets less space. Thread 11 accelerates beyond what the convergence map suggests. Thread 12 hits the right beat but over-explains it.

## Best Passages

### Carver

**"Nobody had seen his hands":**
The chapter's thematic core in five words. The gap between the public Wellspring and the private person, rendered through the most concrete image possible — the hands that are scarred, calloused, and warm, that no one in the room looked at because they were looking at the light.

**The father's tea scene:**
Ash choosing not to cool the tea because the heat was his father's pool. Character, worldbuilding, and the new distance between father and son — all rendered through a cup of tea.

**Dorenne's coaching speech:**
"They will want to see a weapon. A spectacle. A solution to every problem they've been too afraid to name. You are not those things." Dorenne's character in six sentences.

### Hearthkeeper

**The mother and the broadsheet:**
Her hand covering it rather than reading it. Behavior instead of narration.

**The father's quiet precision:**
"She's worried they'll use you. I'm worried you'll let them, because it'll feel like being useful." The sharpest single line of psychological insight across all three drafts.

**Maren's identity shift:**
"I'm used to being the Torren who channels. Now I'm the Torren who's related to the Torren who channels."

### Lyricist

**The lattice activation:**
The most immersive demonstration across all drafts. Amber spreading through cold channels like dawn breaking across a hillside.

**Dorenne's pitch:**
"You're not on trial. You're standing beside me while I explain something wonderful." The word "wonderful" doing enormous work — genuine awe and positioning in the same sentence.

**Ryn's directness:**
"She put you in a room and lit you up for a hundred and fifty people and stood beside you looking like the person who found the treasure." The sharpest Ryn line across all drafts, though it arrives too early for the convergence map's timeline.

## Weakest Passages

### Carver
- **Q&A section:** Should be compressed by ~40%. Institutional back-and-forth dilutes post-demonstration momentum.
- **Repetitive X-as-Y constructions:** "The architecture of a smile," "the grammar of management," "the acoustic equivalent of attention." Keep 2-3 per chapter.

### Hearthkeeper
- **Over-narrated mother interiority:** Explains the character profile rather than showing her in action.
- **Ryn scene pacing:** Contradictory feelings narrated explicitly rather than rendered through behavior.
- **Ryn visit timing:** Next-morning placement dilutes temporal compression.

### Lyricist
- **Four-lens closing passage:** Thematic over-explanation the style guide warns against. Cut entirely or reduce to "It felt exactly the same."
- **Ryn's Dorenne challenge:** Too early for the convergence map's timeline.
- **Compressed family scenes:** Mother, father, and Maren feel dutiful rather than felt.

## Recommended Path

### Primary Recommendation: Build from the Carver

The Carver should serve as the foundation draft. It has the strongest voice match to chapters 4-5, the most disciplined POV, the best emotional architecture, and the most devastating closing passage.

### Passages to Pull from Other Drafts

1. **From the Hearthkeeper: the father's line.** "She's worried they'll use you. I'm worried you'll let them, because it'll feel like being useful." Insert into the Carver's father scene.

2. **From the Hearthkeeper: the broadsheet detail.** Adding a physical artifact to the mother scene grounds it further.

3. **From the Lyricist: the lattice demonstration.** The lattice is a stronger choice than the scaling stone — it demonstrates practical utility, connects to the infrastructure-decline thread, and gives the audience a visceral experience (the room warming) rather than a visual one.

4. **From the Hearthkeeper: Maren's "the Torren who channels" line.** Plants Maren's future identity shift alongside the Carver's "Twenty-three years, and there were none."

### What Not to Pull

- Do not pull the Lyricist's four-lens closing (thematic over-explanation)
- Do not pull the Lyricist's Ryn-challenges-Dorenne dialogue (too early)
- Do not pull the Hearthkeeper's over-narrated mother-interiority paragraph
- Do not pull the Hearthkeeper's next-morning Ryn timing
