# Chapter 7

The new apartment had windows that faced east, and the morning light came through them clean and unobstructed, falling across a floor that was smooth and level and didn't creak.

Ash stood in the main room with a crate balanced on his hip and looked at the light on the stone. Back home—back at the old place—the morning had always come in slanted, filtered through the walkway overhang and the building across the lane, so the kitchen never really brightened until midday. Here the sun arrived like it meant to. It crossed the floor in a wide band and found the walls and made them glow, pale sandstone the color of warm bread, and the room filled with it the way a cup fills with water—steadily, completely, without effort.

He set the crate down. Kitchen things—plates, cups, a wooden spoon his mother had packed in a cloth. The apartment was three rooms plus a washroom, which was one more room than they'd ever had. The heating lattice ran along the base of every wall, the interfaces set into the stone at regular intervals, smooth discs the size of his palm. All of them functional. All of them humming faintly with residual warmth from the previous tenants' channeling, the stones still holding heat the way good stonework did—slowly releasing what had been poured in, keeping the rooms comfortable without anyone having to feed them every morning.

His mother wouldn't have to feed them every morning.

He crossed to the window and looked out. The street below was wide enough for two carts abreast, the paving stones fitted tight, no gaps, no settling, no weeds pushing through the joints. A woman walked past with a basket on her arm, unhurried. Across the way, a transit lift glowed in the wall of the facing building—the interface bright, the disc smooth, clearly serviced within the season. It worked. Everything here worked.

Dorenne had arranged it. Not as a gift—she'd been clear about that. The old building was condemned. The structural team's reinforcement had saved the lower floors, but the fifth floor was gone and the fourth was compromised, and the assessment report had used the word *uninhabitable* with the institutional finality of a door closing. The families needed housing. Dorenne's office managed housing allocation for infrastructure personnel and their dependents. The paperwork moved through channels that Ash recognized from his weeks at the desk—the same deployment briefs, the same allocation forms, the same grammar of resources and priorities.

The result was this. An apartment in the Vellen district, four blocks from the Hall of Infrastructure, in a building where the stairs didn't groan and the walls didn't crack and the heating worked without anyone pouring their pool into it like water into a leaking bucket.

He opened another crate. His father's tools—wrapped in oilcloth, each one nested against the next with the care of a man who understood that a good chisel outlasted the hand that held it. Ash set them on the shelf his father would want them on, the one by the window where the light was best. The hammer first, then the screwdrivers, then the set of files in their leather roll. He knew the order. He'd watched his father arrange these tools in three different workshops and two apartments, and the order never changed.

The front door opened. His father came through sideways, a chair in his arms—one of the kitchen chairs from the old apartment, scarred and reglued at one leg, the seat worn smooth by years. Behind him, the corridor was full of light. The new building had channeling strips in the hallways, bright and even, the kind of lighting that made everything look precise.

"Where do you want this?" his father asked.

"Kitchen's through there."

His father carried the chair through and set it at the table—the new table, provided with the apartment, solid and unmarked. The old chair looked different next to it. Smaller. The wood darker with age, the joints visible where they'd been repaired. His father adjusted it, testing the legs on the level floor, and the chair didn't wobble.

"Huh," his father said. He pressed down on the seat with both hands, testing. The chair sat flat and still and obedient, and his father's face did something complicated—surprise and something adjacent to loss, the expression of a man who had spent years shimming furniture against uneven floors and had just discovered the problem was the floor, not the chair.

"Solid building," his father said.

"It is."

His father looked at him. The look held longer than it needed to—a beat, two beats, the kind of pause that had a shape but no words. Then his father nodded and went back to the corridor for the next load.

* * *

The old apartment was a wound.

Ash stood in what had been the kitchen and looked at the sky through the ceiling. Two floors of stone between here and open air, and he could see clouds through the gap where the structural collapse had opened a channel straight up. The walls were standing but wrong—cracked, shifted, the plaster gone and the stone beneath it exposed in patterns that looked like a language he couldn't read. Dust covered everything. His dust. The force that had poured through him and blown the walls outward had left its signature on every surface—the scorch marks where raw energy had bled off as heat, the impact fractures where rubble had struck, the ragged edge of the exterior wall where the stone had simply ceased to exist.

His mother was in the bedroom.

She moved carefully—the splint on her leg made her gait uneven, a controlled limp that she navigated with the same determination she brought to everything. The elder-generation healer Dorenne had sent was good, and the bone was knitting, but the healing took time even with channeling, and his mother's body remembered the weight of the beam the way the stone remembered the force that had moved through it.

She had a canvas bag over her shoulder. She was packing.

Ash watched her from the doorway. She picked through the rubble with her hands, turning over broken things, examining each one with a focus that seemed disproportionate to the task. A plate, cracked in two—she looked at both halves, turned them over, set them aside. A folded cloth, grey with dust but intact—she shook it out, held it up, folded it again. Into the bag.

"Mum, we don't need—"

"I know what we need."

She said it without looking up. Her voice was even, the way it got when she was holding something in place. She reached into a pile of debris near the bed frame and pulled out a bowl. Small, clay, the glaze chipped on one side and a crack running from the rim to the base. A serving bowl. Ash remembered it on the kitchen table—cumin and onions, meals she made when the budget was tight. The crack was old. The bowl had been cracked for years.

She put it in the bag.

She pulled out a curtain—singed at one edge, the fabric stiff with dust. The curtain that had hung between the kitchen and the hallway, not for privacy but for warmth, to hold the heat from her channeling in the room where the family gathered. It was ruined. The singed edge was brittle, and the fabric tore when she folded it. She folded it anyway. Into the bag.

"We have new curtains," Ash said. "The apartment came with—"

"I heard you."

She moved to the kitchen. What was left of it. The stove was buried under a section of wall, the iron fire grate visible beneath the stone, bent but not broken. She couldn't reach the stove. She stood looking at the rubble that covered it, and her jaw worked, and her hands opened and closed at her sides.

"I'll dig that out," Ash said. "Dad and I can—"

"Leave it."

She knelt—slowly, carefully, the splinted leg extended—and reached under a fallen shelf. Her arm went in to the shoulder. When she pulled back, she was holding a kettle.

The kettle.

Dented. Blackened on one side where the heat had caught it. The handle bent at an angle that made it difficult to hold. Fifteen years of mornings—water poured, tea made, the copper surface darkened by use and burnished by her hands until it had its own color, a color no new kettle would ever have. The base was warped from the years of sitting on the channeling interface, her pool flowing through the stone and into the metal, warming the water with the steady patience of a woman who did one small thing every morning because the small things were what she could do.

She held it in both hands. Her thumbs moved across the dented surface, finding the familiar topography, the bumps and valleys her hands knew. She didn't say anything. She held it and her face was still and her eyes were bright and the morning light came through the hole in the wall and fell across her hands and the kettle and the ruins of the kitchen where she had fed her family for twenty-three years.

She put it in the bag.

Ash watched her stand. The effort showed in her arms, in the bracing of her good leg, in the set of her mouth. She slung the bag over her shoulder and looked at the kitchen one more time—the buried stove, the cracked floor, the sky through the ceiling. Then she turned and walked past him through the doorway.

"Your father's waiting," she said.

He followed her down the stairs. Five flights, then four, then three. She took each step with the deliberate care of someone managing pain, her hand on the railing, her weight shifting with each descent. The stairwell was damaged but passable—Sorren's team had stabilized the main structure, and the lower floors were sound. The cracks he'd measured with his thumb every morning were still there, wider now, but held in place by the reinforcement channeling that ran through the building's bones like sutures.

At the second-floor landing, old Verath's door was closed. A notice from the housing office was tacked to the wood—relocation information, temporary housing allocation, the institutional language of displacement rendered in neat handwriting on official paper. The cat was nowhere.

They emerged into the street. His father was loading a cart—a borrowed thing, wooden-wheeled, the kind the lower districts used for hauling. He had the canvas bags arranged by weight, the heaviest at the bottom, the way he arranged everything: systematically, with the quiet competence of a man who understood loads and leverage and the proper distribution of weight.

Ash's mother handed him the bag. He took it without asking what was inside. He set it on the cart beside the others, and his hand rested on it for a moment—not adjusting the weight, not testing the balance. Just resting. As if the bag contained something that needed a hand on it.

Then he picked up the cart's handles and they walked.

* * *

Ash brought the kettle three days later.

He'd found it in the market district—the upper market, the one near the Hall, where the merchants sold to people who had money and taste and didn't need to haggle. Copper. Polished. The handle was curved for the grip, shaped by a craftsman who understood hands. The base was flat and even, designed for a proper channeling interface, the kind that heated uniformly and didn't warp the metal over time.

It cost more than he'd ever spent on a single object. He didn't hesitate.

He carried it home—home, the new apartment, the word still new in his mouth—and set it on the kitchen table while his mother was preparing dinner. She was at the new stove, which was cleaner and better-maintained than the old one and had a channeling interface that responded to the lightest touch. She hadn't touched the interface. She was using the iron fire grate, the way she always had, her pool warming the metal directly, the old way, the way she knew.

"I got something," he said.

She turned. He held up the kettle. The copper caught the light from the windows—the eastern light that came in clean and strong—and the surface gleamed, unmarked, perfect, without a single dent or scratch or year of hands.

His mother looked at the kettle. She crossed the kitchen and took it from him. She held it the way she'd held the old one—in both hands, testing the weight, feeling the balance. The new kettle was lighter. Better proportioned. The handle fit her grip without the awkward angle of the bent one.

"It's beautiful," she said.

"Copper. Same as the old one, but—" He gestured. Better. He didn't need to say it.

She set it on the stove interface. The copper rang faintly against the stone—a clear, bright note, like a bell, nothing like the dull thud the old kettle made on the old stove. She pressed her palm to the interface and the kettle warmed. Quickly, evenly, the heat moving through the stone and into the copper with an efficiency the old stove never had. Steam rose from the spout within a minute.

"Thank you," she said. She poured the tea. The pour was smooth, controlled, the weight of the new kettle steady in her hand.

That evening, after dinner, Ash went to the washroom. When he came back through the kitchen, the new kettle was on the shelf by the stove, cleaned, gleaming.

The old kettle—dented, blackened, the handle bent—was in the cabinet. He saw her put it there. She opened the lower cabinet, the one by the floor, and set the old kettle inside it on the shelf between a stack of cloth napkins and a ceramic jar he didn't recognize. She arranged it carefully, turning it so the handle faced outward, the way you arrange something you intend to reach for again.

She closed the cabinet door.

"Night, Mum."

"Goodnight."

He went to his room—his new room, with the window and the level floor and the heating lattice that hummed faintly in the wall—and he thought about the kettle on the shelf, the copper catching the light, and the way she'd said *beautiful* like she meant it. He'd done something right. Something tangible. Something his hands had chosen and his money had bought and his love had carried across the market and into her kitchen.

The old kettle sat in the cabinet, in the dark, and he didn't think about it at all.

* * *

He brought blankets the next day. Thick wool, dyed a deep blue, the kind the upper districts sold in bundles of four with matching edges. He spread them on his parents' bed while his mother was out—Maren had taken her to see the healer, the follow-up on the bone, the check that involved the elder channeler pressing her hands to the leg and reading the fracture with the focused intensity of someone counting threads.

His father found the blankets when he came home from a labor shift. He stood in the bedroom doorway and looked at them.

"From Ash?" he asked, though no one else was there to answer.

He crossed the room and touched the wool. His hands—broad, scarred, the hands of a man who'd spent his life carrying and building and repairing—moved across the fabric with a gentleness that looked wrong on them, too careful, like handling something fragile.

Ash came in behind him. "Found them in the upper market. Thought Mum might—"

"She'll love them."

His father sat on the bed. The mattress was new too—provided with the apartment—and it didn't sag under his weight the way the old one had, the one that had developed a valley in the center from twenty years of the same two bodies in the same positions.

"These are good," his father said, running his hand along the edge. "Heavy."

"Warmest they had."

His father nodded. He was quiet for a while, his hand on the blanket, the room around him full of new things. New walls, new floor, new light. The shelf by the window held his tools—Ash had set them out in the right order, and his father hadn't moved them.

"You're doing well," his father said.

"It's just blankets."

"That's not what I mean." His father looked at him. The look was steady, direct, the way his father looked at joints and foundations—assessing not the surface but the structure underneath. "I mean all of it. The apartment. The—what you're doing at the Hall. How you're handling..."

He trailed off. His hands found each other in his lap, the fingers interlacing the way they did when he was working through something that didn't have a shape yet.

"I'm proud of you," he said. "You know that."

"I know, Dad."

"Good." His father stood. He put his hand on Ash's shoulder—the grip firm, the palm warm from his own pool, the low constant heat of a working man's channeling. He held it there for a beat longer than the conversation required.

Then he went to the kitchen to start dinner, because that's what he did—he worked, he provided, he moved through the world with his hands and his body and the quiet certainty that the next task was always waiting. And the question he carried—the one that lived in the extra beat of his grip, in the trailing off, in the look that assessed the structure underneath—stayed where it was. Unasked. Present.

*Did you know I loved you before this?*

* * *

Tools came last. A new set of files, higher quality than anything his father had owned. A plane with a blade that held its edge. A measuring square in brass, the kind the upper-market craftsmen used, precise to the width of a hair.

His father unwrapped them on the kitchen table. He laid each one out in a line—the files, the plane, the square—and touched them the way he'd touched the blankets. Carefully. With the particular attention of a man who understood tools the way some people understood music, each one a voice, a purpose, a potential.

"These are too good for the work I do," he said.

"No they're not."

His father looked up. His mouth opened, closed. He picked up the plane and turned it in his hands, sighting down the blade the way you sight down a straight edge, checking the alignment. It was perfect. Of course it was—it was new, it was well-made, it was designed for hands more important than the ones holding it. That was what Ash was saying with every gift: *you deserve better than what you had.*

What his father heard, underneath, was something else. Something Ash didn't intend and wouldn't have understood if someone had explained it. The gifts said *better.* They said *new.* They said *the old life was insufficient and this one is not.* And his father, who had built the old life with those scarred hands, who had carried his family's weight on shoulders that never quite straightened at the end of the day, heard the gifts the way the building heard the earthquake—as a force that was reshaping things without asking whether the old shape had been worth keeping.

He put the plane down. He put the files in their roll. He put the measuring square in his pocket, where it would stay for weeks, touching it the way you touch a coin—not for the value, but for the weight.

"Thank you," he said. And he meant it. Both things were true.

* * *

The neighborhood was quiet in a way the old one never was.

In the old building, sound had been constant—Verath's cat in the stairwell, the Merrat children running the corridors, doors opening and closing as neighbors moved through each other's days with the unconscious familiarity of people who shared walls and heating lattices and the particular intimacy of poverty. The building had breathed with its residents. You heard laughter through the floor and arguments through the ceiling and someone singing off-key in the washroom at hours that should have been embarrassing, and none of it was, because the building was a body and its people were the pulse.

Here, the walls were thick.

Ash walked the corridor on the fourth day and passed two neighbors. The first, a woman about his mother's age, carrying a basket of folded linens. She nodded—a small, precise motion, courteous, the kind of nod you gave to someone whose name you intended to learn but hadn't yet. Ash nodded back. The corridor smelled like nothing. Not cooking, not stone dust, not the waxy warmth of channeling lattices running hot. Nothing.

The second neighbor was a man, older, standing at his door with a key. He glanced at Ash, and the glance did something—a quick recalculation, a recognition that moved across his face like a shadow. The Wellspring's family. He'd seen the press event, or heard about it, or been told by someone who'd been told by someone. Everyone in this district knew. The building's newest residents hadn't been selected at random.

"Good morning," the man said.

"Morning."

The man went inside. The door closed. The corridor was quiet again.

In the old building, Ash would have known the man's name by now. Would have known his wife, his work schedule, the sound of his cough on cold mornings. Would have borrowed a cup of something or lent a hand with a stuck door or stood in the stairwell arguing about whether the heating lattice on three was truly dead or just sulking. The choreography of proximity, the dance of people who lived close enough to hear each other's kettle boil.

Here, the transit lift hummed in the wall at the end of the corridor. Ash walked past it. He took the stairs—not because he couldn't use the lift. Because the stairs were still his, still the thing his legs knew, still the one piece of the old life that worked the same in any building. Four flights instead of five. The stairwell was clean and wide and smelled like stone and nothing else.

He emerged on the ground floor. The street was sunlit and well-maintained and quiet in the particular way of districts where things functioned: without urgency, without the frantic energy of places where every system was failing and every person was compensating and every day was a negotiation between what you needed and what you could afford.

A man was repairing a walkway across the street. He knelt at the edge of the paving, his hands on the stone, channeling steadily—maintenance work, the kind that kept surfaces level and joints sealed. He worked alone. In the old district, the same repair would have been done by a team, each one contributing their portion, pooling what they had to cover what none of them could afford alone.

Here, one person was enough. The work was manageable. The infrastructure held.

Ash stood in the sunlight and breathed and felt the warmth of the morning on his face and the warmth of the reservoir behind his ribs—twin sources, external and internal, the world and the Wellspring in comfortable alignment. His family was safe. The apartment was warm. The heating worked. The transit lifts ran. His mother had a new kettle and his father had new tools and the blankets on their bed were the best wool he could find, and none of it had cost him anything that mattered. Not his pool—he didn't have a pool, he had an ocean. Not his money—Dorenne's office had provided a stipend, reasonable and regular, the compensation of a Wellspring under institutional care. Not his time, not his effort, not the careful arithmetic of sacrifice that had defined his family's life for as long as he could remember.

He was providing. For the first time. With his own hands and his own resources and his own choices, he was giving his family a life that didn't require his mother to pour her pool into the walls every morning or his father to carry the weight of a building that was falling apart around them.

This was what the power was for.

He walked back inside. Up the stairs. Into the apartment where the light came through clean windows and the floor was level and the heating hummed in the walls without being asked.

His mother was in the kitchen. She was sitting at the new table with a cup of tea in her hands—tea from the new kettle, steam curling in the eastern light. Her face was turned toward the window, and he couldn't read her expression because she wasn't making one. Her hands held the cup and her body was still and she looked at the light on the stone floor the way you look at something you're trying to memorize.

"Morning, Mum."

She turned. She smiled. The smile was complete and genuine and her eyes were warm and she was his mother, sitting in a kitchen that was better than any kitchen she'd ever had, drinking tea from a copper kettle that didn't have a dent in it.

"Morning," she said. "There's tea."

He poured a cup. He sat across from her. The table between them was new and solid and unblemished, and their elbows rested on it in the same positions they'd held at the old table in the old kitchen in the old apartment that was open to the sky now, that was rubble and dust and a bag of broken things in the lower cabinet.

They drank their tea.

* * *

The heating lattice was the first thing he broke.

It happened on the fifth evening. His parents had gone to bed. The apartment was cooling—the residual warmth in the walls fading, the way it always did after sundown, the stones releasing their stored heat in a slow exhale that brought the temperature down gradually, comfortably, the building's natural rhythm.

Ash was in the main room. He pressed his palm to the nearest lattice interface—the smooth disc in the wall, the same kind of disc he'd walked past every day of his life, the disc that every child in Solathis learned to activate before they learned to read.

He pushed.

The disc flared white. The stone around it blazed—not the gentle amber of a heating lattice operating normally, but a searing, eye-aching white that threw shadows against the far wall. Heat erupted from the wall in a wave. The air in the room went from cool to stifling in a single breath, hot enough that sweat broke across his forehead and his shirt clung to his back and the temperature kept climbing.

He pulled his hand away.

The lattice stayed lit. The stones along the base of the wall glowed in a line—white fading to orange fading to red—and the heat continued pouring off them, the energy he'd dumped into the system far exceeding what the lattice was designed to hold. A crack appeared in the interface disc. Then another. The cracks spread outward into the surrounding stone, fine lines radiating from the point of contact like threads in a web.

The room was unbearable. He backed away, and even from across the room the heat pressed against his skin like a hand. His parents' door opened. His father stood in the hallway in his sleeping clothes, his face flushed, his hair damp with sweat.

"What—"

"I overloaded the lattice. I was trying to—I just wanted to warm the room."

His father looked at the glowing wall. The stones were dimming now—the excess energy dissipating, bleeding into the surrounding structure, the building's mass absorbing the shock. But the cracks in the interface remained. The disc was fractured. The lattice, in this section of the wall, was dead.

"I'll fix it," Ash said. "I'll—"

"Leave it." His father's voice was quiet. Not angry. The quiet of a man assessing damage, cataloging what needed repair, adding it to the list he carried in his head. "It'll cool by morning."

His father went back to bed. The door closed. Ash stood in the room that was too hot and smelled like overheated stone and listened to the lattice tick as it cooled, the sound of stressed material contracting, settling, finding its new shape around the fractures he'd made.

* * *

Maren came the next morning.

She arrived the way she always arrived—on time, composed, her coat buttoned to the throat. She stood in the main room and looked at the cracked lattice disc and the scorch marks on the stone and the hairline fractures radiating outward through the wall, and her face did nothing. No reaction. No sigh. No comment. Her eyes moved across the damage the way Sorren's eyes had moved across the building's foundation—clinically, reading the scope of the problem, calculating the cost.

She set her bag on the table. She crossed to the wall. She placed her palms flat against the stone, one on either side of the fractured disc, and closed her eyes.

Ash watched. He stood in the doorway with a cup of tea—the new kettle, the good copper—and watched his sister channel. Her hands were steady. Her breathing slowed, deepened, settled into the rhythm he recognized from every time he'd watched a skilled channeler work: the deliberate, measured cadence of someone directing energy with precision, each pulse calibrated, each output calculated against the task.

The cracks sealed. Slowly. Bottom to top, the fractures closing like wounds healing, the stone knitting back together under her hands. The interface disc was harder—the fracture there was structural, not cosmetic, and the channeling pathways inside the disc had been disrupted by the overload. She spent ten minutes on the disc alone, her fingers moving in small adjustments, her jaw tight with concentration.

When she pulled her hands away, she flexed her fingers. The wince was small—barely visible, the ghost of a flinch that anyone else would have missed. But Ash knew her face the way he knew stairwells. The wince meant she'd used a significant portion of her day's pool on the repair. Not all of it. But enough that the rest of her day would be measured, careful, each remaining expenditure weighed against what was left.

"Thank you," he said.

"It's fine." She picked up her bag. "Try not to—" She stopped. Whatever she was going to say, she decided not to say it. She adjusted her bag on her shoulder and looked at the wall—the repaired disc, the sealed cracks, the stone that looked almost the same as it had before, if you didn't know where to look for the faint lines where the fractures had been.

"I'll be more careful," Ash said.

She nodded. She left.

He was more careful. For two days. Then he broke the lighting strip in the hallway.

It happened the same way—a touch too heavy, a push too large, the vast reservoir behind his ribs responding to his intention with a force that bore no relationship to the task. He wanted light. He got a flash that seared purple afterimages into his vision and blew the channeling strip out of its housing, the thin stone cracking along its length and falling to the floor in three pieces.

He stood in the dark hallway, blinking, the afterimages floating, and heard his mother's voice from the kitchen: "Again?"

A maintenance worker came that afternoon. A young woman, middle-generation, with a toolkit and the patient manner of someone who fixed things for a living. She replaced the strip and the housing, tested the connection, and left without comment. The building's maintenance was covered by the housing allocation—Dorenne's office, the infrastructure budget, the same system Ash had helped manage three weeks ago.

Two days later, he overloaded the washroom's water heater. The interface accepted his touch and then the pipes rattled and steam erupted from the tap and the water went from cold to scalding before he could pull his hand away. The skin across his palm reddened. Not badly—the reservoir's energy had moved through him, not into him, and the heat he'd generated was in the water, not his flesh. But the heater's regulating interface cracked, and the pipes made sounds for an hour afterward, groaning and ticking as the excess heat worked through the system.

Maren fixed it. She came that evening, after her shift—whatever her shift was, wherever she worked, the details she carried with the same composure she carried everything. She knelt by the washroom wall and placed her hands on the pipes and the interface and worked in silence for twenty minutes. The pipes stopped groaning. The interface sealed. The regulating mechanism, which had been designed to meter the energy flowing through it in precise increments, was whole again.

She didn't wince this time. Or she did, and he didn't see it.

"Sorry," he said.

"You need training."

"I know. Vasra's working on—"

"I know what Vasra's doing." She stood. She washed her hands in the basin—the water warm now, properly warm, the heater functioning the way it was supposed to function. Her hands moved under the water with the practiced efficiency of someone who rationed every gesture. "You're flooding every interface you touch. The lattice, the lighting, the heater—they're all designed for pool-level input. You're putting an ocean through a straw."

"I know."

"Then stop touching them until you can control it."

The words landed. Not hard—Maren didn't throw things hard. She placed them precisely, the way she placed her hands on fractured stone, and they went where she aimed them.

Ash said nothing. He watched her dry her hands and straighten and adjust her coat, each motion complete, economical, a person who had never in her life had energy to waste performing the daily maintenance of herself with the same discipline she brought to everything.

"I'm working on it," he said.

She looked at him. Her eyes held something that wasn't anger and wasn't resentment and wasn't any of the things he might have expected. It was flatter than that. Steadier. The look of a person who has been fixing things her entire life and has just realized the fixing might not stop.

"I know you are," she said.

She left. The door closed. The apartment was warm and the lighting worked and the water heater hummed softly in the wall, and every functional thing in the room had been touched by her hands at least once, and he stood in the hallway thinking about how good she was at this—how precise, how skilled, how effortlessly she moved through the world of channeling interfaces and lattice repairs and the small daily work of keeping things running—and the thought was warm, was proud, was the comfortable appreciation of a brother for a sister's competence.

He didn't think about what it cost her.

He didn't think about the wince.

He went to the kitchen and poured tea from the new kettle and drank it standing at the window, looking out at the well-maintained street and the functioning transit lift and the neighborhood that didn't know him, and the reservoir hummed behind his ribs, patient and vast, and the tea was good and the evening was warm and his family was safe and everything—everything—was better than it had ever been.

In the lower cabinet, behind the closed door, the old kettle sat in the dark. Dented. Blackened. The handle bent at an angle that fit a hand that had been holding it for fifteen years.

Nobody reached for it.

* * *

The seventh evening. The apartment was quiet. His parents were in their room. Maren had come and gone—she'd repaired a cracked section of the hallway wall where Ash had leaned against it while channeling, his body radiating heat the way a furnace radiated heat, the waste energy from his inefficiency bleeding through his skin and into the stone and stressing the surface until it fractured. She'd sealed the crack in five minutes, her hands moving with the easy authority of long practice. She hadn't said anything about it. She'd had dinner. She'd talked with their mother about the healer's latest visit, about the bone knitting well, about the timeline for full mobility. She'd asked their father about his work. She'd been warm and present and completely herself, and then she'd left, and the apartment was better for her having been there and nobody had said so.

Ash sat in his room. The window was dark—no streetlight on this side, just the glow from the channeling strips inside reflecting off the glass. His hands rested on his knees. The reservoir pulsed. Patient. Waiting.

He placed his palm flat on the wall. The heating lattice was there, behind the stone, the network of channeling pathways that ran through the building like veins. He could feel it now—not the way Maren felt it, not with the precise diagnostic awareness of a trained channeler, but as a pressure, a presence, the lattice's capacity registering against his own the way a cup registers against an ocean.

*Gentle.*

He pushed. The smallest amount he could manage. The barest whisper.

The wall warmed. Then heated. Then—

He pulled away. The stone was too hot to touch. Not cracked this time, not glowing, but far beyond what the lattice was designed for. His version of a whisper was still a shout.

He looked at his hand. The palm was warm. Faintly reddened. The power sat in his skin, in his blood, in the vast space behind his ribs where the Wellspring lived, and it responded to his will the way an avalanche responded to a nudge—completely, overwhelmingly, without proportion.

He thought about Maren's hands on the wall. The precision. The control. The years of practice compressed into the muscle memory of a woman who had never had power to waste and had therefore learned to spend every fraction exactly where it needed to go.

He had more power than anyone alive.

He couldn't warm a room without breaking it.

He pulled his hand back and set it on his knee and sat in the quiet of his room in the new apartment in the better district, and the heating lattice ticked as it cooled, and the building absorbed his excess the way it absorbed everything—silently, through mass and stone and the patient architecture of walls that had been built by people who knew what they were doing.

Tomorrow, Maren would come. She would find the section of wall he'd overheated. She would place her hands on it and channel—precisely, efficiently, with the disciplined control he couldn't replicate—and she would fix what he'd broken. And she wouldn't say anything. And he would thank her. And she would nod and adjust her coat and leave, and the apartment would be whole again, her work invisible, her pool diminished by exactly the amount his carelessness had cost her.

The pattern was small. Each incident was small. A cracked disc, a blown strip, a stressed pipe, a heated wall. Minor repairs. Minor costs. The kind of thing that happened in any household where someone was learning to channel—children broke things, parents fixed them, the cycle was as old as channeling itself.

But he wasn't a child. And Maren wasn't his parent. And the cycle wasn't going to end the way it ended for children, with the learner gradually gaining control and the repairs becoming unnecessary, because his reservoir wasn't a pool that could be calibrated through practice at the kitchen table. It was something else. Something that no lattice in this building or any building was designed to receive.

He didn't think about this. Not in these terms. Not with this clarity. What he thought about was the window and the dark and the quiet neighborhood and the morning that would come through the eastern glass, clean and warm and full of light, and the tea he would make from the new kettle and the breakfast he would eat at the new table and the day he would walk into—a Wellspring, named, known, the most powerful channeler alive—and the life he was building for his family, one gift at a time, one improvement at a time, each one proof that the power had a purpose and the purpose was love.

*This is what it's for.*

The heating lattice ticked. The wall cooled. The building held. And somewhere in the kitchen, behind a closed cabinet door, the old kettle waited in the dark with the patience of a thing that knows it will be needed again.
