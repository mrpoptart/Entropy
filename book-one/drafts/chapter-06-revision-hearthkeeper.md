# Chapter 6

The suit was new. Ash could tell because the seams were still stiff and the collar sat too high against his throat. Someone from Dorenne's staff had brought it that morning—dark fabric, well-cut, the kind of thing a junior official wore to Council sessions. It fit him the way borrowed authority fits: close enough to pass, tight enough to remind.

He stood in front of the mirror in the temporary housing and pulled at the collar. His reflection pulled back.

"Stop touching it," Dorenne said.

She was behind him, seated at the small table by the window, her notes spread in a neat fan across the surface. She'd arrived an hour before the event—early enough to coach him, late enough to suggest that coaching hadn't been the plan. She wore what she always wore: a dark formal coat cut long, the Hall of Infrastructure crest pinned at the shoulder, her hair pulled back with the severity that wasn't a style choice so much as a statement about where attention should go. Her face.

"When they ask about the activation," she said, "keep it simple. The building was failing. You were inside. The power emerged under stress. You don't need to explain the mechanism—nobody understands the mechanism, including Vasra. You're describing what happened, not why."

Ash nodded. He was still looking at the mirror. His hands—the same hands, the calloused knuckles, the white scar on his left thumb from Haran's workshop—looked wrong against the dark sleeves. Too rough for the fabric. Too familiar for the stranger wearing it.

"What if they ask how much?"

"They will. Say that testing is ongoing and the full picture is still being assessed. That's true and it's enough." Dorenne straightened her notes. "If they press, defer to me. That's what I'm there for."

"And if they ask what it means? For the city, for—"

"That's my part." She looked up. Her eyes held the same quality they always held—the layered attention, the calculations running behind the warmth. "Ash. Listen to me. You don't need to win them over. You don't need to explain yourself. You stand, you answer what you're asked, you demonstrate what we've prepared. I'll do the rest."

He turned from the mirror. "What are we demonstrating?"

"The heating lattice." She let the words sit for a moment. "The gallery's lattice was designed for a six-person elder team working in rotation. It hasn't run at full capacity in years—the elder roster can't support it. You're going to place your palm on the wall and light it up. The whole thing. In one pulse."

"And that's enough?"

"A man who can warm a room that size, alone, in seconds—that's not a spectacle, Ash. That's a city that can heat its buildings again." She paused. "Presentable is exactly the word I was hoping for. Remarkable is my job."

Something shifted behind her expression. Not a smile. The architecture of one—the adjustment of muscles that preceded warmth without quite arriving there.

"One more thing," she said. She stood, and the motion carried the particular economy she brought to all movement—nothing wasted, nothing casual, every gesture a decision. She crossed to where he stood and looked up at him. She was shorter by half a head. He'd never noticed before. In her office, behind her desk, surrounded by the weight of her position, she filled whatever space she occupied. Here, standing close, she was a woman in her late fifties with sharp eyes and a coat that had been pressed that morning by someone who understood the significance of the day.

"They will want to see a weapon," she said. "A spectacle. A solution to every problem they've been too afraid to name. You are not those things. You're a young man who works for my office and who happens to carry something unprecedented. That's the frame. Stay inside it."

*Happens to carry.* As if the ocean behind his ribs were an accident. As if the pressure that beat like a second heart could be reduced to something that happened.

But she was right. He knew she was right. He could feel the rightness of it the same way he could feel the power—as a physical fact, undeniable, present in the room.

"I understand," he said.

"Good." She gathered her notes. "We leave in twenty minutes. Your family will be in the third row—I arranged it. Your mother's chair is positioned near the aisle in case she needs to leave."

His mother's chair. His mother, with her broken leg still healing, the bone knitting under the elder healer's daily visits—his mother sitting in a crowd of strangers while the world learned what her son had become.

"She didn't want to come," Ash said.

"I know. Your father convinced her." Dorenne tucked the notes into her coat. "He was right to. If she's not there, people will notice. People will ask why. Better to control the question."

"Twenty minutes," she said, and left.

Ash stood in the temporary housing—the room that wasn't his, the mirror that held a stranger in new clothes, the quiet that filled the space Dorenne vacated the way water fills a footprint. He pulled at the collar one more time. Then he put his hands at his sides. The warmth in his palms pressed against the fabric of the suit like something trying to get out.

He let it press.

* * *

The Hall of Infrastructure had a main gallery on the second floor that Ash had walked past a hundred times in three weeks and never entered. It was the kind of space that existed for occasions—high-ceilinged, stone-floored, the walls lined with channeling strips that cast even, institutional light. Three tall windows faced west, and the afternoon sun cut through them in bars that divided the room into bright and shadow. The air was cool. The kind of cool that a building held when its heating lattice ran on rotation and no rotation had reached this floor yet today—a bone-deep chill that seeped through the stone and settled in the joints of every chair.

Someone had arranged chairs in rows. A dozen rows, then standing room behind. A raised platform at the front, wide enough for a table and two positions. The table held a water basin, a set of measurement instruments that the audience wouldn't be able to read but that looked, from a distance, like precision. Behind the platform, the eastern wall rose in smooth stone to the vaulted ceiling. Ash could see the lattice channels carved into it—thin lines branching through the rock face like veins through the back of a hand. Dark. Cold. Dormant.

The room was already filling. Ash entered through a side door behind the platform—Dorenne's arrangement, so he wouldn't have to walk through the crowd—and stood where she'd told him to stand. Left of the table. Hands at his sides. The suit's collar pressed against his throat.

He watched the room fill.

Council members in the front row. He recognized two: a heavy-set man with the silver chain of office who leaned toward the woman beside him and spoke behind his hand, and an older woman—elder generation, her pool visible in the unconscious way she warmed the arms of her chair, the wood grain glowing faintly where her palms rested. Behind them, mid-level officials, institutional staff, faces he half-recognized from the Hall's corridors. The second row: press, or what passed for press in Solathis—correspondents from the broadsheet offices, their notebooks already open, their eyes already measuring.

Third row. His family.

His father sat at the end of the row nearest the aisle. He wore his good coat—the one Ash's mother pressed for civic occasions, the one with the mended pocket Ash had never asked about. His hands were on his knees, and his posture was the posture of a man performing calm the way Ash had spent twenty-three years performing cheerfulness. Beside him, Maren. She'd come from work—her hair was still pulled back in the practical knot she wore at her posting, and her coat was her daily coat, not a formal one. She sat straight and still and looked at the platform the way she looked at problems: assessing load, calculating cost, determining what needed to be done.

His mother. She was in a chair with arms—brought specially, wider than the others, positioned at the aisle so her leg could extend. The splint was hidden under a blanket someone had draped across her lap, but Ash could see her hand gripping the chair's arm with a force that blanched her knuckles. Her face was a particular kind of still—the stillness of someone holding everything in place through will alone. She was looking at him. She hadn't stopped looking at him since he'd taken his position on the platform.

He wanted to cross the room and tell her something. He didn't know what. That he was fine, that this was good, that the world wanting her son was not the same as the world taking him.

He stayed where he was. Hands at his sides. Collar tight.

The room filled. Standing room packed. Bodies pressing against the walls, the heat of sixty, seventy people raising the temperature enough that the channeling strips dimmed slightly—the building's systems adjusting, the constant invisible negotiation between what was built and what was spent.

Dorenne entered from the same side door. She didn't hurry. She crossed to the platform with the unhurried precision of a woman who understood that timing was a form of authority, and the room quieted as she reached the table. Not silence—a settling, the acoustic equivalent of attention turning like a compass needle.

She set her notes on the table. She didn't look at them.

"Thank you for coming on short notice," she said. Her voice carried without strain—pitched to the room, to the stone walls that held sound the way they held heat. "I've asked you here because something has changed. Something significant enough that it deserves your attention before it reaches you through channels less equipped to provide context."

She paused. The pause was a tool. Ash had watched her use it in briefings—the calibrated silence that made the next sentence land harder than volume ever could.

"Ten days ago, during a structural failure in the Terrace Road district, a member of my staff manifested channeling capacity for the first time. He is twenty-three years old. He had no prior capacity—none. He was, by every clinical measure, dead weight."

A murmur. The term landed the way she'd intended it to—the bluntness cutting through institutional language, the audience hearing the word they all used privately spoken aloud in a formal setting.

"The activation occurred under extreme stress. His family's building was collapsing. His mother was trapped. What happened next—" She paused again. "What happened next has been assessed by my office's examination team and independently by Scholar Vasra, who I believe most of you know by reputation."

Eyes moved. Heads turned, scanning the room. Vasra wasn't present. Dorenne had said she wouldn't be—that Vasra had declined the invitation with the precise disinterest of a scientist who considered press events beneath her instruments. But the name did work in the room. Ash could see it—the shift in posture among the Council members, the pen-points pressing harder into notebooks.

"The assessment is preliminary," Dorenne continued, "but the findings are clear enough to warrant this briefing. The channeling capacity exhibited by Ashlyn Torren—my aide, the young man standing beside me—exceeds any measurement on record. By a significant factor."

She let the room hold that.

"Scholar Vasra has described it as a wellspring—a deep-source reservoir, unprecedented in documented channeling history. The testing instruments in the Hall basement were exceeded. Every calibration stone provided was destroyed during routine assessment. The resonance measurement—Vasra's personal instrument, calibrated over four decades—was insufficient to determine the reservoir's depth."

Silence. Not a murmur. Not the settling of attention. Silence—the flat, dense quiet of a room full of people who had stopped breathing at the same time.

A Council member in the front row leaned forward. The heavy-set man with the silver chain. His mouth opened.

"Questions will follow the demonstration," Dorenne said. She said it without looking at him. The mouth closed.

She turned to Ash. Her eyes held something he couldn't read—or could read but didn't want to, the layered thing that was genuine care and strategic positioning and the particular warmth of a woman who was brilliant at this and knew it.

"Ash," she said. "If you would."

He stepped forward. The room shifted—seventy people adjusting in their seats, their attention landing on him with a weight he could feel on his skin. He was aware of his hands. His suit. The collar pressing against his throat. The ocean behind his ribs, patient and vast and waiting.

He turned to face the eastern wall.

The lattice channels were carved into the stone in branching lines that spread from a central interface point near the base—a palm-width depression worn smooth by decades of use. Six channelers had worked this wall in rotation once, feeding the lattice in shifts, keeping the gallery warm through the long winters. That was before the elder generation thinned. Before the rotation schedules stretched. Before the lattice went dark and the gallery grew cold and someone started stacking channeling strips in corners as a substitute that everyone knew wasn't enough.

Ash placed his palm against the stone.

The interface was cold under his hand. He could feel the lattice channels spreading outward from his palm like roots—the carved paths through the rock, empty, waiting, the architecture of a system designed for power that had stopped coming.

*Gentle. Find the thread.*

He pushed.

The lattice activated.

Not gradually—not the slow crawl of a pool feeding through channels one at a time, the way a six-person team would have done it, each channeler taking a section, managing their budget, rationing their daily reserves across a careful schedule. This was immediate. The channels lit from the point of contact outward in a single sustained pulse, the stone glowing amber where the energy moved through it, the branching lines spreading across the eastern wall like veins filling with warmth.

The lattice covered the eastern wall. Then turned the corner. Then spread across the northern face, the channels connecting and branching and carrying the energy through the room's skeleton the way blood moved through veins. The stone glowed. The air thickened with warmth—not the harsh, breaking intensity of his first tests, but a sustained, building warmth that moved through the chamber the way sunlight moved through a window. Slow. Steady. Enormous.

He held it.

The cold stone floor lost its bite. The chill that had settled into every chair, every coat, every pair of folded hands—it lifted. Not suddenly, but unmistakably, the way spring arrived in the upper districts: one moment you were cold, and the next you realized you hadn't been cold for a while. The channeling strips in the walls dimmed. They weren't needed anymore.

He held it for ten seconds. Twenty. The lattice was fully active—every channel, every branch, every capillary line carved into the chamber's bones by builders who had assumed this level of power would always be available. The stone sang with it. A low harmonic, felt more than heard, the resonance of mineral carrying more energy than it had in a decade.

He pulled his hand back.

The lattice dimmed—slowly, the channels releasing their stored heat in a long, gradual exhale. But the room was warm. Deeply, thoroughly warm, the way it hadn't been since the elder teams had maintained it at full capacity. The cold that had pressed in from every direction was gone, replaced by an ambient warmth that settled into clothes and skin and the spaces between people.

Ash turned back to face the room. His palm was warm. The ocean behind his ribs was unchanged. The demonstration had cost him nothing he could feel—a cup of water from an ocean, a breath from a wind.

The room was very quiet.

Then someone exhaled—a sharp, involuntary sound from the standing section, the kind of breath that escapes before the mind can hold it. And the silence broke. Not into applause, not into murmuring. Into something more complex—the scratch of pens, the creak of chairs as people turned to each other, the low murmur of questions spoken too quietly to hear. Every face in the room held the same components in different proportions: wonder, fear, calculation. And something underneath all of those that Ash recognized because he'd worn it himself, once, in a stairwell or a landing or a cold morning in a kitchen that couldn't heat itself.

Hope. The terrible weight of it.

Dorenne stepped forward. Her voice filled the room again—steady, precise, carrying the confidence of a woman who had planned for this moment down to the temperature of the air.

"What you just witnessed was a single individual activating a heating lattice designed for a six-person elder team. In approximately four seconds." She let that land. "The energy expenditure, based on our preliminary measurements, represents a negligible fraction of his total reservoir. The implications for the city's infrastructure are significant. I don't need to tell anyone in this room that our maintenance capacity has been declining for years."

She paused.

"Questions," she said.

* * *

The questions lasted forty minutes. Dorenne fielded most of them.

The institutional back-and-forth blurred together—questions about documentation, about oversight, about the proper channels through which information would flow. Dorenne answered each one with the same precise, redirecting confidence. *Proper channels.* The phrase returned three times, placed at the end of different sentences the way a mason placed a keystone—load-bearing, deliberate, holding the arch of her authority in place.

A woman in the second row—press, her notebook full—directed her question past Dorenne. "Mr. Torren. What does it feel like? To carry something like this?"

The room shifted. Pens paused. Dorenne's hand, resting on the table, tightened slightly—a fraction of movement that only someone standing beside her would notice.

He looked at the woman. She was young—his age, maybe a year or two older. Her pen was still. Her expression held none of the institutional calculation that surrounded it. She was asking because she wanted to know.

"It feels like a lot," he said.

A small sound from the room. Not laughter. Something closer to recognition.

Dorenne stepped in. "As I mentioned, the assessment is ongoing. We'll be providing regular updates through my office as the picture becomes clearer. I want to thank you all for—"

"One more question, Director."

The heavy-set Council member. The silver chain caught the light as he leaned forward. His voice carried the particular authority of someone accustomed to being the last speaker in any room.

"The Wellspring. However unprecedented—it changes the resource picture for this city. Every district, every allocation decision. I assume your office has begun calculating the implications."

Dorenne's expression didn't change. "We have."

"And those calculations will be shared with the full Council?"

"At the appropriate time, through appropriate channels. This is a preliminary briefing, Councilor. Not a policy session."

"Of course." The Councilor settled back. His eyes moved to Ash—not to his face but to his hands, resting at his sides. The gaze was appraising. The expression of a man looking at a tool and estimating its uses.

Ash held still. The ocean pressed against his ribs. The collar pressed against his throat. Seventy people in the room, and every one of them was building a version of him that had nothing to do with who he was.

It felt the same as being wanted.

He couldn't tell the difference.

* * *

The room emptied slowly. People lingered—talking in clusters, their voices carrying the particular texture of people processing something they hadn't expected. Dorenne's staff moved through them, managing, redirecting, answering the questions that the formal session hadn't accommodated. A young woman from Dorenne's office positioned herself near the door and handed each departing correspondent a sealed brief—three pages, Ash knew, because he'd watched Dorenne edit it that morning. The approved narrative, printed and distributed while the experience was still fresh enough to shape.

Maren found him near the table.

She'd waited. He could tell by the way she stood—not the composure of someone who'd just arrived, but the composure of someone who'd been holding position while their father took his turn, while the crowd thinned, while the moment became small enough to approach. She looked at the eastern wall, where the lattice channels were fading back to dark stone, the last warmth bleeding out of them in slow pulses.

"The third row was an interesting choice," she said.

"Dorenne arranged it."

"I know." She brushed something from her sleeve—dust, or the ghost of dust, a gesture of maintenance performed on reflex. "Three people asked me about you on the way in. Two I didn't know."

"What did you say?"

"That you're my brother and that the Director would be providing details during the briefing." She paused. The pause held something—not calculation, not the careful arrangement that Dorenne would have deployed, but something simpler and more painful. "I'm used to being the Torren who channels. Now I'm the Torren who's related to the Torren who channels."

She said it lightly. The lightness cost her something—he could see it in the way her fingers pressed against the table's edge, the way her eyes moved to the fading lattice and then away.

"One of them asked if I'd noticed anything before the activation. Signs. Early indicators."

"What did you tell them?"

"The truth. That there were none." Her jaw tightened. "Twenty-three years, and there were none."

He didn't know what to say to that. The space between them held the weight of everything they'd never discussed—his years without magic, her years of quiet compensation, the careful choreography of a family that had organized itself around an absence that was no longer absent.

"You should eat something," Maren said. "You look thin."

"I'm not thin."

"You look thin in that suit." She reached up and adjusted his collar—a quick, precise movement, the kind of small repair she made without thinking. Her fingers were cool and certain. "Better."

She left. Her stride was even, her back straight, her coat buttoned against the October air that leaked through the Hall's tall windows. She passed through the side door without looking back, and Ash watched her go with the awareness that something important had just happened in the margins of the conversation, in the words she'd chosen and the ones she'd left in their place.

*Twenty-three years, and there were none.*

He stood by the empty table and felt the echo of that sentence settle into him like a stone settling into water.

* * *

His mother was waiting in the corridor.

Not standing—sitting, in the wheeled chair Dorenne's healer had provided, her splinted leg extended, the blanket across her lap the same one from the gallery. His father must have brought her here. Or she'd come herself, the way she came to everything that involved her son—through will, through the refusal to be absent, through the particular stubbornness that had once shielded him from a world that had no place for a boy without magic and now pressed against a world that had too many places for a man with too much of it.

A broadsheet was folded on her lap, half-hidden under the blanket. Ash saw the headline before she could cover it—the sober typeface of institutional journalism: UNPRECEDENTED CHANNELING EVENT AT HALL OF INFRASTRUCTURE. Her hand moved over it, not reading, covering. The gesture of a woman who'd been reading the same paragraph for hours and didn't want anyone to see the evidence.

"Come here," she said.

He knelt beside her chair. The corridor was empty—the crowd had thinned to staff, the journalists gone, the Council members dispersed to whatever calculations the afternoon would demand. It was just them. The stone floor was cold under his knee.

She put her hand on his face. Her palm was rough—a working hand, a mother's hand, the hand that had packed a cracked bowl and a dented kettle from the rubble of their home because some things mattered more than function.

"You didn't have to do this," she said.

"It's just a briefing, Ma. It's—"

"Not the briefing." Her fingers tightened against his cheek. "This. All of this. Standing up there. Letting them look at you."

"Dorenne thought it was—"

"I know what Dorenne thought." Her voice was quiet and fierce. Not angry—not at him, not exactly at Dorenne. Angry at the mechanism. At the machine that took her boy and set him on a platform and called it necessary. "I know what she thinks is best. She's very good at what she thinks is best."

Her eyes went to the broadsheet, barely visible under the blanket's edge. "These people. The Council, the scholars—they didn't know your name a week ago. They didn't know you existed. Now they want—"

She didn't finish. She didn't need to.

"What do they want?" he asked. Though he already knew. Though he'd watched it in the room for an hour—the calculating eyes, the notebooks, the Councilor's gaze on his hands.

She looked at him the way she'd looked at him every morning of his life—the look that held everything she knew about him, including the things he didn't know about himself.

"Everything," she said. "They'll want everything."

She dropped her hand. She pulled the blanket tighter across her lap—gathering, holding close. Her jaw set. Her eyes went to the corridor, to the building around them, to the institutional weight of stone and channeling and the Director's name on every door.

"Bring me home, Ash," she said. "Your father's outside with the chair ramp."

He pushed her chair down the corridor. The wheels hummed against the stone floor. His hands on the handles were warm, and she didn't mention it, and he didn't mention the way her shoulders drew up when they passed a group of staff who stepped aside and stared.

* * *

The temporary housing was six blocks from the Hall, in a district Ash's family had never lived in—mid-level, clean-lined, the buildings younger than their old one by fifty years. The heating lattice worked. The water ran warm. The walls were solid and the doors closed true and everything functioned with the quiet competence of infrastructure maintained by someone with the resources to maintain it.

His mother called it the new place the way she might call a borrowed coat warm—accurately, without ownership.

Ash sat at the kitchen table while his father made tea. The kitchen was twice the size of the old one. The stove was channeling-operated, the kind that responded to a palm's touch, and his father used it without comment—his pool, his father's daily budget, spent on heating water for his son. A small act. The kind that mattered.

His father set a cup in front of Ash and sat across from him. He wrapped his hands around his own cup and looked at the steam.

Ash drank. The tea was too hot and he let it burn his tongue rather than cool it with a thought. He could do that now—a pulse of cold, a thermal adjustment so small it would barely register on his reservoir. He didn't. The heat was his father's pool. It would have felt like taking something.

They sat in the quiet. The kitchen's channeling strips hummed faintly—a sound Ash had never lived with, the background frequency of a home that worked properly, every interface maintained, every lattice charged. In the old apartment, silence had been actual silence. Here, even the quiet was powered.

"Dad."

"Mm."

"Are you—" He stopped. The question felt wrong in his mouth—too large, too formless. He tried again. "Is this all right? Everything that's happening?"

His father set down his cup. He looked at Ash for a long time. Not the way the Council member had looked at him—not at his hands, not at the power they held. At his face. At the boy underneath the suit and the collar and the ocean behind his ribs.

"Your mother is scared," his father said. "Not of you. Of what's coming. She spent twenty-three years making sure the world couldn't take too much from you, and now the world wants to take everything, and she doesn't know how to fight that."

"She doesn't need to fight anything. I'm—"

"Let me finish." His father's voice was quiet. Not sharp—just steady, the voice of a man who had something to say and meant to say it. "She's scared, and she's right to be. I'm proud of you—I was proud of you before this, Ash, I need you to hear that—but I'm watching people look at my son the way they look at the heating lattice. Like a thing that provides." His throat moved. He picked up his cup again. Drank. Set it down.

"She's worried they'll use you," he said. "I'm worried you'll let them, because it'll feel like being useful."

The words landed in the kitchen and stayed there, heavy as stone. Ash sat with them. He didn't argue. He didn't reassure. His father's hands were wrapped around the cup—calloused hands, scarred hands, the hands that had shaped the door wedge that survived their building's collapse when the channeling-reinforced walls hadn't. Hands that had spent a lifetime doing careful, small work. Hands that had heated the water for this tea.

"More tea?" his father asked.

"Please."

His father heated the water with his palm on the kettle. The small, deliberate expenditure of a man's daily pool, offered without ceremony. Ash watched his father's hand on the metal and felt, for the first time, the precise shape of the distance between them—not the distance of magic and its absence, which was old and familiar, but a new distance, the kind that came from having too much of what someone you loved could barely afford.

* * *

Ryn came after dark.

He heard her on the stairs—a particular rhythm of footsteps he'd known since childhood, the cadence of someone who climbed stairs the way she did everything, at a pace that suggested she had somewhere to be and wasn't especially worried about getting there. He opened the door before she knocked.

She stood in the corridor with her work coat still on, the collar turned up against the evening cold. Her hair was shorter than the last time he'd seen her—cut blunt at the jaw, practical, the kind of choice that didn't require a reason. She looked at him. At the suit, which he was still wearing. At the collar, which Maren had adjusted. At his face.

"Can I come in?"

He stepped aside. She entered the temporary housing and looked around with the quick, cataloguing attention she gave everything—the layout registered, the kitchen noted, the size of the space compared to the old apartment assessed and filed in the time it took to cross the threshold.

"Nice place," she said.

"Dorenne arranged it."

"I figured." She sat at the kitchen table. She didn't ask where to sit—she picked the chair closest to the door, the one his father had occupied an hour ago, and sat the way she sat everywhere: settled, unperforming, taking up exactly the space she needed and no more.

He sat across from her. The table between them held the ghost of every conversation the day had pressed into its surface—his father's tea, his father's worry, the weight of words that were still settling into the stone of him. The last time they'd sat across from each other—when? Before the activation. Before the building. Before the word *Wellspring* had entered his vocabulary and reorganized his life around it. They'd been at a tavern in the old district, sharing a meal, and she'd made a joke about his coat and he'd made a joke about her cooking and the evening had carried the easy, worn texture of a friendship that didn't need to justify itself.

That was three weeks ago. It felt like a year.

"I heard about the thing today," she said. "The briefing."

"Yeah."

"How was it?"

"Strange."

She nodded. She didn't ask for details. She didn't ask what the room looked like or what Dorenne said or how the Council reacted. She just nodded, the way she nodded when he told her about a bad day at work or a fight with his mother or the particular weight of waking up in a world that wasn't built for him.

"Have you eaten?" she asked.

"My dad made tea."

"Tea isn't food, Ash."

"I'm not hungry."

She looked at him. The look was direct and uncomplicated and entirely Ryn—the quality of seeing that had nothing to do with power or politics or the careful, layered calculations that every other person in his life seemed to be running. She saw him. Just him. The boy from the same street who'd been dead weight for twenty-three years and who was now, apparently, the most significant channeling event in recorded history.

She didn't seem to find the second thing more interesting than the first.

"Are you sleeping?" she asked.

"Some."

"Have you talked to Haran?"

"Not yet. I've been—it's been busy."

"You should talk to Haran."

"I know."

She leaned back in the chair. The silence between them had a different quality than the silences he'd held today—different from the coached silence of the platform, the institutional silence of the gallery, the heavy silence of his father's kitchen. This silence was old and comfortable and smelled like the district they'd grown up in, like cold mornings and shared walks and the accumulated weight of years spent in each other's company.

"The lattice thing," she said. "Did it cost you much?"

"Nothing. Basically nothing."

She was quiet for a moment. Then: "I'm glad."

He looked at her.

"About the—all of it. The Wellspring, or whatever they're calling it. I'm glad it happened to you."

*Happened to you.* The phrasing landed in the same place Dorenne's *happens to carry* had landed, but differently. Dorenne's version had framed the power as incidental—a thing to be managed, positioned, controlled. Ryn's version framed it as weather. Something that had arrived in his life. Not who he was. Not what he was for. Just a thing that had happened, the way a storm happens, or a season, or a piece of luck that falls on you without asking whether you're ready.

He didn't say any of this. He smiled. "Thanks, Ryn."

She studied his face. She saw something—he could tell, because she always could, because she'd known him since they were children and her ability to read him had nothing to do with channeling and everything to do with years. She saw it and she didn't press.

"You look ridiculous in that suit," she said.

"I know."

"The collar's crooked."

"Maren fixed it."

"Maren didn't fix it well enough." She stood and crossed to him and straightened the collar with two quick pulls. Her fingers against his neck were cool from the evening air. She smelled like metal and the sharp tang of channeling—the residue of a day's work, her pool spent on whatever her trade demanded, the ordinary expenditure of an ordinary life.

She stepped back. "Better. Still ridiculous."

"Thank you."

She sat back down. She looked at the kitchen—at the channeling strips, the working stove, the warm water that came from every tap. "Your mom doing okay?"

"Her leg's healing. The healer says another week."

"That's not what I asked."

He exhaled. "She's scared. She doesn't want—she thinks everyone wants something from me."

"Everyone does want something from you."

"That's what she said."

Ryn shrugged. The shrug was small, practical. "She's not wrong. Doesn't mean it has to be bad. Just means you should keep track."

"Keep track of what?"

"Of which ones want something you can give and which ones want something that costs." She looked at him. "You're smart, Ash. You always were. Don't let the fancy suit make you forget how to count."

The silence held. He wanted to tell her about the Councilor who'd looked at his hands instead of his face. About his mother's hand on his cheek in the corridor. About the word *everything* still hanging in the air.

He wanted to tell her that for the first time in his life, people wanted him. That the wanting felt like sunlight after twenty-three years of cold. That he knew, in the part of him that counted and measured and saw systems clearly, that being wanted for what you could do was not the same as being wanted for who you were. That he knew this. And that knowing it didn't help.

He didn't tell her any of it. He sat in the kitchen with his oldest friend and let the silence hold what words couldn't carry.

"I should go," Ryn said. "Early shift tomorrow."

"Thanks for coming."

"Don't thank me for coming. Thank me for not asking you to glow." She stood and pulled her collar up and walked to the door. She stopped with her hand on the frame. "Talk to Haran, Ash. Soon."

"I will."

She left. Her footsteps descended the stairs—the same rhythm, the same unhurried cadence. He listened until they faded. Then he sat in the kitchen of the temporary home and looked at his hands—warm, rough, scarred—resting on the table in front of him.

The ocean behind his ribs pulsed. Steady. Patient. Vast.

Seventy people had watched him today. They'd seen light and scale and the thermal signature of something unprecedented. They'd seen a resource. A lever. A solution.

Nobody had seen his hands.

He stood and took off the suit jacket and hung it over the chair. He loosened the collar. He went to the window and looked out at the city—Solathis, spreading across the plateau in layers of light and shadow, the elder districts glowing at the heights, the lower bands flickering, the infrastructure that held it all together creaking under the weight of what it was built to be and what it was becoming.

Somewhere in those layers, people were talking about him. Council members in their offices. Correspondents at their desks. Neighbors in the new district, introducing themselves to his father with curiosity dressed as courtesy. Colleagues at Maren's posting, asking questions she would answer with discipline and composure and nothing of what she actually felt.

They were building him. Not the person—the idea. The Wellspring. The resource. The thing that the city needed and the Director had found and the Council would argue about and the broadsheets would print and the public would absorb until the name Ashlyn Torren meant something that had nothing to do with a boy who'd washed in cold water every morning and climbed forty flights because he couldn't use the lift.

He pressed his palms against the window glass. The glass warmed under his touch—not deliberately, not channeled, just the ambient leak of a reservoir so vast that his body couldn't contain it entirely, the excess bleeding through his skin like heat from a furnace whose door wouldn't close.

Outside, the city glowed. Inside, the ocean pressed.

He stood there for a long time.
