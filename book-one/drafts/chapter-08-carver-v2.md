# Chapter 8

The workshop smelled the same.

Oil and sawdust and the sharp bite of cut metal, and something underneath those—the particular warmth of a low-ceilinged room where people worked with their hands. Ash stood in the doorway and felt the buzzing behind his ribs begin, reluctantly, to settle. His jaw unclenched. He hadn't known it was clenched.

Three hours at the press event. Three hours of standing in the Hall's outer court while people looked at him with that expression—the careful, assessing attention of strangers calculating what he was worth. He'd calibrated every handshake, the way he'd been learning to calibrate everything—enough pressure to be firm, not enough to accidentally push channeling through his grip. The Wellspring didn't care that he was trying to shake hands normally. It pressed at his palms the whole time, and by the end of the third hour his fingers ached from holding it back.

Here, the light lay across the workbenches in warm yellow bars. Sawdust drifted in the current from the door he'd opened.

Haran was at the central bench. He had his back half-turned, sleeves rolled above the elbow, both hands on a section of copper pipe. A half-assembled pump spread across the work surface—not chaos, arrangement. Each component laid where it would next be needed, an order that only looked random.

"Close the door," Haran said. He hadn't turned around.

Ash closed the door.

He crossed to the central bench. Haran glanced up—the brief, assessing look he gave everything that arrived in his workspace.

"Press event this morning," Ash said.

"I heard."

He didn't ask more than that. He had a joint in his hand—a brass elbow fitting, the curve clean, the threading at each end waiting to be mated. He turned it, examining. "The pump in the Velden building failed. Third time this year. District system's been throttled since winter—residents have been hauling water from the terrace taps." He set the fitting down. "Thought I'd make them something that works."

Two of the other tinkerers were at their stations. Petra at the near bench, a loupe against one eye. Davel at the window, running a file along the edge of a gear blank with the patient, rhythmic attention of a man who knew that hurrying accomplished nothing.

Ash looked at the pump. It was midway through assembly: the housing, the basic cylinder and chamber, the inlet and outlet flanges waiting for fittings that Haran had laid out in sequence. Mechanical. Springs and seals and precisely machined tolerances that would move water by pressure differential and the simple physics of a well-fitted piston. No channeling interface. No stone disc set flush into the surface. Just the mechanism.

But at the outlet flange—a brass sleeve, threaded to receive the elbow joint—the gap between sleeve and housing was visible. A fraction too wide, the threading not quite engaged, the joint stuck at the top of its travel.

The reservoir hummed.

* * *

He reached for the fitting.

A thread of the Wellspring was already moving before he'd decided—a pool of force, exploratory, finding the stuck joint the way a finger finds a tight jar lid. He pushed. The fitting gave. And gave. And fused, the brass deforming under the channeled pressure, the threads pressing together harder than the original design had required.

He took his hand back. The fitting sat perfectly flush against the housing. Solid. Clean.

Then he looked at it again.

The brass was slightly darker where he'd touched it—not discolored, just compressed, the surface texture changed. The threads, which had been machined to mate and release and mate again, had pressed together beyond their designed tolerance. The fitting would hold. It would hold permanently. It would hold when someone three years from now needed to remove it for cleaning, and they would reach for a wrench, and the wrench would not be enough.

He set his hands on the bench.

The next task was the outlet pipe—a copper section, about an arm's length, that needed to angle downward at thirty degrees toward the floor fitting. Haran had a bending jig clamped to the edge of the bench, and copper bent with a small amount of heat and patient pressure.

Ash didn't reach for the jig.

He held the pipe in both hands and ran a current of force through it. The copper moved. It bent cleanly. Thirty degrees, the radius uniform. He set it down and examined the result.

He was already reaching for the next component when he noticed what the pipe felt like under his thumb. Stiff. The channeled force had done something to the grain—pressed it, restructured it at a level below what the eye could see. The copper that had been soft enough to reshape was now copper that was done. It wouldn't flex if someone needed to work around it later.

He set it on the bench.

Davel glanced over from the window. Not a long look. Not unfriendly.

At the far end of the pump, the inlet fitting was a different style of joint—a quarter-turn bayonet mount, designed to be removable, designed for the person who would service the pump when Haran was somewhere else and the residents needed someone to clear a blockage. You built them for maintenance. You built them so the next person could take them apart.

There was a seal at the base of the bayonet mount. It sat slightly proud of its channel—the rubber edge not quite settled, a visible gap. Ash reached out. Ran a thread of channeling into the joint. The seal compressed into its channel and the gap disappeared. Filled. Fused. Permanent.

He stepped back.

The outlet flange, seated and solid. The pipe, bent and rigid. The bayonet mount, sealed shut. Three tasks, five minutes. Each one solved.

Haran, from the far end of the bench, looked at the joint. Then at Ash. Then back at the joint. He turned back to the inlet fitting he was working on.

Petra had straightened from her loupe. She looked at the bench—at the fused fitting, the rigid pipe, the sealed mount—then picked up her own work and went back to it.

The workshop held its sounds. The file. The lathe in its idle clatter. The small creak of the building.

Ash looked at the pump. He had helped with three parts of it in the last five minutes. Each one worked. Each one held.

He stood there and something in the satisfaction shifted.

* * *

The valve seat was the next piece that needed attention.

Haran brought it to the central bench—a small brass disc, precisely machined, with a tapered face that would mate against the interior of the pump body to regulate flow. A calibration task. The valve seat had to be worked gradually into its socket, the taper on the face mated to the taper in the housing until the surfaces matched closely enough to seat cleanly. You used lapping compound. You rotated the valve in the housing, pressed lightly, lifted it and examined the contact pattern on the brass face. You repeated—adding compound, adjusting the angle—until the contact was even and the seal was right.

Ash had done this before. His hands knew the motion, the pressure, the judgment of how much was enough and how much was too much. He'd lapped valve seats in this workshop with Haran watching and occasionally adjusting his grip.

He picked up the valve. Spread a thin film of compound on the face. Placed it in the housing.

And pushed.

Not much. He hadn't intended to push at all—he'd intended to rotate the valve the way he'd always rotated it, with the even, gentle, exploratory pressure of his fingertips. But the reservoir was there, behind everything, pressing against the inside of his hands, and when his fingers touched the valve and the surface resistance came back through his grip, the power leaned into it.

He felt the valve shift under more force than he'd meant. He pulled back. Tried again. Lighter—a conscious effort to keep his hands ahead of the reservoir, to work purely in the mechanical register. Fingers only. No channeling. He rotated the valve one careful quarter-turn.

The resistance changed. He'd used a fraction more force than the compound could compensate for. He lifted the valve and looked.

Uneven. The contact ran heavy on one side and light on the other, the compound's grey residue telling the story: he'd pushed harder on the near edge without meaning to.

He applied more compound. Tried again.

This time his hands were trying to be precise and the reservoir was trying to be helpful and they were fighting each other, and what reached the valve seat was something between the two—not his hands' precision, not the Wellspring's force, but a confused negotiation. He heard the faint sound of brass against brass under too much pressure.

He stopped. Lifted the valve. Looked.

A hairline. The thinnest fracture, running along the edge of the taper where the channeling had concentrated beyond what the brass could absorb. Not catastrophic—a crack you could feel with your thumbnail but barely see. Enough to compromise the seal. Enough to mean this valve seat would need to be remachined or replaced.

He set the valve on the bench. Set down the compound. Stepped back from the housing.

The workshop had gone quiet. Not completely—Davel still moved the file—but the quality of the silence had changed.

Ash stood with his hands at his sides and looked at the cracked valve.

He had done it faster. He had done most of it worse.

* * *

Haran came and stood beside him.

He picked up the valve. Turned it in the light, reading the contact pattern and the hairline fracture. He said nothing for a moment.

"Your hands still work," Haran said.

He set the valve down. He looked at Ash.

"The power doesn't replace them. It's another tool." He picked up the lapping bar from the near rack—a plain wooden handle, a pad of fine abrasive at the end. "You don't use a hammer on glass."

He sat on the stool at the central bench and took a fresh valve in his left hand—from the parts bin, identical to the one Ash had cracked. He spread a thin film of compound on the face. Seated the valve in the housing.

Ash watched his hands.

They moved the way hands moved after decades of the same motion. Deliberate. Haran's right hand applied a light, circular rotation, and the pressure was even across the face—not from any special effort, but because Haran had done this long enough that evenness was the default.

Three rotations. Four. He lifted the valve and looked at the contact pattern. Nodded once. Applied more compound. Seated it again.

The workshop's sounds continued around them. Davel's file. The lathe's idle clatter. Haran's hands, moving in the same patient circle. Ash stood beside the bench and watched, and the reservoir hummed—patient, enormous, and for the moment, completely useless.

Haran completed the calibration in about eight minutes. The valve seat, when he lifted it for the last time, showed a contact pattern even across the full face of the taper—grey compound spread uniformly, no heavy spots, no gaps. He set the valve on the bench with a small, conclusive sound.

"The valve work," Haran said. He turned the valve in his fingers. "When you calibrated it by hand, before. What were you feeling for?"

Ash looked at the valve. The question was simple enough that he answered before he thought about it. "Back-pressure. The cone seating against the socket—there's a specific resistance when it's right. You can feel the whole seat load through the stem."

"What else?"

"Temperature. The compound heats up if you're pressing too hard. If the brass gets warm under your fingers, you're past the limit."

"And the contact pattern. How did you know when to stop?"

"When the rotation felt the same at every point. Even drag. No light spots, no heavy spots. You can feel it—" He stopped. His hands had moved as he spoke, turning in the air in front of him, mapping the motion. "You can feel it before you see it."

Haran set the valve down. "So your hands already know." He paused. "The pool stays where it is."

* * *

They worked for a while after that.

Haran at the pump, which was still less than half-assembled. Ash beside him, doing what his hands remembered how to do: fetching components from the rack, holding a fitting steady while Haran torqued the threading, passing tools without being asked because he'd spent enough time at this bench to know which tool came next.

He did not channel.

After a while, Haran said: "Try it again. The small stuff. But smaller."

He picked up a small gear blank from the scrap bin, set it on the bench between them. "Heat the center. Not the whole thing. Just—" He tapped a finger against the brass, at its exact center. "There."

Ash placed a finger against the blank. He reached for the smallest thread he could find. Pushed.

The whole gear blank heated. Not dramatically—it would cool in a minute—but uniformly, the heat spreading from his touch to the edges, indifferent to his intention.

He pulled his hand back.

"Again," Haran said.

Again. Same result. Even the smallest reach was still an ocean pouring through a crack.

"I can't get it that small," Ash said.

"You can." Haran picked up the gear blank, now cooled, and set it in his own palm. "Watch."

A small warmth bloomed in the exact center of the brass. A circle, barely larger than a thumbnail, a few degrees of difference—Ash could see it in the way the light caught the surface, the faint change in reflection where the metal had accepted heat. Then it cooled. A second later, another brief, precise bloom, slightly off-center. Then another.

No glow, no effort showing in his face or hands. Just the warm spot appearing and vanishing, appearing and vanishing.

Haran turned the blank over and set it on the bench. "Your turn."

Ash tried. Reached for the thinnest connection he could find between the Wellspring and his fingertip. Placed his finger on the blank. Pushed.

The heat came in less than before—not the full bloom, not the edge-to-edge spread, but still more than the thumbnail Haran had produced. Three or four times that.

He held his finger there and tried to narrow it, to concentrate the flow, and felt the power resist—not refusing, just indifferent to the demand, flowing at whatever volume it found natural.

He pulled his hand back.

"Again," Haran said.

He tried again. And again. Each time he pushed, the heat spread past his intention. Each time he pulled back and reset and tried to find something smaller inside the vastness of the reservoir. The gear blank warmed and cooled and warmed and cooled.

On the seventh attempt, something happened. Not mastery—not even close. But for a quarter-second, the thread touched the brass and stopped. Held. Stayed where he'd put it, a circle roughly twice the size of Haran's thumbnail, before the reservoir surged and the heat spread to the edges again.

A quarter-second.

"That's the direction," Haran said.

Ash picked up a second gear blank from the scrap bin. He tried again. The quarter-second didn't repeat. The heat spread. He tried again.

Davel, from the window: "Better than yesterday."

"He wasn't here yesterday," Ash said.

"That's what I mean," Davel said, without looking up from his file.

* * *

The afternoon lengthened. Petra finished her component and left, raising a hand to Ash as she went. Davel moved from the window to the lathe. The workshop's sounds changed register—the lathe's higher voice, the chip tray catching metal, the smell of cutting fluid joining the existing workshop smell.

Ash worked with his hands. He held components. He measured. He fetched from the rack when Haran needed something and put back what Haran was done with. The pump progressed slowly, as the pump would always progress slowly—there was no rushing the calibrations, no skipping the steps.

After a while, he found himself watching Haran. Not the pump—the man.

The way he moved around the bench. His hand reached out as he passed the workshop wall and touched the stone, briefly. Where his palm had been, the cold edge came off the surface—not heating the whole wall, just the spot where he'd been standing. He didn't pause for it. The touch happened between one task and the next, part of the same continuous motion.

A lamp in the corner had dimmed as the afternoon shifted. Haran glanced at it from across the room. The wick brightened. He didn't cross to it. Didn't gesture toward it. The brightness came and steadied, and Haran was already reaching for the next fitting.

Then the stuck fitting at the outlet flange—the one Ash had fused. Haran's hand passed near it as he reached for a tool, and his fingers paused a fraction of an inch from the brass. Not touching. Reading. The pause lasted a breath, and he picked up the wrench and moved on.

Ash looked at the workshop.

The warmth in the room had nothing to do with the small stove in the corner. The stove hadn't been lit in hours.

He had been here a hundred times. He had sat at this bench on cold afternoons and thought the workshop was warm because it was small and full of people and because the stove was good.

He looked at the lamp. At the wall where Haran's palm had rested. At the fitting Haran had read without touching. At the valve seat, calibrated in eight minutes with even contact and no channeling at all because *for this? Not useful.*

None of it was large. All of it was constant.

*He could have maintained a district.*

Haran was fitting a spring into the pump's pressure chamber. His hands were unhurried, the spring seated carefully, testing the compression with the pad of his thumb. He looked up and found Ash's eyes and held them for a moment.

Something around the old man's eyes softened. Then it settled back, and he looked down at the spring. Tested the compression again. Nodded once and picked up the retaining clip.

Ash turned back to the bench. His hands found the outlet valve body on the rack—the component Haran would need next. He fetched it. Set it where Haran could reach it.

They worked.

* * *

The light through the windows had gone orange by the time Ash straightened and pressed his hands to the small of his back. The pump was further along—not dramatically, the work of an afternoon measured in components seated and calibrations completed. Still less than half built. Still weeks from finished.

Davel had gone. The lathe was quiet.

Haran was threading a fitting at the end of the bench, the handle turning in his hands with the even, unhurried rhythm of a man who would finish when the thread was right and not before.

"I'll come back," Ash said. "Help you finish it."

Haran looked at him. "Your hands would be useful."

Not the Wellspring. The hands.

Ash began pulling on his coat.

Haran's hands kept threading. The handle turned. "How much did that cost you?" he said. "The fittings. The pipe."

Ash thought about it honestly. He had not paid attention, which was itself information. "Nothing I can feel."

Haran nodded. The handle turned. "Good. Remember what the answer is." He looked up from the fitting. "You might want to check it later."

Ash waited.

"You're learning what you can do." Haran set the threaded fitting in the parts bin. Picked up the next one. "Good. Don't forget what you don't need to do."

Ash put his arms through the coat. In the scrap bin, the gear blanks he'd practiced on sat among the other cast-offs—warmed and cooled and warmed again, carrying no visible mark of the afternoon's work. He picked up the one that had held for a quarter-second. Put it in his coat pocket.

"See you next week," he said.

"Door's open," Haran said.

Ash opened the workshop door. Cold air from the lane—charcoal and wet stone and the mineral smell of a district where the maintenance channeling hadn't reached the cracks in the buildings. The sky above the roofline held the last deep blue of the hour before dark.

He stepped out. His left boot found the flagstone that always tipped—the one he'd been stepping over for years. He stepped over it.

The reservoir hummed behind his ribs. The evening was cold. He put his hands in his pockets. The gear blank was warm against his fingers, cooling slowly in the way that brass cooled—giving up its warmth in degrees, without hurry.

He walked home through the blue hour with the gear blank cooling in his pocket and the pump still half-built on the bench behind him, weeks from done.
