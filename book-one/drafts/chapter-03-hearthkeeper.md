# Chapter 3

The evening light was doing something generous to the upper city when Ash left the Hall of Infrastructure — catching the western facades at the angle that made the old stonework look the way it must have looked when the Ancients raised it, warm and purposeful, each joint so precise it seemed grown rather than placed. He stood on the steps for a moment and let it settle over him, the particular pleasure of walking out of a building where he'd been useful all day.

Three days in. Three days of carrying documents and memorizing routes and chasing down Laris's late reports and sitting at the table — the actual table, not the corner — while Dorenne dissected the morning brief with the focused intensity of a surgeon who happened to work in infrastructure instead of a clinic. Three days, and the job still fit. Not perfectly — there were gaps, moments where the workflow assumed a channeling capacity he didn't have, interfaces he had to walk around while everyone else walked through. But the gaps were navigable. He was navigating them.

He took the ramp down to the main level, then the stairs. The route had a rhythm by now — forty flights descending through the city's strata, each district announcing itself in the quality of the stone, the state of the channeling, the width of the landings where the stairwell opened onto the streets. The administrative level was polished and humming, the lattice strips in the walls still bright from the day's maintenance. The commercial band below it was busier, noisier, the ozone tang of market channeling hanging in the cooling air. Below that, the middle districts, where the steps were worn to a comfortable concavity and the channeling in the walls held but didn't glow.

He wasn't going home. Not yet.

He kept descending past his parents' district — past the amber band on Dorenne's maps, past the buildings where the heating worked most days and the cracks were managed if not repaired — and into the territory below. The transition was a thing you felt before you saw it: a thinning in the air, a settling, the way a room changes when someone stops channeling and the ambient warmth begins its slow withdrawal. Down here the stairs were rougher, the stone showing its grain where generations of channeled smoothing had faded back to the texture of what the rock actually was. A wall sconce had gone dark — the channeling exhausted, nobody with pool to spare to relight it. The shadows were thicker.

He passed a woman on a landing, coming up. She had a child on her hip and a bag of something heavy in her other hand, and she climbed with the grim efficiency of someone who couldn't afford the lift and didn't waste energy pretending the stairs were easy. The child watched Ash over her shoulder with the frank, unselfconscious stare of someone too young to know that staring was rude. Ash smiled at the kid. The kid didn't smile back. Fair enough.

Two more flights down, and the stairwell opened onto a street that smelled like cook-fire smoke and rust and the particular sweetness of rotting fruit from the vendor carts that served this level. The paving stones were uneven — the channeled leveling cracked and heaved, tree roots and settling substrate pushing through from below like the ground reasserting itself. Someone had filled the worst gaps with gravel and packed earth. A practical solution. A non-magical one.

Ash turned right, following a route he could have walked blindfolded. Past the chandler's shop with the window display that hadn't changed in three years. Past the communal water point where the tap ran slow because the channeled pressure in the pipes was down to a pool — the word had a different weight in a district where a pool was all anyone had left. Past the building with the blue door where the landlord had given up on channeled maintenance and hired Ash's father to reinforce the lintels with timber bracing. The bracing was holding. The channeled walls around it were not. The building had the particular look of something wearing a splint.

Down a side street, narrower, the buildings leaning in overhead until they nearly touched. Laundry on lines between the upper windows. The last of the evening light reaching the street in thin strips, turning the wet stone gold where it landed.

And there it was. The workshop.

* * *

Haran's door was open. It was always open in the evenings, unless the weather was truly terrible — the old man's concession to the idea that a workshop was a place people should be able to walk into, even if most of the city had forgotten the place existed. The door itself was a heavy slab of reclaimed timber, hung on iron hinges that Ash had forged himself two years ago in Haran's forge, and the hinges still moved without sound, which was a point of private pride that he would never mention to anyone.

He ducked under the lintel — the doorframe was built for a shorter generation, or for a time when people didn't build doorframes at all and just channeled the stone out of their way — and stepped inside.

The smell hit him first. It always did. Sawdust, machine oil, the sharp tang of cut metal, and underneath it all the warm, woody breath of the workshop's timber-framed walls, which Haran had built himself from salvaged lumber when the original stonework started shedding dust. The smell was so familiar it bypassed his conscious mind and went straight to the place in his chest where comfort lived — the deep, animal part that didn't care about jobs or performance or the careful architecture of his public self. His shoulders dropped half an inch. He hadn't realized they were up.

The workshop was a single large room, low-ceilinged, cluttered with the organized chaos of a mind that knew where everything was and didn't care if you didn't. Workbenches lined three walls — heavy slabs of oak, scarred and stained, covered with tools and half-finished projects and the detritus of experiments in various stages of success. Shelves above them held jars of screws and bolts sorted by size, coils of wire, sheets of copper and tin, glass bottles of compounds Ash could identify by smell if not by name. A lathe sat in one corner, hand-operated, its gears gleaming with fresh oil. A forge occupied the back wall — a real forge, coal-fired, with bellows and an anvil and a quenching trough that Ash had spent more hours at than he could count.

Haran was at the central bench, his back to the door, doing something to a length of copper tubing that involved a small flame and intense concentration. He was a lean man — not thin, but stripped down, the way old wood gets when the soft parts have weathered away and what's left is grain and strength. His hands moved with the deliberate patience of someone who had been doing this kind of work for longer than Ash had been alive. His hair was white and kept short, almost military, and his face had the particular weathered quality of stone that had been exposed to a lot of weather and come out looking better for it.

He didn't turn around when Ash came in. But one hand left the tubing and reached to his right, where a lamp on the edge of the bench was burning low — a flame lamp, wick and oil, not channeled light — and adjusted the wick up. The room brightened. An invitation, made in light instead of words.

Then, so casually that you'd miss it if you weren't watching closely, he flicked two fingers toward the forge. The coals, which had been banked and gray, flushed red. A soft pulse of heat rolled through the room — not dramatic, not showy, just a breath of warmth settling into the air with the efficiency of something that had cost almost nothing to produce. Haran's channeling was like that. You could live next to him for years and barely notice it, the way you'd barely notice a master carpenter's joints until you tried to make one yourself and realized the simplicity was the hardest part. He used his pool the way he used his tools — precisely, without waste, with the quiet authority of someone who had nothing to prove and no audience to impress.

"Bench is clear," Haran said, still not turning around.

Ash crossed the room and set his bag down on the bench Haran had indicated — the long one under the window, the one that had been Ash's spot for as long as he'd been coming here. He ran his hand along the wood surface, feeling the familiar scars and burns and the smooth patch at the near end where he'd sanded down a gouge last year. The bench was clean, tools arranged at the back in a row that suggested someone had recently tidied. His tools. The set Haran had given him over the years — one at a time, usually without comment, usually left on the bench where Ash would find them the way you find a thing that was always meant to be there.

"I wasn't sure I'd make it tonight," Ash said. "The office runs late."

"But here you are."

"Here I am."

Haran finished whatever he was doing to the copper tubing — some kind of joining, solder flowing into a seam with the controlled precision of a man who'd done this ten thousand times — and set it down. He turned around. His face was the same face it always was: patient, observant, carrying the kind of quiet humor that didn't need to announce itself. His eyes moved over Ash with the brief, comprehensive assessment of someone who was more interested in what he saw than in what he expected to see.

"You look different," he said.

"Different how?"

"Taller." A pause. "Louder, maybe."

"I haven't said anything loud."

"Not your voice." Haran picked up a rag and wiped his hands, slowly, the way he did everything — not because he was slow but because he saw no reason to rush. "You're carrying something. It's making you take up more room."

Ash leaned against his bench and grinned — the real grin, the one he wore in this workshop and nowhere else, the one that had nothing behind it except the fact of being here. "I had a good week."

"Tell me."

So he told him. And the telling was different from the way he'd told anyone else — different from the careful, edited versions he'd given his parents, different from the raw honesty he'd shared with Ryn. With Haran, the telling was practical. He talked about the office the way you'd talk about a mechanism — how it worked, where the moving parts were, what drove what. He described the scheduling systems and the maintenance grids and the way Dorenne's morning briefs functioned as a triage exercise, resources allocated against a deficit that never closed. He described the map on the wall — the green and amber and red — and the way the colors shifted week to week, the amber districts slowly bleeding toward red at the edges.

He described the scheduling conflict he'd solved — the aqueduct junction, the offline gap at junction nine — and watched Haran's face for the response. It was the response he'd wanted from someone all week: not surprise, not congratulations, but recognition. The brief nod of someone who understood the kind of thinking that had produced the solution because it was the same kind of thinking he'd been teaching Ash for years. See the system. Find the gap. Use what's already there.

"And she put me at the table," Ash said. "Not the corner. The table. For the morning briefings. She said—" He stopped. He was talking faster than he meant to, the words stacking up against each other in their rush to get out. He made himself slow down. "She said I'd earned it."

"Had you?"

"I think so. Yes." He paused. "It's the first time I've felt like I'm actually contributing something. Not performing. Not finding workarounds so I don't slow anyone down. Actually contributing."

Haran said nothing. He folded the rag and set it on the bench. Then he turned, walked to the back of the workshop where a shelf held a collection of projects in various states of completion, and picked up a cylindrical device about the length of his forearm — brass and iron, a piston housing, a set of valve assemblies, an intake manifold that Ash recognized immediately because he'd machined it himself over three weeks of careful work.

The water pump. His water pump. The mechanical pump he'd designed last month to replace the channeled pressure systems in the district's lower pipes — the ones that had been running at half capacity for years because nobody in the district had pool enough to maintain them. It was a simple design, elegant in the way Haran had taught him to value: a hand-operated piston that used air pressure and a one-way valve to move water uphill without channeling, without fuel, without anything except a person's arms and the willingness to work them.

Haran held it up, examined it as if seeing it for the first time, turned it over in his hands. Then he set it back on the shelf, precisely where it had been, and looked at Ash.

He said nothing.

He walked back to his bench, sat down on his stool, and picked up the copper tubing again. "Make yourself useful, then," he said. "That valve seat on the number three housing needs lapping. Compound's in the jar."

Ash stood there for a moment — one breath, two — with the grin still on his face and something tugging at the edge of his awareness, something just out of reach, like a word on the tip of his tongue. The pump on the shelf. Haran's silence. The deliberate, pointed act of saying nothing.

He didn't catch it.

He pulled a stool up to his bench, found the number three valve housing where Haran had left it — a brass cylinder the size of a fist, its seating surface scored from use — and uncapped the jar of lapping compound. The work was familiar. His hands knew what to do.

* * *

The workshop filled gradually, the way it always did in the evenings.

Seri arrived first — a lean, sharp-featured woman in her late twenties who worked a maintenance shift on the water system three districts up and came to the workshop afterward with the particular exhaustion of someone whose pool barely covered the demands of her job. She had magic — enough to hold her position, not enough to hold it without spending everything she had every day. She'd started coming to Haran's a year ago, drawn by the same thing that had drawn Ash years before: the idea that there might be a way to do things that didn't leave you empty by sundown.

She nodded at Ash, hung her coat on the hook by the door, and went straight to the bench where she'd been working on a pressure regulator — a mechanical device that could stabilize water flow in the pipes without channeling. She'd been designing it for months. It wasn't working yet. She kept coming back.

"How's the regulator?" Ash asked.

"Leaking." She said it the way you'd say the weather was cold — a fact, not a complaint. "The gasket material degrades. I've tried leather, rubber compound, waxed cotton. Everything softens under pressure."

"Have you tried layering? Leather for structure, waxed cotton for the seal, with a gap between for expansion."

She looked at him. "No. That's — hold on." She was already pulling the housing apart, her hands quick and certain despite the tiredness in her face. Ash watched her work and felt the particular pleasure of offering a solution that someone could actually use. Not a performance of usefulness. Not a scheduling trick that earned him a seat at a table. Just one person's experience handed to another person's problem.

Haran didn't look up from his soldering, but Ash caught the slight movement of his mouth — not a smile, exactly, but the suggestion that a smile had been considered and filed for later use.

Old Terren came in next — a heavyset man in his fifties who'd been a structural engineer before the Council downsized his department. He had a decent pool, middling by his generation's standards, but he'd never been particularly skilled with it, and the declining budgets had made skilled channelers more valuable and competent-but-unremarkable ones expendable. He came to the workshop because he liked problems. He liked taking things apart and understanding why they worked, and he liked putting them back together in ways that didn't depend on magic he wasn't very good at anyway. He was currently building a mechanical clock — not because anyone needed a mechanical clock, but because the act of building it made him happy in a way that his old career never had.

"Evening," Terren said, settling onto his stool with the careful movements of a man whose knees had opinions about cold weather. He pulled his clock mechanism toward him and peered at it through a loupe, adjusting an escapement with the tip of a fine screwdriver. "New boy still here?"

"New boy is twenty-three," Ash said.

"Practically an infant." Terren squinted at his escapement. "Haran, this pivot is binding again. The bore's too tight."

"Tolerance is half a hair's width," Haran said, without looking up. "Are you measuring, or guessing?"

Terren muttered something that might have been an answer and might have been a commentary on the measuring standards of people who'd been building things since before the decline started. He pulled out a pair of calipers and began measuring with the exaggerated care of someone who'd been told to measure and wanted everyone to see him doing it.

The last to arrive — late, out of breath, trailing the particular energy of someone who'd been running — was Pael. Sixteen, gangly, with the restless intensity of a teenager who'd found something to care about and hadn't yet learned to be cool about it. He had a pool — a small one, adolescent-standard, barely enough for his daily needs — and he didn't much care. What he cared about was gears. Specifically, the idea that a set of gears, properly designed, could do things that channeling couldn't: run continuously, transfer force over distance, operate without a person standing there pouring their energy into it. Machines that worked while you slept. The concept had seized him six months ago and hadn't let go.

"Sorry, sorry." He slid onto the bench next to Ash and pulled a sheaf of papers from his bag — sketches, calculations, the margins filled with notes in a hand so small it was nearly illegible. "I was working on the gear train. I think I figured out the reduction ratio for the mill—"

"Slow down," Haran said. Still not looking up.

Pael took a breath. Then another. Then, with visible effort: "I think I figured out the reduction ratio for the grain mill. If we use a three-stage compound gear train instead of two, we can drop the input force to something a single person can sustain. I did the calculations."

He held out the papers. Haran set down his soldering, wiped his hands, and took them. He studied them for a long time — longer than the calculations probably warranted, Ash thought, but that was Haran. He gave things the time they deserved, and he believed everything deserved more time than you thought.

"Your numbers are right," Haran said, eventually. "What material for the gears?"

"Bronze for the main shaft, iron for the reduction gears."

"Why?"

Pael blinked. "Because bronze is — it's smoother, it wears better on the primary—"

"Why not wood?"

"Wood? For gears?"

"Hardwood. Oiled. Replaceable when it wears." Haran set the papers down. "Bronze costs money. Iron costs money. Wood costs an afternoon in the forest and a lathe. If you're building a mill for a district that can't afford to heat their homes, what do you think they can afford for gears?"

The question sat in the air. Pael's face worked through several stages — objection, reconsideration, the particular brightness that came when a problem got bigger and more interesting instead of smaller. "Hardwood gears would need wider teeth," he said, slowly. "More friction. I'd have to recalculate the ratio."

"Then recalculate."

Pael pulled his papers back and began writing, his pencil moving fast, his lips moving faster. Haran watched him for a moment with an expression that Ash recognized — the warm, tired amusement of a man watching someone discover a problem he'd already solved thirty years ago and was choosing to let them discover on their own.

The workshop settled into its evening rhythm. Five people working at benches, the sounds layered and overlapping: the rasp of Ash's lapping compound against brass, the click of Terren's calipers, the scratch of Pael's pencil, the occasional hiss of Seri's soldering iron, the soft, intermittent tapping of Haran's tools. Nobody spoke much. The silence wasn't empty — it was full of the sound of work, the particular music of people making things with their hands, and the music was enough.

Ash looked around the room and felt something he couldn't name settle into his chest. These people. This place. The smell of sawdust and oil and the warmth from the forge and the light from the flame lamps casting everything in amber. He'd been coming here for years — since he was seventeen, since Haran had found him on the street outside trying to fix a broken cart axle with a rock and a borrowed wrench, and had watched him for ten minutes, and then opened the door and said, "There's a vise inside." That was the whole invitation. A vise and an open door.

Since then, this room had been the one place in Solathis where his condition didn't matter. Not because anyone here was generous about it — generosity implied there was something to be generous about — but because the work itself was the currency, and the work didn't care what you could channel. The work cared whether your hands were steady, whether your mind could see the problem, whether you understood that a mechanism was a conversation between forces and your job was to make the conversation coherent. In this room, Ash wasn't the man who couldn't channel. He was the man who could machine a valve seat to within a hair's tolerance, who could design a water pump that ran on muscle instead of magic, who understood materials and forces and the patient logic of things that moved because physics said they should.

He'd never had to explain this to anyone here. He'd never had to perform his okayness. The workshop hadn't asked him to be cheerful or brave or inspiring. It had asked him to hand Haran the right wrench, and he had, and that was enough.

He pressed the valve housing against the lapping plate and worked the compound in slow circles, feeling the surface smooth under his fingers, and the world narrowed to the thing in his hands and the sound of other people doing the same.

* * *

"So the Director of Infrastructure," Seri said, an hour into the evening, breaking the companionable quiet. She'd given up on the gasket problem for the night and was cleaning her bench with the methodical care of someone whose workday had been chaos and whose evening needed order. "That's Dorenne Kharren?"

"That's her," Ash said.

"She spoke at a water system briefing last year. For the maintenance crews." Seri paused, cloth in hand. "She talked about the declining capacity in the western pipes. The numbers were — I already knew the numbers, I work in the system every day. But hearing someone with authority say them out loud, with the charts, with the projections..." She shook her head. "She's not pretending."

"No. She's not."

"Must be something. Working for someone who actually sees it."

"It is," Ash said, and meant it.

"She sees the problem," Haran said, from across the room. "Does she see the solution?"

The question landed the way Haran's questions always did — quietly, without force, like a stone dropped into water from a low height. You felt the ripple before you saw it.

"She's managing the crisis," Ash said. "Allocating resources, fighting the Council for funding, scheduling maintenance rotations. She's — she's holding things together."

"Holding together," Haran repeated. He didn't say anything else. He was testing a joint in the copper tubing, flexing it gently, listening to the metal the way another person might listen to music. The joint held. He set it aside and picked up the next piece.

Ash waited for more — for the observation, the sideways comment, the lesson disguised as a question about load-bearing or material fatigue. But Haran just worked. The silence was its own comment, and Ash didn't know how to read it, so he let it go.

"The heating grid in our building went out last week," Pael said, from behind his papers. "For three days. My mother said it used to go out once a year. Now it's every month."

"The channelers maintaining it are younger," Terren said. "Weaker pools, longer shifts, more buildings per crew. Same story everywhere."

"My grandmother heated our whole floor by herself," Pael said. "She could do it standing in the hallway. Just — stand there. The heat would move through the walls like the stone was breathing."

"Your grandmother had four times the pool of anyone maintaining that grid now," Seri said.

"I know. That's why I'm building a mill." Pael tapped his calculations. "If we can grind grain mechanically, that's one less thing someone's pool has to cover. Free up capacity for heating, for water, for the things you can't do with gears."

"Yet," Haran said.

Pael grinned. "Yet."

The conversation drifted, the way conversations in the workshop always did — touching on the decline, the changing districts, someone's neighbor who'd switched to a wood-burning stove, someone else's cousin who'd moved out to the Hearthlands because the city had gotten too expensive. Not bitter. Not despairing. Just people talking about the world they lived in, the one that was changing whether they liked it or not, and what they were going to do about it from a workbench in a district nobody important visited.

Ash listened and worked. The valve housing was nearly done — the seating surface smooth now, polished to a soft shine, the brass reflecting the lamp light. He tested the fit, pressing the valve against the seat and rotating it, feeling for irregularities. The rotation was smooth. The seal would hold.

He set the housing down and flexed his fingers. His hands were sore — the good sore, the one that came from work you chose, not work you endured. He looked at his hands the way he'd looked at them on the stairwell landing a few days ago — broad, calloused, strong. But here, in this light, they didn't look like hands that couldn't do something. They looked like hands that had just done something well.

"That seat's clean," Haran said, appearing at his elbow. The old man moved quietly for someone his age — or quietly for anyone, really. He picked up the valve housing and held it to the light, turning it, examining the surface with the close attention he gave to anything someone in his workshop had made. His eyebrows moved — an upward shift, brief, the equivalent of a standing ovation from anyone else.

"Put it in the assembly. Third shelf."

Ash carried the housing to the shelf where the pump components were gathered — the parts of a second water pump, identical to the one already running in three homes downhill. He set the valve housing in its place among the other finished components. The pump was nearly complete. Another week, maybe two, and it would be ready for installation.

He stood there for a moment, looking at the shelf. At the pump he'd designed — the whole system, from intake to output, each part drawn and calculated and machined by his hands and Haran's guidance. Running in three homes already. Carrying water uphill without channeling, without fuel, without anyone's pool. Working while people slept.

His mind went to Dorenne's office. To the map on the wall with its green and amber and red. To the maintenance schedules and the channeling budgets and the crews stretched too thin across too many districts. To the feeling of sitting at that table and saying something that mattered, that redirected resources, that changed the color of a district from red to amber for another month.

Both things were real. The table and the bench. The map and the pump. He stood between them and didn't feel the pull — not yet, not consciously — but the weight of his attention had already shifted, leaning toward the thing that was bigger, louder, more visible. The pump was quiet. The pump ran in three homes. Dorenne's office ran the city.

He went back to his bench.

* * *

Later — the hour when the evening had deepened and the city outside the workshop windows had gone quiet, the street sounds fading to the occasional footstep and the distant bark of a dog — Ash found himself working on something he'd been sketching for weeks. A valve mechanism, small and precise, designed to regulate airflow in a ventilation system. The idea had come to him in the market district, watching a vendor's display case fog up because the channeled seal let air in but not out. A mechanical valve that could control airflow in both directions — open for fresh air, closed for insulation, adjustable in between. Simple in concept. Tricky in execution. The valve needed to be light enough to move with minimal force, strong enough to hold against wind pressure, and sealed well enough that it didn't leak when closed.

He was bent over the sketch, redrawing the hinge point for the third time, when his mind stopped thinking about the hinge and started thinking about the problem underneath it. The problem was friction. The pivot had to be smooth enough to move easily but tight enough to hold position. Too loose and the valve would rattle. Too tight and it would stick. The tolerance was narrow — narrower than the lapping compound could manage on its own.

He picked up a scrap of brass and a file and began shaping a pivot pin, working by feel rather than measurement. His hands knew the dimensions — had always known them, the way a musician's hands knew chord shapes before the mind named the notes. The file moved in short, controlled strokes, metal shavings curling away, the pin taking form under his fingers with the inevitability of something that had always been waiting inside the brass and just needed the excess removed.

He fit the pin into the hinge assembly. Tested it. The rotation was smooth — not friction-free, but controlled, the kind of resistance that felt intentional rather than accidental. He opened the valve, closed it, opened it again. The motion was satisfying in a way he couldn't explain to anyone who hadn't spent hours at a bench — the particular pleasure of two surfaces meeting exactly as they were meant to, the physics of the thing speaking through his fingertips in a language that needed no translation.

His face had changed. He didn't know it — couldn't see it — but if someone had been watching, they would have seen the whole careful architecture of his public self go still and quiet, replaced by something simpler and older. Not the cheerful mask. Not the performed ease. Just a man fully inside the thing he was doing, his attention so complete that there was no room left for the person he usually had to be. His jaw was relaxed. His breathing had slowed. The crease between his eyebrows — the one that lived there permanently, the structural cost of twenty-three years of managed expectations — had smoothed.

Haran saw it. He was sitting at his own bench, oiling the joints of a hinge mechanism, and he saw it the way he always saw it — the moment Ash stopped arriving and started being here. The old man's mouth moved in the expression that wasn't quite a smile, and he looked down at his work, and he said nothing, because the saying would have broken the thing he was looking at, and some things were more valuable intact.

Pael had left an hour ago, his pockets full of calculations and his head full of hardwood gears. Terren had gone shortly after, his clock mechanism wrapped in cloth and tucked under his arm, complaining about his knees and the weather and the declining quality of pivot wire, which was somehow the fault of the younger generation's channeling standards. Seri was still there, asleep in the chair by the forge, her head tipped back and her hands still loosely holding the gasket she'd been testing. She'd wake up in twenty minutes, apologize, and walk home through the dark to an apartment that was cold because her pool was spent.

The workshop was quiet. The fire in the forge was low, banked to embers that threw a warm red light across the floor. The flame lamps flickered. The city outside was dark and still.

Ash worked on his valve mechanism and didn't think about the infrastructure office or the morning brief or the map with its changing colors. He didn't think about anything except the piece of brass in his hands and the problem of airflow and the elegant solution taking shape in the space between what he could imagine and what his fingers could make real.

This was who he was when nobody needed him to be anything else. This was the person who existed underneath the scaffolding, the one who didn't need to perform or prove or carry the weight of being looked at. A man at a bench, making something that would work.

* * *

He became aware that time had passed — a lot of time — when Haran set a cup of tea on his bench. The cup was ceramic, chipped at the rim, warm in Ash's hand when he picked it up. The tea was strong and slightly bitter, brewed in the dented kettle that Haran kept on the shelf above the forge. Brewed by hand — water heated on the coals, leaves steeped in a clay pot, poured through a cloth strainer. No channeling. A cup of tea the way people made tea before magic, the way almost nobody in Solathis made it anymore because why would you when you could warm the water with a thought?

Haran made tea this way not because he couldn't do it faster. He made tea this way because the making was the point.

Ash drank. The tea was good. It was always good.

Seri had gone. Her bench was clean, her tools arranged, the defective gasket left in a jar with a note attached — *Ash: try layered. L/WC/gap.* She'd taken his suggestion. He'd find out next week whether it worked.

"Getting late," Ash said, looking at the window. Full dark. Stars visible through the glass, which was old and slightly warped, bending the constellations into unfamiliar shapes.

"Is."

"I should head back. Early morning."

"Always is, for you."

Ash finished his tea and set the cup down. He stood, stretched — his back popping in three places, the cost of two hours bent over a bench — and began cleaning his workspace. Tools wiped and returned to their spots. Metal shavings swept into the bin. The valve mechanism wrapped in a cloth and set on his shelf, next to the pump components and the collection of projects that accumulated here like evidence of a life being lived.

He looked at the shelf. At the finished valve housing. At the nearly complete pump. At the ventilation valve taking shape in its cloth. At the tools Haran had given him — the good file, the calipers, the set of precision screwdrivers in the leather roll. A whole section of a whole wall, dedicated to the work of his hands.

"Haran."

The old man looked up from his bench, where he was capping jars and putting away solder.

"Thank you," Ash said. "For — this." He gestured at the workshop, the bench, the shelf, the room that had been the most important room in his life for six years and that he'd never once thanked anyone for.

Haran's face did something complicated and private. "You keep showing up," he said. "That's the thanks."

"I mean it."

"I know you do. Go home. You look tired."

"I am tired."

"Good tired or bad tired?"

Ash thought about it. "Good."

"Then it'll keep." Haran turned back to his cleanup. Then, almost as an afterthought, without looking up: "The pump in the Gessler house is pulling air. Valve seal, probably. When you have time."

"I'll look at it this weekend."

"No rush. It still works. Just noisy."

"I'll fix it."

"I know you will."

Ash pulled on his coat and ducked out the door. The street was cold and dark and quiet — the district sleeping, the stone under his feet carrying no warmth, the sky above the narrow buildings wide and star-scattered. He stood on the doorstep for a moment, his breath clouding in the air, his body warm from the forge and the tea and the work.

Behind him, through the open door, he could hear Haran moving around the workshop — putting things away, banking the forge, performing the closing rituals of a space that had been lived in all evening and would be lived in again tomorrow. The sounds were as familiar as breathing. The tap of a jar being capped. The scrape of a stool being pushed back. The soft, nearly inaudible hum of the old man's pool as he sealed the forge damper with a casual touch — a pulse of channeled force so small and so precise that it barely registered, the way a master calligrapher's brushstroke barely registers as effort because the effort happened decades ago, in the years of practice that made the stroke inevitable.

Ash started walking. Uphill now — back through the dark streets, back toward the middle districts, back toward his parents' apartment where the heating lattice would be warm because his mother had channeled it before she went to sleep. His legs were tired. His hands were sore. His mind was already moving ahead — to the morning brief, to the maintenance schedules, to the thing Tessaly had said about the southern district's water pressure that he wanted to follow up on.

The workshop fell behind him, step by step. The smell of sawdust and oil faded, replaced by the cold stone smell of the stairwell as he began the climb. He thought about Dorenne's table — his seat at it, the weight of being asked for his thoughts, the way it felt to say something that mattered to people who could do things he couldn't. He thought about the valve mechanism on his shelf, the pivot pin he'd shaped by feel, the airflow problem he was this close to solving.

He thought about both, and the thinking leaned toward the table.

It wasn't a decision. He wasn't choosing one over the other — wasn't even aware that there was a choice being made, the way you're not aware of a current when you're standing in a river that's only ankle-deep. The water moves around your feet and you don't feel it pulling because the pull is gentle and the river is wide and the direction it's going seems like the direction you were going anyway.

He climbed. Behind him, downhill, the workshop light went out. Haran closed the door.

On the shelf inside, the water pump sat where Ash had left it — the one running in three homes, the one that carried water uphill while people slept, the one he'd designed with his own hands and his own mind, the one that worked without channeling, without fuel, without anyone's pool or anyone's table or anyone's recognition.

It worked in the dark. It worked alone.

Ash didn't think about it. He was already thinking about tomorrow.
