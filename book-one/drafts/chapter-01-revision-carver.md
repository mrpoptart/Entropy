# Chapter 1

Ash woke in the dark and lay still for a moment, listening to the house breathe around him. The heating lattice in the walls had gone cold sometime in the night — his parents' channeling faded while they slept, the way it always did, and the stone had given up the last of its warmth by three or four in the morning. He could feel it in the air, that particular stillness of a room that had stopped being cared for.

He pushed the blankets back and set his feet on the floor. The cold came up through the stone like a reprimand.

The washroom was down the hall. He moved quietly — bare feet placed with the deliberateness of long practice, avoiding the board outside his parents' door that groaned under any weight, the spot near the stairs where the floor had settled and a careless step would knock against the wall beneath. He'd mapped these sounds years ago. The house was small enough that clumsiness carried.

The water in the basin was cold. It was always cold. The heating lattice beside the tap was a smooth oval of pale stone set into the wall at shoulder height, sized for a palm, worn to a soft shine by decades of use. Every morning his mother pressed her hand against it and the water ran warm within seconds — a gesture so automatic she did it while still talking, still thinking about something else. Ash turned the tap and washed in cold water. He'd stopped flinching at it years ago. His body knew the routine — face, neck, arms, hands, the shock settling into something duller as the nerves adjusted. He dried off with a rough towel and dressed in layers. Two shirts, a vest with a quilted lining he'd stitched himself, trousers heavy enough for the walk. It was early autumn and the mornings already had teeth.

He was always the first one up. He needed the time. Everything took longer when you did it by hand in a world built for channelers, and the difference between dignity and dependence was measured in the hour before dawn.

Downstairs, the kitchen was dark and cool. He opened the stove — the iron box his father had found secondhand, one of the few in the district that still burned fuel instead of running on a channeling interface. The interface was there too, a smooth disc mounted on the side of the stove, and his mother used it most nights to cook. But the iron box had a fire grate, and Ash had laid kindling the night before, and he struck the flint and steel with the efficiency of someone who'd done it ten thousand times. Three strikes. The spark caught. He fed it — shavings first, then sticks, then a split log from the bin by the back door.

While the stove heated, he moved through the kitchen. He wiped down the counter. He set out cups. He checked the pantry and pulled together what he needed for breakfast — grain for porridge, dried fruit, a quarter-wheel of hard cheese that was getting close to the end. He found a loose hinge on the cabinet above the counter, the one that had been catching for days, and he took a hand tool from the drawer and fixed it. Two minutes of work. The kind of thing that would take a channeler three seconds — a pulse of force into the metal, the screws seated, done — and took Ash a screwdriver and a steady hand and the willingness to get up early enough that no one had to watch him do it.

Small acts of usefulness, performed before dawn. His parents would come downstairs to a warm kitchen, a set table, a cabinet that closed properly. They wouldn't mention it. He wouldn't mention it. The exchange was old enough to have its own grammar: he tended the house, and they pretended not to notice, and the pretending was a kindness they all maintained because the alternative was a conversation none of them wanted to have. About burden. About what he cost them just by being here.

The porridge was thickening on the stove when he heard his father's footsteps overhead. Heavy, careful, the particular rhythm of a man trying not to wake his wife. Ash poured two cups of tea — water from the kettle, leaves from the tin — and had them on the table when his father came down the stairs.

"Morning." His father settled into his chair with the controlled exhale of a body that hadn't slept quite enough. Broad-shouldered, thick-handed, the kind of man who looked like he'd been built to carry things. He wrapped both hands around the cup and drank.

"The Orellen job?" Ash asked.

"Full day. Maybe two." His father did repair work — physical repair work, the kind that used tools and muscle instead of channeling. There was a niche for it, narrow but real: buildings where the channeling had faded and someone needed to shore up the structure the old-fashioned way. Brackets, bracing, mortar mixed by hand. It didn't pay well. It paid enough. "You?"

"Errand for Mum in the market district. Then I thought I'd stop by the Hall on the way back — they posted a notice about aide positions last week."

His father's eyes came up from the cup. Just for a moment. A flicker of something — hope, maybe, or the reflex to suppress it. "That's the infrastructure office?"

"Council annex, but same building. I won't get it." Ash said this lightly, with the smile that had become so habitual it was nearly involuntary. "But I figure I should at least make them tell me no in person."

His father nodded. The nod carried twenty years of carefully managed expectations — not low expectations, exactly, but expectations that had learned to absorb disappointment without breaking. He drank his tea. "The porridge smells good."

"I put the last of the dried cherries in."

"Your mother will have something to say about that."

"She'll have something to say about anything. Might as well make it delicious."

His father's mouth moved in a way that wasn't quite a smile but lived in the same neighborhood. They ate in the comfortable quiet of two people who had been having breakfast together for twenty-three years and didn't need to fill the silence.

* * *

The morning air hit like a wall when he stepped outside. Solathis in autumn was a negotiation between the cold air sweeping down from the Verenthi Range and the residual warmth that channelers poured into the streets and stones and building faces every day — a negotiation the cold was slowly winning as it did every year, a little earlier, a little sharper.

Ash pulled his collar up and started walking.

His parents' building sat in the lower-middle band of the city, where the plateau started its long step down toward the gorge. From the front stoop he could look up and see the grand districts at the heights — gleaming stone, seamless facades, buildings that caught the morning light like they'd been polished. The elder districts, where the powerful lived and maintained their surroundings with trickles strong enough to keep everything effortless. Up there, the heating never faded. The walls never cracked. The transit lifts ran on time because the residents could spare the energy to keep them running, the way you might spare a breath.

Down here, things worked. Mostly. The building across the street had a web of fine cracks running up its eastern face — channeled reinforcement thinning, the original stonework starting to express opinions about load and gravity that had been suppressed for generations. Two doors down, someone had hung a blanket over their window where the sealing lattice had failed. The transit lift at the end of the block was operating, but the schedule posted beside it was three years out of date and everyone knew the real schedule was whatever the maintenance team's trickles could manage on any given morning.

Ash walked past the transit lift without slowing. Stone platforms that rose through vertical shafts on channeled force — you stepped on, pressed your palm to the interface stone, fed it a pulse, and the platform carried you up. Smooth, fast, the basic circulatory system of a vertical city built on a plateau. Every district had them. Every child used them.

He took the stairs.

Forty flights to the market district. He'd counted them once, years ago, when counting had still felt like a way to measure the unfairness. Now it was just the number. His legs knew the rhythm — a pace he could sustain for the full climb without arriving winded, or at least without arriving visibly winded, which was the standard that mattered. He'd learned early that people noticed heavy breathing. They noticed sweat on a cold morning. They noticed the small signs that marked the difference between someone who chose the stairs and someone who had no choice, and the noticing always came with a look he'd catalogued into a precise taxonomy: pity, discomfort, the faint guilt of passing someone on a road you didn't have to share.

So he climbed at a pace that looked easy and cost him more than it should have, and he kept his breathing even, and when he reached the top he stood for a moment in the market district's morning bustle and let his heart rate settle before stepping into the crowd.

The market district ran along a broad terrace near the city's center — open stalls, permanent shops, the smell of bread and spice and the particular ozone tang that hung in the air wherever a lot of channeling was happening at once. Vendors activated display stones with casual touches as they set up — signs that glowed, samples that warmed, little demonstrations of freshness and quality that were half commerce and half social performance. *Look how much I can spare. Look how strong my trickle is. Trust me because I can afford to waste a little.*

Ash moved through it with the practiced choreography of someone who'd been navigating this space his entire life. At a narrow bridge between two terrace levels, he stepped aside to let a woman pass — she was reinforcing the stone as she walked, a casual civic gesture so automatic it was practically unconscious, her palm trailing along the railing and leaving a faint warmth in the stone behind her. A small maintenance, freely given. The kind of thing that kept the city standing, one casual touch at a time. Ash couldn't reciprocate. He stepped aside, and she passed without seeing him, and the stone under her hand was a little stronger, and the stone under his was just stone.

At a spice vendor's stall, the display cases were sealed with channeled locks — touch the interface, feed it a pulse, the case opened. It kept the expensive goods fresh and the casual thieves out. Ash stood in front of one and waited.

"Help you?" The vendor was a middle-aged woman with the distracted efficiency of someone already three tasks deep into her morning.

"The saffron threads, third shelf. My mother called ahead."

The woman pressed her palm to the lock, the case slid open, and she measured out the threads into a paper envelope. "Payment?"

He counted coins. Currency was one of the few systems in Solathis that didn't run on channeling — metal stamped with the Council's seal, the universal language of people who couldn't or didn't want to transact in magical favors. Money was how the magicless lived, and it was how the rest of the city condescended to deal with them.

The vendor took his coins and moved on to the next customer. A simple transaction. He'd barely registered to her as a person — just a hand holding money, a face she'd forget in ten minutes. Which was, in its own way, a mercy. Better than the alternative: the moment of recognition, the recalibration, the careful adjustment of manner that happened when someone realized they were talking to a person who couldn't channel. Like stepping around a puddle. Like lowering your voice near an injury.

He pocketed the saffron and moved on through the market, gathering the rest of his mother's list. Flour. Salt. A new wicking cord for the oil lamp in the hall — another concession to the non-magical infrastructure that kept their household running. His mother channeled the stove, the heating, the water. His father reinforced the walls and maintained the fixtures. Maren — when she visited — would do something structural and generous that neither parent had asked for but both quietly appreciated. And Ash kept the oil lamps trimmed and the flint sharp and the hand tools in working order, because that was what he could contribute. The backup system. The one that worked when everything else stopped.

* * *

He found a quiet landing on the stairs between the market district and the administrative level — a small alcove where the stairwell widened, with a stone bench built into the wall that nobody used because nobody took the stairs. He sat down.

The smile went first. It didn't fall so much as release — like a muscle finally allowed to unclench after hours of sustained contraction. His face settled into something flatter, older, less recognizable as the person he presented to the world. He leaned back against the cold stone and closed his eyes. His shoulders dropped — not relaxing, exactly, but giving up the posture they'd been holding since he left the apartment. His hands uncurled in his lap. He could feel the tendons in his forearms releasing one at a time, like lines being let off a dock.

Not self-pity. He'd burned through self-pity years ago, the way you burn through a fuel that's available in limited quantities. What replaced it was something harder to name — a tiredness that lived in the architecture of his personality, in the scaffolding that held up the cheerful, capable, easygoing person he'd constructed for public consumption. The scaffolding was good. He'd built it carefully, reinforced it daily, and it held. But the effort of maintaining it was cumulative, and on mornings like this, in the rare private spaces between destinations, he let himself feel the weight of it.

Twenty-three years old. Living in his childhood bedroom. Climbing forty flights of stairs to buy saffron for his mother because the errand gave him a reason to leave the house, and leaving the house gave him a reason to pretend he had somewhere to be. His parents fed him, sheltered him, loved him fiercely, and every morning he woke up in their house was a morning he'd failed to become someone who didn't need them to.

He opened his eyes. Looked at his hands — broad, calloused, strong from a lifetime of doing things the hard way. Good hands. Capable hands. Hands that couldn't do the one thing the entire world was built around.

A minute. He gave himself a minute. Then the scaffolding went back up — the set of the shoulders, the ease in the jaw, the eyes that crinkled at the edges the way people expected them to. He stood, pocketed his hands against the cold, and took the stairs up to the administrative level.

* * *

The Council annex was a high-ceilinged building of pale stone and old channeling — the walls still hummed faintly with the work of the architects who'd raised it, generations ago, when building on this scale was effortless. Ash walked through the main hall with the careful confidence of someone who'd been in enough institutional buildings to know that looking like you belonged was half the work of actually being there.

He found the posting board. Aide positions, administrative staff, maintenance coordinators — the infrastructure office was hiring at three levels, and the notice was written in the crisp, impersonal language of institutions that expected their applicants to channel. *Trickle assessment required. Minimum competency in structural sensing preferred. Candidates should be prepared to demonstrate basic channeling during the interview process.*

He read it twice. The words were familiar — he'd read versions of them on every posting board in the city since he was sixteen. The polite, systemic exclusion that didn't need to be hostile because it was structural. You couldn't do the job without channeling, the way you couldn't swim without arms. Nobody was being cruel. The water was just shaped that way.

He was copying down the office number anyway — force of habit, or stubbornness, or the particular optimism of someone who'd learned to find cracks in walls that other people saw as solid — when a voice behind him said, "That lift at the end of the hall. Is it running?"

He turned. The woman was perhaps sixty, with the bearing of someone accustomed to rooms rearranging themselves around her. Sharp face, sharp eyes, gray hair pulled back in a way that meant she'd spent exactly as long on it as she intended to and not a second more. She wore the formal half-cloak of a Council officeholder — dark fabric, the Infrastructure seal on the collar — and she was trailed by two younger staff members who were trying to look unhurried and not quite managing it.

"I haven't tried it," Ash said. "But it was working when I came through."

The lift was twenty paces away — a broad stone platform set in a vertical shaft, its interface stone glowing faintly at waist height. She walked toward it with the particular stride of someone who walked fast enough to set the pace but slow enough that you could keep up if you were paying attention. Ash followed — not intentionally, but the hall narrowed and they were going the same direction, and she was the kind of person who created a current.

She stepped onto the platform and pressed her palm to the interface. The stone pulsed warm under her hand — he could see it, the faint ripple of channeled energy transferring from skin to stone — and the platform hummed, ready to rise. She turned to him.

"Coming up?"

He stepped onto the platform. His hand found the interface stone beside hers — smooth, cool, perfectly shaped for a palm. He pressed.

Nothing.

The stone sat dead under his hand. No warmth, no hum, no pulse. Just stone. The same nothing it had been every time he'd touched every interface in the city since he was five years old and his mother had placed his small hand on the heating lattice and waited, and waited, and nothing had happened then either.

The platform stayed where it was. Her side was active, humming with the energy she'd fed it. His side was silent. The asymmetry hung in the air like a held breath.

Her staff exchanged a glance — the quick, involuntary kind that meant they were recalculating and didn't want to be seen doing it.

"I can't activate it," Ash said. He lifted his hand from the stone and stepped back off the platform with the practiced smoothness of someone who had learned to make his exits look chosen rather than forced. "I can't channel at all. The stairs are probably faster for me anyway."

The words came out in the register he'd developed for them — light, direct, pre-empting the discomfort. The tone of a person delivering routine information that happened to be devastating. *Sorry, I can't channel. At all. The way some people can't carry a tune.* He'd tested variations over the years and this one worked best: it gave people permission to move past it quickly, which was what they always wanted to do.

But the woman on the platform didn't move past it. She took her hand off the interface stone — the hum died, the platform settled — and she looked at him the way she might look at a structural report that had an unexpected number in it. Not with pity, not with the careful gentleness he'd learned to dread, but with the direct attention of someone who'd encountered a problem outside her model and wanted to understand it before she responded.

"At all," she repeated. Not a question. A calibration.

"Since birth. It's not a condition that improves with time."

"How did you get up here?"

"The main stairs. Forty flights from the lower-middle district."

"Every day?"

"Most days. I had an errand."

Something shifted in her expression — not softening, exactly, but a kind of focusing, the way a lens adjusts. "What's your name?"

"Ash."

"Ash. I'm Dorenne Kharren. I run the infrastructure office upstairs — the one you were looking at the posting for." She nodded toward the board behind him. He hadn't realized she'd noticed. She stepped off the platform entirely now and looked down the hall. "The annex stairs are closed for reshoring. Have been for a week." She said this as though stating a fact she found personally inconvenient. "There's a ramp — maintenance corridor along the outer wall. Built for equipment carts. Walk with me."

She started down the hall in a direction he hadn't been before — a corridor that curved along the building's outer wall, sloping gently upward. Ash fell into step beside her. Her staff followed at a distance that suggested they knew when their presence was required and when it wasn't.

"You were looking at the aide posting," she said. It wasn't a question.

"I was looking at the part where it says 'trickle assessment required' and deciding whether to apply anyway."

"And what did you decide?"

"I decide to apply anyway about three times a week. I've gotten very good at interviews. Less good at the part that comes after."

Her mouth did something that might have been amusement. "What do you do?"

"Currently? I run errands. I do physical repair work with my father when he has a two-person job. I keep the house maintained. I spend time at a tinkering workshop in the lower districts — mechanical devices, things that work without channeling. I'm told I'm good with my hands." He paused. "That's the polite version. The honest version is that I'm twenty-three and I live with my parents and I'm the most educated unemployable person in Solathis."

"Why educated?"

"Because I read everything I can get my hands on and I pay attention. Because when you can't participate in the world the normal way, you watch it very carefully." He heard himself and pulled back. Too much. The honesty was a risk — most people heard bitterness when he talked about his situation, even when there wasn't any, and bitterness made them uncomfortable, and discomfort made them retreat. "But mainly because I'm good at carrying things and taking notes, and those skills translate surprisingly well."

Dorenne was quiet for several steps. The ramp curved upward. Through the windows on their left, Solathis spread out in the morning light — the grand districts above, the fraying edges below, the impossible architecture of a city that shouldn't exist on a rocky plateau and did anyway, sustained by generations of channeling that was slowly running thin.

"I lost my aide two weeks ago," she said. "Third one in four years. I train them, I invest in them, they're talented, and the moment another office offers them a better position, they leave. Every one of them has been a gifted channeler, and every one of them has used my office as a stepping stone."

Ash waited. He knew what was coming — could feel the shape of it in the air the way you feel a change in weather — but he'd learned not to reach for things before they were offered. Reaching looked desperate. Desperate made people reconsider.

"I need someone who's smart, organized, and good with people. Someone who can sit in a briefing about aqueduct failures and follow the engineering even if they can't do the channeling. Someone who'll stay."

"Because no one will poach me."

"Because you'd have a reason to be loyal." She stopped walking and turned to face him. The ramp had brought them to the upper level — the infrastructure office was at the end of the hall, its doors open, the sound of voices and the rustling industry of people managing systems that were more fragile than anyone wanted to admit. "I won't pretend this is purely generous. I need an aide I can rely on. But I've been watching you for the last five minutes, and you read the posting board with the kind of attention that tells me you understand what those jobs actually require, not just what they list. You navigated forty flights of stairs to get here because you don't have an alternative, and you're not winded, and you didn't complain about it, and you made a joke. That's a skill set."

"An unusual one."

"I work in infrastructure. I appreciate things that function under constraints." She held out her hand — not to shake, but palm up, the gesture that in Solathis meant an offer was being made in good faith. In most contexts, the other person would touch their palm to yours and let a small pulse of channeling pass between you — a resonance of intent, a confirmation. The most basic social contract, sealed in magic.

Ash looked at her hand. She looked at his face. And she didn't withdraw it.

He reached out and took her hand — his palm against hers, dry and warm and entirely ordinary. No pulse. No resonance. Just the pressure of skin on skin, the way people must have sealed agreements before magic, the way no one in Solathis did anymore.

"When do I start?" he said.

"Tomorrow. Be here at dawn." Something moved behind her eyes — calculation, satisfaction, something warmer than either of those. "And Ash? Use the ramp. I'll make sure it stays open."

* * *

He walked home fast.

The stairs blurred under his feet — forty flights, downhill, and his legs knew the way well enough that his mind could be somewhere else entirely. Somewhere bright and unfamiliar, a room in his chest that had been locked for so long he'd forgotten what it felt like to stand in it.

He had a job. A real job. Not charity, not a favor, not the kind of work people offered him with careful voices and lowered expectations. A job in the infrastructure office, for the Director herself, because she'd looked at him and seen something she could use, and the thing she could use was the thing he actually was — smart, organized, good with people, willing to work harder than anyone around him because he'd never had the option of working less.

The streets passed in a smear of noise and cold air. He dodged a maintenance crew channeling heat into the road surface — a seasonal job, keeping the stone warm enough to prevent frost cracking — and barely noticed the familiar pang of watching people do with their hands what he could never do with his. The pang was there. It was always there. But today it was smaller, pushed to the margins by something bigger, something that made his chest feel strange and full, like his lungs had been replaced with something that held more air.

He was almost running by the time he reached the building. Took the stairs three at a time — their stairs, the familiar five flights to the apartment — and came through the door with enough momentum that the handle banged against the wall and his mother looked up from the kitchen with the startled expression of a woman who'd been expecting a quiet morning.

"I got a job."

He said it before he could modulate it, before the performance could kick in and smooth the edges and make it sound reasonable and measured. He said it the way a child would — raw, too loud, cracking open.

His mother's face went through three things in the space of a heartbeat. Surprise, then understanding, then something that broke. Her eyes filled and her hand came up to her mouth and she crossed the kitchen in two steps and wrapped her arms around him with a fierceness that had nothing to do with the size of her body. She was not a large woman. She held him like she was bracing a wall.

"Ashlyn." Her voice was thick. She said his full name so rarely that the sound of it always landed differently — tender, or worried, or the particular register she used when something mattered too much for the casual version. "Tell me."

He told her. The annex, the posting board, the transit lift, the woman with the sharp eyes and the Infrastructure seal. Dorenne Kharren. Director of Infrastructure. An aide position, starting tomorrow. His mother held his hands while he talked, squeezing at intervals that corresponded to nothing in his sentences and everything in her internal weather.

"I need to tell your father." She was already moving toward the resonance shelf — a pair of smooth stones, palm-sized, that connected to a matching pair his father carried. She pressed her hand to one and channeled — a brief, bright pulse that would buzz against his father's stone wherever he was, a signal that meant *come home* or *call me* or simply *something happened.*

Ash watched her channel — watched the faint warmth leave her palm and enter the stone, watched the stone hum and go still. A gesture so ordinary it was invisible. Like watching someone breathe.

"He'll come home for lunch," his mother said. "We'll tell him then. We'll have a proper meal." She was already calculating — he could see it, the pivot from emotion to logistics, the thing she did when the feelings were too big and needed to be translated into action. "I'll use the good flour. And the saffron — did you get the saffron?"

"I got the saffron."

"Good. Good." She took the envelope from him, her hands steadier now, the tears not dried but managed. "Sit down. You're flushed. Have you eaten?"

"I had porridge. Mum, I'm fine—"

"You climbed eighty flights of stairs this morning and you're telling me you're fine. Sit."

He sat. She made tea. The kitchen was warm — she'd been channeling the stove when he burst in, and the heat had spread through the small room in the comfortable way it did when someone was tending it. He wrapped his hands around the cup and let the warmth soak into his fingers and felt, for a disorienting moment, like he didn't know where to put the happiness. It was too big for the shelf where he usually kept good feelings — the small shelf, the one sized for minor satisfactions and managed expectations. This didn't fit. It kept spilling over the edges.

* * *

His father came home at midday, sawdust in his hair and the Orellen job abandoned mid-repair. He didn't rush — he came up the stairs at his usual pace, hung his coat on the hook by the door, washed his hands at the basin. But when he turned and looked at Ash, his face had already been told the news by everything it saw — his wife's elaborate preparations, the good plates, the smell of saffron in the rice. He crossed the room and gripped Ash's shoulder, and held on.

"The infrastructure office," he said. "For the Director."

"Starting tomorrow."

His father's hand tightened. Not a shake, not a pat — just pressure, sustained, the kind of grip that said everything his voice wouldn't. He was not a man who made speeches. He was a man who held on.

"Good," he said. "That's good, son."

They ate. His mother had channeled the stove to its full capacity — more heat than the cooking warranted, the surplus radiating into the kitchen until the room was almost uncomfortably warm, a small extravagance that said *celebration* in the language of a household that measured comfort in kilojoules. The rice was fragrant with saffron. There was cheese and flatbread and a jar of pickled vegetables that Ash knew had been earmarked for the solstice. His father opened a bottle of plum wine that had been sitting on the high shelf for long enough to collect dust.

"To Ashlyn," his mother said, raising her cup. "Who has been carrying things uphill his entire life and deserves to set them down."

"To carrying things," his father said. "It's good work."

They drank. The wine was sweet and slightly too strong, and Ash felt it in his chest before he felt it in his head — a loosening, a warmth that had nothing to do with alcohol and everything to do with the way his parents looked at each other across the table, the particular quality of relief that comes when a fear you've been carrying for decades lifts by a single degree.

He knew what this meant to them. Not just the job — the proof that the world had room for him somewhere. That he wasn't going to spend his life in this apartment, climbing stairs, fixing hinges, performing cheerfulness until the performance became the whole of him. They had never said this fear aloud. They were too careful for that, too gentle with each other. But he'd seen it in his mother's face when she thought he wasn't looking — the way her hands would still over whatever she was doing, her gaze catching on nothing, and for just a moment the careful architecture of her composure would thin enough for him to see through it.

That face was gone today. In its place was something fierce and proud and cracked right down the center, like a wall that had held for so long it didn't know how to stand without the pressure.

The front door opened as they were clearing the plates. Maren came in on a gust of cold air, her cheeks flushed, her bag slung across one shoulder with the practiced carelessness of someone who always had somewhere to be. She was twenty-one and already carried herself like a person with a completed architecture — every line clean, every gesture efficient, the kind of composure that could be mistaken for ease if you didn't know it was built.

"I could smell the saffron from the stairwell." She kissed their mother on the cheek, squeezed their father's arm, dropped her bag by the door. "What's the occasion?"

"Your brother has news."

Maren turned to him. Her face was open, curious, already calibrating — he could see the process, the quick assessment of mood and context that she ran automatically, the way a channeler sensed structural stress in a wall. She'd always read rooms. She'd been reading this family's rooms since she was old enough to understand that one of the people in it needed more than the others, and that the remainder was hers.

"I got a job," Ash said. "Aide to the Director of Infrastructure. I start tomorrow."

Her face broke into a smile that reached all the way. "Ash." She crossed the kitchen and hugged him — quick, tight, the way she did everything. "That's wonderful. How did you — tell me everything."

He told her. She listened with the focused attention she gave to things that mattered — leaning forward, asking sharp questions, laughing at the right parts. She channeled absently while she listened — warming a dish that had gone cool, sealing a hairline crack in her teacup with a brush of her thumb — in the efficient, disciplined way that defined her. Every gesture was precise. No energy wasted. The kind of channeling that made her supervisors write favorable reports and her colleagues feel, faintly, that they should be trying harder.

"Dorenne Kharren," she said. "She's serious. The infrastructure office is — Ash, that's one of the most important offices in the city right now. You know the condition of the aqueduct system? She's been fighting the Council for funding for two years."

"I know. I've read the public briefs."

"Of course you have." She shook her head, smiling. "You'll be brilliant at it. You'll run that office inside a month."

The celebration folded her in. More wine. More food. Their mother telling the story again for Maren's benefit, embellishing the parts about Dorenne's expression and the handshake with escalating specificity. Their father mostly quiet, mostly smiling, refilling cups when they went empty.

And then — later, when the conversation had settled into the comfortable murmur of a family that had run out of superlatives — Maren said, "I had a good day too, actually."

The table shifted. Everyone looked at her.

"The assessment results came back from the Thornwall survey — the structural diagnostic I've been running for the past three months?" She said it lightly — too lightly, the lightness of someone who'd already decided this was a minor thing. "The one with the foundation cracking along the eastern terrace. I mapped every load path in the substructure. My supervisor said it was the most precise diagnostic work she'd seen from anyone under thirty."

Their mother reached across the table and took her hand. "That's wonderful, sweetheart. We're so proud of you."

"Both of you," their father added. "Good day for this family."

"Both of us," Maren agreed, and lifted her cup, and smiled.

Their mother squeezed Maren's hand once more, then turned back to Ash. "Now — tomorrow. What time do you need to leave? You'll need the ramp, you said — the maintenance corridor she showed you — so that's the main stairs to the annex level and then the ramp up. How long does that take? Have you eaten enough today? You'll need proper shoes, not those boots, they're—"

The logistics of Ash's new future filled the kitchen like warm air, expanding to take up all the available space. Their father asked about the office layout. Their mother asked about meals. And Maren's assessment — the Thornwall survey, three months of work, the most precise diagnostic anyone under thirty had produced — settled to the bottom of the evening like sediment in a glass.

She didn't bring it up again. She didn't sulk or withdraw or do anything that would register as resentment, because she never did, because she had learned at a very young age that the family's bandwidth for celebration had a shape, and the shape was her brother. She finished her wine. She helped clear the table. She channeled the heating lattice in the wall to keep the kitchen warm while their mother rested, and the channeling was so controlled, so quietly generous, that no one noticed the cost.

Ash noticed. Not the cost — the gesture. He saw his sister's hand on the wall, saw the faint pulse of warmth move through the stone, saw the way she did it without being asked and without mentioning it. He'd seen this a thousand times. He'd never once thought to wonder what it meant.

Tonight, buoyed by the first good thing that had happened to him in years, he didn't wonder either. He smiled at her across the kitchen. She smiled back. The smiles were real, both of them, and the thing that lived in the gap between them was something neither of them had ever named.

Later, when the apartment was quiet and his parents had gone to bed and Maren had walked home through the cold to her own small place two buildings over, Ash stood at his window and looked out at the city. Solathis sprawled across the plateau in the darkness, its districts marked by the fading warmth in the stone — the elder heights still glowing faintly, the middle districts dimming, the young and poor districts already dark. A city of layers, cooling from the edges inward.

Tomorrow he would climb the stairs again. He would arrive early, because that was who he was. He would learn the job, and do it well, and give Dorenne Kharren no reason to regret the impulse that had made her stop and look at a man who couldn't activate a transit lift.

He pressed his forehead to the cold glass and closed his eyes. In his chest, in the place where the scaffolding usually lived, something had shifted — not collapsed, not rebuilt, just rearranged. The structure was the same. The performance was the same. But underneath it, for the first time in longer than he wanted to calculate, there was something bearing weight that wasn't just endurance. A looseness behind his ribs. The ability to breathe one degree deeper than usual.

It felt fragile. It felt like a hairline crack in reverse — not a failure, but an opening. Something letting light in instead of letting strength out.

He stayed at the window until his forehead ached from the cold. Then he went to bed, in his childhood room, in his parents' house, and for the first time in a long time, the fact of being here didn't feel like the whole of what he was.
