## Best Passages

### From The Carver

**1. The deployment brief as artifact:**
> He pulled the folded deployment brief from his coat pocket and set it on the bench, between the vise and the calipers. The paper was crisp, official — Dorenne's seal at the top, Tessaly's notations in the margins. It looked like an artifact from a different world, sitting on wood stained with linseed oil.

Clean, precise. The word "artifact" does double duty — literally an object, but also something archaeological, out of place. The Carver trusts the image to do the thematic work.

**2. The irony replacement:**
> The silence after Haran's words held something — a shape Ash could feel but not name, something that pressed lightly against the conversation and then withdrew. He looked at the pump on the shelf, then at the deployment brief on his bench, and the two objects occupied the same line of sight for a moment before he turned away.

The juxtaposition of pump and deployment brief — the two worlds — does what the named irony couldn't. Subtler, more physical.

**3. The walk-home trim:**
Cuts cleanly from the Haran broom scene to the river metaphor to the pump-shelf to the closing. No redundancy. Every sentence earns its place.

### From The Hearthkeeper

**1. The sensory echo:**
> He touched the stone where she had pressed. The warmth was still there — fading, thin, already losing its argument with the evening air. By midnight it would be gone. By dawn the stone would be as cold as if she'd never stopped.

Then later in the workshop: "The woman on the stairs had spent her pool to warm a wall that would forget her by morning. The stove would burn until the wood ran out, and then someone would add more wood, and the warmth would continue because someone tended it."

This is the richest execution of the echo across both drafts. The contrast between expendable and renewable warmth is thematically loaded and never stated.

**2. The Loan tradition seed:**
> "What the old folks used to say — build what lasts longer than you do."

More natural in Haran's voice than the Carver's "The best tool outlasts the hand that made it." Feels like folk wisdom passed down rather than a crafted aphorism.

**3. Torren's arrival:**
> He settled onto his usual stool the way a river finds its channel — inevitably, and with the sense that nothing else had ever been an option.

A single sentence of Hearthkeeper warmth that tells you everything about Torren's relationship to the workshop without a biography.

**4. The deployment brief as guest:**
> The paper looked startled against the oil-stained wood, like a guest who'd been brought to the wrong house.

Personification of the object — warmer, more playful than the Carver's "artifact from a different world," and equally effective.

## Weakest Moments

### From The Carver

**1. Sensory echo is thin.** "Still warm where she'd pressed. It would fade." — Functional but lacks the Hearthkeeper's depth. The echo to the workshop stove is present but easy to miss.

**2. Loan tradition seed feels slightly formal.** "The best tool outlasts the hand that made it" — sounds like a proverb from a book rather than something a workshop elder would say. Slightly too polished for Haran's voice.

**3. The smoothness observation.** "He'd stopped noticing the seams he used to step around. He'd stopped noticing the absence of seams." — The doubled negation is clever but might read as a stylistic tic rather than earned insight.

### From The Hearthkeeper

**1. Torren's stool passage lingers.** "Each evening brought the finish line half a step nearer, and each morning it retreated by a quarter, and the net progress was so slow that only a man who measured satisfaction in fractions could endure it." — This is precisely the kind of narrative editorializing the revision was supposed to cut. It's well-written but violates the trim mandate.

**2. The wall-warmth passage is long.** The sensory echo is the best across both drafts, but "losing its argument with the evening air" is a personification that tips toward the Hearthkeeper's tendency to overserve imagery. The echo in Scene 2 ("The woman on the stairs had spent her pool...") is a full paragraph — effective but heavy for a passage that's supposed to be background texture.

**3. Lenne's arrival is too detailed.** "Her coat slid down her arm and pooled on the bench beside her. She didn't notice." — While vivid, this level of close observation of a secondary character risks shifting the POV focus. The Carver handled this more efficiently.

## Recommendation

### Primary: Build from The Hearthkeeper

The Hearthkeeper revision edges ahead on the items that mattered most to this revision: the sensory echoes, the tinkerer introductions, and the bridging object. Its warmth is more consistent with the workshop setting — this is the chapter where the Hearthkeeper voice feels most at home.

### Steal from The Carver

1. **Replace the Torren stool passage** with the Carver's leaner version. The Hearthkeeper's "net progress" editorializing should go.
2. **Use the Carver's walk-home structure.** Tighter, no redundancy.
3. **Use the Carver's smoothness line** in Scene 1 — "the smoothness had started to feel normal" is cleaner than "something he deserved." The Hearthkeeper's version over-explains the shift.
4. **Trim the Hearthkeeper's wall-warmth echo** to lose "losing its argument with the evening air." Keep the touch, the fade, the prediction.

### What to Cut from the Hearthkeeper

- Torren's stool metaphor (the river finds its channel line is fine; the finish-line paragraph goes)
- The Lenne coat-sliding beat (one detail too many)
- "Losing its argument with the evening air" (one metaphor too many in the echo)
