# Chapter 18

The retaining wall in the Millward block had a fracture that ran diagonal, top corner to foundation, like a crack in ice spreading ahead of weight.

Ash knelt at its base with both palms flat against the stone. The fracture was deep—not surface damage from the earthquake but structural fatigue, the kind that came from decades of weakening maintenance channeling failing to keep up with the load. The wall held back a hillside. The hillside held up a district. The district held four hundred families who slept against the slope and trusted the stone to stay.

He pushed. The reservoir flowed through his hands and into the fracture, sealing it from the inside out, reinforcing the surrounding stone with a density that would hold for years without additional maintenance. The work took fifteen minutes. Clean. Precise. The wall hummed when he was done—a low vibration he could feel through his knees, the sound of stone remembering what it was supposed to be.

He stood. Wiped his palms on his trousers. Moved on.

The next site was two districts south—a bridge support that Dorenne's crew had flagged overnight. After that, a water junction in the lower western quarter. After that, a section of transit shaft wall in the Verengate district that was showing stress cracks from the increased traffic of reconstruction supply wagons.

Four sites. One morning.

This was the schedule now. Three districts per week, sometimes four, each one receiving targeted Wellspring reinforcement according to Dorenne's reconstruction plan. The blue panel from the Council chamber had become a living document—updated daily, adjusted as new damage was discovered or old repairs held better than expected. Her staff tracked everything. Which sites were complete. Which were pending. Which had been flagged as urgent overnight and needed to be slotted into the rotation.

Ash worked. The work was good. The bridge support took twenty minutes. The water junction took thirty—the conduit was buried deep, behind layers of old channeling, and he had to push through them carefully to reach the fractures underneath. The transit shaft wall was simpler. Forty-five minutes for all three.

He was faster now. Not just more efficient—though he was that too, the wild flooding of his early weeks replaced by something steadier, more directed—but faster in the way a craftsman gets faster. He understood stone better. He could feel the grain of it, the fault lines, the places where reinforcement would hold and the places where it would just delay the next failure. Dorenne's deployment plan had taught him the city the way a surgeon learns anatomy: by working inside it, day after day, until the structures became intuitive.

The reservoir sat behind his ribs as he walked between sites. Present. Warm.

Different.

He'd been noticing it for days now—a quality in the reservoir that hadn't been there before the earthquake. Not smaller. He couldn't have said it was smaller, not in any way he could measure or compare. The power was still vast, still overwhelming by any standard that existed. He could still do things that no channeler alive could match. He could still pour force into stone until the stone sang.

But the formlessness was gone.

Before, the reservoir had been an ocean without shores. Depthless, edgeless, a presence so vast that the idea of containing it was absurd—like asking how big the sky was. You didn't ask. It was just there. You moved through it, and it moved through you, and the question of where it ended didn't apply.

Now there were walls.

Not solid. Not close. More like the awareness of walls in a darkened room—the sense that if he reached far enough, his fingers would find something. A boundary. A surface. The geometry of a space that was large but not infinite.

He noticed it most after a deployment. The work itself felt the same—the flow, the push, the reservoir's force moving through his hands into the stone. But afterward, in the minutes between one site and the next, the edges were there. Closer than yesterday. Closer than the day before.

He didn't reach for them. He didn't probe or measure or try to map the shape of what he was feeling. That would mean thinking about it, and thinking about it would mean—

The bridge support was next. He walked.

* * *

Dorenne's office had changed since the earthquake.

The crisis maps were gone. In their place, a new system covered the walls—charts, grids, columns of numbers in her staff's precise handwriting. The deployment schedule occupied the entire western wall: a grid of sites and dates, each cell marked with a shorthand notation that represented the type of work, the estimated duration, and a number.

The number was energy cost.

Ash had seen the charts before. He passed them every time he came in for a briefing or to pick up the next day's site list. He'd never looked at them closely. They were Dorenne's system, Dorenne's language, the administrative machinery that turned his work into something trackable and reportable and defensible before the Council. He did the work. She tracked it. The division felt natural.

Today he stopped.

He'd come in from the morning's deployments—four sites, clean work, the particular satisfaction of a day that was running ahead of schedule. Dorenne's office was empty. She was in a meeting—a briefing with the southern district liaisons, according to the aide at the front desk. Ash had ten minutes before the afternoon rotation began.

He stood in front of the western wall and looked at the charts.

Each deployment was a row. Date, location, type of work, duration, and the energy number. The numbers were labeled in Dorenne's notation—a shorthand that her staff had developed, since nobody had ever needed to quantify a Wellspring before. They'd built their model from observation: watching him work, measuring the effects, estimating backward from the results to the input required. The numbers were approximate. Ash could feel that—the estimates were in the right range but soft at the edges, the way any measurement was soft when you were guessing at the instrument.

But the pattern was clear.

The early deployments—the heating lattice, the first bridge reinforcements, the work before the earthquake—carried smaller numbers. Hundreds, in Dorenne's units. The earthquake entries were different. Three rows, densely annotated, the numbers climbing into ranges that made the earlier entries look like rounding errors. The retaining walls. The dam. The collapsed building. Three days of maximum-output channeling, each one marked with a number that Ash's eye snagged on and couldn't release.

After the earthquake, the numbers settled back to the pre-quake range. But there were more of them. Three districts a week. Four, some weeks. Each deployment a row, each row a number, each number a withdrawal from—

He stepped back from the wall.

The aide appeared in the doorway. "Director Kharren is on her way back. The afternoon rotation starts at the southern transit hub—the crew is already there."

"I'll be there," Ash said.

He left the office. The charts stayed on the wall, patient, precise, doing what charts did: making the invisible visible. Making the abstract countable. Turning the thing he carried behind his ribs into columns of numbers on a grid.

He walked to the southern transit hub and did not think about the numbers.

He thought about the numbers.

* * *

The tinkerer district was three streets down from the transit hub, and Ash was there before he'd decided to go.

The workshop where the wall was cracking belonged to a woman named Pela—a metalworker, middle generation, who made hinges and latches and the small mechanical components that the tinkerer community used in their projects. Her workshop was a ground-floor space in a building that leaned slightly toward the street, the lean held in check by channeled reinforcement that was—like everything in the lower districts—older and thinner than it should have been.

The earthquake had cracked her rear wall. Not badly. Not dangerously. The kind of crack that let cold air in and would get worse over the next winter if nobody addressed it. She wasn't on Dorenne's schedule. The reconstruction plan prioritized infrastructure that served thousands—retaining walls, transit shafts, water systems. A metalworker's rear wall served one.

Ash pressed his palm to the crack and sealed it.

The work took less than a minute. A pulse of reservoir force, directed and precise, the stone knitting together under his hand the way it had knitted a hundred times before. When he lifted his palm, the wall was solid. Smooth. Better than it had been before the earthquake, because his reinforcement was deeper than the original channeling, laid down by a stronger hand.

Pela stood in the doorway of her workshop and watched. She was a compact woman with burn scars on her forearms and the particular stillness of someone who worked with hot metal and had learned not to flinch.

"Thank you," she said.

"Your hinges are good," Ash said. He'd seen her work in Haran's shop—precision hardware, the kind of thing that lasted. "The pivot joints on the new pump housing were yours."

"Haran's design. I just cut the metal."

"The cuts were clean."

She almost smiled. Almost. The expression lived in her eyes more than her mouth, and it carried the particular warmth of a craftsperson who'd been seen by someone who knew what they were looking at.

He moved on. Two streets over, a neighbor's home—a family he didn't know, their front step cracked and tilting, the kind of damage that was too small for the deployment schedule and too persistent to ignore. He knelt. Fixed it. Stood. Walked.

The reservoir hummed. The edges hummed too—faintly, like a sound he could only hear when everything else was quiet. He kept walking.

* * *

Dorenne found him that evening.

He was in the corridor outside his quarters, pulling off his boots. She appeared at the end of the hallway with a folder under her arm and her stride carrying the particular cadence of someone who had been looking for him and was not pleased about the looking.

"The southern transit hub crew waited forty minutes for you this afternoon," she said. She stopped three paces away. Not close. Not far. The distance of a supervisor delivering a correction.

"I was there. I finished at the transit hub by—"

"You finished late. You were scheduled for the hub at second bell. You arrived at nearly third." She opened the folder. Glanced at it. Closed it. She didn't need the folder—she knew the schedule the way she knew her own name. The folder was a prop, a reminder that the system existed and was watching. "Where were you?"

"I stopped in the tinkerer district. A workshop wall. And a neighbor's front step."

Dorenne's expression didn't change. That was worse than if it had. Her face held the particular stillness of someone who was choosing, with precision, which words to use.

"The tinkerer district," she said.

"Pela's workshop. The rear wall was cracked from the earthquake. And a family's front—"

"I know who Pela is." Dorenne set the folder against her hip. The gesture was familiar—the same posture she'd held in the corridor after the Council vote, the same angle of arm and paper. But the warmth was different. "How much did the wall cost you?"

The question stopped him. Not because he didn't know—he could feel the expenditure, roughly, the way you could feel the difference between a full cup and one you'd drunk from. But because she'd asked it in units of cost, not in units of kindness.

"Not much. A minute. Maybe—"

"In energy, Ash. Not time."

He didn't have a number. He didn't think in numbers. The reservoir was there, and he used it, and the work got done.

"I don't know exactly," he said.

"My staff estimates a wall repair of that size at roughly the same expenditure as a minor transit junction reinforcement." She paused. Let the comparison sit. "You could have reinforced the eastern retaining wall with what that cost. That wall protects four thousand people."

The words landed. They landed the way her words always landed—precisely, without excess force, every syllable carrying exactly the weight she intended.

"Pela's workshop protects one person," he said. "One person whose livelihood—"

"I'm not disputing that Pela's workshop matters." Dorenne's voice was even. Patient. The patience of someone who had made this argument a hundred times in committee meetings and Council briefings and budget hearings, and who knew that patience was more effective than heat. "I'm saying that your capacity is the single most valuable resource this city has, and every expenditure that doesn't serve the reconstruction plan is an expenditure that comes from somewhere. The eastern retaining wall is on the schedule for next week. If your reservoir is lower than projected when we get there, the wall gets a shallower reinforcement. Shallower reinforcement means it needs maintenance sooner. Sooner maintenance means someone else's pool—someone with a fraction of your capacity—has to cover the difference."

She wasn't wrong.

That was what made it unbearable.

He stood in the corridor with his boots in his hand and the reservoir pressing behind his ribs—still vast, still enormous, still more power than anyone alive had ever held—and Dorenne's logic wrapped around him like a net, each point connected to the next, each thread reinforcing the others. Pela's wall was real. The family's front step was real. But four thousand people behind a retaining wall were also real, and the arithmetic didn't care about which real felt more personal.

"I understand," he said.

Dorenne studied him. Her eyes moved across his face the way they moved across her charts—reading, assessing, calibrating her next response based on what she found.

"I'm not trying to control you," she said. "I'm trying to make the math work. The reconstruction is a system, and systems require coordination. If you're spending outside the plan, I need to know—not to punish you, but to adjust the projections. My staff can't build an accurate model if the inputs are unpredictable."

"I said I understand."

She nodded. A small movement, precise, the contained acknowledgment of someone who had delivered a correction and judged it received. She turned to leave, then paused.

"The eastern retaining wall," she said, "is the one that nearly failed during the earthquake. The one you held. If it fails again—if it fails because the reinforcement was shallow because the budget was short because the budget was short because of a metalworker's wall and a front step—that's not a hypothetical. That's four thousand people."

She walked down the corridor. Her footsteps were even, measured, the rhythm of a woman who managed a city the way a surgeon managed a body: every cut deliberate, every resource accounted for, every expenditure justified by the lives it served.

Ash stood in the corridor. He put his boots down. He pressed his back against the wall and felt the stone through his shirt—cool, solid, channeled reinforcement humming faintly in the structure. Someone's work. Someone's pool, spent on this building, years ago, so the walls would hold.

*Four thousand people.*

He went into his quarters. Lay on the cot. Stared at the ceiling.

The reservoir was there. Warm. Present. Vast.

Edges.

* * *

He went back to the charts.

Not immediately. Three days passed—three days of clean deployments, the afternoon rotation running on schedule, the sites falling in order like beads on a string. Dorenne's crew flagged a new priority: a water main junction in the Thornwall district that was seeping, not cracked but degraded, the channeled lining thinning faster than the maintenance crews could reinforce it. Ash handled it. The junction took an hour—more than he'd expected, the degradation deeper than the surface suggested, and he had to push further into the stone than his initial assessment had planned for. When he finished, the junction was solid. But the hour had been long, and in the quiet afterward, walking back toward the command post, he felt the edges again.

Closer.

Not much closer. Not dramatically, not alarmingly. But the room in the dark had walls, and the walls were nearer than they'd been three days ago.

He went to Dorenne's office after the evening briefing. She was gone—a dinner with the Council's infrastructure committee, the aide said. Ash nodded. He stood in front of the western wall.

The charts.

He started at the top. The first deployment—the heating lattice, weeks ago, the work that had announced the Wellspring to the city. Dorenne's notation: a number, a date, a result. He didn't know her units. He didn't need to. What mattered was the relative scale—the size of each number compared to the others.

The heating lattice was small.

The bridge reinforcements were small. The aqueduct repair—small. The transit shaft—small. The canal lining, the retaining walls, the northern quarter work—all small, individually, the steady rhythm of a deployment schedule that distributed moderate expenditures across weeks of work.

Then the earthquake.

Three entries. Three days. The numbers were not small.

He'd known this. He'd felt it—the days of sustained channeling, the reservoir pouring out of him like water through an open gate, the retaining walls and the dam and the collapsed building and the hours of holding the city together while the ground tried to shake it apart. He'd felt the expenditure in real time, felt the reservoir drawing down, felt the effort of it—not physical effort, exactly, but the deeper effort of a force moving through him in quantities that made his daily deployments feel like a match flame compared to a forge.

But he hadn't seen it in numbers before.

The earthquake entries sat in the middle of the chart like a mountain range in a field. Everything before: foothills. Everything after: foothills again. The mountain was him, three days of him, the most heroic and most expensive thing he had ever done.

He looked at the entries after the earthquake. The reconstruction deployments. Three districts a week. Four, some weeks. Each one a foothill—reasonable, moderate, the efficient expenditure of a resource that was being managed by the most competent infrastructure director in the city. But there were a lot of foothills. And they added up. And the chart stretched forward, site after site, week after week, the blue panel's reconstruction plan translated into rows and numbers and a timeline that reached into months.

His eyes moved down the chart. Down, and down, and the rows kept going, and the numbers accumulated, and something in his chest—something that lived next to the reservoir but wasn't the reservoir, something older and colder—tightened.

*How many foothills make a mountain?*

He stepped back. He left the office. He walked to his quarters and sat on the cot and pressed his hands to his face and breathed.

The reservoir was there. Still vast. Still more than anyone alive had ever held.

But it had walls. And the walls were closer than yesterday. And Dorenne's charts tracked the distance between where he was and where the walls stood, row by row, number by number, with the patient precision of someone who had been counting what he hadn't.

He lowered his hands. He looked at the ceiling.

The analytical mind—the one that had always been his real weapon, the one that saw systems and solutions and could trace a mechanism's logic from input to output—that mind was awake now. It had been sleeping, or he'd been keeping it asleep, because the thing it wanted to examine was the thing he wanted not to think about. But Dorenne's charts had woken it, and it was doing what it always did: noticing. Counting. Running the numbers that he'd been avoiding.

Not the full calculation. Not yet. That would come later—alone, at night, with the precision that the question deserved and the honesty that the question demanded. But the shape of the calculation was already forming, the way a structure's shape formed in his hands before the reinforcement began. The inputs were there. The outputs would follow.

He lay down. He did not sleep for a long time.

* * *

He went home on a day when the schedule had a gap.

Not a rest day—Dorenne didn't schedule rest days anymore. The reconstruction plan filled every available slot, and the slots that weren't filled with deployments were filled with briefings or crew coordination or the administrative work that kept the system running. But a site in the Verengate district had been reassessed overnight—the damage was less severe than the initial survey suggested, and Dorenne's crew had downgraded it from Wellspring priority to standard maintenance. That opened a window. Three hours. Maybe four.

Ash walked to the family apartment in the middle of the morning, when the streets were full of people doing the ordinary work of a city putting itself back together. He carried nothing. He'd thought about stopping at the Second Terrace bakery for the flatbread his mother liked—the rosemary loaves, the good ones, the ones from the place he'd confused with the bakery near the Hall the last time—but the thought carried a weight he couldn't name, and he walked past the bakery without stopping.

The apartment was in the Third Terrace. The building was old—pre-earthquake, pre-decline, built in an era when channeled reinforcement was laid down thick and permanent and didn't need refreshing. It had held during the earthquake without help. The walls were solid. The stairs were solid. The door was the same door he'd walked through his entire life—a wooden door, not channeled, hung on iron hinges that his father had replaced twice.

He knocked.

Footsteps. His mother's footsteps—he knew the rhythm, the cadence of a woman who moved through her kitchen with the particular efficiency of someone who had been moving through it for thirty years. The door opened.

She stood in the doorway. She was smaller than he remembered—not physically smaller, but the way people get smaller when you see them after an absence that's longer than it should have been. Her hair was pulled back. Her hands were damp. She'd been at the sink.

"Ashlyn," she said.

Not Ash. The full name, the soft name, the name she used when she was deciding which version of herself to be—the fierce protector, the gentle mother, the woman who had held her family together around the shape of his absence of power and was now holding it together around the shape of his presence of too much.

"Hi, Ma."

She looked at him. Her eyes moved across his face the way they always did—searching, cataloguing, the quick inventory of a mother checking her child for damage. She found whatever she found. She stepped back.

"Come in."

He came in.

The kitchen was the same. The counter, the table, the window that let in mid-morning light. The cups in the cabinet—the old ones, the chipped ones, still in front. The new cups he'd bought still in the back.

And the kettle.

The old kettle. The dented one, the one with the rattle in the lid and the spout that poured slightly to the left. It sat on the stove. Steam curled from its spout. She'd been making tea.

The copper kettle—the one he'd bought, a day's wages from the stipend Dorenne had arranged, the one he'd chosen because it was beautiful and solid and the kind of thing his mother deserved after years of dented metal—was not on the counter.

He looked for it. His eyes moved without his permission, scanning the kitchen, the shelf, the counter. There. On the high shelf, behind the oil jar, pushed to the back the way you pushed things you didn't use but couldn't throw away.

His mother poured tea from the old kettle. The dented spout sent the stream slightly to the left, and she compensated without thinking—angling the cup, catching the pour, a motion so practiced it was invisible. She set the cup in front of him.

"Sit," she said.

He sat. The chair was the same chair. The table was the same table. The tea was the same tea—the dark blend from the old district market, bitter and grounding, the taste of every morning he'd ever had in this kitchen.

She sat across from him. Her hands folded on the table. She looked at him, and the old fierceness was there—the flash of it, the thing that lived in her jaw and her eyes, the ferocity of a woman who had spent twenty-three years standing between her son and a world that didn't have room for him. She wanted to touch his face. He could see it—the impulse in her fingers, the slight forward lean. She wanted to ask if he was sleeping, if he was eating, if the people around him were treating him like a person and not like a lattice.

She didn't.

She'd learned. Somewhere in the weeks since the activation, in the visits that got shorter and the gaps that got longer, she'd learned to hold herself back. Not because he'd asked her to—he hadn't, not in words—but because the distance between them had taught her what words hadn't. He was important now. He was busy. He had a deployment schedule and a Director and a Council that needed him, and a mother's worry was a small thing next to all that. A small, dented, slightly-to-the-left thing.

"How's the reconstruction?" she asked.

"Going well. The southern districts are mostly done. Dorenne's moving to the eastern quarter next week."

"I heard about the Thornwall water main. Your father talked to Sera's husband—Tereth?—at the market. He said the water's running full."

"It is. The junction was degraded, but the fix held."

She nodded. Sipped her tea. The old kettle sat on the stove behind her, cooling, the steam thinning to nothing.

"And you?" she said. "Not the reconstruction. You."

"I'm fine."

"You look tired."

"I'm not—" He stopped. He was tired. Not physically—the Wellspring carried no bodily cost, and he slept well enough on the cot in Dorenne's command post. But there was a tiredness underneath the physical surface, a tiredness that lived where the reservoir's edges pressed against the walls of the room in the dark. "I'm fine, Ma. Really."

She looked at him. She didn't believe him. She didn't say so.

"Your father will be back soon," she said. "He went to the market early—the good vendor, the one with the peppers. He'll want to see you."

"I can wait."

They sat in the kitchen and drank tea. The silence between them was not uncomfortable—it was the particular silence of two people who had too much to say and had agreed, without discussion, not to say any of it. The morning light moved across the table. His mother's hands stayed folded. The old kettle cooled on the stove.

Ash looked at the copper kettle on the high shelf. He thought about asking. He thought about saying *Ma, why don't you use the one I bought?* He thought about the answer she would give—something practical, something about the old one working fine, something about not needing new things—and he thought about the answer underneath the answer, the one that lived in the dent and the rattle and the spout that poured to the left.

The old kettle was the life she'd built. The copper one was the life he'd tried to give her. She'd chosen the dent.

He didn't ask.

The front door opened. His father came in with a cloth bag of vegetables and the bright, specific smile that Ash remembered from every homecoming of his life—the smile that said *you're here* without saying it, the smile that rearranged itself around whatever it found and made room.

"Ash." His father set the bag on the counter. He crossed to the table and gripped Ash's shoulder—the same grip, the same pressure, the hand that had held him through every failed attempt and every small victory and every ordinary day of a life that had been, by any measure, hard. The grip lasted two seconds longer than it needed to.

"Hi, Da."

His father sat. His mother poured more tea—from the old kettle, the last of it, the pour compensated and caught. His father wrapped his hands around the cup the way he always did, the calloused fingers encircling the ceramic, the scarred knuckles settling into the familiar position.

"I was at the market," his father said. "Tereth was there—you know Tereth? Sera's husband. The baby."

"I know Tereth."

"He said the water pressure's back. Full pressure, all the way up to the fifth-floor taps. His neighbor was crying about it." His father smiled. The smile was pride—uncomplicated, genuine, the kind of pride that came from a specific good your child had done for a specific person. Not the abstract pride of *my son is the Wellspring*. The pride of *my son fixed the water and a man's neighbor cried*.

"The junction was in bad shape," Ash said. "The lining was degraded—decades of thinning. The maintenance crews had been patching it, but the patches weren't holding."

"Like the cistern," his father said.

Ash looked up.

"The cistern in the old apartment." His father turned his cup in his hands. "You remember? You were—what, fourteen? Fifteen? The cistern on the roof was leaking. The maintenance crew patched it three times, and the patches kept failing because the metal was corroded underneath. You went up there with Haran's tools and a sheet of copper and you cut a new lining. By hand. No channeling. Just you and the tin snips and that soldering iron you borrowed from the workshop."

"I remember."

"Took you two days. The first attempt didn't seal—you came down covered in cistern water, furious. Went back up the next morning. The second attempt held." His father's eyes were bright. "That cistern didn't leak again for three years. Not until we moved."

Ash's hands were on the table. He looked at them. The hands that had cut copper with tin snips, that had soldered seams on a rooftop in the heat, that had come down covered in cistern water with the particular fury of someone who had done everything right and the metal hadn't cooperated. Those hands. The same hands that now pressed against stone and poured a reservoir's force into the bones of a city.

"That was a long time ago," he said.

"Three years," his father said. "A copper lining, hand-cut, hand-soldered, by a boy with no pool and borrowed tools. Three years without a leak." He sipped his tea. "The channeled patches lasted six months."

The room held still. His mother was at the counter, unpacking the vegetables—peppers, onions, a bundle of greens—and her hands moved through the familiar motions of sorting and storing, but her eyes were on his father, and on Ash, and on the space between them where the memory lived.

Ash felt something shift. Not in the reservoir—the reservoir was where it always was, warm and present and walled. In the room. In the kitchen, with its old kettle and its old cups and its morning light, something shifted the way weight shifts on a beam when you add a load to one side. The balance changed. The room leaned, just slightly, toward a version of his life that had existed before the Wellspring—a version where a boy went up on a roof with borrowed tools and did hard, imperfect, lasting work.

"I should check on the eastern retaining wall," he said. He didn't need to check on the eastern retaining wall. "Dorenne's schedule has it for next week, but the crew flagged some new cracking overnight."

"Of course," his father said. The brightness didn't leave his eyes. But something behind it shifted—a flicker, a recognition. The moment had passed. Ash had let it pass. His father had watched him let it.

"Is Maren home?" Ash asked.

His mother shook her head. "Working. She's been picking up extra maintenance shifts—the earthquake left more damaged systems than the Bureau can handle, and the crews are short-staffed." She set the peppers in a bowl. "She's usually home by evening, but lately it's been later."

"She's working too hard," his father said.

"She's always worked too hard," his mother said.

They both looked at Ash, and in the look was something he couldn't quite read—a question, maybe, or the space where a question would have been if either of them had known how to ask it. Something about Maren. Something about the maintenance shifts and the Bureau and the fact that his sister was covering for crews that were short-handed because the earthquake had broken more systems than anyone could fix, and the Wellspring—his Wellspring—was deployed on retaining walls and transit shafts and water junctions according to a reconstruction plan that didn't have a line for *sister's exhaustion*.

He didn't ask about the shifts. He didn't ask what Maren was maintaining, or how her pool was holding, or whether the thinning he'd glimpsed months ago—the slightly lower margin, the second pass that used to take one—had gotten worse. He didn't ask because asking would mean knowing, and knowing would mean adding her to the arithmetic that Dorenne's charts had started and his mind was beginning to continue.

"Tell her I came by," he said.

"You could wait," his mother said. "She might be home for lunch."

"I have the eastern wall."

His mother looked at him. Her mouth opened. Closed. The old gesture—the one from the last visit, the decision not to say the thing she was thinking, the restraint she'd taught herself because fighting with her son when he only visited was a luxury she could no longer afford.

"I'll tell her," she said.

He stood. His father stood. The grip on his shoulder again—firm, warm, the hand staying longer than it needed to.

"The cistern," his father said. Quietly. Not as a story now, but as a reminder. As a thing he wanted Ash to carry out the door and through the streets and into whatever the day held next. "Three years, Ash."

"I remember, Da."

He left. The door closed behind him. He heard his mother's footsteps—back to the kitchen, back to the counter, the rhythm resuming. Through the window at the end of the hallway, if he'd turned, he would have seen her standing at the glass, watching him walk away. He didn't turn.

The stairs carried him down. The street carried him east. The morning was warm, the light clear, the city moving around him with the steady rhythm of reconstruction—hammers and voices and the distant hum of channeling as maintenance crews worked their way through the districts Dorenne's plan had flagged.

The reservoir sat behind his ribs. The edges sat behind the reservoir. And behind the edges, in the space where the arithmetic lived, something quiet and cold was forming—not a number yet, not a calculation, but the awareness that a calculation existed and was waiting for him to be brave enough to do it.

He walked east. The eastern retaining wall was on the schedule for next week.

He went to check on it anyway.

* * *

The eastern retaining wall ran for two hundred meters along the base of the Thornwall district's upper terrace. It was the largest single structure in Dorenne's reconstruction plan—a wall of fused stone, three meters thick at its base, tapering to a meter and a half at the crown, holding back the hillside that supported four thousand people's homes and workshops and lives.

He'd held it during the earthquake. Three hours, his hands on the stone, the reservoir pouring through him as the ground shook and the wall flexed and the hillside behind it pushed with the indifferent force of a world that didn't care what was built on top of it. Three hours. The most expensive single expenditure he'd made. And the wall had held.

But it was cracked now. The earthquake had left fractures—not the deep structural failures that would have brought the wall down, but a network of finer cracks, stress lines, the places where the stone had flexed and not quite returned to its original shape. Dorenne's crew had mapped them. Eighteen fractures, ranging from superficial to concerning. The reinforcement—his reinforcement, the one on the schedule for next week—would seal them all. Deep work. Hours of it. The kind of expenditure that Dorenne's charts would mark with a number that mattered.

He walked along the wall's base. He didn't channel. He just looked.

The stone was warm under his hand—afternoon sun on the south-facing surface, the heat absorbed and radiating back. But underneath the warmth, he could feel the fractures. Not with the reservoir—he wasn't channeling, wasn't pushing force into the stone. He could feel them with his fingers. The faintest unevenness. Hairlines too fine to see but wide enough to feel.

He counted. One, two. Five. Nine. He stopped counting at twelve and stood back and looked at the wall as a whole—the full two hundred meters, the curve of it following the contour of the hillside, the mass of it holding back the weight of a district.

Next week. Dorenne's schedule said next week, and the schedule was right—this was the priority, this was the four thousand people, this was the arithmetic that had made Pela's workshop wall and the neighbor's front step into an accusation.

But next week was also a number on the charts. A large number. Hours of work. A withdrawal from a reservoir that had walls now, walls he could feel, walls that were closer than they'd been yesterday.

He pressed his hand flat against the stone. The fractures pulsed faintly under his palm—not with energy, just with absence, the particular vibration of stone that should have been whole and wasn't.

*Four thousand people.*

He pulled his hand away.

The walk back to the command post took twenty minutes. He walked slowly. The city moved around him—the reconstruction, the crews, the steady work of rebuilding. People recognized him. Some nodded. Some smiled. A child pointed from a window and pulled her mother's sleeve.

The reservoir sat behind his ribs. Present. Warm.

Walled.

In three days, he would sit down with Dorenne's charts and his own memory and the analytical mind that had always been his real weapon, and he would do the calculation. He would add the columns. He would project forward. He would trace the line from where he was to where the walls stood and he would measure the distance between them.

But that was three days from now.

Tonight he lay on the cot in his quarters and pressed his hands to his face and felt the reservoir and its edges and the quiet cold of a fear that hadn't fully formed yet—not panic, not despair, just the recognition. The simple, terrible recognition.

The ocean had a floor.

He breathed. He breathed again. Outside the window, the city hummed—the patchwork glow of districts rebuilding, the light mapping capacity, the warmth where it existed and the dark where it didn't. Somewhere in the Third Terrace, in an apartment with solid walls and an old kettle and cups that had been carried from a previous life, his mother was washing the tea things. His father was putting away the vegetables. The peppers from the good vendor. The morning's conversation settling into the silence of a house where two people lived around the shape of their children's absences.

And Maren. Somewhere in the middle districts, on a maintenance shift that stretched past dinner, channeling with discipline and finitude into systems that cracked faster than anyone could fix them. Her pool spent. Her hands on the stone. The second pass that used to take one.

He didn't know this. He hadn't asked.

The old kettle on the stove. The copper one on the high shelf. The cistern that held for three years.

The reservoir pressed against its walls, and the walls pressed back, and Ash lay in the dark and felt the geometry of a space that was large but not infinite, and the fear was a cold thread in his chest, thin and precise, and he followed it down and down and down to where the floor was waiting.

Not tonight. Not yet.

But soon.
