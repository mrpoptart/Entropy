# Chapter 9

The quarry opened like a wound in the plateau's edge.

Ash stood at the rim and looked down. Thirty meters of sheer rock face, then rubble, then the gorge floor where the river had run centuries ago before someone with enough power had diverted it north. The quarry walls were scored with the marks of old extraction—clean faces where stone had been cut by channeling, rougher surfaces where the work had been done by hand. Nobody came here anymore. The stone they'd needed had been taken, and the quarry had been left to settle into itself, the way things did when no one maintained them.

Ryn climbed up behind him. She was breathing hard from the trail—it was steep, loose gravel over exposed rock, the kind of path that existed because people had walked it rather than because anyone had built it. She stopped a few paces back from the edge and put her hands on her knees.

"This is far enough from the city?"

"Should be."

She straightened and looked around. The quarry rim was a rough circle of open ground, flat enough, scattered with boulders that had been too small or too fractured to bother quarrying. Scrub grass grew between them in thin lines. The sky was wide and cloudless and the sun had been up long enough to warm the stone.

"All right," she said. "Show me."

He'd been thinking about it the whole walk up. Not planning, exactly—his body had been doing that without his permission, the reservoir pressing outward like a second heartbeat, eager in a way that had nothing to do with his mind. At the workshop, there had been structure. Fittings and pipe and Haran's careful questions. Here there was nothing but open ground and a friend who wouldn't flinch.

He started small. A rock the size of his fist, sitting in the grass three meters away. He reached for it with the reservoir—not his hands, just the channeling, pushing force through the air between them.

The rock lifted. Rose a meter off the ground and hung there, wobbling slightly. He could feel the inefficiency—the force bleeding outward, warming the air around the stone, a halo of wasted energy dissipating in all directions. He held it for a moment, then set it down.

"Bigger," Ryn said.

He looked at her. She was standing with her arms crossed, her head tilted, the expression she wore when she was assessing something—not impressed, not unimpressed, just taking it in.

He turned to a boulder. Waist-high, roughly cylindrical, the kind of loose stone that quarry work left behind. He planted his feet and pushed.

The boulder lifted. Not smoothly—it jerked upward, rotating as the force found its balance point. Chips of stone broke from its underside where it had been sitting in the ground. It rose to head height, then higher, then he was looking up at it and it was still climbing.

He threw it.

Not threw—launched. The force came out of him in a single pulse and the boulder left the quarry rim in a long arc, spinning, catching the sunlight on its surfaces. It cleared the far wall by ten meters and dropped into the gorge. Two seconds of silence. Then a sound like distant thunder, stone meeting stone, and a plume of dust rose from the gorge floor.

The silence afterward was different from ordinary silence. It had weight.

Ryn was staring at the gorge. Her arms had uncrossed somewhere during the throw. She turned to him. Her mouth opened, closed. Opened again.

"Do that again."

He did it again. A different boulder, larger—chest-high, rough-cut, the kind that took two people and a lever to shift. It rose faster this time. He threw it in a flatter trajectory, lower, and it hit the far wall of the quarry instead of clearing it. The impact cratered the rock face. Fragments sprayed outward and rattled down the wall in a cascade that lasted several seconds.

Dust hung in the air. The crater was visible—a shallow depression in the quarry wall, the stone around it fractured in radiating lines.

They looked at each other.

Then they were laughing. Not the kind of laughter that had a joke behind it—the other kind, the breathless, slightly unhinged kind that came from the body instead of the mind. Ryn sat down in the grass and covered her face with both hands and laughed into her palms. Ash stood at the quarry's edge with dust settling on his shoulders and laughed until something behind his ribs ached.

"Again," she said from behind her hands.

* * *

He worked through the quarry's loose stone in the next hour.

Not systematically—there was no system. He picked targets and moved them. Lifted them, hurled them, shattered them against the walls or sent them sailing into the gorge. The motions got easier as he stopped thinking about them. Early on, each throw required a kind of internal negotiation—reaching for the reservoir, gauging the output, directing it through the air—but by the tenth or twelfth, his body was doing the translation without consulting his mind. He pointed and pushed and the world moved.

The waste heat was constant. Each act bled warmth into the surrounding air—he could feel it on his skin, a radiant presence that ebbed and surged with every expenditure. Inefficient. He knew that. At the workshop, Haran would have winced at the loss. But here there was nothing to damage and no one to teach him and the reservoir behind his ribs hadn't shifted. He checked it the way you checked a pocket for your keys—a brief internal inventory, automatic. Full. Still full. The boulders he'd thrown, the craters he'd made, the raw force he'd poured into the morning—none of it registered as cost. The ocean hadn't noticed.

He tried sustained force. Pressed down on the ground with both palms flat against the stone, channeling into the rock beneath him. The surface cracked in a ring around his hands, then buckled, then dropped—a shallow crater forming under the pressure, the stone compressing and fracturing. The shockwave rippled outward through the ground. The scrub grass twenty meters away lay flat, pressed down in a radial pattern. Dust rose from between every stone on the quarry rim.

Ryn, sitting on a boulder she'd moved to after the first few throws, lifted her feet off the ground. She looked at the flattened grass. She looked at the crater.

"Maybe not that one," she said.

He stood up. His palms were gritty with stone dust. The crater was the size of a table, maybe a hand's width deep—shallow, but the fracture pattern around it extended further than he'd intended. He could see the stress lines in the stone, radiating outward like a web.

The air was warm. Not hot—warm, like standing near a fire that had been burning for an hour. All waste. All the energy that hadn't reached its target, bleeding into the atmosphere.

He tried something different. A focused push—not broad force, but a column of energy directed at a single point on the quarry wall. He stood at twenty meters and channeled.

The rock at the impact point glowed. Not immediately—first it darkened, a circle of compression where the force was concentrating, then the heat followed and the stone began to redden at the center. A moment later, a disc of rock the size of his head melted and ran. Liquid stone dripped down the quarry wall and cooled in long streaks.

He let go. The molten disc solidified, a dark glass circle on the quarry face, smooth and slightly reflective.

Ryn walked to the wall. She touched the glass with one finger—carefully, testing the heat—then flattened her palm against it. She turned around.

"You melted stone."

"I melted stone."

"You melted a wall."

"Part of a wall."

She leaned against it. Crossed her arms again. Her face was doing something complicated—not any single expression but a sequence of them, moving too fast to read, each one arriving and departing before it fully formed. She settled on something close to her normal face. Close, but not exact.

"How much did that cost you?"

It was Haran's question. The same question, the same words, asked in a different voice. Ash checked the reservoir.

"Nothing," he said.

She nodded. She looked at the glass disc behind her, then back at him.

"Right," she said. "Okay."

* * *

"Match me," Ryn said.

She had a stone in her hand. River-smooth, flat, the size of a plum—she'd picked it up from the quarry floor while he was catching his breath. She held it between her palms and closed her eyes.

Ash watched. He could see what she was doing from the outside—the slight tension in her shoulders, the steadiness of her breathing, the focused stillness that channeling required. A warmth rose from the stone, gradual and controlled. After ten seconds, Ryn opened her eyes and held the stone out.

He took it. Warm. Not hot—the temperature of skin that had been in sunlight, pleasant, alive-feeling. The warmth was even, distributed through the stone without gradient or concentration. Clean channeling. The kind of efficiency you got from someone who'd used her pool every day since childhood, who'd learned the feel of it the way you learned to walk, without memory of the learning.

"Your turn," she said. "Same thing. Just warm."

She picked up another stone and handed it to him.

He held it between his palms. Closed his eyes. Reached for the reservoir—

The stone cracked. A sharp sound, like a knuckle popping, and heat bloomed between his hands. He opened his eyes. The stone was in two pieces, a clean fracture through the center, both halves too hot to hold. He dropped them in the grass and shook his hands.

Ryn picked up one half with the hem of her sleeve. Turned it over. The fracture surface was slightly glazed—the stone had started to melt at the point of highest concentration before the thermal shock split it.

She set it down.

"So," she said.

"Yeah."

She picked up another stone. Handed it to him.

He held it more carefully this time. Let the reservoir rise to the surface—just the surface, the membrane Haran had taught him. He thought about the pipe in the workshop, the thread of warmth, the wire that hadn't smoked.

The stone warmed. Faster than Ryn's—much faster—and the warmth wasn't even. He could feel it concentrating at the points where his palms made contact, blooming outward from there. Too much. He pulled back, clamped down on the output—

The stone detonated.

Not dramatically—no explosion, no fragments flying. The stone simply came apart, fracturing along its grain into a handful of pieces that fell through his fingers like coarse sand. The pieces were hot. Some of them glowed faintly at their edges, a dull red that faded as they cooled in the grass.

They stood over the remains.

"Same thing," Ryn said. Her voice was flat. "Just warm."

And then they were laughing again. Ryn first—a short, sharp sound that she tried to hold back and couldn't—and then Ash, and then both of them, standing over a handful of glowing rock fragments in the quarry grass. But the laughter had a different texture than before. The boulder-throwing had been giddy, breathless, the shared amazement of two people watching the impossible. This was something else. A frequency underneath it that neither of them examined. The laughter of two people who had just measured a gap and found it wider than either of them had imagined.

Ryn's stone had glowed warm. His had disintegrated.

She sat down. She was still smiling but her hands were busy—picking grass, pulling it apart, the small repetitive motions of a body that needed to do something with the information it was processing.

"Try again," she said. She didn't pick up another stone.

* * *

The micro-pulse was harder out here.

In the workshop, he'd had the pipe—a thing to interact with, a material with properties his hands knew, a task that could be felt. Here there was stone and air and open sky and no system to engage with. The precision technique needed something to be precise *about*.

Ryn figured it out before he did.

"You need a target," she said. She was sitting cross-legged on the quarry floor, a dozen stones arranged in front of her in a rough line. "Not something to throw. Something to match."

She picked up a stone. Held it. Channeled—and the stone glowed, a faint warmth visible as a shimmer in the air above her palm. She held it steady.

"Match that."

He picked up a stone from her line. Held it. Tried to feel for the output she was producing—the scale of it, the volume—

Too much. The stone cracked.

She picked up another one. Held it. Same glow, same shimmer.

"Try something different," she said. "Don't think about small. Think about me. Pretend you've got what I've got. A normal pool, a normal day. Pretend you're tired from work and you've got enough left for this one thing and you need to be careful with it because there's dinner to heat and hands to warm and you can't afford to waste it."

He looked at her. There was limestone dust in her hair and she was looking at him with the directness that had been the most Ryn thing about her since they were children—the quality that didn't flinch, didn't soften, just said the thing in front of her for what it was.

He placed his palm against the stone.

*Pretend this is all you've got.*

He tried again. This time he felt the reservoir rise and clamped it before it reached his palms. Let just the leading edge through—a trickle, the thinnest stream he could manage.

The stone warmed. Cracked. But slower this time—the crack came after four or five seconds instead of instantly, and the halves weren't glazed. Just warm and broken.

"Closer," Ryn said.

She was good at this. Not the channeling—the coaching. She didn't explain or theorize or build frameworks the way Haran did. She just demonstrated and said *match me* and let the gap between their outputs be its own instruction. She had the practical directness of someone who'd learned by doing and taught the same way—show the thing, try the thing, adjust.

He went through a dozen stones. Each one broke, but the breaking changed. The first few shattered or split or glazed. By the sixth or seventh, they cracked—cleaner fractures, less heat, the excess energy dropping as he learned to feel for the ceiling of her output and press down toward it. By the tenth, one stone lasted nearly twenty seconds before a hairline fracture appeared along its equator.

"Hold it," Ryn said. She was watching his hands, not the stone. "You're tensing at the wrists. Let them go soft."

He let his wrists go soft. The reservoir shifted—not in quantity but in character, the output moving from a pressurized stream to something broader, less concentrated. The stone in his hands was warm. It stayed warm. Five seconds. Ten.

Fifteen.

The hairline appeared. He let go before it propagated.

Ryn picked up the stone. Examined the fracture—thin, clean, barely visible. She held it next to the stone she'd warmed with her own channeling.

Hers was intact, warm, glowing faintly with even heat.

His was cracked but whole. Warm. Not glowing—the output had been close enough to hers that the stone hadn't reached visible heat.

"Better," she said.

He thought about Haran, saying the same word over a coil of wire that hadn't smoked. The direction, not the destination.

"I can't get it smaller," he said. "I can feel where your output is. I can feel the shape of it. But the minimum I can do is still—" He gestured at the cracked stone.

"Three or four times too much."

"At least."

Ryn set both stones down side by side. Her warm, whole stone and his warm, cracked one. She looked at them for a moment.

"You know what's strange," she said. She didn't phrase it as a question.

He waited.

"I spent my whole pool on that stone. Everything I have for the day." She flexed her hand—the slight wince of a young channeler who'd emptied her reserve, the tiredness that settled into the tendons. "And you cracked yours with less than you can feel."

She said it matter-of-factly. The way she said things—without performance, without editorializing. A measurement taken and reported.

He looked at her hand. The tendons standing out, the slight curl of her fingers. She'd given everything she had to make a stone glow warm, and the effort had taken something from her—not dramatically, not dangerously, but perceptibly. She'd feel it for the rest of the day. She'd sleep tonight and refill and tomorrow she'd have the same amount to spend again.

He'd given less than he could measure and shattered three stones.

"You know what's strange," she said. She was looking at the gorge, not at him. "You've always been bad at the big stuff. The channeling, the magic—you couldn't do any of it. And I was the one who could. I warmed the cups. I lit the path. A thousand small things you couldn't do and I didn't think about."

"I thought about them."

"I know you did." She looked at him. "The point is, now you can do all of that. You can do it a thousand times over. You can do it until a quarry looks like this." She gestured at the ruined landscape below. "And the thing that's genuinely hard for you—the thing you're still working on—is the thing I do every day. The normal amount. The regular human dose."

She said it plainly, the way she said everything. No sentiment. Just the observation, set down like a tool on a workbench.

He felt something shift in his chest. Not the reservoir—something older and smaller. The feeling of hearing a truth that had been in the room for hours, waiting.

"Come on," she said. She stood up, brushing grass from her legs. "You've got more rocks to break."

* * *

The afternoon stretched. Shadows moved across the quarry floor.

He kept practicing. Ryn sat on her boulder and watched—she had nothing left to channel, her pool spent on the demonstration, but she stayed. She handed him stones when he ran out. She told him when his wrists were tensing. She said "closer" and "too much" and "hold it" with the steady patience of someone who had decided to be useful and was being useful and didn't need the usefulness to mean anything beyond itself.

The precision came in increments so small he couldn't feel them arriving. Each stone lasted a little longer than the last. Each crack was a little finer. The waste heat in the air dropped—not to nothing, not to anything close to nothing, but from a constant radiant warmth to something intermittent, pulsing with his attempts rather than bleeding continuously.

He was getting closer. Not close—closer. The distinction mattered and he knew it.

Between stones, he threw things. Not for practice—for the feeling. He'd pick up a boulder and hurl it into the gorge because his arms were tired of being still and the reservoir was pressing against his ribs and the sheer physical joy of moving something enormous was a thing he hadn't known his body could feel. The sounds were extraordinary—the rush of displaced air, the impact, the secondary crashes of fragments. Each throw was a conversation with physics that he was winning.

"Watch this," he said.

He'd found a slab of quarry stone, flat and broad, half-buried in the ground. He worked it loose with force—channeling into the earth around it, breaking the soil's grip—and lifted it. It was the size of a door. It weighed more than he did, more than both of them. It hung in the air three meters up, tilting slightly as he adjusted the balance point.

He spun it. Channeled a rotational force at its edge and the slab began to turn, slowly at first, then faster, the stone blurring at its edges. Chips flew off. The air around it hummed—a low, throbbing sound, the vibration of something massive moving through space faster than it should.

He let it go. The slab flew like a discus, spinning flat, and hit the quarry wall with a sound that was less an impact than a detonation. Stone dust filled the air. The wall cracked—a wide, branching fracture that ran from the impact point to the rim.

He turned to Ryn. Dust in his hair, on his shoulders, the particular grit of pulverized stone. He was breathing hard but not from effort—from the feeling. His hands were open at his sides and the reservoir pulsed behind them, steady and full and available.

"Watch this," he said again.

Ryn watched.

She was sitting on her boulder with her knees drawn up, her chin resting on her arms. Her face was still in the way that Ryn's face got still when she was thinking—not blank, not closed, just very present. She was looking at him. Not at the wall he'd cratered or the slab he'd shattered or the dust hanging in the air. At him.

"You're having a good day," she said.

"The best day." He meant it. Every part of his body felt correct—his hands, his arms, the muscles of his back and shoulders, all of them aligned with something they'd been built to do and had never known it. The reservoir was full and his body was its instrument and the world responded to him the way it had never responded before. Twenty-three years of not being able to do the things everyone else did without thinking, twenty-three years of working around a gap that sat at the center of his life, and now the gap was gone and in its place was this—this capacious, inexhaustible ability to act on the world and have the world answer.

He picked up another stone. Not a boulder—a flat piece, palm-sized.

"Watch this."

He held it in front of him and channeled warmth. Carefully. The stone glowed—brighter than Ryn's stone had glowed, but not by as much as before. The heat was concentrated in the center, spreading outward in a gradient instead of erupting everywhere at once. He held it. Five seconds. Ten. Fifteen. Twenty.

The stone cracked. A single clean line through the center, thin as a hair.

He looked at it. Looked at Ryn. Held up the cracked stone like an offering.

"Almost," he said. His voice was full of something that didn't have a name—not pride exactly, not triumph. Something simpler. The sound a body made when it was doing what it was supposed to do.

"Almost," Ryn said.

* * *

The sun was low when they stopped.

The quarry looked different. The walls were cratered and scored. Glass discs from his focused burns caught the late light and threw it back in unexpected angles. The ground was churned—craters, shattered stone, the prints of boulders that had been moved or lifted or launched. The scrub grass was flattened in radiating patterns around his impact points. It looked like something had happened here that shouldn't have been possible.

Both things true.

They sat on the quarry rim with their legs hanging over the edge. The gorge below was deeper in shadow than in light, the evening moving up from the bottom. Ash's boots knocked gently against the rock face. His arms ached—not from channeling but from the hours of physical motion, the throwing and lifting and reaching. His body had been working even when the reservoir did the heavy labor.

Ryn sat beside him. Close enough that their shoulders almost touched. She'd been quiet for a while—not the silence of someone who had nothing to say but the silence of someone who had decided not to say it. Her hands were in her lap, fingers interlaced.

"Ryn."

"Mm."

"Thanks for coming."

She didn't answer immediately. The gorge below them was filling with blue shadow, the last direct light retreating up the far wall.

"Where else would I be," she said. It wasn't a question. She said it the way she said practical things—here's the fact, take it or leave it. But there was something under it that she didn't lift to the surface, and he didn't reach for.

They sat. The stone was still warm under them—residual heat from his afternoon of waste, the quarry rim holding the energy he'd bled into it. Ryn's hands were still in her lap. Her pool was empty—she'd spent it hours ago on a single warm stone—and the emptiness was in her posture, the particular tiredness of a body that had given what it had and was waiting for sleep to refill it.

He wasn't tired. The reservoir was full. After an entire afternoon of hurling boulders and melting walls and shattering stones—after hours of raw, extravagant, wildly inefficient channeling—he was exactly where he'd started. The ocean hadn't noticed.

He thought about telling her that. Thought about saying the number, making the comparison—her day spent on a single stone, his afternoon that hadn't registered. He thought about the comedy of it, the absurdity, the two of them sitting side by side with their different emptinesses.

He didn't say it. Something in the way she was sitting told him not to.

"I can almost do it," he said instead. "The precision thing. Haran's technique."

"Almost."

"Another session. Maybe two. I can feel where it needs to go—my hands know the shape. It's just calibration. Getting the output small enough."

She nodded. The light was going and her face was half in shadow, the angles of her jaw and cheekbone catching what remained. She was looking at the gorge.

"You'll get it," she said.

"Yeah?"

"You'll get it because you'll keep trying it until you get it. That's what you do."

It was a generous thing to say. It was also a precise thing to say—she knew him, knew the way he worked at problems, the stubbornness that had built calluses on his hands and kept him at Haran's workbench for years when anyone reasonable would have found something else to do with a life that didn't include channeling.

He wanted to say something back. Something that matched what she'd given him—not the words but the quality of them, the steadiness, the way she'd spent her entire afternoon sitting on a rock in a quarry handing him stones and telling him his wrists were tight and saying *closer, closer* without any indication that she'd rather be anywhere else.

"Ryn."

"Mm."

The thing he wanted to say was too large for the space between them and too fragile for the quarry air and he didn't have the right shape for it yet. He could feel it in his chest—not the reservoir, something else, something the channeling couldn't reach and the power couldn't move.

"Same time next week?" he said.

She looked at him. Something moved across her face—not a smile, not the absence of a smile. Something that lived in the space between those two things and didn't resolve into either one.

"Sure," she said. "Bring your own stones."

He laughed. She almost laughed. The almost was a hairline fracture—invisible, structural, the kind of crack you couldn't see until you knew where to look. Then she did—a small sound, expelled, as if the laugh had been sitting in her chest and she'd decided to let it go rather than hold it any longer.

They sat in the cooling quarry as the light left. His body was warm from the reservoir and hers was cooling in the evening air and neither of them moved to close the space between their shoulders. Below them, the gorge filled with shadow. Above them, the sky held onto its blue.

"Watch this," he said. One more time.

He held up his hand. Palm open, facing the sky. He let the reservoir rise—just to the surface, the thinnest membrane—and channeled light. Not heat, not force. Light. A small, pale glow in the center of his palm, barely brighter than the fading sky. He held it there. Steady. Controlled. The waste heat was almost nothing—he could feel a faint warmth on his wrist but the air around his hand stayed cool. The glow didn't flicker.

He held it for thirty seconds. A minute. The sky darkened around it.

It was the smallest thing he'd done all day. It was also the most difficult. The output was so low that the reservoir barely recognized the demand—a whisper when it wanted to shout. His hands trembled with the effort of restraint.

Ryn looked at the light in his palm. Her face in its glow was close and clear—the lines of tiredness around her eyes, the particular set of her mouth that meant she was holding something back. She looked at the light the way she'd looked at the boulders and the craters and the melted wall: not performing awe, not withholding it. Seeing it for what it was.

She reached out and closed his hand. Her fingers folded over his, pressing his palm shut, and the light went out. Her hand was cool against his. She held it for a moment—one breath, two—and then let go.

"That's the one," she said.

He looked at his closed hand. The reservoir pressed behind it, vast and patient.

He looked at Ryn. She was standing up, brushing off her legs, turning toward the trail down. Her shoulders were set and her hands were quick and her face was already pointed at the walk home, the path, the next thing.

"Coming?" she said.

He stood. The quarry was dark now, the craters and glass discs and shattered stone reduced to shapes in the falling light. Tomorrow the sun would come up and the damage would be visible and someone might wonder what had happened here. Or no one would come, and the quarry would hold it without being asked.

He followed Ryn down the trail. The path was dark and loose underfoot and she walked it without hesitating, her feet finding the ground the way feet found familiar ground. He could have lit the way. He could have flooded the trail with light brighter than noon. He could have moved the loose stones, smoothed the path, lifted them both and carried them down in a column of force.

He walked behind her in the dark. His feet found the stones. His hands found the scrub brush at the trail's edge when he needed balance. The reservoir was full and his body was tired and somewhere behind him the quarry held the evidence of the best afternoon of his life.

Ahead of him, Ryn walked. She didn't look back.

She didn't need to. She knew he was there. She'd always known where he was—the specific awareness of someone who'd grown up beside you, who knew your stride, your breathing, the sound your boots made on gravel. She knew he was there the way she knew the path was under her feet.

He watched her walk. The shape of her shoulders against the darkening sky, the particular rhythm of her stride—quick, certain, not quite graceful but entirely competent. He'd been watching her walk since they were children. He knew this walk.

He was watching her walk away from something, and the something was him.

Not tonight. Not on this trail, where they were going the same direction and their boots crunched the same gravel and the city's lights were beginning to appear at the plateau's edge. But in some other sense. In the sense that every time he threw a boulder or melted a wall or held light in his palm without effort, the distance between what he was and what she was stretched a little further, and neither of them knew how to talk about the stretching, and both of them could feel it.

She'd spent her whole pool to warm a stone.

He'd spent nothing to shatter the world.

Both things true. Both things sitting in the space between their shoulders where the gap was exactly the width it had always been and also infinite and also nothing.

The city's lights grew brighter as they descended. The trail leveled out. The paving started—uneven, slightly heaved, the kind of paving that existed in the districts where maintenance was a negotiation rather than a given. Ryn's boots hit the stone with a different sound and her stride changed and she was back in her world, the world of daily pools and careful spending and shifts at whatever infrastructure job kept her hands busy and her heating on.

"Same time next week," she said. Not a question.

"Same time next week."

She turned left at the first junction. Her street. She raised one hand without turning around—a wave, or a goodbye, or a gesture that meant something more specific than either of those and that she didn't elaborate on.

He stood at the junction. The street was dim—district lighting, the kind that cost someone's evening channeling to maintain. A man crossed the road carrying a bundle. A window was lit three stories up, warm yellow, the kind of warmth that came from someone spending part of their daily pool on comfort.

Ryn's shape diminished down the street. Smaller. Still certain. Still walking with the stride that didn't need to look back.

He turned right. Toward the Third Terrace, the transit lift, the apartment where Maren's calibrated heating lattice would be holding its steady warmth. The reservoir pressed against his ribs—patient, enormous, unmoved by everything he'd done and everything he'd felt and the particular quality of a hand closing his and the light going out.

The cracked gear casing from Haran's workshop was still in his coat pocket. He touched it through the fabric. Small, hard, broken in a specific way. He carried it home.
