# Chapter 18

The wall chart had grown.

Ash stood in the doorway of Dorenne's operations room and studied the fourth panel, the blue one, the reconstruction plan. Someone on her staff had added a second layer of markings since the last time he'd been here. Red hash marks now crossed each blue deployment site, each one annotated with a number. He'd been seeing these numbers for weeks without reading them. Now he read them.

They were energy estimates. Rough, approximate, calculated from the work he'd already done: how long he'd spent at each site, what he'd accomplished, what the measurable outputs had been. Dorenne's staff couldn't see the reservoir. Nobody could. But they'd been watching him work, timing him, measuring the results, and building a model backward from the evidence. The numbers on the chart were their best guesses at what each deployment cost him.

He stepped closer. The hash marks ran in columns down the panel's right side. Each column was a week. Three districts per week in the first phase of reconstruction. Four in the second. The columns stretched forward across months, each one filled with numbers that added up to a sum he hadn't been calculating.

Dorenne's handwriting at the bottom of the panel, in small precise letters: *Cumulative estimated expenditure (weeks 1–6): see attached.*

He didn't look for the attachment.

* * *

The morning deployment was in the Ashward district, a name that made him flinch, because it hadn't been called that before the earthquake. The residents had renamed it. He'd reinforced three buildings there during the crisis response, and apparently that was enough to rename a neighborhood.

The work itself was straightforward. A retaining wall at the district's northern edge had developed stress fractures during the quake, hairline cracks running through the channeled stone like veins in a leaf, each one a failure point waiting for the next tremor. He knelt at the base of the wall, pressed his palms flat against the stone, and pushed the reservoir through.

The cracks sealed. The stone hummed, dense and warm under his hands, the reinforcement spreading outward from his contact points the way heat spread through metal. He held the channeling for fifteen minutes, moving along the wall's length, finding each fracture by feel and closing it. Clean work. Efficient. More efficient than he would have been a month ago. His control was better. His waste was lower. He could feel the difference in the way the reservoir flowed, directed rather than flooded.

He pulled his hands away and stood. The wall was solid. A generation's worth of stability, at least.

The crew chief marked it on her clipboard. Ash watched her write the number: a duration, a date, a shorthand note he couldn't read from this distance. Another hash mark for Dorenne's chart.

He moved to the second site. A water conduit junction, two streets south. He knelt at the manifold and pressed his palms flat. For a moment, just a moment, he tried the other way. Haran's way. The thin thread, the nudge at the point of least resistance. His fingers found the fracture line and he pushed a micro-pulse at the narrowest point, the way Haran would have nudged a valve seat.

The pulse came through too wide. Not catastrophically; he could feel the shape of what Haran did, the precision that made force look like luck. But his hands defaulted to deployment scale before the pulse was half-formed. He let the reservoir take over, sealed the fractures the usual way, and moved on. Twenty minutes. The crew chief followed, clipboard in hand.

Third site. A bridge support in the lower terrace, where the foot traffic of reconstruction supply wagons had widened a crack that predated the earthquake. Fifteen minutes. Another mark on the clipboard.

He finished by midmorning. Three sites, three deployments, each one a withdrawal from an account he'd never bothered to check. The reservoir sat in his chest, warm and vast, but the quality of it had shifted again since the night on the overlook. The ghost of geometry he'd felt on the terrace overlook was clearer now. Not walls, exactly. Not edges he could touch or measure. But the sense that the reservoir had a shape, that it existed inside something rather than simply existing, was stronger than it had been a week ago. Stronger than it had been yesterday.

He reached for the feeling. Tried to hold it.

It didn't slip away this time. It stayed, faint, distant, like a sound at the edge of hearing. The reservoir was there. It was vast. But it was *contained*. It had a floor somewhere below him, too deep to see, too far to measure, but present in a way that floors hadn't been before.

He let the feeling go. Not because it dissolved, but because he chose to stop looking.

There was a fourth site on the schedule. He checked the deployment list Dorenne's aide had given him that morning, a folded sheet of heavy paper, sites and addresses and estimated durations. The fourth site was the eastern retaining wall, a major structural element that protected the lower districts from slope failure. Priority: high. Estimated duration: two hours.

He folded the list and put it in his pocket and walked south instead.

* * *

The tinkerer district, what remained of it, sat in a bowl between the middle and lower terraces, a cluster of workshops and small homes built around a shared courtyard that had cracked during the earthquake and been patched by the residents themselves. The repairs were rough. Mortar and salvaged stone, braced with wooden supports, held together by determination and the modest pools of the people who lived there. It would hold. It wouldn't hold forever.

Ash had been here before the Wellspring. Before Dorenne. Before any of it. Haran's workshop was three streets east, but the tinkerer district was larger than Haran, a community of people who built things with their hands and their minds and whatever magic they could spare. Mechanics, inventors, repair workers. The people who fixed what channeling couldn't reach and built what channeling couldn't build.

He found the workshop he was looking for on the district's southern edge. A small building, two rooms, a front workspace and a back storage area, the kind of place where a person spent more time than they spent at home. The front wall had cracked during the earthquake. A diagonal fracture ran from the foundation to the window lintel, wide enough to see daylight through, held together by a wooden brace that was already bowing under the strain.

A woman sat on a stool inside, working at a bench despite the crack. She was older, middle generation, maybe early forties, with short gray-streaked hair and hands that were stained with oil and metal filings. She was assembling something small and intricate, a mechanism Ash couldn't identify from the doorway. She looked up when his shadow fell across the threshold.

"Door's open," she said. Then she saw him, and her hands went still.

"The wall," Ash said. "Can I?"

She stared at him. Looked at the crack. Looked back at him. "You're—"

"I was in the district. I saw the brace."

She set down the mechanism. Stood. Wiped her hands on her apron, a gesture that had nothing to do with cleanliness and everything to do with needing something to do with her hands. "It's on the list," she said. "The Director's schedule. We were told third month, maybe fourth."

"I'm here now."

She stepped back. He knelt at the base of the wall, pressed his hands to the cracked stone, and pushed the reservoir through.

The fracture sealed in seconds. The stone fused, the brace became unnecessary, the wall solidified under his palms with the clean hum of channeled reinforcement that would last decades. He reinforced the foundation while he was there; it was compromised too, the old channeling thinned by time and weakened by the quake. He pushed deeper, found the original work, layered his own force over it. The building settled. Tightened. Became what it had been when it was built, or better.

He stood. The woman was watching him with an expression he'd seen before: gratitude layered over disbelief layered over something more complicated. He'd just done in thirty seconds what her daily pool couldn't accomplish in a year.

"The mechanism," he said, nodding at the bench. "What is it?"

She blinked. "It's—a valve assembly. For a water filter. Mechanical filtration, no channeling required." She paused. "It's a project. Not important."

"It's important."

She looked at him for a long time. "Thank you," she said. "For the wall."

He left. Walked two streets north, found a house with a sagging lintel, a home, not a workshop, the kind of place where a family lived and ate and slept. The damage was old. Not from the earthquake. Just age and weak channeling and the slow accumulation of deferred maintenance that defined the lower districts. He pressed his hands to the lintel and reinforced it. Thirty seconds. The stone straightened, the door frame squared, the sag vanished.

A man opened the door. Ash was already walking away.

Small things. Unstrategic. Each one a withdrawal he could feel now, not as depletion, not as loss, but as presence. Each time he channeled, he was aware of the reservoir in a way he hadn't been before. Aware of its size. Aware that size was a number, and numbers had limits, and limits meant edges.

He pushed the awareness down and kept working.

* * *

Dorenne found him at midday.

He was sitting on a low wall in the tinkerer district, eating bread he'd bought from a cart, not the communal kitchen bread from Sera and Tereth, just ordinary bread, dense and dark. The deployment list was in his pocket, the fourth site uncompleted. The eastern retaining wall waited, unrepaired, protecting four thousand people with the same cracks it had carried since the earthquake.

Dorenne crossed the courtyard with the particular stride she used when she was containing something. Not anger; Dorenne didn't do anger. Precision under pressure. She stopped three paces from him and stood with her hands clasped behind her back.

"The eastern wall," she said.

"I know."

"Verin's crew—the eastern wall team—has been waiting since ten."

"I'll go this afternoon."

"You were supposed to be there this morning." She let that sit. Not accusatory. Factual. "Where were you?"

"Here." He gestured at the district around them. "The tinkerer workshop on the south edge had a cracked wall. The house on Colter Street—"

"I know which buildings you repaired. The crew chiefs report to me." She unclasped her hands and pulled a folded paper from her jacket, a list, shorter than his, written in her own hand. "Two sites. Neither on the deployment schedule. Neither priority-flagged."

"They needed it."

"Everything needs it." She unfolded the paper. He could see the writing from where he sat: building addresses, durations, the same shorthand the crew chiefs used. She'd already calculated what he'd spent. "The workshop wall. The lintel on Colter Street." She folded the paper again. "Roughly five minutes of active channeling. At your current efficiency, that's—" She paused, not for drama but for accuracy. "That's not negligible, Ash. Time and output you could have given the eastern wall."

He looked at her. The bread was still in his hand. He set it down on the wall beside him.

"Those were homes," he said. "Workshops. Places where people live and work."

"The eastern retaining wall protects four thousand people." She said it without emphasis, without judgment. A fact placed beside a fact. "The slope behind it shifted three centimeters during the earthquake. If it fails, the entire lower-east district slides. Every home in that district. Every workshop." She held his gaze. "You could have been there an hour earlier."

The silence between them was the kind that had weight. Not hostile. Not warm. The silence of two people who were both right and who both knew it.

"I'll go this afternoon," he said again.

"Ash." She stepped closer. Lowered her voice, not because anyone was listening, but because the register of the conversation had changed. "I'm not telling you what to do. I am telling you what things cost. You don't have unlimited time. Neither does the city."

She turned and walked back across the courtyard. Straight spine. Steady stride. The folded paper back in her jacket, the list she carried everywhere, the accounting of his days.

He sat on the wall and looked at the bread he'd stopped eating. After a while, he picked it up and finished it. It tasted like nothing.

* * *

He went to the eastern retaining wall. Spent two hours with his palms pressed to the stone, channeling reinforcement into a structure that held the weight of a hillside and four thousand lives. The wall accepted his force the way old channeled stone always did: grudgingly at first, then with a resonance that felt almost like gratitude, the ancient work recognizing the new work layered over it.

The crew chief timed him. Noted the duration on her clipboard. Another number for Dorenne's chart.

Afterward, he didn't go back to the command post. He walked downhill, through the middle terraces and into the lower districts, and then kept walking, past the neighborhoods he'd reinforced, past the cleared lots and the repaired buildings and the transit shafts that ran again because of him. He walked until the reconstruction thinned and the streets were just streets, ordinary and unimproved, the Solathis that existed before the earthquake and would exist after the reconstruction ended.

He stopped at a terrace overlook. Below, the city spread in the late-afternoon light, the patchwork of repaired and unrepaired, green and amber on Dorenne's maps. The reservoir sat in his chest. Warm. Present.

Smaller.

Not by much. Not by any amount he could measure or name. But the sense of it had changed again. Not the ghost of edges now, but something more solid. The reservoir had a shape, and the shape had dimensions, and the dimensions were not infinite. He could feel the floor the way you could feel the bottom of a deep lake when you dove far enough—not touching it, not close to touching it, but knowing it was there. Knowing the water ended somewhere.

The thought surfaced and he let it sit. He'd known this. Dorenne had said it in the Council chamber: "unprecedented but not infinite." The examiners had hedged. Haran had asked, months ago, a question about cost that Ash had shrugged away. Everyone had been telling him, and he'd listened, and he'd heard, and he hadn't believed. Because the reservoir had felt like an ocean, and oceans didn't have floors. That was what made them oceans.

But this ocean had a floor. And he could feel it now, every day, a little closer than the day before. Not because the floor was rising but because the water was going down.

He stood at the overlook and watched the city until the light changed. Then he turned and walked east, toward the district where he'd grown up.

* * *

The house looked the same. Smaller than he remembered; it always looked smaller now, every time he came back, as if the building itself was retreating from him or he was outgrowing it. The front step had a new crack. He noticed it the way he noticed every crack now, cataloging it automatically, calculating the repair cost without meaning to. Trivial. Five seconds of channeling. He could fix it without breaking stride.

He left it alone.

His mother opened the door before he knocked. She'd seen him from the window, the kitchen window that overlooked the street, and she spent her evenings there, he knew, the way she'd always spent her evenings there, watching the neighborhood settle into dark. She stood in the doorway and looked at him, and for a moment her face did the thing it always did when she saw him: the flash of fierce, protective attention, the quick scan—is he eating, is he sleeping, is he whole. Then she caught herself. Pulled the fierceness back behind something more careful. Something she'd learned to wear since the Wellspring, since the earthquake, since her son became too important for a mother to fuss over.

"Ashlyn," she said. "Come in."

The kitchen smelled like root broth, the thick, peppery kind she made in winter, though the cold season hadn't properly arrived. She made it because she always had. Because the recipe was her mother's, and her mother's mother's, and the kitchen was the one room in the house where time moved the way she wanted it to.

He sat at the table. The same table. The same chairs.

His mother moved to the stove. On the counter beside it, a kettle steamed. The old one. Dented tin, the handle wrapped with cord where the original grip had worn through. He recognized it the way he recognized his own hands. That kettle had heated every cup of tea he'd drunk in this house for twenty years. The dent on its left side was from the time Maren had knocked it off the counter as a child. The cord on the handle was his father's repair, careful and functional, the work of a man who fixed things because things were worth fixing.

The copper kettle was in the cabinet. He could see its edge through the gap in the cabinet door: bright, expensive, the kettle he'd bought with his first real salary from Dorenne's office. A gift. An apology dressed as generosity. *I can afford things now. I can give you things. I am not a drain anymore.*

His mother was using the tin one.

She poured two cups. Set one in front of him. The tea was dark, oversteeped the way she always made it, and the cup was the ceramic one with the chip on the rim that he'd drunk from since he was twelve. She sat across from him and held her own cup and didn't drink.

"You look thin," she said.

"I'm eating."

"You look thin," she said again, as if repetition could make him hear it differently. "When did you last sit down for a meal?"

He couldn't remember. The deployment days blurred together: bread eaten standing, water between sites, the body forgotten in the work.

He drank the tea. Too bitter, too hot. He drank it anyway.

"How is the work?" she asked. The question had a particular shape, careful, general, designed to not presume. She didn't ask about the reconstruction, about Dorenne, about the Council or the districts or the thousands of people who depended on him. She asked about *the work*, as if it were a job at a shop, as if he came home with sawdust on his clothes and stories about difficult customers.

"It's good," he said. "Busy."

"You're sleeping?"

"Enough."

She nodded. Lifted her cup and blew across the surface, a gesture so familiar it ached, the particular angle of her wrist, the small O of her mouth. She'd done this every evening of his life. She'd been doing it before he was born. She would do it after he left tonight, alone at this table, with the tin kettle and the chipped cup and the root broth simmering on the stove.

*She kept the old kettle.*

He looked at it again. Dented, cord-wrapped, steaming on the counter. The copper one in the cabinet, unused. Not rejected; she would never reject something he'd given her. Set aside. Preserved behind a cabinet door the way you preserved something that meant something different from what was intended. He'd bought it as proof he could provide. She'd kept the tin one because the tin one was hers.

The front door opened. His father came in, carrying a canvas bag, tools from the sound of them, the metallic clink of wrenches and files. He was a maintenance worker, lower-terrace infrastructure, the kind of work that used your body because your pool wasn't enough to carry the load. He'd done it for thirty years. His hands showed it: thick-knuckled, scarred across the palms, the hands of a man who gripped things for a living.

"Ash." His father set the bag down and clasped his shoulder. The grip was the same as always, firm, held a beat too long, as if he were checking that his son was solid. "Didn't know you were coming."

"I was in the district."

His father sat at the table. His mother poured a third cup without being asked, the choreography of a household, decades of practice compressed into gesture. His father wrapped both hands around the cup and sighed the sigh of a man whose body was tired and whose chair was exactly where it should be.

"Passed by Comyn's place on the way home," his father said. "That shelf you built, the one in his workshop. Still standing."

Ash looked at him. "That was years ago."

"Seven. He had a load of iron stock fall on it during the quake. Shelf held. The brackets bent but the joints didn't crack." His father sipped his tea with the satisfaction of a man delivering good news. "He told me to tell you. Said you'd want to know."

Ash remembered the shelf. Haran's workshop, a rainy afternoon, sixteen years old and looking for something to do with his hands because he couldn't do anything with his pool. Comyn, Haran's neighbor, another tinkerer, an old man with a workshop full of scrap metal and a patience for teaching that bordered on stubbornness, had needed a shelf for his stock. Ash had built it. Mortise and tenon joints, no channeling, no reinforcement, just wood and precision and a boy who wanted to prove he could make something that stood.

Seven years. A load of iron. An earthquake. The shelf held.

"Good joints," his father said. "You always had good joints."

His mother was watching him. He could feel it without looking, the quality of her attention, the way she held herself when she was choosing not to speak. Her hands were tight around her cup. Her mouth was set in the line that meant she was holding something back, something that lived in the way she looked at his hands, at the shelf, at the distance between the boy who had built it and the man sitting at her table.

She sipped her tea and let the silence carry what she couldn't.

"Maren working tonight?" Ash asked.

His mother's expression shifted, not much, a tightening around the eyes. "She's been picking up extra shifts. Maintenance work, the middle districts. They're shorthanded since the earthquake."

He hadn't known that. He hadn't asked, before now. Maren, working extra maintenance shifts, spending her pool, her small and precise and diminishing pool, covering for crews that were stretched thin because the infrastructure demanded more channeling than the available workers could provide. Doing the math of her own capacity every day, the way everyone in her generation did, the way everyone except him did.

The thought caught and held. He sat with it—Maren's pool against his reservoir. Her daily budget, rationed down to the last useful drop, stretched across shifts and repairs and the quiet maintenance that nobody thanked her for. His ocean, with its new geometry of edges. She spent what an elder produced in a day and called it a full shift. He spent what an elder produced in a year and called it Tuesday.

The disproportion was grotesque. He'd known it intellectually, had known it since the activation, since the numbers started. But knowing it as arithmetic and feeling it as his sister's hands flexing in the dark were different things. She came home tired the way he came home tired, except her tired meant the well was dry and his tired meant he'd used a cupful of the sea.

He pushed the thought away. Not gently—with the practiced force of a person who had learned that some comparisons, followed to their conclusion, made the work impossible.

"She'll be sorry she missed you," his mother said.

He didn't ask what time she'd be home. He didn't ask which districts. He looked at the table, at the cup, at the dent in the tin kettle visible from where he sat. His mind was doing the thing it always did now: calculating. Not the shelf, not the kettle, not his mother's careful silence. Dorenne's chart. The hash marks. The columns stretching forward across months. The numbers that added up to a sum he could almost feel in his chest, the way he could almost feel the floor of the reservoir.

*How much did the tinkerer workshop cost? The lintel on Colter Street? The stairway, the garden wall, the second workshop? Twelve minutes. A quarter of the eastern retaining wall. Four thousand people.*

He set down the cup. "I should go."

His mother nodded. She didn't argue. There was a time—before—when she would have. When she would have insisted on dinner, on an hour more, on the particular necessity of a mother feeding her son. That time was gone. She'd learned the new choreography. The son who came and left on a schedule she couldn't see, who belonged to a city that needed him more than she did, who sat at her table and drank her tea and thought about something else.

She walked him to the door. His father stayed at the table, cup in hand, watching him go with the expression he always wore when Ash left: pride and a question, tangled together, neither one resolved.

At the door, his mother touched his arm. Quick, light—the way you'd touch something you weren't sure you were allowed to touch. "Come back," she said. "When you can."

He stepped into the street. The evening air was cool, carrying the smell of someone's dinner from an open window, the ordinary smell of an ordinary street. Behind him, the door closed. He could picture her: returning to the kitchen, to the counter, to the tin kettle that was still warm. Standing at the window to watch him walk away. Holding herself still until he turned the corner, because if she moved, she might follow, and she'd learned not to follow.

He walked. The street was dark; this district's light channeling was maintained by residents, and the residents had less to give every year. He passed houses where windows glowed warm and houses where they didn't. The gradient was familiar. It was the gradient of the whole city, the whole world, the slow narrowing of what was available measured in lit windows and dark ones.

The reservoir sat in his chest. He could feel its edges.

* * *

The next morning, he arrived at the command post before Dorenne.

Her operations room was empty at this hour; the staff wouldn't arrive for another thirty minutes. The four panels stood at the front of the room, the crisis maps and the reconstruction plan, the blue sites and the red hash marks. He crossed to the fourth panel and stood in front of it.

The numbers were clearer in the early light. Each hash mark: a site, a date, a duration, an estimated energy cost. The estimates were crude; Dorenne's staff were guessing, extrapolating from observable outputs, building a model from the outside because nobody could see the reservoir from within. But they were in the right range. He could feel that. The proportions made sense. The bigger deployments—the retaining walls, the transit shafts, the deep structural work—were marked with larger numbers. The smaller ones, water junctions, building reinforcements, the routine tightening of the city's bones, were marked with smaller numbers. The model was rough, but it was honest.

He followed the columns forward. Weeks one through six: completed. The hash marks filled in, each one a site visited, a deployment finished, a withdrawal made. The cumulative numbers at the bottom of each column climbed in a progression that looked, from a certain angle, like a countdown.

Weeks seven through twelve: planned. Blue sites without hash marks, waiting for him. Three districts per week in the early columns. Four in the later ones. The deployments were getting denser as the reconstruction expanded: more sites, more structural work, more of the deep foundation reinforcement that cost the most and lasted the longest.

He scanned forward. Weeks thirteen through twenty. Twenty-one through twenty-eight. The plan stretched across months, each column a week, each week a set of deployments, each deployment a number. The numbers were getting larger as the work moved from surface repairs to deep infrastructure: the retaining walls, the terrace anchoring, the bones of the city that hadn't been properly maintained in a generation.

He stood in front of the chart and did what he'd been avoiding.

He added.

Not formally. Not with paper and calculations and the precise methodology he'd use if he sat down to do it properly. Just the rough arithmetic of a mind that had always been good with numbers, a mind that had spent twenty years optimizing around constraints, counting coins, measuring portions, calculating how to make not-enough into just-barely-enough. The same mind, applied to a different resource.

The early weeks: high expenditure. Inefficient. He'd been learning, flooding where he should have pulsed, spending three units of force to accomplish one unit of work. The waste heat alone had warmed entire rooms. The numbers on the chart reflected this: larger estimates for smaller outputs, the profile of a novice channeler doing elder-scale work with untrained hands.

The middle weeks: better. His efficiency had improved. The numbers showed it: similar outputs at lower estimated costs. He was learning to channel the way a journeyman learned to use a lathe, through repetition and attention and the gradual elimination of waste.

The earthquake weeks: enormous. Two columns of hash marks so dense they overlapped, the numbers beside them larger than anything else on the chart by an order of magnitude. Days of continuous channeling. Buildings held. A dam sealed. Retaining walls pushed back into place by force of will and the full pressure of the reservoir applied without restraint. The most heroic thing he'd ever done. The most expensive.

He added the columns. The total was a number. Not abstract. Not symbolic. A number, sitting in his chest beside the reservoir, beside the edges he could feel, beside the shape that had dimensions he was only beginning to perceive.

He didn't calculate what was left. Not yet. The addition was enough: the total of what he'd spent, the rough shape of the withdrawal, the pattern visible in the climbing columns. He would do the full calculation later. He would sit down, alone, and be precise. He would use Dorenne's estimates and his own sense of the reservoir and the analytical intelligence that had always been his real weapon, and he would find the number.

Not today. Today, there was a deployment schedule. Three districts. Six sites. The work waiting.

He stepped back from the chart. Dorenne's handwriting at the bottom of the panel, small and precise: *Cumulative estimated expenditure (weeks 1–6): see attached.*

He found the attachment this time. A single sheet of paper, clipped to the panel's frame. A column of numbers, weekly totals, summed at the bottom. The sum was large. Larger than he'd expected, even from his rough arithmetic. Dorenne's staff had been more careful than he thought; they'd accounted for waste heat, for inefficiency, for the overhead of channeling at scale. Their number was higher than his.

He read it. Read it again.

The door opened behind him. Dorenne entered, saw him standing at the chart, and stopped.

They looked at each other across the operations room, the man with the reservoir and the woman with the plan, the early light falling across the four panels between them. She held her morning list. He held nothing.

"The schedule for today," she said. Not a question.

"I have it."

She nodded. Crossed to her desk. Set the list down. Began her day.

He folded the attachment back against the panel's frame and left the room. The number was in his head now, Dorenne's number, the sum of what she'd been counting while he hadn't been. It sat beside the feeling in his chest, the edges he could sense, the floor he couldn't see but knew was there.

The city waited. The work waited. The reservoir, with its new and terrible geometry, waited too.

He walked out into the morning and went to the first site on the list.

* * *

That evening, in his quarters at the command post, he sat on the cot and pressed his palms together and felt for the reservoir.

It was there. Warm. Present. Vast—still vast, still more than anyone alive had ever held, still enormous by any measure except the one that mattered: his own. He held the feeling the way he'd learned to hold channeled force: with attention, with precision, with the awareness of dimension that came from weeks of practice.

The edges were there. Not close. Not crowding him. But present, the way the walls of a large room were present even when you stood in the center, invisible if you didn't think about them, undeniable once you did. The reservoir had a shape. The shape had limits. He didn't look at them too closely.

He thought about the chart. The columns climbing forward across months. The hash marks multiplying. The sum at the bottom of Dorenne's attachment, a number he would carry now the way he carried the reservoir itself, not visible to anyone, not measurable from outside, but present. Always present.

He thought about the shelf. Seven years. Mortise and tenon. The joints that didn't crack.

He thought about the kettle—the tin one, dented, cord-wrapped, steaming on his mother's counter. The copper one in the cabinet, bright and unused. The distance between them measured in something he didn't have a unit for. Something that wasn't energy or power or force. Something older.

He lay down on the cot. The ceiling above him was stone, channeled stone, reinforced, the kind of work that lasted generations when it was done well. Someone had built this ceiling with their hands and their pool, years ago, spending what they had to make something that would stand. The stone was still standing. The person was gone.

The reservoir sat in his chest. The edges sat with it.

He closed his eyes and waited for sleep, and the sleep was a long time coming, and when it came, it didn't carry him very far.
