# Chapter 2

He arrived at the Hall of Infrastructure an hour before dawn, which meant he'd left the apartment ninety minutes before that, which meant he'd been awake since some lightless hour he'd stopped tracking after the second cup of tea. The stairs were the same forty flights he'd climbed yesterday and every day before that, but today they felt different — charged with something, the stone under his feet carrying him upward instead of just enduring his weight.

The ramp Dorenne had shown him was open. A maintenance corridor that curved along the building's outer wall, wide enough for equipment carts, lit by lattice strips that glowed faintly with channeled warmth from the previous shift. He climbed it in long, easy strides, his breath barely quickening, and arrived at the upper level with his heartbeat steady and his vest already damp with sweat that he blotted away before anyone could see.

The infrastructure office was dark. He was the first one here.

He stood in the doorway and let his eyes adjust. The room was large — larger than he'd registered yesterday, when Dorenne had walked him through it with the brisk narration of someone who'd given the tour too many times. High ceilings, arched in the old style, the stonework so precise that the joins were invisible. A long central table covered in rolled charts and weighted with stones that served as paperweights and, he suspected, channeling interfaces. Desks along the walls, each one buried under stacks of reports and assessment folios. A shelf of calibration instruments he couldn't name. Maps pinned to a board showing the city's districts in colored overlays — green for stable, amber for stressed, red for critical. There was more amber than green, and more red than he'd expected.

The room smelled like ink, stone dust, and the faint metallic tang that channeling left in the air — the same ozone sharpness he'd noticed in the market district, but older here, soaked into the walls by decades of concentrated work.

He found what he assumed was the aide's desk — the smallest one, pushed into a corner near the door, bare except for a blotter and a jar of pens. He sat down. He straightened the jar. He looked at the empty desktop and thought: *This is mine.*

The thought was absurd. It was a desk. A surface. But his hands rested on it with a weight that felt like claiming something, and he let them stay there for a long moment before he stood up again, took off his vest, rolled his sleeves, and began trying to make himself useful.

He started with the obvious: the central table was chaos. Reports stacked without visible logic, charts curling at the edges, a cold cup of tea that someone had abandoned days ago judging by the skin floating on its surface. He didn't know the filing system yet, so he didn't move anything — but he sorted by size, aligned the edges, cleared the cup, and wiped down the surface with a cloth he found in a cabinet. Then he moved to the maps on the board, studying the colored overlays until the districts started resolving into names and shapes he recognized. His parents' neighborhood was amber, shading toward the red of the districts below it.

He was still studying the maps when the first of the staff arrived.

* * *

Her name was Tessaly, and she was Dorenne's senior scheduler — a lean, sharp-featured woman in her forties who walked into the office carrying two leather-bound ledgers and a look of permanent assessment. She stopped when she saw Ash standing at the map board.

"You're the new one."

"Since yesterday."

"The one who can't—" She caught herself with the smooth recovery of someone who'd been in institutional settings long enough to know which words landed badly. "The aide. Right. Dorenne mentioned you."

"Good things, I hope."

"She said you were early." Tessaly set her ledgers on the central table and glanced at the cleared surface with an expression that might have been approval or might have been irritation at someone touching her workspace. "She didn't say this early."

"The stairs take longer when you can't use the lift."

"Hmm." She studied him the way she might study a report that had arrived with incomplete data — reserving judgment pending further evidence. "Do you know how we organize the daily briefs?"

"Not yet."

"Then let me show you before Dorenne gets here and assumes you already know."

Tessaly's orientation was thorough and delivered at a pace that suggested she had learned to compress information the way a channeler compressed force — efficiently, without waste, and with the implicit expectation that you would keep up or be left behind. The office managed the systems that kept Solathis functioning: water distribution, structural reinforcement, heating grids, transit networks, and the emergency reserves that were supposed to handle crises but had been quietly drained by the ongoing demands of daily maintenance. Each system had a team. Each team submitted daily assessments. The assessments were consolidated into a brief that Dorenne reviewed every morning before her first meeting, which was always with the Council's resource committee, which was always difficult.

"The brief needs to be on her desk by the seventh bell," Tessaly said. "That means the reports need to be in by the sixth, which means the team leads need to submit by the fifth, which means half of them will be late and you'll need to chase them."

"How do I chase them if I can't use the resonance stones?"

Tessaly paused. He watched her recalculate — a brief, visible adjustment as she accounted for a limitation she wasn't used to working around. Resonance stones were the office's communication backbone: palm-sized stones attuned to each other, activated by channeling, carrying brief messages between paired sets. Every desk had one. Every team lead carried one. The entire logistics of the office flowed through them.

"You walk," she said. "Or you find someone who can ping for you."

"Walking it is."

She gave him a list of the team leads, their locations in the building, and the order in which they were most likely to be late. He memorized it on the first pass — not because he had an extraordinary memory, but because he'd spent his life compensating for the tools he couldn't use by sharpening the ones he could. Names, faces, sequences, spatial relationships. The map in his head was always more detailed than the one on the wall, because the one on the wall assumed you could ask for directions through a resonance stone, and his couldn't.

* * *

The morning unfolded in a blur of names and tasks and the particular exhaustion of learning a new system while simultaneously operating inside it.

He carried documents between floors — physically carried them, in his arms, up and down the ramp and through corridors that were designed for people who could send a channeled pulse ahead of them to announce their arrival. He learned to knock. He learned which doors were latched by channeled locks that he couldn't open and which ones had manual handles. He learned that the records room on the third level was staffed by an elderly archivist named Osten who would talk for twenty minutes about anything if you let him, and that the fastest way to get a document from Osten was to ask him a question about its contents, which flattered his expertise and made him produce it from the stacks with the speed and precision of a man who had organized this room for thirty years and resented anyone who implied it needed reorganizing.

He sat in on the morning brief. Dorenne presided from the head of the central table, her staff arrayed around her in an order that Ash recognized, after twenty minutes of observation, as a hierarchy of function rather than rank. The people closest to her weren't the most senior — they were the ones whose systems were currently in crisis. Today that meant the water team and the structural reinforcement team, whose leads sat at Dorenne's right and left like anxious bookends.

The water team's report was bad. The main aqueduct serving the western districts — the Thornwall line — had developed a channeling fracture at a junction point, reducing flow by a third. The repair required a sustained channeling effort that would pull resources from two other maintenance schedules. The structural team's report was worse: three buildings in the lower districts had been flagged for emergency reshoring, and they didn't have the channelers to cover all three before the weather turned.

Dorenne listened with the focused stillness of someone who was already solving the problem while the problem was still being described. She asked questions — short, precise, cutting through the reports' cautious language to the numbers underneath. How many kilojoules to repair the junction? What was the daily maintenance cost of holding the buildings? Which schedule could absorb the delay?

Ash sat in the corner with a notebook and wrote down everything. Not because he'd been told to — Tessaly had said to listen — but because his mind worked better when his hands were moving, and the act of transcription forced him to parse the technical language in real time. He didn't understand half of what was being discussed. He understood the structure of it — the way resources were being allocated like a finite budget, every expenditure weighed against every alternative, every decision a trade-off between something urgent and something important.

When the brief ended, Dorenne's staff dispersed with the controlled urgency of people who had too much to do and not enough to do it with. Dorenne caught his eye across the room.

"Thoughts?"

He blinked. "On—"

"On anything. You just sat through your first brief. What did you see?"

He'd been hoping to have a few days before she tested him. He'd been hoping for time to learn the vocabulary, understand the systems, build a foundation before anyone asked him to stand on it. But she was watching him with the same direct attention she'd shown in the corridor yesterday — not testing him the way a teacher tested a student, but the way an engineer tested a material. What can this bear?

"The scheduling," he said. "The water team and the structural team are competing for the same channelers. Laris — the water lead — said his repair needs four channelers for three days. Venn — structural — needs three channelers for five days. That's nineteen channeler-days total, and you have maybe twelve available."

"That's the math. What's the solution?"

"I don't know enough to know the solution. But I noticed that Laris's repair is at the Thornwall junction, and two of Venn's buildings are in the Thornwall district. If the same channelers could do the aqueduct repair first and then move to the buildings, they'd save transit time between sites. That's not a solution — it's a scheduling optimization. Maybe half a day saved."

"Half a day is four buildings' worth of heating for the Thornwall district."

"I didn't know that."

"You do now." She almost smiled — not quite, but the architecture of it was there, the way the muscles around her eyes shifted. "Keep taking notes. And learn the district maps — I want you to know every maintenance route in the western quarter by the end of the week."

She turned back to her desk. The conversation was over. Ash sat with his notebook and the faint, disorienting warmth of having been useful — actually useful, not performing usefulness, not making himself small enough to help, but seeing something that mattered and saying it aloud and having it received as if it were a normal thing for him to contribute.

He looked down at his notes. His handwriting was terrible — always had been, the price of hands that learned tools before they learned pens. He didn't care. He turned to a fresh page.

* * *

By midday he'd learned the names of everyone in the office, the location of every supply closet, and the particular sound that Tessaly's chair made when she was about to stand up and deliver a correction. He'd carried fourteen separate documents between floors. He'd been sent to the records room twice and had successfully extracted what he needed from Osten in under five minutes both times by asking about the provenance of the filing system, which turned out to be Osten's doctoral subject from forty years ago.

He'd also made a mistake. A scheduling conflict between two district maintenance teams — the Thornwall crew and the Bridgeway crew — had landed on his desk because Tessaly was in a meeting and the team leads were sending messages faster than anyone could sort them. Both crews needed access to the same section of the western aqueduct at the same time. The Thornwall crew was doing emergency repairs. The Bridgeway crew was doing routine maintenance. Priority should have been obvious, but the Bridgeway lead had a standing authorization from the Council's resource committee, and the Thornwall lead didn't, and the bureaucratic precedence was tangled enough that neither would yield.

Ash read both requests. Then he read them again. Then he did something that felt, in the moment, either very smart or career-endingly presumptuous: he pulled the aqueduct maintenance schedule from the wall and traced the route with his finger.

The Thornwall repair was at junction seven. The Bridgeway maintenance was at junction twelve. Between them — junctions eight through eleven — the aqueduct ran through a section that both crews needed to depressurize before they could work. The conflict wasn't about access to the same physical location. It was about access to the same section of the channeling grid. If one crew depressurized the line, the other couldn't maintain pressure on their end.

But the schedule showed that junction nine had been taken offline three days ago for an unrelated repair. The line was already depressurized between junctions eight and ten. Which meant the Thornwall crew could work from junction seven toward junction nine without affecting the Bridgeway crew's pressure at junction twelve. They didn't need access at the same time. They needed someone to tell them that the gap between them was already open.

He wrote it up. A short note — here's the conflict, here's the schedule, here's junction nine, here's the solution. He walked it down to Tessaly's desk and left it under her ledger where she'd find it when she came back.

Twenty minutes later, Tessaly appeared at his desk. She was holding the note. Her expression was the same one she'd worn that morning — assessment, judgment withheld — but the calibration had shifted.

"This is right," she said.

"I wasn't sure about the pressure tolerances. I assumed—"

"The assumption was correct. Junction nine creates a natural break in the line." She looked at him. "How long have you been reading aqueduct schedules?"

"About two hours."

She set the note down on his desk. "Do you want to see the full maintenance grid?"

"Yes."

"I'll have it sent to your desk. Read it before tomorrow." She turned to go, then paused. "The Thornwall lead's name is Parren. He's been fighting with the Bridgeway crew for six months. You just solved a problem that three senior staff couldn't resolve because none of them bothered to check the offline junction list."

"They probably had more important things to check."

"They probably assumed someone else had checked it." She left.

Ash sat at his desk and looked at the note — his handwriting, his reasoning, his solution — and felt the warmth again. It was different from the warmth of the porridge this morning, different from the warmth of his mother's arms yesterday. This was a professional warmth. The warmth of function. Of a gear finding its housing and beginning to turn.

* * *

The afternoon brought a visiting official from the Council's resource committee — a broad-shouldered man named Aldren whose title Ash didn't catch and whose manner suggested that titles were the most important thing about a person. He arrived at the infrastructure office with two aides of his own and a grievance about the reallocation of channeling resources from his district to the Thornwall emergency repairs.

Ash was at his desk when the argument started. Aldren's voice carried — the kind of volume that came from years of speaking in rooms where everyone else was paid to listen. Dorenne's voice did not carry, which was worse.

"The allocation was approved by the committee three months ago," Aldren was saying. "Three months. My district's heating grid is already behind schedule because your office pulled channelers for that bridge project in the seventh quarter, and now you're telling me you need more?"

"I'm telling you the Thornwall aqueduct is failing," Dorenne said. "Your district's heating grid is important. Clean water is essential."

"Everything is essential. That's what your office says every time you redirect resources. Everything is a crisis. Everything is urgent. And my constituents are the ones who go cold."

The argument continued. Ash watched from his corner, tracking the rhythm of it — the way Aldren escalated and Dorenne absorbed, the way each of them was performing for different audiences. Aldren was performing outrage for his aides, who would carry the story back to his constituents. Dorenne was performing patience for her staff, who needed to see that their director could handle political pressure without flinching.

Nobody was talking about the actual problem, which was that there weren't enough channelers to do both things. The scarcity was real. The argument was theater.

Aldren's frustration was building toward something unproductive — Ash could see it in the set of his shoulders, the way his hands moved when he talked, the particular flush that rose in men who were used to getting what they wanted and had encountered an obstacle that wouldn't yield. In another minute, he was going to say something personal, and Dorenne was going to respond with something precise and devastating, and the meeting was going to end with both of them less willing to cooperate than when it started.

Ash stood up. He crossed to the side table where the tea service sat — an actual kettle on a channeled warming plate, kept hot all day by whoever passed it last — and poured three cups. He brought them to the central table on a tray he'd found in the cabinet that morning.

"Councilor Aldren," he said, setting a cup in front of the man. "I'm new here, so I apologize if this is out of line. But I was looking at the maintenance schedule earlier, and I noticed that the channelers assigned to your district's heating grid are all from the southern pool. Is that right?"

Aldren looked at the tea. He looked at Ash. The interruption had broken his rhythm, which was the point — not to solve the problem, but to change the temperature in the room from confrontation to conversation.

"That's correct," Aldren said, his tone recalibrating from combative to merely suspicious.

"The southern pool shares a rotation with the lower market district. Their schedule has them on a four-day cycle, but the lower market's maintenance was completed early this quarter — I saw the close-out report in the records room. Which means there might be capacity in the rotation that hasn't been reassigned yet."

He didn't know if this was true. He'd seen the close-out report, and he'd seen the southern pool's rotation schedule, and the gap between them suggested available capacity — but he didn't have the technical knowledge to know if the channelers in question could actually be reassigned. What he had was a plausible enough observation to turn the conversation from accusation to investigation.

Dorenne's eyes moved to him. He couldn't read the expression — it was too layered, too controlled — but she didn't shut him down.

"That's worth checking," she said. "Tessaly, pull the southern pool's current rotation and the lower market close-out. Councilor, if there's capacity in the rotation, I'll redirect it to your district's heating grid by end of week. If there isn't, I'll come to your office personally and we'll find another path."

Aldren drank his tea. The flush was receding. "I'd appreciate that," he said, and the words had the cadence of a man who'd been given a way to leave the room without losing face and was willing to take it.

After Aldren and his aides left, the office settled back into its working hum. Dorenne passed Ash's desk on her way to the records room.

"The southern pool rotation," she said. "Was that real?"

"The close-out report was real. The gap in the schedule was real. Whether the capacity is actually available — I don't know."

"It is, as it happens. Half a squad freed up last week." She paused. "You defused that in under a minute."

"I brought him tea. Tea defuses most things."

"You brought him tea and a reason to stop shouting. Those are different skills." She studied him. "You've done that before. Managed someone's anger."

"I grew up in a world where everyone around me could do something I couldn't. You learn to read the room."

She nodded — once, decisive, the nod of someone cataloging an asset. "Tomorrow I want you in the morning meeting from the start. Not in the corner. At the table."

She walked away. Ash sat down and realized his hands were shaking — not from fear, not from the confrontation, but from the sustained effort of performing competence in an environment where he'd expected to fail. The shaking was adrenaline wearing off. He pressed his palms flat against the desk and held them there until they stilled.

* * *

In the late afternoon, with the light going amber through the high windows and the office thinning as staff left for the day, Ash found himself alone in the corridor outside the briefing room. He'd been sent to retrieve a folio that someone had left on the table after the morning brief, and he was on his way back when he heard voices through a door that wasn't fully closed.

Two engineers — he recognized them from the morning meeting, both from Venn's structural team — were bent over a chart spread across a small table. He wouldn't have stopped, except that he heard a word he knew: *Kharren*.

"—fault line's been stable for decades," one of them was saying. "The reinforcements absorb the stress. That's what they're for."

"The reinforcements are weakening." The other engineer — older, with the lined face of someone who spent too much time staring at numbers that worried her — tapped the chart. "Look at the rate. This isn't linear decline. The channeling in the deep anchors is degrading faster than the surface structures, and the gap between them is widening."

"How fast?"

"Fast enough that the projected failure window moved up by three years in the last assessment. The anchors were supposed to last another forty years. Now it's thirty-seven. And the next assessment will probably say thirty-four."

"That's still thirty-four years."

"That's thirty-four years if the decline continues at the current rate. If it accelerates — and everything else is accelerating — it could be twenty. Could be fifteen." She straightened up and rubbed her eyes. "The Kharren Fault has been held in place by channeled reinforcements for six generations. Each generation was stronger than the one that came after it. The anchors were set by people with trickles we can't match anymore. When those anchors fail, there's nothing underneath them but geology, and geology doesn't negotiate."

Ash stood in the corridor with the folio in his hands and the conversation settling into him like cold water. He didn't understand the engineering — the specifics of channeled anchors and stress absorption and fault dynamics were beyond him. But he understood the math. A declining resource. An accelerating demand. A gap that widened with each generation. He'd lived inside that math his entire life. He knew what it felt like when the numbers stopped being theoretical and started being personal.

He filed it away. He'd think about it later — later, when he had the vocabulary to know what questions to ask, and the standing to ask them.

He brought the folio back to Dorenne's desk and said nothing about what he'd overheard.

* * *

He left the Hall of Infrastructure as the last of the light was draining from the sky — the western edge of the plateau catching the sun's final effort, the grand districts above him glowing in borrowed warmth while the lower districts below had already gone cold and gray. He was tired in a way he'd never been tired before: not the physical exhaustion of stairs and labor, which his body knew how to metabolize, but a mental tiredness, the depletion of a mind that had been processing new information at full capacity for ten hours.

He took the ramp down, then the stairs. Forty flights in the growing dark, his legs moving on the autopilot of years. His mind was still in the office — replaying conversations, cataloging faces, sorting the day's information into structures he could access tomorrow. The scheduling conflict. The maintenance grid. Aldren's flush and Dorenne's layered expression. The engineers' conversation about the Kharren Fault, tucked away in a corner of his memory where the things he didn't yet understand went to wait.

He was so deep in his own head that he almost missed her.

"You look like you're solving something."

Ryn was sitting on the low wall at the base of the stairwell where it opened onto the street — the stone wall that ran along the edge of the terrace, looking out over the gorge. She had her legs pulled up, her back against a support column, and a paper bag in her lap that smelled like fried bread and sharp cheese.

"How long have you been waiting?" he asked.

"Not long. Your mother told me what time you'd probably be done. I subtracted twenty minutes because I know you." She held up the bag. "I brought food. You didn't eat lunch."

"How do you know I didn't eat lunch?"

"Because you don't know where to eat lunch yet and you were too embarrassed to ask."

This was so precisely accurate that he couldn't do anything but sit down beside her on the wall and take the bag.

They'd grown up three doors apart. Ryn's family lived in the same building as his — upper floor, better heating, her parents' trickles strong enough to maintain their apartment without the daily struggle that defined his family's existence. She'd been the girl down the hall before she'd been anything else: the first child his age who'd knocked on his door without her parents making her, the first friend who'd beaten him at a game and not apologized for it, the first person outside his family who'd learned the choreography of his limitations and performed it without performing it.

She warmed his cup when it went cold. She lit the path when they walked home late. She channeled the door open when his arms were full. Not as charity — as the automatic, unremarkable courtesy of a friend who happened to have a thing he didn't, the way you'd hold an umbrella over someone who'd forgotten theirs. She never made it an event. She never made it about her generosity or his lack. It was just what you did when you were walking next to someone and the path was dark and your hands could make light and his couldn't.

He'd loved her for that since he was twelve years old, in the quiet, unexamined way you love the person who makes the world livable. He'd never said so. The reasons were practical and terrible: romantic relationships in Solathis were built partly on shared magical intimacy — the vulnerability of letting someone feel your trickle, the domestic magic of shared channeling, the physical closeness that came with attuning to another person's energy. He couldn't offer that. The absence was a wall between them that neither acknowledged and both navigated around, the way you navigate around a piece of furniture that's too heavy to move and too familiar to notice.

Tonight, though, the wall was thin. Tonight everything was thin — the barriers between the person he performed and the person he was, the careful architecture of managed expectations and preemptive self-deprecation that he wore like a second skin. The day had stripped something away. Not the armor — he wasn't ready to lose the armor — but some of the weight behind it.

"So," Ryn said. "First day. Tell me."

He told her. Not the way he'd told his mother — not the raw, cracking-open version — and not the way he'd told Maren — not the polished, edited version. He told her the way he could only tell her: the full thing, with the embarrassing parts intact. The desk in the corner. The cleared table. Tessaly's assessment. The scheduling conflict he'd solved and the aqueduct routes he'd memorized and the moment with Aldren where he'd brought tea to a man who was about to say something stupid and redirected the conversation with a question he wasn't entirely sure was correct.

"Was it correct?" she asked.

"It turned out to be correct. But I didn't know that when I said it."

"You bluffed a Council member."

"I brought a Council member tea and made a reasonable inference."

"You bluffed him with tea."

"If that's what we're calling it."

She was laughing — the real laugh, the one that came from her chest and not from politeness, the one she used when something genuinely surprised her. He'd been cataloging her laughs for years the way he cataloged stairwells and district maps, automatically, without deciding to. The polite laugh was short and high. The real laugh was low and slightly uneven, as if one side of it arrived before the other.

They ate. The fried bread was from the vendor on the lower terrace — the one who worked late because her trickle was too weak to compete with the morning market channelers and their glowing displays and sealed cases. Ryn bought from her because the bread was good and because Ryn had a quiet, stubborn loyalty to people who worked harder than they had to. This was not a thing Ryn would ever say about herself. It was a thing Ash had observed over years of watching her make choices that were slightly harder than they needed to be, for reasons she'd dismiss as practical if you pointed them out.

"Are you going to be all right?" she asked, after the food was gone and they were sitting in the last of the twilight, the gorge below them a darkness that the city's fading channeling couldn't reach. "At the job. Is it going to be—"

"It's going to be fine."

"That's your automatic answer. I'm asking for the real one."

He looked out at the gorge. Below them, the river was a sound more than a sight — a low, constant presence, the one thing in the city that ran on its own power without channeling or maintenance or someone's daily trickle holding it together.

"I don't know," he said. "There are a hundred things I can't do. I can't use the resonance stones. I can't activate the locks. I can't read the channeling instruments. Every system in that office runs through interfaces I can't touch, and every time I need something done that requires even the most basic channeling, I have to ask someone. And asking costs something. Every time I ask, I remind someone that I'm the person who can't, and they recalibrate, and I see them recalibrate, and—"

He stopped. He'd gone further than he meant to. The day's exhaustion had thinned the filter between thinking and saying, and things were coming through that he usually caught before they reached his mouth.

Ryn didn't fill the silence. She was good at that — at letting a pause be a pause instead of rushing to comfort or deflect. She sat with her arms around her knees and waited.

"But today someone asked me for my opinion," he said. "And I had one. And it was the right one. And nobody looked at me like I'd performed a trick."

"They looked at you like a person who had a good idea."

"Yes."

"Imagine that."

He laughed. It came out surprised, which was the only way real laughter came from him — squeezed past the defenses before he could decide whether it was allowed. She smiled at the sound of it, and the smile had the quality of something private and carefully held, like a coin she'd been carrying in her pocket for a long time and was briefly letting catch the light.

They sat. The city dimmed around them, district by district, the heating in the stones fading as the channelers who maintained it went home and slept and let their trickles recover for tomorrow's effort. The sky above the gorge was enormous and unmanaged — no channeled lights, no lattice warmth, just stars appearing in the gaps between clouds with the patient indifference of things that predated magic and would outlast it.

"I should go," he said, eventually. "I need to be there before dawn again."

"Before dawn." She shook her head. "You're going to wear out the stairs."

"The stairs were already worn out. I'm just maintaining the tradition."

He stood, and she stood, and there was a moment — there was always a moment — where the distance between them was exactly the distance across which you'd reach for someone if reaching were an option. He felt it in his hands, the impulse to close the gap, and she felt it too, he was almost certain, in the way she didn't step back but didn't step forward, in the slight shift of her weight that could have been the beginning of movement or the decision against it.

Neither of them moved. Neither of them named it. The moment passed the way all their moments passed — acknowledged by the silence, preserved by the silence, left where it was.

"Tomorrow," she said.

"Tomorrow."

He walked home through the dark streets, his legs tired from the stairs and his mind quiet for the first time all day. The district was shutting down around him — workshops dark, windows curtained, the faint residual warmth in the stones already fading. A man passed him on the street, heading the other direction, and reinforced the paving stone under his foot with a casual touch as he walked — the automatic civic maintenance of someone with trickle to spare, leaving the city slightly stronger behind him. Ash walked over the same stone and left it unchanged.

It didn't sting. Tonight, for the first time he could remember, the small daily evidence of his limitation didn't carry the weight it usually did. Not because anything had changed — his hands were still his hands, empty of channeling, incapable of the basic participation that every other person in Solathis performed without thinking. The absence was the same. The difference was that today he'd found something else to put in the space where the absence lived. Not power. Function. A desk with his name on it, a problem he'd solved, a director who'd put him at the table.

He climbed the five flights to the apartment. His mother had left the stove on low — he could feel the warmth when he opened the door, the particular quality of channeled heat that she maintained through the evening so the apartment would still be warm when he came home. An act of care so automatic she probably hadn't thought about it. A kindness measured in kilojoules and performed in his absence, the way he performed his own kindnesses in hers — the fixed hinges, the laid kindling, the table set before dawn.

He ate standing up in the kitchen — leftover rice, cold, the saffron still fragrant. He wasn't hungry. He ate because his body needed fuel for tomorrow's stairs. He washed the plate by hand. He banked the stove.

In his room — his childhood room, the narrow bed, the window that looked out over the district's uneven roofline — he sat on the edge of the mattress and unlaced his boots. His hands were sore from a day of carrying things. His shoulders ached from the ramp. His mind was still cataloging, still sorting, still running through the day's conversations and filing them into the architecture of understanding he was building about the office, the systems, the people.

He thought about the engineers and the Kharren Fault. About the numbers that moved in the wrong direction, the gap that widened, the geological patience of stone that didn't care about the channeling above it. He didn't know what it meant yet. He knew it mattered.

He thought about Dorenne's nod — the cataloging nod, the one that said *asset identified*. He'd seen his mother nod that way at a cut of meat in the market, assessing value against cost. The comparison was uncomfortable. He let it go.

He thought about Ryn on the wall, laughing. The paper bag. The bread from the vendor who worked late. The uneven laugh and the moment that passed and the silence that held it.

He lay back on the bed and stared at the ceiling, which was stone, which was cracked, which was held together by his parents' channeling and the memory of stronger channeling before it. Above him, the whole city was the same — stone and magic and the slow recession of one from the other, the tide going out so gradually that you could only see it if you measured the waterline against the years.

Tomorrow he would climb the stairs again. He would arrive before dawn. He would learn more names and study more schedules and carry more documents and find more problems he could solve with the tools he had — eyes, hands, a mind that worked, and the relentless, practiced, bone-deep willingness to be useful.

But tonight, sitting on the wall with Ryn, he'd bought them food without counting the cost. He'd reached into his pocket and pulled out coins and paid without the familiar calculus — *can I afford this, does this cut into tomorrow, is this the kind of expenditure that a person who contributes nothing can justify*. He'd just paid. The way people paid. The way you did when the money was earned and the spending was yours to decide and the person sitting next to you was someone you wanted to feed.

Ryn had noticed. He'd seen her notice — the brief flicker of her eyes to his hand and then away, the thing she didn't say, the not-saying louder than any words she could have chosen. She knew what it meant. She knew what all of it meant — the money, the job, the desk, the notes, the fact that tonight he was tired from work instead of tired from performing.

For the first time in as long as he could measure, the happiness wasn't a performance. It wasn't the smile he wore like architecture, the warmth he generated as currency, the cheerfulness that paid his rent in a world that had never charged anyone else admission. It was just happiness. Unstructured. Unperformed. Sitting in his chest like a warmth that didn't require channeling or maintenance or anyone else's trickle to sustain.

The distinction was everything.

He closed his eyes. He slept.
