# Chapter 15

The floor moved.

Not the way floors move when someone walks across them, not the small vibrations of a city built on stone and sustained by effort. This was different. This was the floor deciding it was no longer a floor.

Ash was in Dorenne's office. Morning briefing. He was standing at the deployment map on the eastern wall, pointing to the Threadneedle corridor where the water main had been losing pressure for a week, and Dorenne was at her desk with a pen in her hand and a cup of tea steaming beside her elbow, and Dalla was in the doorway with a stack of inspection reports, and the light through the tall windows was the pale gold of early autumn, and the day was a day like any other day, and then it wasn't.

The pen rolled off Dorenne's desk.

A small thing. The kind of thing you notice and don't notice — your eyes track it and your brain dismisses it. The pen rolling. The tea rippling in its cup. A low sound from somewhere deep, not a sound you heard with your ears but with your feet, with your teeth, with the base of your skull where the oldest part of your brain lived and remembered what it meant when the ground stopped being solid.

"Was that—" Dalla said.

The windows shattered.

Not cracked. Not splintered. Shattered — all of them, all at once, the tall panes that ran floor to ceiling in the Director's office exploding inward in a spray of glass that caught the morning light and turned it into something terrible, something bright and sharp and everywhere. Ash threw his arm up. Glass hit his forearm, his shoulder, the side of his neck. Dalla screamed. The sound was swallowed by a deeper sound — a roar that came from below them and around them and through them, a sound that had no direction because it was everywhere, because it was the building itself, the stone and timber and channeled foundations of the Hall of Infrastructure resonating with something that was bigger than sound, older than sound, the voice of the planet speaking through the bones of everything that had been built on top of it.

The floor tilted.

Ash grabbed the edge of the deployment table. His fingers closed on wood and the wood was vibrating so fast it felt alive, felt like something thrumming, and the map pinned to its surface jumped and blurred and the colored markers he'd placed that morning — red for critical, yellow for scheduled, blue for complete — scattered across the tilting surface and rolled and fell and the blue ones hit the floor and bounced and kept bouncing because the floor wouldn't stop moving.

*Earthquake.*

The word arrived in his mind with a clarity that cut through the roar. He'd read the reports. He'd filed them himself — the Kharren Fault assessment, the declining reinforcement projections, the annual stress analysis that had been flagged as "manageable" in Dorenne's neat handwriting three months ago. Manageable. The building groaned around him like a ship in a storm and the groan was the sound of manageable arriving at its limit.

"Out!" Dorenne was on her feet. Glass in her hair. Blood on her hand where she'd braced against the desk. Her voice carried the way it always carried — precise, commanding, louder than the circumstances that surrounded it. "Everyone out of the building. Now. Use the stairs. Do not use the transit shafts."

Ash was already moving. The floor bucked under him and he staggered, caught himself on the doorframe, felt the doorframe shift in its housing — stone grinding against stone, the joints that had been channeled true a hundred years ago losing their alignment in a single shuddering breath. He grabbed Dalla's arm. She was pressed against the wall, reports scattered around her feet, her face white. He pulled her toward the corridor.

The corridor was wrong. The floor sloped where it shouldn't slope. The ceiling had cracked — a single clean line running the length of the hallway, plaster dust falling from it in a steady white rain. People were emerging from offices on both sides, stumbling, shouting, a man carrying a ledger as if the numbers inside it still mattered. The building shook again — a second wave, deeper than the first, and Ash felt it in his chest, the Wellspring stirring as if the ground's shaking had reached something inside him. Not real resonance — not the reservoir responding to the quake — but the overwhelming wrongness of standing on moving ground while power pressed at his palms with nowhere to go.

"Stairs," he said to Dalla. "Can you walk?"

She nodded. Her hands were shaking. Everyone's hands were shaking, or everything was shaking, and the distinction had stopped mattering.

They reached the stairwell. The stairs were stone — carved from the plateau rock a century ago, the treads worn smooth by ten thousand days of feet. The stone held. He could feel it holding, could feel the deep-set channeling in the foundation absorbing the shockwaves the way it had been designed to absorb them, the ancient reinforcement doing its work. The Hall of Infrastructure was one of the strongest buildings in Solathis. It had been built by the generation that knew what the Kharren Fault could do, built to withstand exactly this.

But the groaning didn't stop.

Down. Two flights, three, the stairwell shaking around them, dust in the air thick enough to taste. Dorenne was ahead of him — she'd passed them on the second landing, moving fast, her hand on the railing, her mouth set in a line that meant she was calculating, always calculating, running the numbers even as the building tried to shake itself apart. Staff followed in a ragged line. Someone was crying. Someone else was channeling — Ash could feel it, a frantic pulse of energy directed into the wall, a maintenance worker trying to reinforce the stairwell as they descended through it. A small pool. A young worker. The channeling was desperate and inefficient, energy bleeding off as heat, and the wall warmed under Ash's palm as he brushed past it.

He could help. The thought arrived with the same clarity as the word *earthquake* — clean, immediate, obvious. He could pour force into the foundation, stabilize the stairwell, hold the walls steady. His hand was already on the stone. The Wellspring was already reaching, the reservoir responding to the vibration the way a tuning fork responds to the right pitch — sympathetic resonance, the power inside him wanting to meet the power shaking the ground.

Not yet. Get out first. Get everyone out.

Ground level. The doors were jammed — the frame had shifted, stone settling into a new geometry that hadn't existed three minutes ago. Ash put his shoulder against the door and shoved. It didn't move. He channeled — a burst of force, raw and blunt, the door exploding outward off its hinges and clattering across the courtyard stones. Too much. Wildly too much. He didn't care.

Daylight. Dust. The courtyard of the Hall of Infrastructure was cracked — a fissure running diagonally from the fountain to the gate, wide enough to catch a foot. The fountain had stopped. The water in its basin sloshed and spilled over the rim in rhythmic waves that matched the shaking, and the shaking was still happening, still building, the ground beneath his feet rolling like something breathing, like the plateau itself was drawing a long, deep breath it had been holding for three hundred years.

People poured out of the Hall behind him. Dalla. The maintenance staff. Clerks and engineers and deployment coordinators, all of them stumbling into the courtyard with dust in their hair and fear on their faces. Dorenne was already at the gate, looking out into the city, and the expression on her face—

Ash turned to look.

Solathis was screaming.

* * *

The sound came first. Before the sight, before the understanding — the sound. A chorus of stone and terror that rose from the city like heat rising from a fire, layered and enormous and impossible to parse into its components. Stone cracking. Stone falling. People shouting. People screaming. The deep structural groan of buildings flexing beyond their tolerance. The sharper crack of masonry separating from masonry. And beneath it all, the bass note — the fundamental — the Kharren Fault itself, still speaking, still releasing centuries of accumulated stress in a voice that vibrated at the frequency of bedrock.

Ash stood in the courtyard and the courtyard shook beneath him and the city spread out below the Hall's elevation and he could see it. He could see it all.

The Hall of Infrastructure sat on the Third Terrace — the border between elder and middle districts, high enough to look down over the descending tiers of the city, all the way to the gorge rim where the poorest buildings clung to the edge of the plateau. On a normal morning, the view was the city at work: smoke from kitchens, the pale glow of heating lattices in walls, the movement of people on walkways and bridges, the vertical transit shafts rising and falling with their loads. Solathis in its daily rhythm, spending itself to survive.

This morning, the city was coming apart.

The elder districts held. He could see it — the First and Second Terraces above him, where the strongest residents lived, where the strongest residents maintained buildings that had been reinforced for a century. Stone cracked and was repaired in the same breath. He watched an elderly man on a balcony three streets up press his palms to a fracturing wall and the fracture sealed itself in a pulse of warmth that Ash felt from fifty meters away — casual, practiced, a shrug of power that would cost the man a fraction of his morning. A bridge between two elder-district towers swayed and steadied, the residents at either end channeling in concert, their combined pools more than enough to absorb the stress. Dust rose from the elder districts. Damage was real. But the damage was met, answered, held.

The middle districts cracked.

He could see it spreading. The Third Terrace — his terrace, Dorenne's terrace, the institutional heart of the city — taking hits that the residents could only partially absorb. A wall collapsed on Threadneedle Lane — he recognized the building he'd reinforced weeks ago, and the reinforcement held, the stone solid where his power had fused it, but the buildings behind it lost their eastern faces, masonry sliding off like skin peeling from bone. Transit shafts buckled. He watched one of the vertical lift shafts — the one near Soren's Crossing — crumple at its midpoint, the channeled column of force that held it rigid failing as the shaft operator's pool gave out, and the shaft folded like a bent reed, slowly, almost gently, dust blooming from the break point. A water main burst on the Fourth Terrace, sending a sheet of water across a residential street, the water catching the morning light and turning it silver.

The maintenance workers were channeling. He could feel them — dozens, maybe hundreds of small pools flaring to life across the middle districts, desperate hands pressed against cracking walls, frantic effort poured into systems that were failing faster than any one person could hold them. Young workers. Middle-generation workers. People with barely enough to warm a room, spending everything they had in the first ninety seconds because the shaking wasn't stopping and the buildings were cracking and there was nothing else to do, no other option, no response that wasn't *pour yourself into the stone and hope it's enough.*

It wasn't enough.

The young and poor districts collapsed.

Below the Fourth Terrace, below the middle band where things worked but barely, the city's lower tiers came apart with a sound like the world ending. Ash watched a residential block on the Fifth Terrace fold — the entire structure, five stories of stone and timber, the channeled reinforcement in its joints failing simultaneously because the reinforcement had been thin for years, maintained by overstretched workers whose pools couldn't reach the specifications the building had been designed to demand. The block didn't explode. It folded. The upper floors tilted inward, the walls leaning toward each other like hands closing in prayer, and then the whole structure descended into itself with a grinding roar that ate every other sound in the city for the space of three heartbeats.

Dust billowed up from the collapse — a grey-white cloud that rolled outward and upward, catching the morning light, turning the Fifth Terrace into a landscape of shadow and powder. And before the dust had settled, another building went. And another. The collapses cascaded down the terraces like dominoes falling downhill — each failure shaking the ground enough to trigger the next, each next building weaker than the last because the lower you went in Solathis, the weaker the pools that maintained the structures, the thinner the reinforcement, the longer the list of deferred repairs.

The gorge rim. The lowest tier. The poorest district in the city, where families lived in buildings that hadn't been properly maintained in a decade, where the maintenance crews were young people with tiny pools who showed up every day and poured everything they had into structures that needed ten times what they could give. The gorge rim came apart like wet paper. Whole blocks disintegrated. The sound was — he had no word for it. Not a crash. Not a roar. A destruction. The sound of hundreds of homes ceasing to be homes at the same time.

Ash stood in the courtyard of the Hall of Infrastructure and watched the city stratify itself in rubble, and his hands were shaking and the Wellspring was screaming inside his chest and the ground was still moving beneath his feet and he couldn't breathe.

"Ash."

Dorenne's voice. Close. Sharp. He turned. She was standing beside him with glass still in her hair and blood still on her hand and a look on her face that he recognized — the look of someone who had just watched the worst-case scenario from the Kharren Fault assessment come true, the one she'd marked "manageable" and filed and moved on from because there were fourteen other crises on her desk and you couldn't manage everything, you couldn't prepare for everything, you had to triage and this was the thing that got triaged.

"How bad?" he said. The words came out hoarse.

She didn't answer immediately. She was looking at the lower city — at the dust cloud rolling across the gorge rim, at the silhouettes of buildings that were no longer buildings, at the absence of structures that had been there thirty seconds ago.

"Bad," she said.

* * *

The shaking stopped.

Not all at once — it faded, the way a struck bell fades, the vibrations diminishing in waves that grew longer and shallower until the ground was still, or almost still, or still enough that the difference between stillness and tremor was smaller than the shaking in Ash's own legs.

The silence that followed was worse than the sound.

It wasn't silence, exactly. The city was full of noise — shouting, crying, the creak and groan of damaged structures settling into new positions, the hiss of ruptured water lines, the crackle of heating lattices that had overloaded and set their walls smoldering. But beneath all of that, the fundamental had stopped. The bass note. The voice of the Kharren Fault. The ground was quiet again, and the quiet was the quiet of something finished, something spent, something that had said everything it had to say and was done.

Ash's hands were on his knees. He was bent over in the courtyard, breathing hard, though he hadn't run. The dust was settling around him in a fine grey layer. It was on his tongue. In his eyes. On his skin. He straightened up and looked at the city and the city looked different — not just damaged but *revealed*, as if the earthquake had peeled away a layer of pretense and shown the truth that had been underneath all along.

The elder districts were bruised. Cracks in facades. A collapsed garden wall. A bridge with a new lean to it. Damage that would be repaired by dinner — a day's inconvenience for people with the power to erase it.

The middle districts were wounded. Missing walls. Buckled streets. The transit system down. Water running where it shouldn't run. Weeks of repair work, maybe months, a drain on pools that were already stretched thin. Livable but diminished. The difference between comfort and survival made visible in broken stone.

The lower districts were gone.

Not entirely. Not every building. But enough. Enough that the skyline of the Fifth Terrace and the gorge rim had changed — gaps where structures had been, rubble fields where streets had been, dust-choked voids where families had been eating breakfast three minutes ago. Ash looked at the lower city and his mind did what his mind always did: it calculated. It counted. The number of buildings in the gorge-rim district — roughly six hundred. The percentage that appeared to have collapsed — he couldn't tell exactly from this distance, but the dust cloud, the changed skyline, the sheer *absence* — forty percent. Maybe fifty. Six hundred buildings at forty percent was two hundred and forty buildings. Average occupancy per building in the gorge-rim district was — he'd seen the census reports on Dorenne's desk — eight to twelve families. Two hundred and forty buildings times ten families was two thousand four hundred families. Eight thousand people. Ten thousand.

The numbers kept multiplying. He couldn't stop them. The middle districts. The Fifth Terrace. The transitional blocks between the maintained and the unmaintained. The numbers stacked and grew and his mind fed them more data — the Kharren Fault stress models, the structural assessment reports, the maintenance backlog that listed every building in the city that was running on too little channeling — and the numbers got worse and worse and he was standing in a courtyard doing arithmetic while people were buried.

"Ash." Dorenne again. Closer now. Her hand on his arm. "I need you to focus."

He looked at her.

"I'm dispatching every maintenance crew we have. The Council will declare emergency within the hour. I need to know—" She paused. The pause was unusual for her. Dorenne did not pause. "How much can you do?"

The question landed in his chest. Not in his mind — in his chest, where the Wellspring sat, where the reservoir hummed with its deep, patient warmth. How much can you do. The question every channeler answered every day — how much is in your pool, how far will it stretch, what can you afford. For everyone else in the city, the answer was a daily calculation, a careful rationing. For Ash, the answer was—

He reached inward. The Wellspring was there. Vast. Oceanic. Formless — no ceiling he could feel, no edges, just depth after depth after depth. More than any work crew. More than any department. More than any district.

"A lot," he said. "I can do a lot."

She nodded. Something moved behind her eyes — something he'd seen before when a resource came through at the last moment. She had the Wellspring. The city had the Wellspring. The worst day in Solathis's history, and she had the single most powerful tool any Director of Infrastructure had ever commanded.

"The gorge rim first," she said. "Structural triage. Find what's still standing and hold it. Find who's trapped and get them out. I'll send crews behind you."

He was already moving.

* * *

The walk down broke him.

Not all at once. Not in a single moment. The breaking happened in increments — terrace by terrace, street by street, each step deeper into the city's damage a step further from the assumption that Solathis was fundamentally sound, that the magic would hold, that the systems Dorenne managed and Ash reinforced and everyone depended on were adequate to the task of keeping people alive.

The Third Terrace was manageable. Cracked facades. A collapsed awning. Glass everywhere — every window in the district had shattered, and the streets glittered with it, a carpet of sharp light that crunched under his boots. People were outside, dazed, helping each other. An elderly woman was channeling a wall repair, her hands steady, her expression annoyed rather than frightened. A man was sweeping glass from his doorstep. The Third Terrace had resources. The Third Terrace would be fine.

The Fourth Terrace was harder. He came around the corner onto Millward Street and the retaining wall he'd reinforced — his work, his power, solid and holding — was a dam against a tide of destruction. Behind it, the residential towers had lost their eastern faces. He could see into apartments — beds, tables, a chair on a ledge that had been a floor. A woman was standing on a surviving balcony, three stories up, holding a child. She was looking down at the street with an expression that Ash recognized because he'd spent twenty-three years wearing versions of it: the expression of someone calculating how bad things were and arriving at a number they couldn't manage.

He channeled upward. Force into the balcony's support structure, reinforcing the stone, stabilizing the cantilever. It was crude — too much energy, too broad a focus, heat bleeding off into the air around him — but the balcony stopped swaying. The woman looked down at him. He called up to her: "Stay there. Rescue crew is coming."

She nodded. She didn't ask who he was. She held the child tighter.

He kept moving. Down the switchback stairs between the Fourth and Fifth Terraces — the stairs cracked now, treads shifted out of alignment, one section missing entirely where the stone had sheared and tumbled into the street below. He jumped the gap. Below him, the Fifth Terrace spread out in a landscape he didn't recognize.

He had walked through this district two weeks ago. He'd reinforced a heating grid. He'd passed the bakery on the corner of Threadneedle and Lowgate where the bread was cheap and the woman behind the counter called everyone *love*. He'd seen the children's school — a squat stone building with murals painted on the outside walls, bright colors fading in the weather, a building that was always slightly too cold because the channeling crew responsible for its heating had three members with a combined daily pool that couldn't warm the main hall for an hour.

The bakery was rubble. The corner where it had stood was a mound of broken stone and timber, dust still rising from it in slow curls. The school — he could see part of it. One wall still stood. The mural faced outward, a painting of children playing in a field of flowers, and the colors were still bright, still cheerful, and the wall behind the mural was open air where there should have been a building.

The sounds changed as he descended. The upper terraces had given him the sounds of shock — exclamations, the crunch of glass, the controlled channeling of people with power enough to respond. The Fifth Terrace gave him the sounds of desperation. Screaming. Not the sharp, startled screams of the first moments — sustained screaming, the sound of people trapped under stone, the sound of people digging through stone to reach other people, the sound of voices calling names into rubble that didn't answer.

A man was kneeling in the street. His hands were on a collapsed wall — a section of residential building that had pancaked, floor onto floor, five stories compressed into two stories of jagged wreckage. He was channeling. Ash could feel it — a weak pulse, a young pool, everything the man had directed into the stone in a hopeless attempt to lift what couldn't be lifted by one person, by twenty people, by a hundred people with pools this size. The man's hands were bleeding where the stone had cut them. His face was twisted with the effort. The channeling was doing almost nothing — shifting rubble by inches, the energy hemorrhaging as heat, the man's shirt darkening with sweat in the cool morning air.

"Help me," the man said. He wasn't looking at Ash. He was looking at the rubble. "My wife is in there. My daughter. They're in there. I can hear them. Help me."

Ash put his hands on the stone.

The Wellspring surged. He didn't control it — it responded to the contact the way it always responded, the reservoir rushing to meet the need, power flooding through his hands into the collapsed structure. He could feel the entire wreckage — the compressed floors, the twisted beams, the spaces between, the gaps where air still existed, where people might still be breathing. He pushed. The rubble shifted. A section of wall lifted — not smoothly, not carefully, but upward, away, the stone grinding as it moved, dust cascading from the edges.

"There!" The man scrambled forward into the gap Ash had opened. "Lenna! Lenna, can you hear me?"

A sound from inside. Thin. Alive.

Ash held the rubble up and the man crawled in and the Wellspring burned through energy at a rate he couldn't calculate and didn't try to calculate. Two figures emerged from the gap — a woman, bleeding from the head, carrying a girl of maybe five whose eyes were wide and white and perfectly silent, the silence of a child who had gone past screaming into something quieter and worse.

The man took his daughter. He pressed his face into her hair and his whole body shook and he didn't say anything and Ash lowered the rubble back down — gently, slowly, because the man and his wife were still in the gap's shadow — and the stone settled with a sound like a long exhale and the gap closed and the wreckage was solid again and whatever else was underneath it would stay underneath it, and Ash straightened up and looked at his hands and they were grey with dust and the dust was warm where the waste heat from his channeling had heated it and the warmth was obscene, a warmth made from his own inefficiency while people were buried in cold stone.

He kept moving.

* * *

The gorge rim was a different world.

He'd thought the Fifth Terrace was bad. The gorge rim made the Fifth Terrace look lucky. The buildings here had been the weakest in the city — the oldest structures, built in the high-magic era from stone that expected perpetual reinforcement, maintained by workers who had less to give every year. The earthquake had treated them the way a boot treats paper.

Whole streets were unrecognizable. The layout he'd memorized from Dorenne's deployment maps — the grid of narrow roads and tight residential blocks that pressed up against the plateau's edge — had been rewritten in rubble. Buildings he'd never reinforced, never visited, never been scheduled to visit because there were fourteen systems at critical status and the gorge rim was number fifteen on a list that only had funding for ten. Buildings that had been someone's home an hour ago and were now a pile of stone no taller than his waist.

People were digging. Everywhere he looked, people were digging. With hands. With broken wood. With fragments of the buildings that had fallen on them. Young people, mostly — the gorge rim was young, the cheapest district, the place you lived when your pool was too small for anywhere else. They dug with the energy of the desperate and the efficiency of the untrained and the courage of people who had no other option, because no maintenance crew was coming, no elder channeler was walking down the terraces to help, no institutional response was fast enough for the people who were running out of air under the rubble right now, in this minute, while the upper city counted its cracked windows and repaired them before lunch.

Ash channeled. He moved through the gorge rim like something unleashed — lifting rubble, bracing collapsing walls, opening gaps where people were trapped. The Wellspring poured out of him in great uncontrolled waves of force, the efficiency irrelevant, the waste heat irrelevant, the only relevant thing the speed at which he could move stone and the number of people he could reach before they stopped making sounds from underneath it. He pulled a family from a collapsed basement — two adults, three children, all alive, all dust-covered and shaking. He braced a leaning tower that was swaying over a street full of people — held it steady with both hands pressed to the stone, power flooding the structure, the building groaning and creaking and finally settling, and the people in the street ran while he held it and the running was the sound of lives being saved and the sound was the only sound that mattered.

He lost track of time. The sun moved. The dust settled and rose and settled again as buildings continued to crumble in slow aftershock collapses — structures that had survived the initial quake giving up their last resistance, sagging, folding, the delayed casualties of reinforcement that had finally run out. He was everywhere. He was at the water main break on Gorge Street, channeling the ruptured stone pipe closed before the flood reached the populated blocks. He was at the collapsed school annex on Rimfall Lane, where a teacher had shielded three students in a channeled bubble that was failing, her pool almost empty, her face rigid with the effort of maintaining something she couldn't maintain for one more minute — and he fed power into her barrier and felt it snap solid and the teacher's legs gave out beneath her and one of the students caught her and held her up.

He was at the edge. The edge of the plateau, where a section of the gorge rim had sheared away entirely — a chunk of the city breaking off like ice calving from a glacier, sliding down the cliff face toward the river gorge below. Three buildings on that section. Three buildings still occupied. He stood at the new edge of the world with the gorge below him — two hundred meters of empty air and broken stone — and he channeled downward, channeled into the cliff face, channeled force into the sliding rock with everything he could reach and the Wellspring reached far, reached deep, and the slide slowed and stopped and the three buildings sat at an angle on their new cliff-face perch and the people inside them were alive and the people inside them needed to be evacuated immediately because the rock he was holding would not hold forever, not even for him, not even for the Wellspring.

"Get them out!" he shouted. His voice was hoarse. He didn't know when it had gone hoarse. The dust, maybe. The screaming he didn't remember doing. "Get them out now!"

Young channelers. Three of them — a man and two women, barely older than Ash, gorge-rim residents with small pools and terrified faces. They scrambled to the tilting buildings. They channeled reinforcement into the walls — small, precise amounts, everything they had — and they helped the residents out, one by one, an elderly couple from the first building, a young mother and two children from the second, a man alone from the third who kept saying "my tools, I need my tools" and the young channeler guiding him out said "we'll come back for your tools" and the lie was obvious but the lie was necessary.

Ash held the cliff face until the last person was clear. Then he released it. The section of plateau slid in a slow, grinding descent — stone on stone, a geological sound, the sound of the ground reclaiming what the channelers had borrowed from it three hundred years ago. It took a long time to reach the bottom. The sound of impact carried upward through the rock, a deep thud that Ash felt in his knees.

Three buildings. Gone. Three addresses that would never exist again. The city was smaller now than it had been this morning.

He reached inward — briefly, the way you touched a pocket to check its contents. The reservoir was there. Still vast. But it had a direction now. It went one way. He noted it and let it go.

He turned back from the edge. The young channelers were standing in a rough group, the rescued residents behind them, and one of the young women was looking at Ash and her expression was — he couldn't read it. Not gratitude. Not awe. Something harder. Something that had a question in it.

"Where were the reinforcement teams?" she said.

Ash blinked. "What?"

"The Kharren Fault teams. The ones who were supposed to keep this from happening." She gestured at the rubble, at the absence where three buildings had been, at the dust that covered everything. "Where were they?"

"The fault monitoring teams have been—" He stopped. He'd read the reports. He'd filed the reports. Declining pool strength in the reinforcement crews. Reduced shift coverage. Budget constraints. Dorenne's assessments: *manageable*. "The crews have been understaffed."

"Understaffed." The word came back at him like a thrown stone. She was young — his age, maybe younger, with dark hair cut short and callused hands and a smear of masonry dust across her forehead. Kael — he would learn the name later, from a broadsheet, from a quoted voice he recognized before he recognized the name. The particular anger of someone who was looking at the ruins of their neighborhood and seeing a pattern. "The upper terraces held. We collapsed. How is that understaffed? That's a choice. That's someone choosing where to send the crews and where not to. That's someone deciding which buildings get reinforced and which buildings get—" She waved at the rubble. "This."

He opened his mouth to respond. The words that came were Dorenne's words — allocation, triage, resource constraints, the mathematics of insufficient pools distributed across too many systems. The words were accurate. The words were true. The words were also the words that a woman on the Third Terrace would say to a woman on the gorge rim, and the distance between those two terraces was everything that was wrong with the city, measured in broken stone.

"I know," he said. It was all he could think of.

"You know." She looked at his hands — at the power still crackling faintly around his fingers, the residual channeling visible as heat shimmer in the cool air. "You're the Wellspring. You could have reinforced every building on this rim. You reinforced the Threadneedle building instead. The heating grid in the eastern district. The southern cisterns. All up there." She pointed upward, toward the terraces above them. "All above us."

His chest tightened. The Wellspring hummed behind his ribs and the hum felt different now — not warm, not reassuring, but heavy. She was wrong. The deployments had been chosen by need — by structural urgency, by population served, by Dorenne's triage calculations that weighed every system against every other system and chose the ones that mattered most. The gorge rim had been on the list. The gorge rim had been number fifteen. And number fifteen had collapsed while numbers one through ten were reinforced and fine.

"I'm here now," he said.

"Now," she said. The word held everything.

Behind her, another young channeler — the man who'd helped evacuate the cliff-face buildings — was on his knees beside a collapsed wall, his hands pressed to the stone, his pool spent, his channeling producing nothing but heat. He was trying anyway. His face was streaked with dust and tears and the effort of pushing against something immovable with everything he had and arriving at nothing. A woman next to him — older, middle-generation, her arm in a makeshift sling — put her hand on his shoulder. She wasn't channeling. She was just standing there. The gesture was small and enormous and Ash looked away from it because looking at it hurt in a place the Wellspring couldn't reach.

The young woman was still watching him. Waiting. Not for a speech. Not for a promise. Waiting the way you waited when you already knew the answer and wanted to hear someone say it out loud.

"I know," he said.

She held his gaze for a long moment. Then she turned away and went back to digging.

* * *

He worked until the light changed.

The afternoon came and went. The rescue crews arrived — Dorenne's people, maintenance workers from the middle districts, off-duty channelers who'd come down from the upper terraces. They came with tools and pool strength and organizational efficiency and they were too late for some of the people trapped under the rubble and exactly on time for others. The line between the two was the cruelest calculation Ash had ever witnessed — the difference between the family pulled out alive after six hours under collapsed stone and the family pulled out silent after seven, the gap between those two numbers so small and so absolute that it made him want to scream.

He didn't scream. He channeled. He moved from site to site, lifting, bracing, reinforcing. His hands were on the stone and the Wellspring flowed and the reservoir diminished by amounts he didn't track and wouldn't track until later, until the numbers mattered in a different way, until the cost of this day became the cost of something else he couldn't do.

But that was later. That was a reckoning for a quieter hour.

Now was the sound of stone shifting. Now was the cry of a child found alive under a bed frame. Now was the grunt of effort as a team of young channelers and an old man with a strong pool worked together to clear a street, the old man's power doing in one push what the young people couldn't do in ten, and the young people watching him do it with expressions that mingled gratitude and something sharper, something that didn't have a name yet but would, soon, in the weeks after the dust settled.

Ash found a girl. Eight years old, maybe nine. Alone under a collapsed stairwell — the heavy stone treads had fallen in a pattern that created a pocket, a small triangle of air in the wreckage. She was conscious. She was still. Her legs were pinned under a block of masonry too heavy for anyone but him.

He lifted it. Carefully — not the wild surge of force he'd been using all day, but slowly, controlled, because the block was on her legs and the block was heavy and moving it wrong would crush what it was currently only trapping. He lifted and the girl didn't scream. She looked at him with enormous dark eyes and said, very quietly: "My cat is still in there."

Something in his chest broke. Not the Wellspring. Something behind it, something underneath it, something that the power couldn't armor. He set the block aside. He picked the girl up. She was light — light and warm and alive, and the aliveness of her was the most important thing that had ever existed.

"We'll find your cat," he said.

He didn't know if the cat was alive. He didn't know if the building had a survivable space for a cat. He said it anyway, the way the young channeler had said *we'll come back for your tools*, and the words were a bridge over an unbearable gap and the bridge held because it needed to hold.

He carried the girl to the medical station the rescue crews had set up on the one clear stretch of road in the gorge-rim district. A healer was there — an elder, her pool deep, her hands steady, the kind of healer the gorge rim hadn't seen in years because elder healers worked the upper terraces where the pay was better and the patients had insurance. The healer took the girl. Examined her legs. Began channeling — warm, precise, the focused energy of someone who knew exactly what she was doing.

"Fractures," the healer said, not looking up. "Both legs. She'll walk. Not today."

Ash nodded. His throat was closed around something that wouldn't dissolve. He turned back toward the rubble.

The sun was setting. The light had gone gold and then amber and then the deep burnt orange that meant the day was ending, and the dust in the air caught the light and turned it to something that would have been beautiful in any other context — the whole city glowing in a haze of sunset and destruction, the colors warm and terrible over the broken stone.

He stopped. Not because he chose to stop. Because his body stopped — his legs refusing to take another step, his hands hanging at his sides, the muscles in his back and shoulders and neck aching with a physical exhaustion that shouldn't exist, because the Wellspring carried no bodily cost, because the power didn't tire him, because the tiredness was something else entirely. Not depletion. Not fatigue. The weight of what he'd seen pressing down on him until his spine couldn't carry it standing up.

He sat down on a block of masonry. Someone's wall. Someone's home. The stone was cold under him and the cold was the cold of a building that would never be warm again because the people who warmed it were somewhere under the rubble or somewhere at the medical station or somewhere gone.

The city spread around him. Above, the elder districts were lighting up — heating lattices glowing, windows warm, the nightly rhythm of people who had enough. Below, the gorge rim was dark. No lattices. No warmth. Just the scattered light of rescue teams' channeled lamps, small orbs of illumination in a landscape of shadow and broken stone.

The stratification. He'd known it existed — he'd lived in it, he'd mapped it on Dorenne's deployment charts, he'd filed the reports that documented it in careful, institutional language. *Uneven distribution of maintenance resources. Declining pool coverage in lower terraces. Structural risk assessments prioritized by population density and strategic value.* The language of systems and optimization. The language that turned *the poor districts are falling apart* into *resource allocation reflects current constraints.*

Now the language was rubble. The language was a girl with two broken legs and a missing cat. The language was a young woman's voice saying *Where were our reinforcements?* and the answer being: somewhere else. Somewhere higher. Somewhere that mattered more on the charts.

He looked at his hands. Dust-grey. Warm. The Wellspring hummed inside him — diminished, but vast. Still vast. He had poured power into this district for hours and the reservoir was still there, still deep, still more than anyone else in the city could comprehend. He could feel it the way he always felt it — an ocean behind his ribs, patient and immense.

But the ocean had done nothing for the buildings that fell this morning. The ocean had been deployed to the upper terraces, to the strategic priorities, to the systems that Dorenne's charts ranked above the gorge rim. The ocean had been his — his to direct, his to spend — and he had spent it where he was told to spend it, and the people who died today died in buildings he'd never been sent to reinforce.

*I could have been here.*

The thought arrived without ceremony. Not an accusation. Not a judgment. Just the truth, sitting in his chest beside the Wellspring like a stone that the power couldn't lift.

*I could have been here. I could have reinforced these buildings. I had enough. I had more than enough. I was reinforcing heating grids and retaining walls and transit shafts while these buildings sat on Dorenne's list at number fifteen and nobody sent me down here because the numbers said other things mattered more.*

The numbers were right. The Threadneedle building held eight families. The eastern heating grid served four thousand people. The southern cisterns supplied drinking water to six districts. The numbers were right and the numbers were monstrous and the numbers had left the gorge rim to die.

Not die. That wasn't fair. The earthquake was a natural disaster. The Kharren Fault had released centuries of stress. No deployment plan could have prevented it. No amount of Wellspring power poured into the gorge rim would have stopped the ground from shaking.

But it could have held the buildings up.

He sat on the cold stone and the Wellspring hummed and something was changing. Not in the reservoir. In the space behind the reservoir, where he was still the person he'd been before any of this. Something shifting there — a splinter he couldn't quite reach, couldn't quite name, couldn't quite ignore.

He stood up. His legs ached. His hands were filthy and warm with his own waste heat. The rescued families were huddled along the clear road, their faces lit by channeled lamps that floated above them like small, steady stars.

He looked at them. He looked at the dark gorge rim and the lit upper terraces and the line between them.

The ground had spoken. He didn't have an answer yet.
